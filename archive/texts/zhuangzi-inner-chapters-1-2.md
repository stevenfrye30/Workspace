# Zhuangzi, Inner Chapters 1–2

The first two of the Zhuangzi's Inner Chapters open one of the two principal works of classical Daoist literature, alongside the Daodejing. Inner Chapter 1, *Xiaoyao You* — "Free and Easy Wandering" — opens with the giant fish Kun who becomes the Peng bird with wings like clouds, and turns through a sequence of short stories toward the use of what looks useless. Inner Chapter 2, *Qi Wu Lun* — "On the Equalizing of Things" — is denser and more argumentative, turning on the relativity of perspective and the limits of human distinctions; it closes with Zhuang Zhou dreaming he is a butterfly, and waking unable to say whether he is the man who dreamt or the butterfly that dreams of being a man.

## Context

The Zhuangzi is attributed to Zhuang Zhou (Master Zhuang, c. 369–286 BCE), a Warring States–period figure whose biography is barely recoverable. The text as it survives runs to thirty-three chapters in three groups: the Inner Chapters (1–7), the Outer Chapters (8–22), and the Miscellaneous Chapters (23–33). Modern scholarship generally treats the Inner Chapters as the oldest core, possibly the work of Zhuang Zhou himself or his immediate circle, and the Outer and Miscellaneous Chapters as the work of later followers writing in his style or under his name. The received text was edited and reduced in the late third century CE by Guo Xiang, who pruned an earlier fifty-two-chapter version down to the thirty-three chapters that survive.

The two Inner Chapters that open the book set the form. Chapter 1, *Xiaoyao You*, proceeds by accumulation of stories — the giant Peng bird, the cicada and the dove, the giant gourd, the useless tree of Huizi — each turning on the question of how to find one's own scale and use. Chapter 2, *Qi Wu Lun*, is sustained reflection on the relativity of perspective, the limits of language, and the uncertain boundary between waking and dream. Together the two chapters introduce the dual register that appears throughout the book: anecdote and argument, narrative and reflection.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the Zhuangzi works through stories. Where the Daodejing is gnomic and aphoristic, the Zhuangzi proceeds by anecdote — the giant fish, the monkey trainer, the butterfly — and the arguments emerge from the stories rather than the other way around. Reading the Inner Chapters slowly, allowing each story to register before moving to the next, is closer to the form than reading them as illustrations of doctrines stated elsewhere.

Second, the chapters do not develop a single argument. Chapter 1 turns through several stories on the use of what looks useless; Chapter 2 turns through several short reflections on the relativity of categories. Each unit stands on its own and is rarely picked up again later. Reading the chapters as collections of related but independent passages, rather than as continuous treatises, recovers their native shape.

## The text

The text is held in the Archive library under `zhuangzi`, chapters 1–2 (Giles translation, 1889 — Project Gutenberg edition). The library indexes Giles's translation by chapter, with library chapters 1 and 2 corresponding to the Zhuangzi's first two Inner Chapters. Roughly forty pages in modern translation; about an hour's reading.
