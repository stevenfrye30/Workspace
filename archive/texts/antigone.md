# Antigone

Antigone is the tragedy by Sophocles in which a woman defies a king. After the deaths of her two brothers — Eteocles and Polynices — in single combat for the throne of Thebes, the new king Creon decrees that Eteocles be buried with honor as the city's defender, while Polynices, who attacked the city, be left unburied as a traitor, under penalty of death for anyone who buries him. Antigone, their sister, refuses. She buries Polynices and accepts the consequence. The play traces what follows from her refusal: her arrest, her sentence to be entombed alive, her death, and the deaths that come to Creon's own house in turn. Antigone is one of seven surviving plays of Sophocles and among the Greek tragedies most often taught and translated.

## Context

Antigone is one of the three Theban plays of Sophocles, together with *Oedipus the King* and *Oedipus at Colonus*. It is the earliest of the three, first performed at the Dionysia festival in Athens around 441 BCE. The action follows the war for the throne of Thebes: the two sons of Oedipus, Eteocles and Polynices, have killed each other in single combat for the city, and their uncle Creon has assumed the kingship.

The play has seven characters and a chorus of Theban elders. It runs to roughly thirteen hundred lines in iambic trimeter and lyric meters. The action takes place in a single day, in front of the royal palace at Thebes. Antigone is in dialogue with her sister Ismene, with her uncle Creon, with the prophet Tiresias, and with Creon's son Haemon, who is also her betrothed. The chorus interrupts at intervals with odes — including the *Ode to Man* (lines 332–375) — that respond to the action without resolving it.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, Antigone is a play, not a treatise. The conflict at its center — Creon's decree against Antigone's burial of her brother — is dramatic action, not philosophical argument. Antigone gives reasons for her refusal, but the play's force comes from what she does and what is done to her in return, not from the moral case she makes. Reading the play with attention to action and consequence, rather than to the arguments alone, keeps it in proportion.

Second, the chorus is part of the play. Its odes interrupt the action and are not commentary on it from outside. The *Ode to Man*, sung in praise of human ingenuity ("many wonders are there, and none more wondrous than man"), comes shortly before Antigone is brought in, caught for the second burial; the chorus's praise sits beside the situation it cannot answer. Reading the choral odes alongside the scenes they border, rather than skimming past them, recovers the form the play has.
