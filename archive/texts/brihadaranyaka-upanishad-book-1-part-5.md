# Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 1, Part 5

Book 1, Part 5 of the Brihadaranyaka Upanishad — the Fifth Brahmana of the First Adhyaya — is a long and structurally layered Brahmana. The opening verse is a four-quarter sloka on Pragapati's seven kinds of food, set off in Müller's quotation marks and marked with internal pada-numbers `(1)`, `(2)`, `(3)`, `(4)`: *when the father of creation had produced by knowledge and penance the seven kinds of food, one of his foods was common to all beings, two he assigned to the Devas, three he made for himself, one he gave to the animals; in it all rests, whatsoever breathes and breathes not; why then do these not perish, though they are always eaten? he who knows this imperishable one, he eats food with his face; he goes even to the Devas, he lives on strength.* Verse 2 then turns to the analytical commentary on the sloka, running across several passages: the seven kinds of food explained (*one common* is the food eaten in this world; *two to the Devas* are the *huta* and *prahuta* offerings; *one to animals* is milk on which both men and beasts live; the imperishable Person produces the food again and again).

Verse 3 names the three the father made for himself — *mind, speech, and breath* — and gives the brief observation by which they are known together: *my mind was elsewhere, I did not see; my mind was elsewhere, I did not hear* — a man sees with mind and hears with mind. Whatever sound there is, that is speech; whatever the breaths are, that is breath. The Self consists of speech, mind, and breath. The verses that follow map this triad across one field after another. *Earth is speech, sky mind, heaven breath.* *The Rig-veda is speech, the Yagur-veda mind, the Sama-veda breath.* *The Devas are speech, the Fathers mind, men breath.* *The father is mind, the mother speech, the child breath.* Verses 8 through 10 give the threefold *what is known, what is to be known, what is unknown* mapped onto speech, mind, and breath, with the line that each, having become this, *protects man*.

Verses 11 through 13 give the bodily-cosmic mapping. Of speech, *earth is the body, light the form, viz. this fire*; so far as speech extends, so far extends earth, so far extends fire. Of mind, *heaven is the body, light the form, viz. this sun*; so far as mind extends, so far extends heaven, so far extends the sun. The two — fire and sun — embracing each other produce wind, and that wind is Indra, and Indra is without a rival; *verily a second is a rival, and he who knows this has no rival.* Of breath, *water is the body, light the form, viz. this moon*; so far as breath extends, so far extends water, so far extends the moon. *These are all alike, all endless. He who worships them as finite obtains a finite world; he who worships them as infinite obtains an infinite world.*

Verses 14 and 15 name Pragapati as the year of sixteen digits — fifteen are the nights, the fixed point is the sixteenth — increased and decreased by the nights. The same line is then turned on the man who knows this: *his wealth constitutes the fifteen digits, his Self the sixteenth digit; he is increased and decreased by that wealth; his Self is the nave, his wealth is the felly.* Verses 16 and 17 give the three worlds — men, Fathers, Devas — and how each is gained: *the world of men by a son, the world of the Fathers by sacrifice, the world of the Devas by knowledge*. The Devas' world is the best of worlds, *therefore they praise knowledge*. The handing-over follows: when a man thinks he is going to depart, he says to his son *Thou art Brahman, thou art the sacrifice, thou art the world*; the father enters into the son together with his speech, mind, and breath, and what was done amiss the son delivers him from.

Verses 18 through 20 give the entry of divine speech, mind, and breath into the knower from the cosmic pairs. *From the earth and from fire, divine speech enters into him.* *From heaven and the sun, divine mind enters into him.* *From water and the moon, divine breath enters into him* — divine breath being that which, whether moving or not, does not tire and does not perish. The knower becomes the Self of all beings.

Verse 21 turns again to the senses-contest. Pragapati created the actions; once created, they strove among themselves — voice held *I shall speak*, the eye *I shall see*, the ear *I shall hear*, and so on; in the end they recognized that the central breath, which moving or not does not tire and does not perish, is the best, and *let all of us assume his form*, and they were all called after him *prāṇāḥ* — breaths. Verse 22 turns the same contest onto the deities — Agni, Aditya, Chandramas — each with its own action — and as it was with the central breath among the breaths, so it is among the deities. Verse 23 closes the Brahmana with a sloka: *He from whom the sun rises, and into whom it sets — him the Devas made the law, he only is to-day, and he to-morrow also.* The line is glossed: he verily rises from the breath and sets in the breath; whatever these Devas determined then, that they perform to-day also. The Brahmana closes on the instruction *let a man perform one observance only — let him breathe up and let him breathe down — that the evil death may not reach him.*

## Context

The Brihadaranyaka Upanishad is one of the two earliest of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads, sharing that position with the Chandogya. It belongs to the Shukla Yajurveda branch of the Vedic corpus and forms the closing portion of the Shatapatha Brahmana — the *Aranyaka* in its name marks it as a forest-text recited at the late stages of Vedic study. Modern scholarship places the Brihadaranyaka in roughly the eighth to sixth century BCE, prior to the Buddha; the text is anonymous, and the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship. The text is the longest of the principal Upanishads and is typically structured in six *adhyayas* (chapters), each containing several *brahmanas* (sections).

Book 1, Part 5 is the fifth Brahmana of the First Adhyaya. The Fourth Brahmana closed the cosmogonic-recognition-social arc with the threefold desire for wife, wealth, and sacrifice; the Fifth Brahmana opens with a quoted sloka and a long prose commentary on it, then unfolds a sustained chain of triadic mappings around the threefold *speech, mind, breath* (*vāc, manas, prāṇa*) the Brahmanic literature has used elsewhere as the central faculty-triad. The Brahmana is among the longest in the Brihadaranyaka and is composed of multiple movements that hold the same triad across different fields without subordinating one mapping to another. The closing senses-contest at verses 21–22 is a second telling of the same mythic figure that opened the Third Brahmana — the contest in which the central breath alone is un-strucken — given here in shorter form and turned onto the deities as well as the body. The closing sloka and the *one observance only* instruction return the Brahmana to the same recognition the Third Brahmana reached: that the rising and setting of the sun is in the breath, and the practice that holds this is the practice of breathing up and down.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the Brahmana works by accumulated parallel mappings rather than by argument. The triad *speech, mind, breath* is taken once and mapped onto five outward fields in turn (worlds, Vedas, classes of beings, family roles, kinds of knowledge); each mapping is given in one short sentence; the mappings are not merged into a single doctrine. The bodily-cosmic mapping that follows extends the same triad into a sixth field (body, light, form) and gives the most elaborate development — the embracing of fire and sun producing wind. Reading the Brahmana with attention to which field is being filled in each mapping, and to the fact that no mapping is preferred over the others, recovers the form the late-Vedic prose has when it works at this scale.

Second, the Brahmana shifts register repeatedly without announcing the shift. From the cosmogonic sloka and its prose gloss it moves to the triadic mappings; from the mappings it moves to the year-as-sixteen-digits image; from that image to the three worlds and the son-inheritance ritual; from the inheritance ritual to the entry of divine speech, mind, and breath; from that entry to the senses-contest among the breaths and again among the deities; from the senses-contest to the closing sloka and the one-observance instruction. Reading the Brahmana with the shifts of register held alongside one another, attending to the way each movement uses or refers back to the central triad, keeps the form the Brihadaranyaka has when its prose is most heavily layered.

## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 1, Part 4 — paired immediately consecutive Brahmanas of the First Adhyaya; the Fourth Brahmana developed cosmogony, the *aham brahmāsmi* recognition, and the four-fold social order in three movements, the Fifth Brahmana opens with a quoted sloka on Pragapati's seven kinds of food and develops through layered triadic mappings, the year-as-sixteen-digits image, the son-inheritance ritual, and the senses-contest. The two Brahmanas hold the same procedure of accumulated parallel mappings inside long prose units, with the Self or the central triad held at the centre.
- **structural-parallel** → Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 1, Part 3 — paired Sanskrit senses-contest narratives within the same Adhyaya; the Third Brahmana holds the contest in mythic form as the Devas-Asuras struggle at the *udgītha*, with the breath in the mouth scattering the Asuras, the Fifth Brahmana holds the same contest in compressed form at verses 21–22, with Pragapati's senses striving among themselves and the central breath named as the one that does not tire, then the same form turned onto the deities. The same recognition given twice within one Adhyaya.
- **structural-parallel** → Taittiriyaka Upanishad, Book 1, Part 5 — paired Sanskrit multi-field mapping pedagogies; the Taittiriyaka's Fifth Anuvaka of the Sikshavalli holds the *vyāhṛti* lesson — *Bhū*, *Bhuvaḥ*, *Suvaḥ*, *Mahas* — mapped across four parallel fields (worlds, heavenly elements, Vedas, breathings), the Brihadaranyaka's Fifth Brahmana holds the triad *speech, mind, breath* mapped across five outward fields (worlds, Vedas, classes of beings, family roles, kinds of knowledge) and one bodily-cosmic field (body, light, form). Both texts trust accumulated parallel mappings to do the work the doctrine itself does not state.
