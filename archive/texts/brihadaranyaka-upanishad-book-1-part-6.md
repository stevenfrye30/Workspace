# Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 1, Part 6

Book 1, Part 6 of the Brihadaranyaka Upanishad — the Sixth Brahmana of the First Adhyaya — is the shortest Brahmana of the Adhyaya and seals it with a single tight triple-mapping. The opening line names the triad: *trayaṁ vā idam, nāma rūpaṁ karma* — *verily this is a triad, name, form, and work.* Verse 1 then takes the first member of the triad, *name*, and identifies its representative — Speech — and gives the same Speech under three names: *Uktha* (hymn, supposed to mean also origin), for from it all names arise; *Saman* (song, supposed to mean also sameness), for it is the same as all names; *Brahman* (prayer, supposed to mean also support), for it supports all names. Verse 2 takes the second member, *form*, and gives the same procedure with the Eye in place of Speech: *Uktha*, for from it all forms arise; *Saman*, for it is the same as all forms; *Brahman*, for it supports all forms. Verse 3 takes the third member, *work*, and gives the same procedure with the Body: *Uktha*, for from it all works arise; *Saman*, for it is the same as all works; *Brahman*, for it supports all works. The Brahmana then closes with a single short summary that turns the triple into a recognition of unity: *that being a triad is one, viz. this Self; and the Self, being one, is that triad.* The line that follows is one of the Brihadaranyaka's distinctive formulations — *etad amṛtaṁ satyena cchannam* — *this is the immortal, covered by the true.* The closing equation gives the terms: *verily breath is the immortal, name and form are the true, and by them the immortal is covered.* The Brahmana ends here; the next passage in the library opens the Second Adhyaya.

## Context

The Brihadaranyaka Upanishad is one of the two earliest of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads, sharing that position with the Chandogya. It belongs to the Shukla Yajurveda branch of the Vedic corpus and forms the closing portion of the Shatapatha Brahmana — the *Aranyaka* in its name marks it as a forest-text recited at the late stages of Vedic study. Modern scholarship places the Brihadaranyaka in roughly the eighth to sixth century BCE, prior to the Buddha; the text is anonymous, and the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship. The text is the longest of the principal Upanishads and is typically structured in six *adhyayas* (chapters), each containing several *brahmanas* (sections).

Book 1, Part 6 is the closing Brahmana of the First Adhyaya. Where the First Adhyaya opened with the cosmic horse, then the cosmogony of Death and Hunger, the Devas-Asuras *udgītha* contest, the cosmogony of the Self alone with the *aham brahmāsmi* recognition and the four-fold social order, and then the long Fifth Brahmana with its layered mappings on speech, mind, and breath — the Sixth Brahmana now closes the Adhyaya in a single short unit. The triple *name, form, work* — *nāma-rūpa-karma* — is one of the basic Brihadaranyaka analytic divisions and will return in later Adhyayas; the closing equation that breath is the immortal and name-and-form are the true is one of the Upanishad's most condensed identity formulas. After this Brahmana the Second Adhyaya opens directly into a different mode — the dialogue between Gargya Balaki and Ajatasatru of Kasi — without further introduction.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the Brahmana is built on exact triple repetition. The same three names — *Uktha*, *Saman*, *Brahman* — are applied to Speech, then to the Eye, then to the Body, with the same three reasons — *origin*, *sameness*, *support* — given each time. The form is the work the Brahmana is doing. Reading the Brahmana with attention to which term is held stable across the three iterations and which term is substituted, recovers the procedure: the triple of *name, form, work* receives a representative member, and that member is given the same triple of names under the same triple of reasons. The repetition is exact and visible.

Second, the closing line moves from the triple to a unity, but the unity does not flatten the triple. *The triad is one, viz. this Self; and the Self, being one, is that triad* — the recognition runs both directions. The closing equation that follows — *the immortal covered by the true*, with breath as the immortal and name-and-form as the true — places the unity back inside a structured pair, with the inner term (breath, the immortal) covered by the outer terms (name and form, the true). Reading the closing line with attention to its bidirectional movement, and to the way it preserves the structure rather than dissolving it, keeps the form the Brahmana has when it closes the Adhyaya.

## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 1, Part 5 — paired closing pair of Brahmanas of the First Adhyaya; the Fifth Brahmana ran across twenty-three numbered verses with multiple shifts of register and a sustained chain of triadic mappings on speech, mind, and breath, the Sixth Brahmana closes the Adhyaya in three short verses with the triad of name, form, and work and a single closing equation. The procedure of accumulated parallel mapping — held across the long Fifth Brahmana — is held in compressed form across the short Sixth, the Adhyaya closing on the same kind of mapping it has been performing throughout.
- **structural-parallel** → Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 1, Part 4 — paired Sanskrit identity-statements within the same Adhyaya; the Fourth Brahmana holds the *aham brahmāsmi* mahāvākya at its tenth verse — *I am Brahman* — as the recognition from which all this sprang, the Sixth Brahmana holds the closing recognition that *the triad is one, viz. this Self; and the Self, being one, is that triad*. The Adhyaya holds two identity-recognitions — one in the first person, one as a structural unity — and closes on the second.
- **structural-parallel** → Taittiriyaka Upanishad, Book 1, Part 5 — paired Sanskrit triple-mapping pedagogies; the Taittiriyaka's Fifth Anuvaka of the Sikshavalli holds the *vyāhṛti* — *Bhū*, *Bhuvaḥ*, *Suvaḥ*, *Mahas* — mapped across four parallel fields (worlds, heavenly elements, Vedas, breathings) with each multiplication producing the recognition that the one who knows them knows Brahman, the Brihadaranyaka's Sixth Brahmana holds the triad *name, form, work* mapped through three representative members (Speech, Eye, Body) under three names (*Uktha*, *Saman*, *Brahman*), and closes on the recognition that the triad is one. Both texts trust the multiplied-mapping form to do the work the doctrine itself does not state.
