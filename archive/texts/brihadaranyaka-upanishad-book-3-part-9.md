# Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 3, Part 9

Book 3, Part 9 of the Brihadaranyaka Upanishad — the Ninth Brahmana of the Third Adhyaya — closes the Janaka Vaideha contest opened in the First Brahmana, and closes the Adhyaya itself. The court frame is not reset. The Eighth Brahmana closed on Gargi's public concession that no one would defeat Yajnavalkya in any argument concerning Brahman; the Ninth opens directly into the eighth named challenger of the Adhyaya, who is the only challenger to put the contest's procedure to the test after Gargi has named its outcome in advance. *Then Vidagdha Sakalya asked him.* The Brahmana that follows is the longest of the Adhyaya by a wide margin, and it works through six successive sections of question and answer before its closing slokas, with the threat that the prior Brahmanas had given as a warning brought to fulfilment in the body of the dialogue.

The Brahmana's first section — verses 1 through 9 — works through the enumeration of the gods. Sakalya opens with a single short question: *How many gods are there, O Yagnavalkya?* Yajnavalkya answers first with the *Nivid* number — *as many as are mentioned in the Nivid of the hymn of praise addressed to the Visvedevas, viz. three and three hundred, three and three thousand* — and Sakalya then drives a stepped descent through the question. The form is repeated verbatim: *Yes, he said, and asked again: How many gods are there really, O Yagnavalkya?* — and Yajnavalkya answers each iteration with a smaller number. Thirty-three. Six. Three. Two. One and a half (*adhyardha*). One. The cascade rests at one. Sakalya then asks the substantive content of each number, and Yajnavalkya names them: the *Nivid* number is *only the various powers of them, in reality there are only thirty-three gods*; the thirty-three are the eight Vasus, the eleven Rudras, the twelve Adityas — making thirty-one — with Indra and Pragapati making the thirty-three. The eight Vasus are Agni (fire), Prithivi (earth), Vayu (air), Antariksha (sky), Aditya (sun), Dyu (heaven), Kandramas (moon), and the Nakshatras (stars), so called *for in them all that dwells (this world) rests*. The eleven Rudras are the ten vital breaths and Atman as the eleventh, so called *because, when they depart from this mortal body, they make us cry (rodayanti)*. The twelve Adityas are the twelve months of the year, so called *because they move along (yanti), taking up everything (adadanah)*. Indra is thunder, Pragapati is the sacrifice; thunder is the thunderbolt; the sacrifice is the (sacrificial) animals. The six are Agni, Prithivi, Vayu, Antariksha, Aditya, Dyu — six of the eight Vasus, *for they are all this, the six*. The three are the three worlds, *for in them all these gods exist*. The two are food and breath. The one and a half is *He that blows*; the etymology of *adhyardha* is given in verse 9: *Because, when the wind was blowing, everything grew (adhyardhnot)*. The one is *Breath (prana), and he is Brahman (the Sutratman), and they call him That (tyad)*. The cascade is the contest's first cycle: a single question reduced through seven iterations, with the substantive content of each iteration unfolded after the descent has closed.

The second section — verses 10 through 17 — works through the eight-purusha cycle. The form is held verbatim across each verse. *Sakalya said: 'Whosoever knows that person whose dwelling is X, whose sight is Y, whose mind is light, — the principle of every (living) self, he indeed is a teacher, O Yagnavalkya.'* Yajnavalkya answers each time in the same shape: *I know that person, the principle of every self, of whom thou speakest. [The Z person], "he is he." But tell me, Sakalya, who is his devata?* Sakalya then names the deity. The eight verses, each with its (dwelling, sight, person, devata) tuple in order: (earth, fire, the corporeal/earthy person, the Immortal); (love — *a body capable of sensual love*, the heart, the love-made/loving person, the women); (colours, the eye, the person in the sun, the True); (ether, the ear, the person who hears and answers, Space); (darkness, the heart, the shadowy person, Death); (bright colours, the eye, the person in the looking-glass, Vital breath / *asu*); (water, the heart, the person in the water, Varuna); (seed, the heart, the filial person, Pragapati). The reversal of contest-roles inside the cycle is one of the Brahmana's distinctive features: the challenger asks Yajnavalkya to name the person, and Yajnavalkya — having named the person — asks the challenger to name the deity. The Brahmana's procedure now runs in both directions across the named participants.

The third section — verse 18 — is the Brahmana's first explicit confrontation. *Yagnavalkya said: 'Sakalya, did those Brahmanas (who themselves shrank from the contest) make thee the victim?'* The line names what the prior Brahmanas of the Adhyaya had not named: that the assembled Brahmanas of the Kurus and the Pankalas, who had stood silent through Asvala, Artabhaga, Bhugyu, Ushasta, Kahola, Gargi, Uddalaka Aruni, and now Vakaknavi (Gargi) again, had put Sakalya forward as the one to be defeated on their behalf. Sakalya's response is sharp: *Yagnavalkya, because thou hast decried the Brahmanas of the Kuru-Pankalas, what Brahman dost thou know?*

The fourth section — verses 19 through 24 — works through the cardinal quarters. Yajnavalkya begins with a declaration: *I know the quarters with their deities and their abodes.* Sakalya then puts five questions, one for each direction (East, South, West, North, Zenith), and walks each chain through deity, abode, and progressively further support until the chain reaches the heart. Verse 20 (East): Aditya (the sun) → eye → colours → heart, *for we know colours by the heart, for colours abide in the heart*. Verse 21 (South): Yama → sacrifice → Dakshina (gifts to the priests) → Sraddha (faith) → heart, *for by the heart faith knows*. Verse 22 (West): Varuna → water → seed → heart, *for the seed abides in the heart*. Verse 23 (North): Soma → Diksha (consecration) → the True → heart, *for with the heart do we know what is true*. Verse 24 (Zenith): Agni → speech → heart. Each chain receives Sakalya's seal at its close: *So it is indeed, O Yagnavalkya.*

The fifth section — verses 25 and 26 — opens with Sakalya's final question of the chain: *And in what does the heart abide?* Verse 25 holds Yajnavalkya's response on its own short passage: *O Ahallika, when you think the heart could be anywhere else away from us, if it were away from us, the dogs might eat it, or the birds tear it.* The address *Ahallika* is the Brahmana's only explicit insult, and Müller's footnote glosses it as *blockhead*. Verse 26 then turns to a final cascade. *In what dost thou (thy body) and the Self (thy heart) abide?* — Yajnavalkya: *In the Prana (breath)*. *In what does the Prana abide?* — *In the Apana (down-breathing)*. *In what does the Apana abide?* — *In the Vyana (back-breathing)*. *In what does the Vyana abide?* — *In the Udana (the out-breathing)*. *In what does the Udana abide?* — *In the Samana*. The cascade closes at the Samana, and the substantive answer turns to the *neti neti* line. *That Self (atman) is to be described by No, no! He is incomprehensible, for he cannot be (is not) comprehended; he is imperishable, for he cannot perish; he is unattached, for he does not attach himself; unfettered, he does not suffer, he does not fail.* The form is the form Müller had given at the close of the Third Brahmana of the Second Adhyaya — *the teaching of Brahman by "No, no!"* — and the form is repeated here verbatim at the close of the contest's fifth section.

The eight-fold summary follows, with the closing threat: *These are the eight abodes (the earth, &c.), the eight worlds (fire, &c.), the eight gods (the immortal food, &c.), the eight persons (the corporeal, &c.). He who after dividing and uniting these persons, went beyond (the Samana), that person, taught in the Upanishads, I now ask thee (to teach me). If thou shalt not explain him to me, thy head will fall.* The threat — *thy head will fall* — repeats the form Yajnavalkya had given Gargi in the Sixth Brahmana and the form Uddalaka Aruni had given Yajnavalkya in the Seventh. Both prior threats had been used as warning, with no head actually falling. The Ninth Brahmana brings the threat to fulfilment. The narrator's line follows: *Sakalya did not know him, and his head fell, nay, thieves took away his bones, mistaking them for something else.* The death of the named challenger is given in two short clauses on a single passage; the second clause — that thieves took away the bones, mistaking them for something else — is the only post-mortem detail the Adhyaya holds, and it stands without further commentary.

The sixth and final section — verses 27 and 28 — is the contest's aftermath and the Adhyaya's closing slokas. *Then Yagnavalkya said: 'Reverend Brahmanas, whosoever among you desires to do so, may now question me. Or question me, all of you. Or whosoever among you desires it, I shall question him, or I shall question all of you.'* The narrator's line: *But those Brahmanas durst not (say anything).* Yajnavalkya's invitation puts the contest's offer back to the assembled Brahmanas of the Kurus and the Pankalas, with the procedure reversed: he now offers to question them. The assembly's silence is the contest's last collective act.

Verse 28 holds the seven slokas with which Yajnavalkya questions them. *Then Yagnavalkya questioned them with these Slokas:* The slokas are numbered 1 through 7 and work through a single sustained tree-and-man simile. *As a mighty tree in the forest, so in truth is man, his hairs are the leaves, his outer skin is the bark.* The body's correspondences with the tree are walked through three slokas: hair as leaves, skin as bark; blood from the wounded body as sap from the wounded tree; the lumps of flesh as layers of wood, the fibre as tendons, the bones as the hard wood within, the marrow as the marrow of the tree. Slokas 4 through 6 turn the simile into a question on resurrection. *But, while the tree, when felled, grows up again more young from the root, from what root, tell me, does a mortal grow up, after he has been felled by death?* The negation of seed as the root is the next sloka — *Do not say, "from seed," for seed is produced from the living; but a tree, springing from a grain, clearly rises again after death* — and the negation of root-and-trunk is given in sloka 6: *If a tree is pulled up with the root, it will not grow again; from what root then, tell me, does a mortal grow up, after he has been felled by death?* Sloka 7 holds the cryptic answer: *Once born, he is not born (again); for who should create him again?* The Adhyaya then closes on a single declarative line: *Brahman, who is knowledge and bliss, he is the principle, both to him who gives gifts, and also to him who stands firm, and knows.* The line is not given a sloka-number and is not given a verse-number; it sits at the close of the Brahmana on its own short passage, and it carries the Adhyaya across into the boundary with the Fourth.

## Context

The Brihadaranyaka Upanishad is one of the two earliest of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads, sharing that position with the Chandogya. It belongs to the Shukla Yajurveda branch of the Vedic corpus and forms the closing portion of the Shatapatha Brahmana — the *Aranyaka* in its name marks it as a forest-text recited at the late stages of Vedic study. Modern scholarship places the Brihadaranyaka in roughly the eighth to sixth century BCE, prior to the Buddha; the text is anonymous, and the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship. The text is the longest of the principal Upanishads and is typically structured in six *adhyayas* (chapters), each containing several *brahmanas* (sections).

Book 3, Part 9 holds the Adhyaya's closing in a form distinct from any of the prior Brahmanas. The contest's prior eight Brahmanas had each closed on a named challenger held in silence — *After that Asvala held his peace*; *After that Bhugyu Lahyayani held his peace*; *After that Ushasta Kakrayana held his peace*; *After that Kahola Kaushitakeya held his peace*; *After that Gargi Vakaknavi held her peace*; *After that Uddalaka Aruni held his peace*; *After that Vakaknavi held her peace* — with the Second Brahmana's private withdrawal as the only deviation. The Ninth Brahmana does not close on the *held his peace* form. The named challenger does not fall silent; he dies. The Adhyaya's closing pattern is broken at the close, and the contest's two head-falling threats — Yajnavalkya's to Gargi in the Sixth Brahmana, Uddalaka's to Yajnavalkya in the Seventh — are both brought to bear on the eighth challenger, with the threat moved from warning into fulfilment.

The Brahmana also holds the Adhyaya's most concentrated literary signatures. The *neti neti* line — *That Self (atman) is to be described by No, no!* — was given by Müller's translation at the close of the Third Brahmana of the Second Adhyaya in nearly the same form; the Ninth Brahmana of the Third Adhyaya holds the line at the close of its fifth section, with the same wording carried across the Upanishad and the same doctrine attached to the breath-cascade rather than to the two-forms doctrine. The eight Vasus, eleven Rudras, and twelve Adityas of the gods-enumeration cascade hold one of the Vedic literature's most-cited divine catalogues, and the form of the cascade — *thirty-three, six, three, two, one and a half, one* — gives the catalogue the Brahmana's distinctive descending procedure. The eight-purusha cycle gives the Adhyaya's only formula in which the contest's procedure runs in both directions across the named participants, with the challenger asking and the priest asking in turn.

The closing slokas hold the Adhyaya's only verse-form in the strict sense. The eight prior Brahmanas had been given in Müller's prose presentation throughout, with the verse-numbered markers as paragraph-divisions of prose rather than as formal verse-units. The seven slokas of verse 28 — Sloka 1 through Sloka 7 — are sub-numbered as a verse-sequence inside verse 28 and given in the formal sloka measure, with the tree-and-man simile carried through them as a single sustained figure. The closing line — *Brahman, who is knowledge and bliss, he is the principle, both to him who gives gifts, and also to him who stands firm, and knows* — is not given a sloka-number and is not given a verse-number; it sits at the close of the Brahmana as the Adhyaya's last line and carries the contest's outcome into the boundary with the Fourth Adhyaya.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the Brahmana is composite. Six successive sections work through six different formal procedures — gods-enumeration cascade, eight-purusha cycle, narrative confrontation, quarter-cycle, breath-cascade with *neti neti* close, slokas with tree-and-man simile — and the contest's procedure is altered at each section's boundary. Reading the Brahmana with the six sections held alongside each other rather than absorbed into a single rhetorical line — neither smoothed away into one continuous dialogue nor re-segmented into nine smaller Brahmanas — recovers the form the Adhyaya has when its closing Brahmana is given as a sequence of distinct contest-procedures rather than as a single sustained one.

Second, the Brahmana's threat is fulfilled. The contest's two prior head-falling threats — Yajnavalkya's to Gargi at `5.742`, Uddalaka's to Yajnavalkya at `5.744` — were given as warnings inside the procedure of the dialogue. The Ninth Brahmana places the threat at the close of the eight-fold summary at `5.922` (*If thou shalt not explain him to me, thy head will fall*), and the narrator's next line at `5.923` brings it to fulfilment (*Sakalya did not know him, and his head fell, nay, thieves took away his bones, mistaking them for something else*). The contest's prior procedure had silenced its challengers — voluntarily, by induced threat, by private withdrawal — and the Ninth Brahmana takes the contest's threat-language past silencing and into death. Reading the close with the threat-language held alongside its earlier appearances recovers the Adhyaya's literary procedure: the same form-language is held across the contest's nine Brahmanas with its meaning escalated at the close.

The narrative is given in two short clauses: the head-falling and the bones-taken. The contest's first and only fatality is held on a single passage without further commentary.

- **Verse-28 sloka-introduction.** Passage `5.926` carries the introductory line *Then Yagnavalkya questioned them with these Slokas:* with the verse-28 marker. The introduction does not give substantive teaching; it announces the sloka-sequence that follows.
- **Slokas 1–7 each as numbered sub-units.** Passages `5.927` (Sloka 1), `5.928`–`5.929` (Sloka 2, split mid-clause at *from the wounded / man*, with footnote `[*1]` on *from the wounded man*), `5.930` (Sloka 3, with footnote `[*2]` on *tendons*), `5.931` (Sloka 4), `5.932` (Sloka 5, with footnotes `[*3]` on *from the living*, `[*4]` on *clearly*, and `[*5]` on *after death*), `5.933` (Sloka 6), and `5.934` (Sloka 7, with footnote `[*6]` on *for who should create him again*) carry the seven slokas each as a numbered sub-unit inside verse 28. The slokas are the Adhyaya's only formal verse-form and are given in the sloka measure.
- **Adhyaya closing line at `5.935`.** Passage `5.935` carries the closing declarative line *Brahman, who is knowledge and bliss, he is the principle, both to him who gives gifts, and also to him who stands firm, and knows*, with footnote `[*1]` on *gives gifts*. The line is not given a sloka-number, is not given a verse-number, and is not given a *held his peace* narrative-line. The Adhyaya closes on the substantive declaration without the contest's silence-form.

Passage `5.936` is the "Fourth Adhyaya" header that opens Book 4 of the Brihadaranyaka and is not part of this entity. The library chapter continues past this entity into the rest of the volume; the Brihadaranyaka extends well beyond the boundaries of library chapter 5. About sixteen minutes' reading.

## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 3, Part 1 — paired bookend Brahmanas of the Third Adhyaya. The First Brahmana opened the Janaka Vaideha contest with Asvala the Hotri priest as the first challenger, the enclosing of the one thousand cows with ten padas of gold tied to the horns of each, the angering of the Brahmanas, and Asvala's two cycles of substitutional questions on freedom and on the numbers of the ritual. The Ninth Brahmana closes the contest with Vidagdha Sakalya as the eighth and final challenger, with six successive sections of question and answer including the gods-enumeration cascade, the eight-purusha cycle, the quarter-cycle, the breath-cascade, the *neti neti* close, and the seven slokas. Asvala fell silent — *After that Asvala held his peace* — at the close of the First Brahmana; Sakalya's head fell at the close of the Ninth, with thieves taking away his bones. The Adhyaya is held between the contest's first silence and the contest's first death, with the same court frame and the same priest carrying the contest from first challenger to last.
- **structural-parallel** → Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 3, Part 6 — paired Brahmanas of the Janaka Vaideha contest holding Yajnavalkya's head-falling threat in different stages of fulfilment. The Sixth Brahmana closed with the threat used as warning — *do not ask too much, lest thy head should fall off* — and Gargi held in induced silence; the Ninth Brahmana places the threat at the close of the eight-fold summary at `5.922` (*If thou shalt not explain him to me, thy head will fall*) and brings it to fulfilment at `5.923` (*Sakalya did not know him, and his head fell*). The same threat-language carries across the Adhyaya from warning at the Sixth Brahmana's close into fulfilment at the Ninth Brahmana's body. The Sixth Brahmana's threat had silenced its challenger; the Ninth Brahmana's threat killed him.
- **structural-parallel** → Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 2, Part 3 — paired loci of the *neti neti* doctrine within the Brihadaranyaka. The Third Brahmana of the Second Adhyaya closes the two-forms doctrine and the color-figures of the person in the right eye with the prose statement *the teaching of Brahman by "No, no!" — for there is nothing else higher than this* and the formula *the name "the True of the True"*; the Ninth Brahmana of the Third Adhyaya holds the same form at the close of its breath-cascade at `5.921` — *That Self (atman) is to be described by No, no! He is incomprehensible, for he cannot be (is not) comprehended; he is imperishable, for he cannot perish; he is unattached, for he does not attach himself; unfettered, he does not suffer, he does not fail.* The two loci hold the *neti neti* line in nearly the same wording, with the Second Adhyaya attaching it to the two-forms doctrine and the Third Adhyaya attaching it to the breath-cascade and the Atman.
