# Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 3, Part 5

Book 3, Part 5 of the Brihadaranyaka Upanishad — the Fifth Brahmana of the Third Adhyaya — continues the Janaka Vaideha contest opened in the First Brahmana and continued through the Second, Third, and Fourth. The court frame is not reset. The Fourth Brahmana closed on silence — *After that Ushasta Kakrayana held his peace* — and the Fifth opens directly into the next challenger: *Then Kahola Kaushitakeya asked.* The closing form returns again to silence, with the same narrator's line and the new name in place: *After that Kahola Kaushitakeya held his peace.*

The Brahmana is paired with the Fourth at the level of textual surface. Kahola's opening question — *tell me the Brahman which is visible, not invisible, the Self (Atman), who is within all* — is verbatim Ushasta's opening question of the Fourth Brahmana. Yajnavalkya's formulaic reply — *This, thy Self, who is within all* — is verbatim the formulaic reply of the Fourth Brahmana. Kahola's follow-up — *Which Self, O Yagnavalkya, is within all?* — is verbatim the follow-up of the Fourth Brahmana. The Brahmana opens by repeating the entire Q-and-A frame of the prior Brahmana, with no narrator's marker that it is a repetition and no introduction by Kahola signalling that he is asking the same question Ushasta asked. The contest's procedure carries the same form across the Brahmana boundary, and the form is held verbatim across the change of named challenger.

Where the Fourth Brahmana then required two substantive answers — the four-breaths identification, refused as cow-and-horse pointing, and the seer-of-sight negation — the Fifth gives one. Kahola does not refuse the answer; the doubled cycle of the Fourth is not repeated; the substantive answer is given once, and the Brahmana closes on it. The compression at the level of cycle-count contrasts with the verbatim repetition at the level of opening words.

Yajnavalkya's substantive answer turns to the form of life of the knower. The opening clause identifies the Self as that which is past the body's afflictions: *He who overcomes hunger and thirst, sorrow, passion, old age, and death.* The next clause turns the identification into a teaching on the renouncing-Brahmana: *When Brahmanas know that Self, and have risen above the desire for sons, wealth, and (new) worlds, they wander about as mendicants.* The chain of desires is then collapsed into a single descent — *for a desire for sons is desire for wealth, a desire for wealth is desire for worlds. Both these are indeed desires* — and the wandering-mendicant form of life is named as the procedure of the knower.

The closing of the substantive answer holds a four-step sequence on the form of the Brahmana. *Therefore let a Brahmana, after he has done with learning, wish to stand by real strength; after he has done with that strength and learning, he becomes a Muni (a Yogin); and after he has done with what is not the knowledge of a Muni, and with what is the knowledge of a Muni, he is a Brahmana. By whatever means he has become a Brahmana, he is such indeed.* The line *Everything else is of evil* sits at the close, on the same passage as the substantive answer and the closing narrative line; it is paired in form with the line *Everything also is of evil* that closed the Fourth Brahmana, with one preposition changed and the rest of the form preserved. The narrator's line then closes the Brahmana: *After that Kahola Kaushitakeya held his peace.*

## Context

The Brihadaranyaka Upanishad is one of the two earliest of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads, sharing that position with the Chandogya. It belongs to the Shukla Yajurveda branch of the Vedic corpus and forms the closing portion of the Shatapatha Brahmana — the *Aranyaka* in its name marks it as a forest-text recited at the late stages of Vedic study. Modern scholarship places the Brihadaranyaka in roughly the eighth to sixth century BCE, prior to the Buddha; the text is anonymous, and the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship. The text is the longest of the principal Upanishads and is typically structured in six *adhyayas* (chapters), each containing several *brahmanas* (sections).

Book 3, Part 5 holds two of the most distinctive features of the contest in concentrated form. The first is the verbatim repetition of the Fourth Brahmana's opening. The Adhyaya's prior Brahmanas had each opened with a different question — Asvala on freedom and ritual numbers, Artabhaga on the Grahas and the dispersal of the body, Bhugyu on the destination of the Parikshitas, Ushasta on the Brahman who is visible-not-invisible — and the change of question across challengers had been one of the contest's literary signatures. The Fifth Brahmana opens by giving the same question Ushasta gave, in the same wording, and not naming it as repetition. The challenger is new; the question is not. The procedure of the *brahmodya* in the Fifth Brahmana is not the formulation of a new question but the offering of a different substantive answer to a question that has already been asked, with the textual frame of the Fourth Brahmana preserved across the boundary. The form the Adhyaya now has — same question, new challenger, new answer — is one the contest had not yet held, and the verbatim opening is the textual mark of it.

The second is the renouncing-Brahmana teaching. Within the Brihadaranyaka, the form of life of the knowing Brahmana — wandering as a mendicant after passing beyond the desire for sons, wealth, and worlds — has its first sustained statement here. The Yajnavalkya-Maitreyi conversation of the Second Adhyaya opened on Yajnavalkya's own departure for the forest; the Fifth Brahmana of the Third Adhyaya gives the form of life that conditions the departure. The chain *desire for sons → desire for wealth → desire for worlds* is given as the structure of the desire that the wandering-mendicant has put aside, and the four-step Muni/Brahmana sequence — *learning → real strength → Muni → Brahmana* — is given as the form of the procedure. The closer *Everything else is of evil* is paired with the closer *Everything also is of evil* of the prior Brahmana; the two lines stand in the Adhyaya as a matched pair, with one rendering of the Müllerian preposition followed by the other. Müller's translation does not gloss the difference; the source carries the two prepositions on adjacent Brahmanas, and the difference is preserved as the source has it.

The closing form is silence again. The contest's five closings now sit in the pattern silence / private withdrawal / silence / silence / silence — with the Second Brahmana's private withdrawal as the single deviation in five. The Adhyaya's silence-closing predominates, and the deviation continues to stand alone.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the Brahmana repeats the prior Brahmana's opening verbatim and does not mark the repetition. The Q-and-A frame the Fourth Brahmana doubled internally — opening question, formulaic reply, follow-up — is given once at the open of the Fifth Brahmana with the same words and the same form, with the named challenger changed and no narrator's interruption indicating that this is the same question. Reading Part 5 with Part 4 held alongside it, attending to the way the textual surface of the prior Brahmana is reused without flag, recovers the form the Adhyaya has when a question has been asked once and is to be answered again with different substance. The contest's procedure makes room for repetition without abbreviation; the procedure does not require the question to be paraphrased on its second appearance.

Second, the substantive answer is neither a positive identification of the Self with bodily processes nor a negative formula about the Self that cannot be witnessed. The Self is given as that which has overcome the body's afflictions, and the form of life of the one who knows that Self is given as the wandering mendicancy of the Brahmana who has risen above the desire for sons, wealth, and worlds. The Brahmana works the doctrine of the Self into a teaching about how the knower lives, not into a teaching about what the Self is in itself. Reading the substantive answer with attention to the way it answers the same question Ushasta asked — visible-not-invisible, within all — without using either of Ushasta's two answer-forms, recovers the literary form the Adhyaya has when a third answer is offered to a question that already has two: the third answer addresses the form of life rather than the form of the doctrine.

## The text

The text is held in the Archive library under `upanishads-muller-part2`, chapter 5 (Müller translation, 1884 — *The Sacred Books of the East*, Volume XV; via SacredTexts.com). Library chapter 5 is shared across fifty-four Archive entities — four parts of the Katha Upanishad, the six parts of the Mundaka Upanishad, twenty-eight anuvakas of the Taittiriyaka, the eleven Brahmanas of the Brihadaranyaka's First and Second Adhyayas, and the First, Second, Third, and Fourth Brahmanas of the Third Adhyaya — and continues here into the Fifth Brahmana. Book 3, Part 5 of the Brihadaranyaka — the entity here — occupies passages `5.714` through `5.719`. Passage `5.714` is the "Fifth Brahmana" header carrying its own footnote reference `[*1]`. Passages `5.715` through `5.719` carry the body in Müller's prose presentation across five short passages, with the verse-numbered marker at `1.` (`5.715`) only. The rest of the dialogue is unnumbered.

The Brahmana contains several boundary features that should be preserved honestly:

- **Verse-1 single-passage opening.** Passage `5.715` carries Kahola's full opening question on a single passage without internal splits and without footnotes. The verse-1 marker sits at the open of the passage; the question is given verbatim from the Fourth Brahmana's verse-1 question without further commentary.
- **Single-cycle Q-and-A frame.** Passages `5.716` and `5.717` carry Yajnavalkya's formulaic reply *This, thy Self, who is within all* and Kahola's follow-up *Which Self, O Yagnavalkya, is within all?* — each on its own short passage, with no verse-marker. The two passages are verbatim repeats of `5.706` and `5.707` of the Fourth Brahmana. The doubling that the Fourth Brahmana required at `5.711` and `5.712` is not present here; the Fifth Brahmana works the question through a single cycle.
- **Substantive answer split mid-clause.** The substantive answer is split across passages `5.718` and `5.719` mid-clause at *Therefore let a Brahmana, after he has done with learning, / wish to stand by real strength*. Passage `5.718` carries the open of the answer — the Self that overcomes hunger, thirst, sorrow, passion, old age, and death; the renouncing-Brahmana clause; the chain of desires; and the open of the four-step sequence — with footnotes `[*2]` on *sons* and `[*3]` on *(new) worlds*. Passage `5.719` resumes with the four-step sequence — *real strength → Muni → Brahmana* — and the closer *Everything else is of evil* and the closing narrative line *After that Kahola Kaushitakeya held his peace*, with footnotes `[*1]` on *real strength* and `[*2]` on *he is such indeed*.
- **Closing answer-and-narrative-line at `5.719`.** The closing narrative line is folded into the same passage as the close of the substantive answer, in the same compression as the Third Brahmana at `5.703` and the Fourth Brahmana at `5.713`. The First Brahmana's closing line had sat on its own short passage at `5.676`; the Third, Fourth, and Fifth Brahmanas' closing lines are held together with their final answer-passages.
- **Paired closer with the Fourth Brahmana.** The line *Everything else is of evil* on `5.719` sits in textual pairing with the line *Everything also is of evil* on `5.713` of the prior Brahmana. The two lines occupy the same position — at the close of Yajnavalkya's substantive answer, immediately before the narrator's *held his peace* line — and the difference between *also* and *else* is preserved as the source has it. Müller's translation does not gloss the variation, and the variation is not smoothed in the digitized source.

Passage `5.720` is the "Sixth Brahmana" header that opens Book 3, Part 6 of the Brihadaranyaka and is not part of this entity. The library chapter continues past this entity into the rest of the Third Adhyaya and the remaining portions of the volume; the Brihadaranyaka extends well beyond the boundaries of library chapter 5. About three minutes' reading.

## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 3, Part 4 — paired immediate-predecessor Brahmanas of the Janaka Vaideha contest with verbatim repetition of the opening Q-and-A frame; the Fourth Brahmana opened with Ushasta Kakrayana's question *tell me the Brahman which is visible, not invisible, the Self (atman), who is within all*, the formulaic reply *This, thy Self, who is within all*, and the follow-up *Which Self, O Yagnavalkya, is within all?* — the Fifth Brahmana opens with Kahola Kaushitakeya asking the same question in the same wording, receiving the same formulaic reply, and giving the same follow-up. The two Brahmanas hold the contest's procedure across a verbatim seam, with the substantive answer changing — four breaths and seer-of-sight in the Fourth, renouncing-mendicancy and the Muni/Brahmana sequence in the Fifth — and the closing line *Everything also is of evil* / *Everything else is of evil* preserved as a paired textual variant across the Brahmana boundary.
- **structural-parallel** → Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 2, Part 4 — paired Yajnavalkya teachings on renunciation; the Fourth Brahmana of the Second Adhyaya opens on Yajnavalkya's announcement *Maitreyi, verily I am going away from this my house (into the forest)* and works through the *Self-is-dear* refrain across the household, the figures of the drum-conch-lute, the smoke-from-fire of the Vedas, the salt-in-water, and the closing question *How, O beloved, should he know himself, the Knower?* — the Fifth Brahmana of the Third Adhyaya gives the form of life that conditions the departure: *they wander about as mendicants; for a desire for sons is desire for wealth, a desire for wealth is desire for worlds; both these are indeed desires.* The Second Adhyaya holds the moment of the renouncing-Brahmana, the Third Adhyaya holds the doctrine of his form of life.
- **structural-parallel** → Mundaka Upanishad, Book 1, Part 2 — paired Sanskrit teachings on the wandering Brahmana who has done with works and learning; the Second Khanda of the First Mundaka catalogues the form of Vedic sacrifice as *frail boats* by which fools imagine they reach the highest good and closes with the figure of the Brahmana who, having examined the worlds gained by works, approaches a teacher with fuel in his hand and is given the knowledge of Brahman — the Fifth Brahmana of the Brihadaranyaka's Third Adhyaya gives the same configuration in another form: the Brahmana, having done with learning and with strength, becomes a Muni and then a Brahmana, and *they wander about as mendicants*. Both texts hold the renouncing-Brahmana as the form of life of the knower of Brahman, with the Mundaka working the figure through the teacher-with-fuel scene and the Brihadaranyaka working it through the Muni/Brahmana sequence.
