# Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 2, Part 4

Book 2, Part 4 of the Brihadaranyaka Upanishad — the Fourth Brahmana of the Second Adhyaya — is one of the most-cited dialogic units of the Upanishadic literature: the conversation between Yajnavalkya and his wife Maitreyi at the moment Yajnavalkya is preparing to leave his house. The Brahmana opens with a domestic frame. *Now when Yagnavalkya was going to enter upon another state*, he says to Maitreyi, *Maitreyi, verily I am going away from this my house (into the forest). Forsooth, let me make a settlement between thee and that Katyayani, my other wife.* The conversation begins inside the household, between two named persons, with Yajnavalkya's other wife Katyayani named (but not present) and the impending renunciation as the framing condition.

Maitreyi's first question turns the settlement on its head: *My Lord, if this whole earth, full of wealth, belonged to me, tell me, should I be immortal by it?* Yajnavalkya answers directly: *No. Like the life of rich people will be thy life. But there is no hope of immortality by wealth.* Maitreyi's second question is the question the Brahmana will spend the rest of its space answering: *What should I do with that by which I do not become immortal? What my Lord knoweth (of immortality), tell that to me.* Yajnavalkya answers with a preface: *Thou who art truly dear to me, thou speakest dear words. Come, sit down, I will explain it to thee, and mark well what I say.*

The teaching that follows is built on repetition. The *Self-is-dear* refrain is given ten times in succession, each time with a different beloved object placed in the same sentence-template. *Verily, a husband is not dear, that you may love the husband; but that you may love the Self, therefore a husband is dear.* The same form is given for *a wife*, *sons*, *wealth*, *the Brahman-class*, *the Kshatra-class*, *the worlds*, *the Devas*, *creatures*, *everything*. Each repetition holds the same shape; each substitutes a different object; the cumulative effect is the closing line that follows the tenth: *Verily, the Self is to be seen, to be heard, to be perceived, to be marked, O Maitreyi! When we see, hear, perceive, and know the Self, then all this is known.* Verse 6 then turns the form on its negative side: *whosoever looks for the Brahman-class elsewhere than in the Self, was abandoned by the Brahman-class*; *whosoever looks for the Kshatra-class elsewhere than in the Self, was abandoned by the Kshatra-class*; *whosoever looks for the worlds elsewhere than in the Self, was abandoned by the worlds.*

Verses 7 through 12 give the figures the teaching is held in. The first three — drum, conch-shell, lute — share the same shape: *as the sounds of a drum, when beaten, cannot be seized externally by themselves, but the sound is seized, when the drum is seized or the beater of the drum*; the same for the conch-shell and the lute. The fourth figure (verse 10) gives the breath of the great Being: *as clouds of smoke proceed by themselves out of a lighted fire kindled with damp fuel, thus, verily, O Maitreyi, has been breathed forth from this great Being what we have as Rig-veda, Yagur-veda, Sama-veda, Atharvangirasas, Itihasa (legends), Purana (cosmogonies), Vidya (knowledge), the Upanishads…* The fifth figure (verse 11) gathers the senses' centres: *as all waters find their centre in the sea, all touches in the skin, all tastes in the tongue, all smells in the nose, all colours in the eye, all sounds in the ear, all percepts in the mind, all knowledge in the heart, all actions in the hands, all movements in the feet, and all the Vedas in speech.* The sixth figure (verse 12) is the lump of salt: *as a lump of salt, when thrown into water, becomes dissolved into water, and could not be taken out again, but wherever we taste the water it is salt — thus verily, O Maitreyi, does this great Being, endless, unlimited, consisting of nothing but knowledge, rise from out these elements, and vanish again in them. When he has departed, there is no more knowledge, I say, O Maitreyi.*

Verse 13 holds the Brahmana's structural turn. Maitreyi does not assent. She replies: *Here thou hast bewildered me, Sir, when thou sayest that having departed, there is no more knowledge.* Yajnavalkya answers: *O Maitreyi, I say nothing that is bewildering. This is enough, O beloved, for wisdom.* The duality-vs-Self argument follows: *for when there is as it were duality, then one sees the other, one smells the other, one hears the other, one salutes the other, one perceives the other, one knows the other; but when the Self only is all this, how should he smell another, how should he see another, how should he hear another, how should he salute another, how should he perceive another, how should he know another?* The Brahmana closes on the question rather than on a settled formula: *How, O beloved, should he know (himself), the Knower?* — *yenedaṁ sarvaṁ vijānāti, taṁ kena vijānīyāt* — *by what should one know the Knower?*

## Context

The Brihadaranyaka Upanishad is one of the two earliest of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads, sharing that position with the Chandogya. It belongs to the Shukla Yajurveda branch of the Vedic corpus and forms the closing portion of the Shatapatha Brahmana — the *Aranyaka* in its name marks it as a forest-text recited at the late stages of Vedic study. Modern scholarship places the Brihadaranyaka in roughly the eighth to sixth century BCE, prior to the Buddha; the text is anonymous, and the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship. The text is the longest of the principal Upanishads and is typically structured in six *adhyayas* (chapters), each containing several *brahmanas* (sections).

Book 2, Part 4 is the Fourth Brahmana of the Second Adhyaya. The First Brahmana ran the Gargya Balaki / Ajatasatru dialogue and closed on *the True of the True*; the Second Brahmana shifted to compact symbolic prose mapping the head onto cosmic figures; the Third Brahmana held the two-forms doctrine and the *neti neti* teaching, closing again on *the True of the True*. The Fourth Brahmana now opens directly into the Yajnavalkya-Maitreyi dialogue — a return to named-speaker dialogue after the two short symbolic Brahmanas, but with a different domestic frame than the Gargya-Ajatasatru conversation: husband and wife at the moment of renunciation, rather than Brahmana and Kshatriya at the king's court. The dialogue will be repeated almost verbatim later in the Brihadaranyaka (at Adhyaya 4, Brahmana 5), suggesting it was an early piece transmitted in two settings within the Upanishad. Yajnavalkya is one of the central named teachers of the Brihadaranyaka and will return as the speaker in the major dialogues of Adhyaya 3 and Adhyaya 4. The closing question — *by what should one know the Knower?* — is one of the most-cited Upanishadic formulations of the limit of inquiry, repeated in close form across the later Vedanta tradition.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the Brahmana is built on the *Self-is-dear* refrain at its centre and on a sequence of figures around it. The refrain is given ten times in identical form with the substituted object the only thing that changes. Reading the refrain aloud, allowing the repetition to register before any attempt to abstract a doctrine, recovers what the form is doing: each beloved object is held inside the same recognition; the recognition does not take the place of the object's dearness but is named as the ground of it. The figures that follow — drum, conch, lute, smoke from fire, waters in the sea, salt in water — accumulate the same recognition through different vehicles. Reading them in sequence, allowing the figures to compound rather than picking out the most-quoted, keeps the form the dialogue has when its work is most carried by figure.

Second, the dialogue does not end in agreement. Maitreyi voices her bewilderment — *here thou hast bewildered me, Sir* — and Yajnavalkya does not retract; he says *I say nothing that is bewildering, this is enough, O beloved, for wisdom*, and goes on to give the duality-vs-Self argument that closes on the question *How, O beloved, should he know himself, the Knower?* The Brahmana ends on a question, not on a confirmation. Reading the closing with attention to the unresolved question — and to the way the dialogue holds the bewilderment as part of the teaching rather than as a misstep — keeps the form the Brihadaranyaka has when the dialogue's recognition is held at the limit of what can be said.

- **structural-parallel** → Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 2, Part 1 — paired named-speaker dialogues of the Second Adhyaya; the First Brahmana holds the dialogue between the Brahmana Gargya Balaki and the Kshatriya king Ajatasatru — a public, court-set, twelve-fold catalogue-and-rejection — the Fourth Brahmana holds the dialogue between the husband Yajnavalkya and the wife Maitreyi — a domestic, household-set, refrain-and-figure conversation at the moment of renunciation. The same Adhyaya holds the question of Brahman across two named-dialogue settings of opposite social form.
- **structural-parallel** → Katha Upanishad, Book 1, Part 2 — paired Upanishadic moments where the inquirer refuses wealth in favor of the immortality-teaching; the Katha's Second Valli opens with Yama's distinction between the good (*śreyas*) and the pleasant (*preyas*) and praises Nachiketas for refusing the easier boons of long life and worldly prosperity to press for the deeper question, the Brihadaranyaka's Fourth Brahmana of the Second Adhyaya holds the closely related figure: Maitreyi, asked whether she would like the whole earth full of wealth, asks instead *what should I do with that by which I do not become immortal? What my Lord knoweth, tell that to me.* Both texts hold the inquirer's refusal of wealth as the moment at which the immortality-teaching can be received.
