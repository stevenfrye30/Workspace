# Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 9 (Raja Vidya Raja Guhya Yoga)

Chapter 9 of the Bhagavad Gita — the *Raja Vidya Raja Guhya Yoga*, conventionally translated "The Yoga of Royal Knowledge and Royal Mystery" (Arnold's translation gives the chapter the parallel name "The Book of Religion by the Kingly Knowledge and the Kingly Mystery") — is the chapter in which Krishna's self-description deepens from cosmological breadth into direct relational speech. The chapter opens with Krishna saying he will reveal what is most secret because Arjuna does not reject the teaching. The thirty-four verses develop the picture Chapter 7 began — Krishna pervading the world, the world resting in him without him resting in it — and turn it toward relation: those who worship him with steady mind, the offering of even a leaf or a flower made with devotion, the practice of doing whatever one does as offering, and the universalist claim that no one who turns to him with sincerity is lost. The chapter closes with a direct charge: fix the mind, be devoted, surrender.

## Context

The Bhagavad Gita is a 700-verse philosophical poem in eighteen chapters, set inside Book VI (the *Bhishma Parva*) of the Mahabharata. Most scholars place its present form between roughly the second century BCE and the second century CE — a layered composition that took shape during the long growth of the epic from an older bardic core. The traditional attribution is to Vyasa, the legendary compiler of the Mahabharata; the historical authorship is unknown.

Chapter 9 sits at the heart of the Gita's middle movement. Chapters 7 and 8 introduced the cosmological frame — Krishna's higher and lower natures, his pervasion of the world, the orientation that holds at the moment of death. Chapter 9 intensifies that frame and turns it toward the relation between Krishna and the one who recognizes him. The opening verses name the chapter's content as the "royal knowledge," the most-guarded secret; the central verses develop two strands of that knowledge in alternation — the cosmological (Krishna's pervasion of all things, the cycle of creation and dissolution under his eye) and the relational (devotion and the practice of offering). The closing verses give the chapter's most-quoted single line — that a leaf, a flower, a fruit, or water offered with devotion is received — and the universalist claim that any sincere turn toward him, regardless of station, is sufficient. The chapter ends with Krishna instructing Arjuna directly to fix the mind, be devoted, and surrender.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the chapter alternates between universal claims and direct address. A passage on Krishna's pervasion of all beings sits beside a passage on those who worship him with steady mind; a description of the cosmic cycle in which beings are sent out and drawn back sits beside the close-range instruction that whatever Arjuna does should be done as offering. Reading the chapter with the alternation visible — universal description giving way to direct relation, then returning — recovers the form the chapter has.

Second, the chapter widens its tone rather than abruptly changing it. The cosmological frame Chapters 7 and 8 built does not disappear in Chapter 9; the closing direct charge is given in the same dialogue Krishna has been conducting since Chapter 2. The change is one of register: the same teaching is rendered in language that emphasizes nearness and accessibility — the offering need not be elaborate, the practice can be done in any action, no one who turns sincerely is lost. Reading Chapter 9 as a widening of voice within the same argument, rather than as the beginning of a separate doctrine, follows the way the chapter is composed.

## The text

The text is held in the Archive library under `bhagavad-gita`, chapter 9 (Edwin Arnold's verse translation, *The Song Celestial*, 1885 — Project Gutenberg edition). Arnold's verse translation compresses the chapter's thirty-four Sanskrit shlokas into seventeen English verse-paragraphs followed by a closing colophon line (passages `9.1`–`9.18` in the library's verse-level subdivision). A modern prose translation by the International Gita Society is held in the library as a parallel translation. About twenty-five minutes' reading.

- **structural-parallel** → Dhammapada, Chapter 1 (The Twin Verses) — paired chapters in which the inner condition of the actor is treated as the determining factor in what an action becomes; the Twin Verses with mental orientation preceding action and its outcome, Chapter 9 with the practice of doing whatever one does as offering, in which the disposition of the offerer determines what the offering reaches.
