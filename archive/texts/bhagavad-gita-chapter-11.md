# Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 11 (Vishvarupa Darshana Yoga)

Chapter 11 of the Bhagavad Gita — the *Vishvarupa Darshana Yoga*, conventionally translated "The Yoga of the Vision of the Universal Form" (Arnold's translation gives the chapter the parallel name "The Book of the Manifesting of the One and Manifold") — is the visionary culmination of the Gita's middle movement. Arjuna asks Krishna to show him the universal form he has been describing through Chapters 7 to 10. Krishna grants him divine sight — the form is not visible to ordinary eyes — and assumes the cosmic appearance: every world in his body, infinite faces and eyes and mouths, blazing as a thousand suns, gods and sages and warriors visible at once, all the warriors of both armies rushing into the flaming mouths to be ground. Arjuna's response — awe, terror, his hair standing on end, repeated prostration — runs through the long middle of the chapter. Krishna names himself: "I am Time, the destroyer of worlds, come here to consume people." Arjuna asks for the familiar form to be restored. Krishna does so, and closes with the teaching that the universal form is reached only by single-minded devotion.

## Context

The Bhagavad Gita is a 700-verse philosophical poem in eighteen chapters, set inside Book VI (the *Bhishma Parva*) of the Mahabharata. Most scholars place its present form between roughly the second century BCE and the second century CE — a layered composition that took shape during the long growth of the epic from an older bardic core. The traditional attribution is to Vyasa, the legendary compiler of the Mahabharata; the historical authorship is unknown.

Chapter 11 is the culmination of the middle movement Chapters 7–10 prepared. Where those chapters described Krishna's nature in widening terms — pervasion, the cycle of creation and dissolution, the catalogue of representative forms — Chapter 11 turns description into direct sight. Arjuna asks to see; Krishna grants divine sight; the universal form is shown. The chapter is unusually long for the Gita (fifty-five verses in the standard Sanskrit numbering) and unusually visual; its imagery — the form blazing as a thousand suns, the warriors rushing into the flaming mouths, the river of bodies — is among the most concentrated in the work. The chapter is also where Sanjaya, the frame narrator who has been carrying the Gita's report to the blind king Dhritirashtra since Chapter 1, returns to the foreground; some of the most famous descriptions are spoken in his voice. Chapter 12 will resume the dialogue in its earlier register; Chapter 11 stands alone as a sustained visionary scene.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the chapter is structured as request, sight, response, and return. Arjuna asks (11.1–4); Krishna grants divine sight (11.5–8); the vision is described, first by Sanjaya and then by Arjuna's awestruck speech (11.9–31); Krishna names himself as Time, the destroyer of worlds (11.32–34); Arjuna trembles, prostrates, and asks for the familiar form to be restored (11.35–46); Krishna resumes the four-armed form and instructs Arjuna that this universal form is reached only by single-minded devotion (11.47–55). Reading the chapter as this five-fold structure, rather than as a single continuous vision, follows the way the chapter is composed.

Second, the chapter's main work is perception, not doctrine. Arjuna's response — fear, the hair standing on end, the repeated bowings, the asking who this is — is the chapter's central content. The famous declaration "I am Time, the destroyer of worlds" (11.32) is spoken inside that response, in answer to Arjuna's direct question. Reading the chapter with attention to perception and response rather than to extracted doctrine recovers the form the chapter has, and keeps the chapter's own sense of what is being shown.

## The text

The text is held in the Archive library under `bhagavad-gita`, chapter 11 (Edwin Arnold's verse translation, *The Song Celestial*, 1885 — Project Gutenberg edition). Arnold's verse translation here is unusually close to the Sanskrit verse-by-verse: the chapter's fifty-five Sanskrit shlokas correspond to forty-nine English verse-paragraphs followed by a closing colophon line (passages `11.1`–`11.50` in the library's verse-level subdivision). The lighter compression — striking against the heavier compression of Chapter 10 (forty-two shlokas in three verse-paragraphs) — reflects the visionary density of the Sanskrit, which Arnold rendered closer to its line-by-line texture than the more discursive earlier chapters. A modern prose translation by the International Gita Society is held in the library as a parallel translation. About forty minutes' reading.
