# Mundaka Upanishad, Book 2, Part 1

Book 2, Part 1 of the Mundaka Upanishad — the First Khanda of the Second Mundaka — opens the second book of the Upanishad. Where the First Mundaka closed with the inquirer turning from the worlds gained by works to the teacher who holds the higher knowledge, the Second Mundaka begins to give that knowledge in the form of an articulated account of source. The first verse sets the figure: as from a blazing fire sparks fly forth a thousandfold, being like unto fire, so are various beings brought forth from the Imperishable and return there again. The second verse names the heavenly Person — without body, both within and without, not produced, without breath and without mind, pure, higher than the high Imperishable — and the verses that follow unfold the emergence: from him are born breath, mind, the senses, ether, air, light, water, and earth; the sky is his head, the sun and the moon his eyes, the wind his breath, the universe his heart; from him come Agni and the sun as its fuel, the moon and rain, the herbs of the earth, the seed by which living beings are begotten; from him the four Vedas, the initiatory rites, the sacrifices, the priestly fees, the year, the sacrificer, the worlds; the gods, the sadhyas, men, cattle, birds, the up and down breaths; the sevenfold lists of the senses and their objects and acts; the seas and mountains, the rivers, the herbs and the juice by which the inner Self subsists. The Khanda closes with the verse that the Person is all this — sacrifice, penance, Brahman, the highest immortal — and that the one who knows him, hidden in the cave of the heart, scatters the knot of ignorance here on earth.

## Context

The Mundaka Upanishad is one of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads — a small group of texts traditionally received as the philosophical core of the late Vedic literature. It is associated with the Atharvaveda branch of the Vedic corpus and is composed in Sanskrit verse. Modern scholarship places its composition somewhat later than the Katha, in roughly the fourth to second century BCE; the principal Upanishads as a group are dated to the centuries on either side of the Buddha. The text is anonymous; the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship.

Book 2, Part 1 is the third of the six parts of the Mundaka and continues directly from Book 1, Part 2. The First Mundaka set the question, named the two kinds of knowledge, gave the lower in detail, and closed with the inquirer approaching the teacher; the Second Mundaka opens by giving the higher in the form of a sustained account of source. The literary movement is from inquiry through critique into instruction. Within the Vedic textual line, the Khanda's account of the cosmic Person from whom the elements, the gods, the sacrifices, and the worlds emerge stands close to the older Purusha-sukta (Rigveda 10.90), with the Upanishadic Person now identified with the Imperishable and held in the cave of the heart rather than in the cosmic offering.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the Khanda is structured by recurring source imagery. The first verse gives the central figure — sparks from a fire, beings from the Imperishable — and the verses that follow unfold the same figure through different vocabulary: from him is born breath; from him comes Agni; from him come the Vedas; from him the gods are begotten; the seven senses spring from him; the seas and the mountains come from him. The procedure is layered rather than argumentative: each verse names another field that emerges from the same source, and the cumulative effect is the recognition the closing verse states — that the Person is all this. Reading the Khanda with attention to the recurrence of the formula "from him" — and to the slight rotation of vocabulary it gathers each time — recovers the form the teaching has.

Second, the Khanda moves from emergence to recognition. The first nine verses unfold what comes from the Imperishable; the closing verse turns: the Person who is sacrifice, penance, Brahman, and the highest immortal is hidden in the cave of the heart, and the one who knows him there scatters the knot of ignorance. The outward unfolding of the cosmos is not the goal of the verses; the goal is the inward recognition that the same Person who is named as the source of the elements is present in the heart of the one who hears the teaching. Reading the Khanda with the inward turn at the close held alongside the long outward unfolding that prepares it, rather than treating the cosmological verses as a separate doctrine, keeps the form the Mundaka gives this part of its instruction.

## The text

The text is held in the Archive library under `upanishads-muller-part2`, chapter 5 (Müller translation, 1884 — *The Sacred Books of the East*, Volume XV; via SacredTexts.com). Library chapter 5 is shared across the Katha entries (Book 1, Part 3 and Book 2, Parts 1–3) and the Mundaka entries already in the Archive (Book 1, Parts 1 and 2 at passages `5.77` through `5.105`); Book 2, Part 1 of the Mundaka — the entity here — occupies passages `5.106` through `5.118`. Passage `5.106` is the "Second Mundaka" book header and `5.107` is the "First Khanda" part header. Passages `5.108` through `5.118` carry the ten numbered verses of the Khanda, with verse 6 split across passages `5.113` and `5.114` where Müller's footnote interrupts the verse mid-line; the split is explicitly marked in the source by the editor's bracketed note "[paragraph continues]" at the start of `5.114`. Passage `5.119` is the "Second Khanda" header that opens Book 2, Part 2 of the Mundaka and is not part of this entity. The library chapter continues past this entity into the rest of the Mundaka and the remaining Upanishads of the volume. Ten verses; about eight minutes' reading.

## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 7 (Jnana Vijnana Yoga) — paired Sanskrit passages on the one source from which all proceeds; the Gita's Chapter 7 names Krishna as the origin and dissolution of all that is, the thread on which the universe is strung as gems on a string, and identifies him with each form the senses meet — the taste in water, the light in the sun and the moon, the sound in the ether — the Mundaka's Second Mundaka opens with the same recognition through the figure of sparks from a blazing fire, with beings coming forth from the Imperishable and the cosmic Person named as the source from which breath, mind, the senses, the elements, the gods, men, and the worlds emerge.
- **structural-parallel** → Katha Upanishad, Book 2, Part 2 — paired Upanishadic passages on the one becoming many; the Katha's Fifth Valli holds the recognition through the figures of the one fire that becomes many forms and the one air that becomes many forms while remaining themselves one, the Mundaka's Second Mundaka opens with the closely related figure of sparks flying forth a thousandfold from a blazing fire — paired moments in the same Upanishadic family, the figure of source-and-emergence given in two close vocabularies that may be read together.
- **structural-parallel** → Daodejing, Chapters 1–10 — paired short passages on a source not directly named; the Daodejing's early chapters return to the recognition that the named is the mother of the ten thousand things and the unnamed the source from which named and nameless issue together, the Mundaka's Second Mundaka holds a comparable recognition in the figure of the Imperishable from which beings emerge and to which they return — the same care for an origin that is not itself among the things that come from it, held in two distinct civilizational vocabularies.
