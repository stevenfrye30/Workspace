# Taittiriyaka Upanishad, Book 1, Part 10

Book 1, Part 10 of the Taittiriyaka Upanishad — the Tenth Anuvaka of the Sikshavalli — is short and a small form-shift from the school-lessons of the surrounding anuvakas. Where the Eighth and Ninth Anuvakas held the *Omkara*-praise and the *svādhyāya-pravacana* refrain, and where the Eleventh Anuvaka will hold the convocation address that the teacher gives the graduating student, the Tenth Anuvaka places between them a brief quoted verse: a sequence of six first-person self-affirmations attributed to the poet Trisanku. *I am he who shakes the tree* — Müller glosses the line *the tree of the world, which has to be cut down by knowledge*. *My glory is like the top of a mountain. I, whose pure light has risen high, am that which is truly immortal, as it resides in the sun. I am the brightest treasure. I am wise, immortal, imperishable.* The closing line names the source — *this is the teaching of the Veda, by the poet Trisanku* — and identifies the verse as something received and given to the student to recite. The Anuvaka does not explain Trisanku's verse; it places the verse where it can be carried.

## Context

The Taittiriyaka Upanishad is one of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads — a small group of texts traditionally received as the philosophical core of the late Vedic literature. It belongs to the Krishna Yajurveda branch of the Vedic corpus and to the Taittiriya school of that branch; the Upanishad sits inside the Taittiriya Aranyaka. Modern scholarship places it among the older prose Upanishads, in roughly the sixth to fifth century BCE — earlier than the verse Upanishads of the Katha and Mundaka. The text is anonymous; the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship.

Book 1, Part 10 sits inside the Sikshavalli, the chapter on *śikṣā*. The Anuvaka does not extend the school-lesson form of the Eighth and Ninth Anuvakas; it inserts a quoted verse from a named poet, Trisanku, into the recitation sequence — a piece of received material the student is to carry as part of *svādhyāya*. The procedure is characteristic of the late-Vedic prose Upanishads: technical lessons, refrains, and quoted verses are interleaved without breaking the surrounding instruction, and the named-source verse is given the same recitational weight as the structured catalogues that surround it. The Trisanku verse has been kept in liturgical and pedagogical use ever since, often recited at the close of recitation sessions.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the Anuvaka is built on first-person self-affirmation. Each of the six short statements opens with *I* — *I am he who shakes the tree, I, whose pure light has risen high, I am the brightest treasure, I am wise, immortal, imperishable.* The form is the form of the seer's mantra: the speaker takes up the recognition that the verse holds and says it in the first person, not as a description of someone else. Reading the Anuvaka aloud, allowing the parallel openings to register before any attempt to extract a single doctrine, recovers the mantric form the Trisanku verse has and the place such recitation holds inside the school's daily *svādhyāya*.

Second, the Anuvaka closes with an attribution rather than an exposition. The last line names the source — *this is the teaching of the Veda, by the poet Trisanku* — and stops. The Anuvaka does not interpret the verse; it places the verse, names its source, and lets the recitation carry the rest. Reading the Anuvaka with the closing attribution held alongside the verse, rather than treating the verse as a self-contained metaphysical claim, keeps the form the school-form gives to received material.

## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Taittiriyaka Upanishad, Book 1, Part 9 — paired immediately consecutive Anuvakas of the Sikshavalli; the Ninth Anuvaka holds the *svādhyāya-pravacana* refrain in which each named obligation is paired with *learning and practising the Veda*, the Tenth Anuvaka places a short received verse — Trisanku's first-person mantra — inside the same recitational sequence. The Ninth gives the discipline; the Tenth gives the kind of verse the discipline carries.
- **structural-parallel** → Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 10 (Vibhuti Yoga) — paired Sanskrit passages that hold a sequence of first-person self-affirmations attributed to a named voice; the Gita's Chapter 10 is Krishna's long catalogue of *I am* statements — *I am the Self, seated in the heart of all beings; I am the beginning, the middle, and the end; among the Adityas I am Vishnu; among the lights I am the radiant sun* — placed inside Krishna's instruction to Arjuna, the Taittiriyaka's Tenth Anuvaka holds the same form in compressed school-recitation: six *I am* statements attributed to the poet Trisanku and given as a verse the student is to carry. Both texts trust the first-person mantric form to do work that exposition would not.
- **structural-parallel** → Mundaka Upanishad, Book 3, Part 1 — paired Upanishadic moments of self-recognition; the Mundaka's First Khanda of the Third Mundaka holds the recognition that when the seer sees the brilliant maker as the Person who has his source in Brahman, then he is wise — *and shaking off good and evil, he reaches the highest oneness, free from passions* (Mundaka 3.1.3) — and that the true Brahman *shines forth grand, smaller than small, far beyond what is far and yet near here* (Mundaka 3.1.7), the Taittiriyaka's Tenth Anuvaka holds the recognition in the first person — *I, whose pure light has risen high, am that which is truly immortal* — paired moments in the late-Vedic literature where the same recognition is given once as observation and once as mantra.
