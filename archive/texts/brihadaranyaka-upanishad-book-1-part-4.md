# Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 1, Part 4

Book 1, Part 4 of the Brihadaranyaka Upanishad — the Fourth Brahmana of the First Adhyaya — is one of the longest and most concentrated Brahmanas of the Upanishad and gathers, inside seventeen verses, a sequence of teachings that the later tradition has continued to draw on as the Brihadaranyaka's central material. The opening cosmogony differs in form from the cosmogony of the Second Brahmana: there Death, here the Self alone — *ātmā vā idam agra āsīt, puruṣavidhaḥ* — *in the beginning this was Self alone, in the shape of a person.* The first verse gives the *I*-name etymology: looking around and seeing nothing but his Self, he first said *this is I*, and therefore he became *I* by name; even now, when a man is asked, he first says *this is I*. The second verse gives the *fear-from-a-second* line: he feared, and a man who is lonely fears; he thought, *as there is nothing but myself, why should I fear?* — and his fear passed away. The third verse gives the husband-and-wife etymology: he felt no delight, and a man who is lonely feels no delight; he wished for a second; he was so large as man and wife together; he made his Self to fall in two (*pat*), and from it arose husband (*pati*) and wife (*patnī*). The fourth verse gives the pursuit-cosmogony: she thought *how can he embrace me, after having produced me from himself? I shall hide myself*; she became a cow, the other a bull; mare and stallion; male and female ass; she-goat and he-goat; and so all paired creatures. The fifth verse gives the recognition: *I indeed am this creation, for I created all this*; the knower of this lives in his own creation. The sixth verse gives fire produced by rubbing — from the mouth and from the hands — and the line that each god to whom one is told to sacrifice is but his manifestation. The seventh verse gives the *name and form* development of the undeveloped, with the figure of the Self entering to the tips of the finger-nails as a razor in a razor-case or as fire in a fire-place; the names of breath, speech, eye, ear, and mind are but the names of his acts.

The middle movement turns to the dearness of the Self. The eighth verse holds the line that the Self is nearer than anything, dearer than a son, dearer than wealth, dearer than all else; the one who declares another than the Self dear will lose what is dear. The ninth verse asks the question: *if men think that by knowledge of Brahman they will become everything, what then did that Brahman know, from whence all this sprang?* The tenth verse gives the answer that has been most quoted from the Brahmana — *brahma vā idam agra āsīt, tad ātmānam evāvet, ahaṁ brahmāsmi iti* — *verily in the beginning this was Brahman; that Brahman knew its Self only, saying I am Brahman.* From it all this sprang. Whatever Deva was awakened so as to know Brahman, he indeed became that. The line continues with the warning that the man who worships another deity, thinking the deity is one and he another, is like a beast for the Devas.

The closing movement is a social cosmogony. The eleventh verse holds that Brahman, one only, was not strong enough; it created Kshatra — power — and the Kshatras among the Devas: Indra, Varuna, Soma, Rudra, Parganya, Yama, Mrityu, Isana. The twelfth verse holds that he was still not strong enough, and created the Vis, the people, in their orders: Vasus, Rudras, Adityas, Visve Devas, Maruts. The thirteenth verse holds that he was still not strong enough, and created the Sudra colour as Pushan, the nourisher; this earth is Pushan because it nourishes all this. The fourteenth verse holds that he was still not strong enough, and created *dharma* — Law — the Kshatra of the Kshatra; therefore there is nothing higher than the Law, and even a weak man rules a stronger with the help of the Law. The fifteenth verse names the four — Brahman, Kshatra, Vis, Sudra — among the Devas (as Agni and the rest) and among men (as Brahmana, Kshatriya, Vaisya, Sudra), and gives the warning that the Self not known is the world of all creatures. The sixteenth verse continues that Self-as-world-of-all-creatures recognition through the recitation, the offering, the cattle-feeding, and the support of guests. The seventeenth verse closes the Brahmana on the threefold desire — wife for offspring, wealth for sacrifices — and the line that, even wishing for more, he would not find it.

## Context

The Brihadaranyaka Upanishad is one of the two earliest of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads, sharing that position with the Chandogya. It belongs to the Shukla Yajurveda branch of the Vedic corpus and forms the closing portion of the Shatapatha Brahmana — the *Aranyaka* in its name marks it as a forest-text recited at the late stages of Vedic study. Modern scholarship places the Brihadaranyaka in roughly the eighth to sixth century BCE, prior to the Buddha; the text is anonymous, and the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship. The text is the longest of the principal Upanishads and is typically structured in six *adhyayas* (chapters), each containing several *brahmanas* (sections).

Book 1, Part 4 is the fourth Brahmana of the First Adhyaya and is among the most-cited single units of the Upanishadic literature. Its tenth verse — *aham brahmāsmi* — is one of the four *mahāvākyas* (great sayings) the later Vedanta tradition has gathered as the central identity-statements of the Upanishadic corpus, alongside *tat tvam asi* (Chandogya), *prajñānaṁ brahma* (Aitareya), and *ayam ātmā brahma* (Mandukya). Its third and fourth verses give the husband-and-wife and the pursuit-cosmogony — figures repeated in close form in other Upanishadic and Puranic literature. Its eleventh through fifteenth verses give the four-fold social cosmogony in close relation to the older *Puruṣa-sūkta* of the Rigveda (10.90). The Brahmana's procedure is characteristic: cosmogony, recognition, and social order held inside one continuous Brahmana with the same Self at the centre of each.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the Brahmana works through three connected movements that share a single procedure of self-recognition. The first movement gives the cosmogony as the Self's self-division — into name (*aham*), into pair (*pati/patnī*), into species (cow/bull and the rest), into name-and-form, into the senses by which it is known indirectly. The second movement turns inward and gives the recognition that the Self is the dearest, and that *I am Brahman*. The third movement gives the social cosmogony as Brahman's progressive creation of Kshatra, Vis, Sudra, and *dharma* through the same recognition that what is created is what it itself becomes. Reading the Brahmana with the three movements held alongside one another, attending to the way each movement holds the same recognition in a different field, recovers the form the Brihadaranyaka has when its work is most ambitious.

Second, the Brahmana proceeds by etymology and by figure rather than by argument. *Aham* (I) is named by the moment of self-saying; *pati* and *patnī* are named by the falling-in-two; *Aditi* (in the Second Brahmana) is named by eating; the Self is named indirectly through the senses; Pushan is named by the earth's nourishing. The figures — the razor in the razor-case, the fire in the fire-place, the beasts that nourish the Devas — work alongside the etymologies. Reading the Brahmana with the etymologies and figures held as part of the form rather than as commentary on it keeps the Brihadaranyaka's procedure visible. The Brahmana does not state the Self; it names it through what the Self does and through what is named after what the Self has done.

## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 1, Part 3 — paired immediately consecutive Brahmanas of the First Adhyaya; the Third Brahmana grounded the recitation of the *udgītha* in the contest of the Devas and the Asuras and in the breath in the mouth, with the *Pavamana* prayer at its close, the Fourth Brahmana opens with the cosmogony of the Self alone — *ātmā vā idam agra āsīt, puruṣavidhaḥ* — and develops through self-division, recognition, and social order. The two Brahmanas hold the same kind of internal three-movement structure (contest/etymology/prayer in the Third; cosmogony/recognition/social-order in the Fourth) and use the same procedure of etymological derivation across their substance.
- **structural-parallel** → Taittiriyaka Upanishad, Book 2, Part 6 — paired Sanskrit creation formulas built on the source's wish for a second; the Taittiriyaka's Sixth Anuvaka of the Brahmanandavalli holds the formula *so 'kāmayata, bahu syām, prajāyeyeti* — *he wished, may I be many, may I grow forth* — placed inside the dialogic close of the recursive descent, the Brihadaranyaka's Fourth Brahmana holds the closely related formula *sa dvitīyam aichat* and *sa eṣo 'kāmayata, dvitīya ātmā mā syād iti* — *he wished for a second, let me have a second body* — and develops the wish into the husband-and-wife etymology and the pursuit-cosmogony of paired creatures. Both texts hold the source's becoming-two as a wish that produces a partner.
- **structural-parallel** → Mundaka Upanishad, Book 3, Part 2 — paired Upanishadic identity-statements; the Mundaka's closing Khanda holds *brahma veda brahmaiva bhavati* — *he who knows that highest Brahman becomes Brahman* (Mundaka 3.2.9) — as the closing recognition of the Mundaka, the Brihadaranyaka's Fourth Brahmana holds *ahaṁ brahmāsmi* — *I am Brahman* — at its tenth verse as the recognition from which all this sprang. Paired moments where the late-Vedic literature names the same recognition in close vocabulary, the Mundaka in the third person and the Brihadaranyaka in the first.
