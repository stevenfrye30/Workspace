# Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 3 (Karma Yoga)

Chapter 3 of the Bhagavad Gita — the *Karma Yoga*, conventionally translated "The Yoga of Action" — continues Krishna's reply to Arjuna and turns directly to the question Chapter 2 left open: if the imperishable self is undisturbed by event, why should Arjuna act at all? Arjuna's complaint that opens the chapter — that Krishna's earlier counsel has confused him, that he has heard meditation is nobler than action and yet is being urged to fight — is answered across forty-three verses. Krishna's argument is that action cannot be avoided; that one must act according to one's place in the world without attachment to the fruits; that the welfare of the world requires disciplined action even from those who have nothing personal to gain by it; and that what drives action wrong is not the action itself but the desire and anger that bind a person to its outcomes.

## Context

The Bhagavad Gita is a 700-verse philosophical poem in eighteen chapters, set inside Book VI (the *Bhishma Parva*) of the Mahabharata. Most scholars place its present form between roughly the second century BCE and the second century CE — a layered composition that took shape during the long growth of the epic from an older bardic core. The traditional attribution is to Vyasa, the legendary compiler of the Mahabharata; the historical authorship is unknown.

Chapter 3 takes up the ethical consequence of Chapter 2's foundational teaching. Where Chapter 2 stated the position — that the self is imperishable, that action should be performed without attachment to its fruits, that the steady-minded sage is undisturbed by event — Chapter 3 turns to the practical question this raises: if the inner self is untouched, why act at all? The forty-three verses of the chapter give Krishna's sustained answer. The argument moves through several stages: the impossibility of inaction (the body must act simply to live); the cosmic cycle of sacrifice, in which human action sustains the order that sustains human life; the responsibility of the wise to act for the welfare of the world (*lokasamgraha*), with the ancient king Janaka as example; and a closing diagnosis of why action goes wrong, in which Krishna names desire (*kama*) and anger (*krodha*) as the inner forces that drive a person to act against his own better understanding.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the chapter is recursive rather than linear. Krishna does not give a single argument and move on; he answers Arjuna's question from several angles in sequence — by appeal to the necessity of bodily existence, by appeal to the cosmic order maintained through sacrifice, by appeal to the example of past rulers, by appeal to his own divine action, and by diagnosis of the inner causes of misconduct. Reading the chapter with the awareness that the same answer is being given several times in different idioms, rather than as a single linear proof, recovers the form the chapter has.

Second, the closing third of the chapter shifts from the metaphysics of action to a short practical analysis of why action goes wrong. Verses 3.36–43 begin with Arjuna's question — what impels a person to act against his own better understanding? — and answer it by naming desire and anger as the underlying forces, before Krishna instructs Arjuna how to subdue them. Reading the closing section as a hinge between Chapter 3 and the chapters that follow — which take up the inner forces in greater depth — keeps the chapter's role in the wider argument visible.

## The text

The text is held in the Archive library under `bhagavad-gita`, chapter 3 (Edwin Arnold's verse translation, *The Song Celestial*, 1885 — Project Gutenberg edition). Arnold's verse translation compresses the chapter's forty-three Sanskrit shlokas into six English verse-paragraphs followed by a closing colophon line (passages `3.1`–`3.7` in the library's verse-level subdivision). A modern prose translation by the International Gita Society is held in the library as a parallel translation. About twenty-five minutes' reading.
