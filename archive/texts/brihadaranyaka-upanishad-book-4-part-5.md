# Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 4, Part 5

Book 4, Part 5 of the Brihadaranyaka Upanishad — the Fifth Brahmana of the Fourth Adhyaya — is the second recension of the Yajnavalkya–Maitreyi dialogue. The same dialogue had been given in the Fourth Brahmana of the Second Adhyaya. The Brihadaranyaka holds the dialogue twice — once near the close of the Second Adhyaya and once here — and the two recensions stand together as one of the Upanishadic literature's clearest instances of textual doublet within a single work. Reading the present Brahmana with the prior recension held alongside is the only way to recover what is unique to this version of the dialogue.

The Adhyaya's setting now shifts entirely. The Adhyaya's first four Brahmanas had given the Janaka Vaideha–Yajnavalkya teaching across the audience-chamber of the king of the Videhas, with the king's bow at the close of the Second Brahmana, the priest's fear of the king at the narrative interruption of the Third, the death-and-rebirth-and-karma teaching closing on the fearless Brahman in the Fourth, and Janaka's two kingdom-and-self surrenders at the close of the Second and Fourth. The Fifth Brahmana opens with no king, no audience-chamber, no priestly contest, no offer of cattle. The setting is Yajnavalkya's house, on the day Yajnavalkya is leaving it for the forest, and the named interlocutor is his wife.

The Brahmana opens with a framing line not given in the prior recension. *Yagnavalkya had two wives, Maitreyi and Katyayani. Of these Maitreyi was conversant with Brahman, but Katyayani possessed such knowledge only as women possess.* The Fourth Brahmana of the Second Adhyaya had not introduced the two wives in this distinguishing form; the Fifth Brahmana of the Fourth Adhyaya gives the introductory line at the open of verse 1 as a procedural setup that places Maitreyi as the named partner of the substantive teaching and Katyayani as the wife held aside. The same Katyayani is named in the dialogue's body — *let me make a settlement between thee and that Katyayani* — but she does not speak; the dialogue runs entirely between Yajnavalkya and Maitreyi.

Verse 1 closes the framing setup with the announcement that the dialogue rests on: *Yagnavalkya, when he wished to get ready for another state of life (when he wished to give up the state of a householder, and retire into the forest)*. The clause is the same as the prior recension's at Book 2, Part 4. Verse 2 holds the announcement directly to Maitreyi: *Maitreyi, verily I am going away from this my house (into the forest). Forsooth, let me make a settlement between thee and that Katyayani.*

Verses 3 and 4 hold Maitreyi's two questions and Yajnavalkya's responses. Verse 3: *My Lord, if this whole earth, full of wealth, belonged to me, tell me, should I be immortal by it, or no?* Yajnavalkya: *No, like the life of rich people will be thy life. But there is no hope of immortality by wealth.* Verse 4: *What should I do with that by which I do not become immortal? What my Lord knoweth (of immortality), tell that clearly to me.* The two-question form is held verbatim across the two recensions. Verse 5 holds Yajnavalkya's preface to the substantive teaching: *Thou who art truly dear to me, thou hast increased what is dear (to me in thee). Therefore, if you like, Lady, I will explain it to thee, and mark well what I say.*

Verse 6 holds the Self-is-dear refrain. The form is *Verily, X is not dear, that you may love X; but that you may love the Self, therefore X is dear.* The refrain runs across twelve entries in the present recension, given each on its own short passage at `5.1146`–`5.1157`: husband, wife, sons, wealth, **cattle**, the Brahman-class, the Kshatra-class, the worlds, the Devas, the Vedas, creatures, everything. The eleventh of these (creatures) and the twelfth (everything) close the refrain; the fifth (cattle, at `5.1150`, with footnote `[*2]`) is unique to this recension and was not given in the prior version of the refrain at Book 2, Part 4. The substantive close of the refrain at `5.1158` returns to the procedural form: *Verily, the Self is to be seen, to be heard, to be perceived, to be marked, O Maitreyi! When the Self has been seen, heard, perceived, and known, then all this is known!*

Verse 7 holds the inversion of the refrain on `5.1159`. The form: *Whosoever looks for X elsewhere than in the Self, was abandoned by X.* The same seven categories are run — the Brahman-class, the Kshatra-class, the worlds, the Devas, the Vedas, the creatures, anything — and the inversion gives the form a closure that the affirmative refrain had not given. The closing line at `5.1160` holds the substantive identification: *This Brahman-class, this Kshatra-class, these worlds, these Devas, these Vedas, all these beings, this everything, all is that Self.*

Verses 8–10 hold the three-fold simile of the drum, the conch-shell, and the lute. *As the sounds of a drum, when beaten, cannot be seized externally (by themselves), but the sound is seized, when the drum is seized, or the beater of the drum.* The same form is given for the conch-shell (verse 9) and the lute (verse 10). The figure holds the Self as that-by-which-the-sounds-are-seized rather than the sounds themselves; the substantive clause is preserved across each of the three iterations.

Verse 11 holds the smoke-from-fire simile applied to the Vedas. *As clouds of smoke proceed by themselves out of lighted fire kindled with damp fuel, thus verily, O Maitreyi, has been breathed forth from this great Being what we have as Rig-veda, Yagur-veda, Sama-veda, Atharvangirasas, Itihasa, Purana, Vidya, the Upanishads, Slokas, Sutras, Anuvyakhyanas, Vyakhyanas, what is sacrificed, what is poured out, food, drink, this world and the other world, and all creatures. From him alone all these were breathed forth.* The Vedic-textual list runs the same fourteen-item catalogue as the First Brahmana of the Fourth Adhyaya at `5.948` (Yajnavalkya's substantive elaboration on the Sailini-as-speech teaching), with the same closing list of ritual-and-cosmic items. The two appearances of the catalogue in the Adhyaya hold the same Vedic-textual procedure across two distinct teachings.

Verse 12 holds the all-waters-finding-centre catalogue. The form runs through eleven substantive resting-places: *all waters find their centre in the sea, all touches in the skin, all tastes in the tongue, all smells in the nose, all colours in the eye, all sounds in the ear, all percepts in the mind, all knowledge in the heart, all actions in the hands, all movements in the feet, and all the Vedas in speech*. The catalogue closes on speech with the same eleventh-item closing as the prior recension. Verse 13 holds the salt-in-water simile. *As a mass of salt has neither inside nor outside, but is altogether a mass of taste, thus indeed has that Self neither inside nor outside, but is altogether a mass of knowledge; and having risen from out these elements, vanishes again in them. When he has departed, there is no more knowledge (name), I say, O Maitreyi — thus spoke Yagnavalkya.* The substantive close of verse 13 carries the line that Maitreyi's bewilderment will turn on: *when he has departed, there is no more knowledge*.

Verse 14 holds Maitreyi's bewilderment. *Here, Sir, thou hast landed me in utter bewilderment. Indeed, I do not understand him.* Yajnavalkya's response is given on the same passage: *O Maitreyi, I say nothing that is bewildering. Verily, beloved, that Self is imperishable, and of an indestructible nature.*

Verse 15 holds the closing teaching. *For when there is as it were duality, then one sees the other, one smells the other, one tastes the other, one salutes the other, one hears the other, one perceives the other, one touches the other, one knows the other.* The eight-clause duality-list is paired with the contrasting non-duality formula: *but when the Self only is all this, how should he see another, how should he smell another, how should he taste another, how should he salute another, how should he hear another, how should he touch another, how should he know another?* The closing of the contrast turns to the *neti neti* line: *That Self is to be described by No, no! He is incomprehensible, for he cannot be comprehended; he is imperishable, for he cannot perish; he is unattached, for he does not attach himself; unfettered, he does not suffer, he does not fail.* The form is the four-clause but-construction given in the same wording as `5.921` of the Ninth Brahmana of the Third Adhyaya, `5.1024` of the Second Brahmana of the Fourth Adhyaya, and `5.1131` of the Fourth Brahmana of the Fourth Adhyaya. The closing question follows: *How, O beloved, should he know the Knower?* The same closing question had been the close of the prior recension at Book 2, Part 4.

The Brahmana then closes on a narrative line not in the prior recension. *Thus, O Maitreyi, thou hast been instructed. Thus far goes immortality. Having said so, Yagnavalkya went away (into the forest).* The closing-narrative is unique to this recension; the Fourth Brahmana of the Second Adhyaya had closed on the question without a narrator's report on Yajnavalkya's departure. The present Brahmana places the household-renunciation explicitly inside the Adhyaya's narrative, with the priest's physical departure named as the closing event.

## Context

The Brihadaranyaka Upanishad is one of the two earliest of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads, sharing that position with the Chandogya. It belongs to the Shukla Yajurveda branch of the Vedic corpus and forms the closing portion of the Shatapatha Brahmana — the *Aranyaka* in its name marks it as a forest-text recited at the late stages of Vedic study. Modern scholarship places the Brihadaranyaka in roughly the eighth to sixth century BCE, prior to the Buddha; the text is anonymous, and the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship. The text is the longest of the principal Upanishads and is typically structured in six *adhyayas* (chapters), each containing several *brahmanas* (sections).

Book 4, Part 5 sits in the Brihadaranyaka as the second recension of one of the Upanishad's most-cited dialogic units. The doublet has been read in two principal ways in the scholarly literature. One reading takes the Fourth Brahmana of the Second Adhyaya as the original and the Fifth Brahmana of the Fourth Adhyaya as a recapitulation placed at the close of the Yajnavalkya cycle to bring the Adhyaya to a household-renunciation closing; on this reading, the Adhyaya's prior four Brahmanas hold the public-and-royal teaching of Yajnavalkya, and the present Brahmana holds the domestic-and-renunciatory closing of his life as a householder. The other reading takes the two recensions as parallel transmissions of the same teaching attached to two different parts of the Brihadaranyaka by the editorial work of the *vamsha* (lineage) tradition; on this reading, the doublet is not narrative but redactional, and the Brihadaranyaka's editors held the dialogue twice because two transmission-streams gave it twice. Both readings are compatible with the source's preservation of the doublet, and the Archive's preservation of the two recensions in two separate Brahmana entries follows the source rather than collapsing the two into a single entry.

The Brahmana also holds the Fourth Adhyaya's closing narrative on Yajnavalkya's householder-state. The Adhyaya's Brahmanas hold the teaching across two named interlocutors — Janaka Vaideha in Brahmanas 1–4 and Maitreyi in Brahmana 5 — and across two settings — the public audience-chamber and the private household. The closing narrative line *Having said so, Yagnavalkya went away (into the forest)* is the only narrator's-voice report in the Adhyaya of Yajnavalkya's physical departure for renunciation, and it places the household-renunciation explicitly inside the Adhyaya's structure. The Sixth Brahmana of the Adhyaya will hold the *vamsha* lineage list of the teachers, and the Adhyaya as a whole will close with the lineage rather than the substantive teaching; the present Brahmana is the Adhyaya's last sustained substantive Brahmana and its narrator's closing.

The Self-is-dear refrain at verse 6 holds twelve entries in the present recension. The Fourth Brahmana of the Second Adhyaya had given the refrain across eleven entries (husband, wife, sons, wealth, the Brahman-class, the Kshatra-class, the worlds, the Devas, creatures, all this, the Vedas — the order varies in editions). The Fifth Brahmana of the Fourth Adhyaya inserts cattle as the fifth entry: *Verily, cattle are not dear, that you may love cattle; but that you may love the Self, therefore cattle are dear.* The cattle-entry is the only substantive addition the present recension makes to the refrain. The reasons for the addition are not given in Müller's apparatus; the editorial procedure of the recension is preserved in the source rather than smoothed.

The Brahmana also pairs with the First Brahmana of the Fourth Adhyaya at the level of the Vedic-textual catalogue. The First Brahmana's verse-2 substantive elaboration on Sailini's speech-as-Brahman teaching at `5.948` had given the catalogue *Rig-Veda, Yagur-veda, Sama-veda, Atharvangirasas, Itihasa, Purana-vidya, the Upanishads, Slokas, Sutras, Anuvyakhyanas, Vyakhyanas, what is sacrificed, what is poured out, what is (to be) eaten and drunk, this world and the other world, and all creatures*. The present Brahmana's verse 11 at `5.1164` gives a closely-paired catalogue applied to the smoke-from-fire simile — *Rig-veda, Yagur-veda, Sama-veda, Atharvangirasas, Itihasa, Purana, Vidya, the Upanishads, Slokas, Sutras, Anuvyakhyanas, Vyakhyanas, what is sacrificed, what is poured out, food, drink, this world and the other world, and all creatures*. The two catalogues hold the same form across two Brahmanas, with one substituted item (*food, drink* in the present Brahmana, *what is (to be) eaten and drunk* in the First Brahmana) and the same fourteen-item core list. The Adhyaya holds the same Vedic-textual catalogue twice, attached to two distinct teachings.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the Brahmana should be read as a recension rather than a fresh teaching. The Brahmana is not a continuation of the Janaka–Yajnavalkya dialogue of the prior four Brahmanas; it is a household-renunciation Brahmana that holds the same teaching the Brihadaranyaka had given at the close of its Second Adhyaya, here placed at the close of the Fourth Adhyaya's substantive Brahmanas. Reading the Brahmana with the prior recension held alongside — neither flattened into a single dialogue nor read as a wholly independent teaching — recovers the form the Upanishad has when it preserves a textual doublet across two of its Adhyayas. The Brahmana's distinctive elements are the two-wives introductory framing, the cattle-entry inserted into the Self-is-dear refrain, and the closing narrative line on Yajnavalkya's physical departure; the rest of the substance is held verbatim from the prior recension and should be read with attention to that fact.

Second, the Brahmana's literary form is the Self-is-dear refrain itself, and the refrain's twelve verbatim repetitions should be read at full length. The form *Verily, X is not dear, that you may love X; but that you may love the Self, therefore X is dear* is held verbatim across twelve substitutional iterations at `5.1146`–`5.1157`, with only X changing at each iteration. The Brahmana's procedure is the same kind of verbatim-repeated multi-step substitution that the Sixth Brahmana of the Third Adhyaya had used in the warp-and-woof ladder, the Seventh Brahmana of the Third Adhyaya in the antaryamin formula, and the First Brahmana of the Fourth Adhyaya in the recurring six-fold dialogue template. Reading the refrain at full length — neither smoothed away into *Yajnavalkya gave the Self-is-dear teaching across twelve substantives* nor compressed into doctrine — recovers the Brahmana's literary procedure: the doctrine of the Self as the locus of all love is given through the verbatim repetition of the same two-clause formula across twelve named loci, with cattle inserted as the fifth member in this recension.

The two consecutive Brahmanas of the Fourth Adhyaya hold the same four-clause negation in nearly identical wording at their substantive closings, with one held inside the public-Janaka teaching and the other inside the private-Maitreyi teaching.
- **structural-parallel** → Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 3, Part 5 — paired Brihadaranyaka loci of the wandering-mendicant teaching at the close of the householder-state. The Fifth Brahmana of the Third Adhyaya gives Yajnavalkya's substantive answer to the public challenger Kahola Kaushitakeya on the Brahmanas who *have risen above the desire for sons, wealth, and (new) worlds, they wander about as mendicants*, with the four-step Muni/Brahmana sequence and the closer *Everything else is of evil*. The Fifth Brahmana of the Fourth Adhyaya is the narrative event the doctrine had named in the public-contest setting: Yajnavalkya himself, having taught his wife the Self-is-dear refrain and the *neti neti* line, *went away (into the forest)*. The two Brahmanas hold the wandering-mendicant teaching and its narrative enactment across the Adhyaya boundary — the Third Adhyaya's public form-of-life teaching and the Fourth Adhyaya's private narrative of the priest leaving his house.
