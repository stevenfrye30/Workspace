# Mundaka Upanishad, Book 2, Part 2

Book 2, Part 2 of the Mundaka Upanishad — the Second Khanda of the Second Mundaka — turns the inquiry inward. Where the First Khanda gave the long unfolding of the Person who is the source of breath, the elements, the gods, and the worlds, the Second Khanda names the same Person as manifest, near, moving in the cave of the heart, and gives the disciplined approach by which the seeker may reach him. The first two verses set the recognition — the great Being in the heart is the indestructible Brahman, the breath, the speech, the mind, the true, the immortal. The third and fourth verses give the figure that has carried the teaching ever since: the Upanishad itself is the bow, devotion the arrow that sharpens upon the bow, the mind directed toward what is the drawing of the bow, the mark to be struck the indestructible. Then in compressed form: *Om* is the bow, the Self the arrow, Brahman the aim — the arrow becomes one with the target, and the seeker becomes one with Brahman. The verses that follow gather the centering imagery — the heart where the arteries meet like spokes fastened to the nave; the heavenly city of Brahman in the ether of the heart; the highest golden sheath in which the Brahman without passions and without parts is held — and reach the verse that the sun does not shine there, nor the moon and the stars, nor these lightnings, and much less this fire — when he shines, everything shines after him; by his light all this is lighted. The Khanda closes with the line that Brahman is before, behind, right and left, below and above; Brahman alone is all this.

## Context

The Mundaka Upanishad is one of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads — a small group of texts traditionally received as the philosophical core of the late Vedic literature. It is associated with the Atharvaveda branch of the Vedic corpus and is composed in Sanskrit verse. Modern scholarship places its composition somewhat later than the Katha, in roughly the fourth to second century BCE; the principal Upanishads as a group are dated to the centuries on either side of the Buddha. The text is anonymous; the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship.

Book 2, Part 2 is the fourth of the six parts of the Mundaka and continues directly from Book 2, Part 1 without break. The First Khanda of the Second Mundaka set out the cosmic Person from whom the elements and the worlds emerge and closed by naming the same Person as hidden in the cave of the heart; the Second Khanda begins where the closing verse of the First left off. The literary movement is from emanation to approach: the verses no longer say what comes from the source but how the seeker reaches it. The verse on the sun and the moon that do not shine there (Mundaka 2.2.10) is one of the most-quoted verses of the Upanishadic corpus — close in language to the same line in the Katha Upanishad's Fifth Valli (Katha 2.2.15) and in the Bhagavad Gita's Chapter 15 (Gita 15.6), each text holding the same verse in its own setting.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the Khanda is built on movement inward. The opening verses place the great Being in the cave of the heart; the bow-and-arrow figure draws the seeker's attention to a single mark; the closing verses gather centering imagery — spokes around the nave, the heavenly city in the ether of the heart, the golden sheath in which the Brahman is held. Each figure narrows the field of view further than the last, and the cumulative effect is the closing line that Brahman is before, behind, right and left, below and above — recognition that the inward narrowing has not lost the outward extent but found its source. Reading the Khanda with attention to the progressive narrowing — and to the moment at which the narrowing widens again into "Brahman alone is all this" — recovers the form the teaching has.

Second, the Khanda gives instruction in figures rather than in steps. The bow, the arrow, the target, the spokes and the nave, the city of Brahman, the golden sheath — these are not stages of a method but the same recognition shown from different angles. The figure of the Upanishad as the bow and *Om* as the bow holds the same place in the teaching as the figure of the spokes and the nave: each gives the disciplined approach by which the seeker is brought into relation with what is sought. Reading the figures alongside one another, rather than collating them into a single doctrine, keeps the form the Mundaka gives to inward instruction.

## The text

The text is held in the Archive library under `upanishads-muller-part2`, chapter 5 (Müller translation, 1884 — *The Sacred Books of the East*, Volume XV; via SacredTexts.com). Library chapter 5 is shared across the Katha entries (Book 1, Part 3 and Book 2, Parts 1–3) and the Mundaka entries already in the Archive (Book 1, Parts 1 and 2 at passages `5.77` through `5.105`, and Book 2, Part 1 at passages `5.106` through `5.118`); Book 2, Part 2 of the Mundaka — the entity here — occupies passages `5.119` through `5.131`. Passage `5.119` is the "Second Khanda" header. Passages `5.120` through `5.131` carry the eleven numbered verses of the Khanda, with verse 7 split across passages `5.126` and `5.127` where Müller's editorial paragraphing interrupts the verse mid-line. Passage `5.130` carries verse 10 with a small transcription artifact in the source: the verse opens "The sun does not thine there," where "thine" is a typographical error for "shine" carried over from the digitization of Müller's volume; the surrounding line is otherwise correct. Passage `5.132` is the "Third Mundaka" header that opens Book 3, Part 1 of the Mundaka and is not part of this entity. The library chapter continues past this entity into the rest of the Mundaka and the remaining Upanishads of the volume. Eleven verses; about ten minutes' reading.

## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 15 (Purushottama Yoga) — paired Sanskrit passages that hold the same verse in close form; the Gita's Chapter 15 verse on the sun, the moon, and fire that do not illumine that supreme abode (Gita 15.6) is the close textual neighbour of Mundaka 2.2.10, both lines holding that the source is not among the things lit and that by his light everything else is lit — the same verse carried across the late-Vedic and the epic settings, with the Gita's wider chapter on the supreme Person developing the recognition the Mundaka's Second Khanda gathers in its centering figures.
- **structural-parallel** → Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 6 (Dhyana Yoga) — paired passages on disciplined inward attention; the Gita's Chapter 6 teaches the practice of meditation as the steady drawing of the senses and the mind toward a single object, the Mundaka's Second Khanda holds the same recognition in the figure of the bow and the arrow — *Om* the bow, the Self the arrow, Brahman the aim — and in the centering of the heart where the arteries meet like spokes fastened to the nave, both texts giving the inward approach in figures rather than in a system of steps.
- **structural-parallel** → Katha Upanishad, Book 2, Part 3 — paired Upanishadic closings on the breaking of the heart's bonds; the Katha's Sixth Valli reaches the line that when all the ties of the heart are severed here on earth, then the mortal becomes immortal (Katha 2.3.15), the Mundaka's Second Khanda holds the same recognition in the verse that the fetter of the heart is broken, all doubts are solved, all his works perish when He has been beheld who is high and low (Mundaka 2.2.8) — paired moments in the same Upanishadic family, the figure of the bound and unbound heart given in two close vocabularies.
