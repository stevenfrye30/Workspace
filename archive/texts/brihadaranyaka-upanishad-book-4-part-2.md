# Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 4, Part 2

Book 4, Part 2 of the Brihadaranyaka Upanishad — the Second Brahmana of the Fourth Adhyaya — is a brief and concentrated teaching that closes the unfinished instruction of the First Brahmana. The First Brahmana had run six iterations of the same dialogue template across speech, life, sight, hearing, mind, and the heart, with Yajnavalkya correcting each prior teacher's identification and refusing each iteration's reward-offer with the line *one should not accept a reward without having fully instructed a pupil*. The Second Brahmana opens directly with the gesture that names what the First had withheld: *Ganaka Vaideha, descending from his throne, said: 'I bow to you, O Yagnavalkya, teach me.'* The king has left his seat. The contest's prize-and-substance economy of the First Brahmana — the cattle and the subtle questions named together at `5.938` — is here stripped to substance alone, with the king physically below the priest and the request given without offer of reward.

Yajnavalkya's reply is the journey-figure. *Your Majesty, as a man who wishes to make a long journey, would furnish himself with a chariot or a ship, thus is your mind well furnished by these Upanishads. You are honourable, and wealthy, you have learnt the Vedas and been told the Upanishads. Whither then will you go when departing hence?* The simile names the prior instruction — the Vedas, the Upanishads, the teaching the First Brahmana had given through six iterations — as the chariot or the ship for the journey out of the world. The question that follows is the death-question of the Upanishadic literature: *whither then will you go when departing hence?* Ganaka's answer is short: *Sir, I do not know whither I shall go.* The form of the dialogue is now teacher-and-pupil; the king who has been instructed by six prior teachers and now by Yajnavalkya has reached the question that the prior instruction has not answered, and the Brahmana's substance is given as the answer to that question.

Verse 2 names the person in the right eye. *That person who is in the right eye, he is called Indha, and him who is Indha they call indeed Indra mysteriously, for the gods love what is mysterious, and dislike what is evident.* The naming is double — Indha first, Indra second — and the gloss-line on the gods' preference for the mysterious over the evident is given as the reason for the doubling. The line is one of the Upanishadic literature's most-cited statements on the form of divine naming, and it sits inside the Brahmana as the prelude to the further interior anatomy of the Self.

Verse 3 names the wife, Virag, in the left eye, and gives the heart's anatomy as the meeting-place of Indha and Virag. The anatomy is given in five named parts. The **meeting-place** is the ether within the heart. The **food** is the red lump within the heart. The **covering** is that which is like net-work within the heart. The **road** on which they move from sleep to waking is the artery that rises upwards from the heart. The **veins** of the heart — Müller's translation gives the Sanskrit name *Hita* — are *like a hair divided into a thousand parts, placed firmly within the heart*. Through these veins, food flows on flowing, and the substantive line at the close of the verse — *he (the Taigasa) receives as it were purer food than the corporeal Self (the Vaisvanara)* — names the two states of the Self that Vedanta will work out at length: the Taigasa (the dream-state self) and the Vaisvanara (the waking-state, corporeal self), with the Hita-veins as the conduit between the two.

Verse 4 turns to the six quarters, but applies the directional language now to the pranas, the breaths. *His Eastern quarter are the pranas (breath) which go to the East. His Southern quarter are the pranas which go to the South. His Western quarter are the pranas which go to the West. His Northern quarter are the pranas which go to the North. His Upper (Zenith) quarter are the pranas which go upward. His Lower (Nadir) quarter are the pranas which go downward.* Each of the six quarters is given on its own short passage; the gathering line follows: *All the quarters are all the pranas.* The substantive answer then closes with the *neti neti* form. *And he (the Atman in that state) can only be described by No, no! He is incomprehensible, for he cannot be comprehended; he is undecaying, for he cannot decay; he is not attached, for he does not attach himself; he is unbound, he does not suffer, he does not perish.* Yajnavalkya's seal closes the substantive answer in the same passage: *O Ganaka, you have indeed reached fearlessness — thus said Yagnavalkya.* The teaching of the journey-question has reached the term *fearlessness* (abhaya).

The Brahmana closes with Ganaka's reply on a single short passage. *Then Ganaka said: 'May that fearlessness come to you also who teachest us fearlessness. I bow to you. Here are the Videhas, and here am I (thy slave).'* The closing is the Adhyaya's first instance of the named challenger offering substantive recompense in non-cattle terms. The kingdom of the Videhas is offered to Yajnavalkya, and the king offers himself as slave. The First Brahmana's six refused offers of *a thousand cows with a bull as big as an elephant* are paired here with one acceptance-offer that no longer counts in cattle. The Brahmana's closing form is wholly new in the contest's literature: not silence, not private withdrawal, not death, not declined-reward, but the kingdom-and-self offered.

## Context

The Brihadaranyaka Upanishad is one of the two earliest of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads, sharing that position with the Chandogya. It belongs to the Shukla Yajurveda branch of the Vedic corpus and forms the closing portion of the Shatapatha Brahmana — the *Aranyaka* in its name marks it as a forest-text recited at the late stages of Vedic study. Modern scholarship places the Brihadaranyaka in roughly the eighth to sixth century BCE, prior to the Buddha; the text is anonymous, and the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship. The text is the longest of the principal Upanishads and is typically structured in six *adhyayas* (chapters), each containing several *brahmanas* (sections).

Book 4, Part 2 holds three of the most distinctive features of the early Janaka–Yajnavalkya teaching in concentrated form. The first is the right-eye / left-eye anatomy. The doctrine of the person in the right eye and his wife in the left eye, with the heart's interior anatomy as their meeting-place, is one of the Brihadaranyaka's signature teachings, and it appears in two main loci of the Upanishad. The First Brahmana of the Second Adhyaya — the Gargya Balaki / Ajatasatru dialogue — gave the person in the right eye in the framework of the three-states analysis, with the Hitā arteries running through the body during deep sleep and the figure of the spider-and-thread as the closing image. The Second Brahmana of the Fourth Adhyaya gives the same person in the right eye now named Indha, with the wife Virag in the left eye, and the Hita veins given the same image — *like a hair divided into a thousand parts, placed firmly within the heart*. The doctrine carries across the Brihadaranyaka and is given two named figures in the Fourth Adhyaya that the Second Adhyaya had held without naming.

The second is the journey-question and the close on *fearlessness*. The Adhyaya's First Brahmana had identified six prior teachers' Brahman-identifications and corrected each with the body, place, and worship-as-X formula, with the closing of each iteration in *having become a god, he goes to the gods*. The Second Brahmana takes up the further question — *whither will you go when departing hence?* — and gives the answer not as a cosmographic destination but as a state of the knower: *fearlessness*. The term *abhaya* — fearlessness — is one of the principal terms of the Upanishadic doctrine of liberation, and the Brahmana's closing line *O Ganaka, you have indeed reached fearlessness* gives it as the form of the king's standing after the teaching has been received. The Adhyaya's first two Brahmanas hold the form of the journey-and-arrival doctrine across the boundary between them: the First gives the chariot (the prior instruction); the Second gives the journey's destination.

The third is the *neti neti* line. The Brihadaranyaka holds the form *No, no!* in three principal loci: the closing prose of the Third Brahmana of the Second Adhyaya — *the teaching of Brahman by "No, no!" — for there is nothing else higher than this* — the close of the breath-cascade in verse 26 of the Ninth Brahmana of the Third Adhyaya, and the close of the substantive answer here at verse 4 of the Second Brahmana of the Fourth Adhyaya. The form at the present locus is closely paired with the form at `5.921`. *He is incomprehensible, for he cannot be comprehended; he is undecaying, for he cannot decay; he is not attached, for he does not attach himself; he is unbound, he does not suffer, he does not perish.* The wording is not identical — *imperishable / undecaying*, *unfettered / unbound*, *fail / perish* — and Müller's translation preserves the variants as the source has them. Reading the two loci alongside each other recovers the form the Upanishad has when it gives the same doctrine through closely-paired but not-identical wordings at separate Brahmanas.

The closing form is the Adhyaya's first new closing-form. The Third Adhyaya had closed seven Brahmanas with *held his peace*, one with private withdrawal, and one with death. The First Brahmana of the Fourth Adhyaya closed with declined-reward and the instruction explicitly held as incomplete. The Second Brahmana closes with the king offering kingdom and self. The kingdom-and-self offering will be repeated and named in the Fourth Adhyaya's later Brahmanas; the Second Brahmana is its first appearance, and it sits as the closing form of the Janaka–Yajnavalkya dialogue at the moment the king has been told *whither he will go when departing hence*.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the Brahmana's substance is held inside an interior anatomy. The doctrine of the Atman is given through the named persons in the two eyes, the heart's named parts (meeting-place, food, covering, road, veins), and the directional pranas. The substance is not given through the substitutional ladder of Gargi's warp-and-woof, the antaryamin formula of Uddalaka, or the eight-purusha cycle of Sakalya; it is given through a single sustained anatomical figure that walks the body's interior from the eyes to the heart to the breaths to the six quarters. Reading the Brahmana with the anatomical figure held at full length — neither flattened into doctrine nor expanded into an external cosmology — recovers the form the Adhyaya has when its substance is held inside the body's interior.

Second, the Brahmana's authority dynamic is reversed from the contest's procedure. The Third Adhyaya had given Yajnavalkya the priest answering challengers' questions in public and silencing them one by one; the Fourth Adhyaya's First Brahmana had given Yajnavalkya the priest correcting the king's prior teachers in private, with the king as the asker and the cattle as offered reward. The Second Brahmana gives the king physically below the priest, asking for instruction without offering reward, and closing the dialogue by offering kingdom and self. Reading the Brahmana with the authority dynamic held at full reversal — the king who in the First Brahmana had named Yajnavalkya's two motives (cattle and subtle questions) now lays his throne, his kingdom, and himself down — recovers the Adhyaya's literary procedure: the contest's exchange-economy is held openly in the First Brahmana and dissolved in the Second.

## The text

The text is held in the Archive library under `upanishads-muller-part2`, chapter 5 (Müller translation, 1884 — *The Sacred Books of the East*, Volume XV; via SacredTexts.com). Book 4, Part 2 of the Brihadaranyaka — the entity here — occupies passages `5.1008` through `5.1025`. Passage `5.1008` is the "Second Brahmana" header without footnote. Passages `5.1009` through `5.1025` carry the body in Müller's prose presentation across seventeen short passages, with verse-numbered markers at `1.` (`5.1009`), `2.` (`5.1015`), `3.` (`5.1016`), and `4.` (`5.1018`).

The Brahmana contains several boundary features that should be preserved honestly:

- **Verse-1 split mid-clause with footnote on Upanishads.** Verse 1's chariot-or-ship simile is split across passages `5.1010` and `5.1011` mid-clause at *thus is your mind well / furnished by these Upanishads*. Passage `5.1011` carries footnote `[*1]` on *Upanishads*, glossing the term as it sits inside the simile.
- **Verse-2 footnote cluster on the right-eye doctrine.** Passage `5.1015` carries verse 2 — the naming of Indha and Indra — with footnote `[*2]` on *right eye* and footnote `[*3]` on *indeed*. Müller's notes attach at the substantive identification and at the rhetorical particle of the doubling.
- **Verse-3 four-footnote cluster on the heart's anatomy.** Passage `5.1016` carries verse 3 — the wife Virag and the heart's anatomy — with four successive footnotes: `[*4]` on *Virag*, `[*5]` on *meeting-place*, `[*6]` on *covering*, and `[*7]` on *Hita*. Müller's four notes attach at the four named parts of the anatomy and are the principal apparatus on the verse.
- **Verse-3 split mid-clause at the Hita-veins line.** Verse 3 is split across passages `5.1016` and `5.1017` mid-clause at *which are called Hita, placed / firmly within the heart*. The split falls inside the Hita-veins clause at the open of the *placed firmly within the heart* segment.
- **Verse-3 footnote on the Taigasa-Vaisvanara distinction.** Passage `5.1017` carries the substantive line on the Taigasa receiving *purer food than the corporeal Self (the Vaisvanara)* with footnote `[*1]` on *purer food*. Müller's note glosses the comparative-substantive of the food-flow.
- **Verse-4 directional ladder across six short passages.** Verse 4 is given across seven short passages from `5.1018` to `5.1024`. Each of the six quarters — Eastern, Southern, Western, Northern, Upper (Zenith), Lower (Nadir) — sits on its own short passage with the same syntactic shape (*His X quarter are the pranas which go to/Y*), with no internal verse-marker on the four directional passages of `5.1019`–`5.1023`. The gathering line *All the quarters are all the pranas* opens passage `5.1024`.
- **Verse-4 *neti neti* close with footnote.** Passage `5.1024` carries the substantive close of verse 4 with the *neti neti* line and footnote `[*2]` on *No, no!*. The line *And he (the Atman in that state) can only be described by No, no! He is incomprehensible... he does not perish* sits on the same passage as the gathering line on the quarters and pranas, and as Yajnavalkya's seal *O Ganaka, you have indeed reached fearlessness — thus said Yagnavalkya*. The closing seal is given inside the same passage as the *neti neti* line and the gathering line; the Brahmana's substantive answer is held compressed on a single passage.
- **Closing line at `5.1025`.** Passage `5.1025` carries Ganaka's closing reply on its own short passage. *Then Ganaka said: 'May that fearlessness come to you also who teachest us fearlessness. I bow to you. Here are the Videhas, and here am I (thy slave).'* The line is not given a verse-marker; the closing of the dialogue is held outside the verse-numbered body. No *held his peace* narrator's line follows; the closing is the king's substantive offer of kingdom and self, and the Brahmana ends inside the offered surrender.

Passage `5.1026` is the "Third Brahmana" header that opens Book 4, Part 3 of the Brihadaranyaka and is not part of this entity. The library chapter continues past this entity into the rest of the Fourth Adhyaya and the remaining portions of the volume; the Brihadaranyaka extends well beyond the boundaries of library chapter 5.
