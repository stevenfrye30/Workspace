# Epictetus, Enchiridion, Chapters 1–5

Chapters 1 through 5 of the Enchiridion are the opening of Epictetus' short Stoic manual of conduct. In five compressed chapters — each a paragraph or two — the manual sets the distinction on which the rest of the book turns: between what is within one's power (judgment, intention, desire, aversion) and what is not (body, property, reputation, office). The four chapters that follow extend the distinction into practice: a discipline of desire and aversion (chapter 2), a discipline of attachment to things and persons (chapter 3), the rehearsal of an action's likely course in advance (chapter 4, the bath example), and the principle that the disturbance which follows events comes from the judgments one makes of them, not from the events themselves (chapter 5). The chapters are short and aphoristic, addressed in the second person.

## Context

Epictetus (c. 50 – c. 135 CE) was a Roman Stoic philosopher, born a slave in Phrygia in Asia Minor, freed in his early adulthood, and active as a teacher first in Rome and later in Nicopolis on the western coast of Greece, after the emperor Domitian banished philosophers from Italy in 93 CE. He wrote nothing himself. His teaching survives through the work of his student Arrian — also the historian of Alexander the Great — who preserved a transcript of Epictetus' classes in eight books of *Discourses*, of which four survive, and compiled from them a much shorter abridgement called the *Encheiridion*, the "handbook" or "manual," from a Greek word meaning "in the hand." The Enchiridion was assembled around 125 CE.

The Enchiridion is composed of fifty-one short numbered chapters, traditionally arranged in roughly the order of Stoic ethical formation: discipline of desire, discipline of action, discipline of assent. The opening five chapters set the foundation. Chapter 1 names the central distinction between what is and is not within one's power; chapters 2 through 5 take up that distinction in particular domains — the management of desire and aversion, the careful valuing of attached objects, the rehearsal of any action begun, and the principle of disturbance through judgment rather than event. The chapters that follow develop these and related practices through the rest of the manual.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the chapters are short and instructional; they do not develop arguments. Each chapter names a distinction or a practice and gives a short illustration. Chapter 4 — the bath example — is characteristic: when you are about to bathe, picture in advance that there will be people who splash, push, abuse, and steal, and prepare your judgment accordingly. Reading the opening five chapters as a sequence of short prescriptions — what to attend to, what to refuse, what to rehearse, what to remember — rather than as compressed argument, follows the form.

Second, the same distinction returns through each chapter. Chapter 1 sets it; chapters 2 through 5 work it across different domains. Reading the chapters with the distinction held visible in each, and noticing where the line falls in each new context, recovers the manual's recursive form.

## The text

The text is held in the Archive library under `enchiridion` (Thomas W. Higginson translation, 1865 — the library's default and so far its only loaded translation), library chapter 1. The library uses Higginson's chapter scheme, which groups the Enchiridion's fifty-one standard numbered chapters into five larger thematic chapters; the library's chapter 1 contains standard chapters 1 through 11. The opening five chapters in the standard numbering — the focus of this entity, comprising the manual's foundational distinction and its first practical extensions — fall within the first half of the library's chapter 1 (passages `1.1`–`1.8` in the library's verse-level subdivision). The five chapters together run to about a page and a half of compressed prose; about ten minutes' reading.
