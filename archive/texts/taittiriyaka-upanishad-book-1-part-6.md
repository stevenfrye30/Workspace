# Taittiriyaka Upanishad, Book 1, Part 6

Book 1, Part 6 of the Taittiriyaka Upanishad — the Sixth Anuvaka of the Sikshavalli — places the *vyāhṛti* lesson of the Fifth Anuvaka inside the body of the speaker. The opening line names the ether within the heart and the Person who dwells there — *consisting of mind, immortal, golden* — and the second part of the verse turns to the body that holds the recitation: between the two palates the uvula hangs like a nipple; that is the *indrayoni*, the starting-point of Indra; and where the root of the hair divides, at the crown of the head, the seer opens the two sides of the head. From that opening the four sacred interjections of the previous Anuvaka are now traced as a path: saying *Bhū*, the speaker enters Agni; saying *Bhuvaḥ*, he enters Vayu; saying *Suvaḥ*, he enters Aditya; saying *Mahas*, he enters Brahman. There he obtains lordship — *lord of the mind, lord of speech, lord of sight, lord of hearing, lord of knowledge* — and the verse names what follows: there is the Brahman whose body is ether, whose nature is the true, whose joy is breath, whose mind is bliss, who is full of peace, who is immortal. The Anuvaka closes with the personal address that anchors the lesson to a named hearer: *iti prācīnayogyopāssva* — "worship thus, O Prakinayogya."

## Context

The Taittiriyaka Upanishad is one of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads — a small group of texts traditionally received as the philosophical core of the late Vedic literature. It belongs to the Krishna Yajurveda branch of the Vedic corpus and to the Taittiriya school of that branch; the Upanishad sits inside the Taittiriya Aranyaka. Modern scholarship places it among the older prose Upanishads, in roughly the sixth to fifth century BCE — earlier than the verse Upanishads of the Katha and Mundaka. The text is anonymous; the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship.

Book 1, Part 6 sits inside the Sikshavalli, the chapter on *śikṣā*. The Fifth Anuvaka set out the *vyāhṛti* — *Bhū*, *Bhuvaḥ*, *Suvaḥ*, *Mahas* — across four parallel fields and closed on the recognition that the one who knows them knows Brahman; the Sixth Anuvaka turns the same four interjections into a contemplative path located in the body of the speaker. The literary movement is from outward correspondence to inward placement: the same lesson is given again, in a different register. The closing personal address to *Prakinayogya* (Müller's spelling for Prācīnayogya, "the descendant of Prācīnayoga") is the only named hearer in the Sikshavalli and is the school's marker that the contemplative version of the lesson is given to a specific student inside a specific lineage.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the Anuvaka returns the structural lesson of the previous Anuvaka without changing its content. The four interjections are the same four; the order is the same order; what differs is that they are now placed along a path through the body of the reciter rather than in fields outside it. Reading the Anuvaka with the Fifth Anuvaka held alongside, recognising each of the same four terms in the new placement, recovers the form the Sikshavalli has at this point: the same lesson given outward and inward, both held under the same school-frame.

Second, the contemplative imagery does not introduce a new doctrine but locates the recitation. The ether within the heart, the golden Person, the uvula between the palates, the parting of the root of the hair — these are not given as objects of speculation; they are placed in the order in which the speaker's recitation passes through them. The Anuvaka teaches the *vyāhṛti* as something the speaker says with the body as well as the voice. Reading the imagery as the placement of the recitation rather than as a separable contemplative practice keeps the form the Sixth Anuvaka has inside the Sikshavalli's wider procedure.

## The text

The text is held in the Archive library under `upanishads-muller-part2`, chapter 5 (Müller translation, 1884 — *The Sacred Books of the East*, Volume XV; via SacredTexts.com). Library chapter 5 is shared across fifteen Archive entities — four parts of the Katha Upanishad, the six parts of the Mundaka Upanishad, and Books 1, Parts 1 through 5 of the Taittiriyaka — and continues here into the Sixth Anuvaka without break in the chapter. Book 1, Part 6 of the Taittiriyaka — the entity here — occupies passages `5.197` through `5.201`. Passage `5.197` is the "Sixth Anuvaka" header. Passages `5.198` through `5.201` carry the body of the Anuvaka in Müller's prose presentation, with the verse-numbered markers visible at `1.` (start of `5.198`) and `2.` (start of `5.200`); verse 1 is split across passages `5.198` and `5.199` where Müller's editorial paragraphing interrupts the verse mid-line. Passage `5.201` opens with the editorial sub-marker `(1)` carried over from the source — Müller's bracketed grouping that signals the closing personal address to Prakinayogya as a continuation of verse 2 rather than as a separate verse. Passage `5.202` is the "Seventh Anuvaka" header that opens Book 1, Part 7 of the Taittiriyaka and is not part of this entity. The library chapter continues past this entity into the rest of the Taittiriyaka and the remaining Upanishads of the volume. About three minutes' reading.

## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Taittiriyaka Upanishad, Book 1, Part 5 — paired immediately consecutive Anuvakas of the Sikshavalli that hold the same lesson in two registers; the Fifth Anuvaka maps the four sacred interjections — *Bhū*, *Bhuvaḥ*, *Suvaḥ*, *Mahas* — across four outward fields (worlds, heavenly elements, Vedas, breathings), the Sixth Anuvaka traces the same four interjections along an inward path through the body of the reciter (entry to Agni, Vayu, Aditya, Brahman) — the same recitation given outward and inward in two adjacent units.
- **structural-parallel** → Mundaka Upanishad, Book 2, Part 2 — paired Upanishadic passages on the heart's ether and the golden Person; the Mundaka's Second Khanda holds the recognition that the Self is placed in the ether, in the heavenly city of Brahman in the heart, and that in the highest golden sheath there is the Brahman without passions and without parts (Mundaka 2.2.7–9), the Taittiriyaka's Sixth Anuvaka opens with the closely related line that there is the ether within the heart, and in it the Person consisting of mind, immortal, golden — paired moments in the late-Vedic literature where the inward placement of the recognition is given in the same vocabulary.
- **structural-parallel** → Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 4 (Jnana Karma Sanyasa Yoga) — paired Sanskrit passages on teaching framed as a transmissible discipline given to a named student; the Gita's Chapter 4 names Arjuna as the disciple to whom the imperishable yoga is given again, and develops the disciplines by which the teaching is held, the Taittiriyaka's Sixth Anuvaka closes with the personal address to Prakinayogya — the only named hearer of the Sikshavalli — placing the inward version of the lesson inside a specific lineage of recitation. Both texts mark the moment at which a structured teaching is handed to a specific recipient.
