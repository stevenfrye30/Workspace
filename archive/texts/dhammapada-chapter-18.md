# Dhammapada, Chapter 18 (Impurity)

Chapter 18 of the Dhammapada — the *Mala-vagga*, "Impurity" — is a chapter of twenty-one verses on impurity (*mala*, the taint or stain) and the work of removing it. The opening four verses hold two paired couplets that share a refrain. *Thou art now like a sear leaf, the messengers of death (Yama) have come near to thee; thou standest at the door of thy departure, and thou hast no provision for thy journey* (235). *Make thyself an island, work hard, be wise! When thy impurities are blown away, and thou art free from guilt, thou wilt enter into the heavenly world of the elect (Ariya)* (236). The next two verses repeat the form: life come to an end, no resting-place, no provision (237); make thyself an island, blow away impurities, *thou wilt not enter again into birth and decay* (238). Verses 239 and 240 hold two metallurgical similes: *let a wise man blow off the impurities of his self, as a smith blows off the impurities of silver one by one, little by little, and from time to time* (239); *as the impurity which springs from the iron, when it springs from it, destroys it; thus do a transgressor's own works lead him to the evil path* (240). Verses 241–243 hold a catalogue of taints. *The taint of prayers is non-repetition; the taint of houses, non-repair; the taint of the body is sloth; the taint of a watchman, thoughtlessness* (241). *Bad conduct is the taint of woman, greediness the taint of a benefactor; tainted are all evil ways in this world and in the next* (242). The catalogue closes on a single declaration: *but there is a taint worse than all taints,--ignorance is the greatest taint. O mendicants! throw off that taint, and become taintless!* (243). Verses 244–245 hold the paired contrast on what is easy and what is hard. *Life is easy to live for a man who is without shame, a crow hero, a mischief-maker, an insulting, bold, and wretched fellow* (244). *But life is hard to live for a modest man, who always looks for what is pure, who is disinterested, quiet, spotless, and intelligent* (245). Verses 246–248 hold a catalogue of evil acts and a warning. Verses 249–250 hold a paired contrast between fretting about others' food and finding rest. Verse 251 holds the chapter's negative-superlative tetrad: *there is no fire like passion, there is no shark like hatred, there is no snare like folly, there is no torrent like greed.* Verses 252–253 hold the figure of looking at one's own faults: the fault of others is easily perceived, but that of oneself is difficult, and a man winnows his neighbour's faults like chaff but hides his own. The closing pair (254–255) shares a refrain: *there is no path through the air, a man is not a Samana by outward acts.* The first verse closes with *the world delights in vanity, the Tathagatas (the Buddhas) are free from vanity*; the second with *no creatures are eternal; but the awakened (Buddha) are never shaken*.

## Context

The Dhammapada is one of the books of the Khuddaka Nikaya, the fifth and shortest section of the Sutta Pitaka in the Pali Canon. It collects 423 verses arranged in twenty-six chapters by theme — pairs, heedfulness, mind, flowers, the sage, evil, and so on. The verses are attributed to the Buddha and were transmitted orally for several centuries before being committed to writing in Sri Lanka in the first century BCE. The collection took shape in roughly the third century BCE, in connection with the Theravada transmission of the canon.

Chapter 18 is the *Mala-vagga*, "Impurity." Its twenty-one verses are numbered 235 through 255 in the standard continuous numbering of the Dhammapada — and in F. Max Müller's 1881 translation, the version held in the Archive library, the count and numbering match the standard exactly, with no merged or missing verse of the kind that occurs at the close, middle, or hinge of Chapters 4, 6, 8, 11, 14, and 17. This is one of the chapter's structural features in Müller's text: it preserves the standard count cleanly through twenty-one passages. The Pali word *mala* — taint, stain, impurity — is rendered by Müller variously as "impurity," "taint," "guilt," and "fault" through the chapter; the central catalogue at verses 241–243 keeps the word "taint" for each item. Each verse is short — two or four lines in the Pali — and each completes one statement of the figure.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the chapter is built across several patterned groups. The opening four verses (235–238) hold two paired couplets that repeat a single warning-and-counsel form: a warning verse on the nearness of death and the absence of provision, followed by a counsel verse on making oneself an island, working hard, blowing away impurities, and entering either the heavenly world (236) or freedom from re-birth (238). The two metallurgical similes (239–240) hold the figure of impurity as a thing the worker can remove from silver and a thing that destroys iron from within. The catalogue of taints (241–243) holds eight specific taints culminating in ignorance as the greatest. The closing paired refrain (254–255) holds *there is no path through the air, a man is not a Samana by outward acts* twice, with two different second halves on the Tathagatas and on the awakened. Reading the chapter as a chain of patterned groups, rather than as twenty-one separate aphorisms, recovers the form the chapter is built on.

Second, verse 251 is the chapter's negative-superlative tetrad — *no fire like passion, no shark like hatred, no snare like folly, no torrent like greed* — and is the structural twin of Chapter 15 verse 202, which uses the same form (*no fire like passion, no losing throw like hatred, no pain like this body, no happiness higher than rest*). The Dhammapada uses the *no X like Y* form in several chapters; Chapter 18's tetrad is the form's most extended use in this stretch of the collection. Reading verse 251 as a deliberate structural echo of the earlier tetrad, rather than as a fresh aphorism, keeps the form the verses share.

## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Dhammapada, Chapter 15 (Happiness) — the earlier chapter with the same negative-superlative tetrad form; Chapter 15 verse 202 holds *no fire like passion, no losing throw like hatred, no pain like this body, no happiness higher than rest*, Chapter 18 verse 251 holds *no fire like passion, no shark like hatred, no snare like folly, no torrent like greed*. The form is reused with a fresh content set, sixteen chapters apart but still recognisable.
- **structural-parallel** → Dhammapada, Chapter 11 (Old Age) — the earlier chapter that already worked the figure of the body as a heap of corruption and the warning of approaching death; Chapter 11 verse 148 holds the body as wasted, frail, and ending in death, Chapter 18 verses 235 and 237 hold the figure of the sear leaf and the messengers of death (Yama) come near. Both chapters use the figure of approaching mortality as the ground of urgent counsel.
- **structural-parallel** → Dhammapada, Chapter 17 (Anger) — the immediate predecessor on the shelf, with the same kind of catalogue-and-summary structure; Chapter 17 with thirteen verses on anger and its inversion, Chapter 18 with twenty-one verses on impurity and its purification. The two chapters share the figure of the work that has to be done — Chapter 17 by inversion (anger by love, evil by good), Chapter 18 by removal (impurities blown away as a smith blows off silver).
- **structural-parallel** → Matthew 5 — the Christian shelf-pair on the figure of looking at one's own faults rather than the faults of others; the Sermon on the Mount with its *speck in thy brother's eye / beam in thine own*, Dhammapada 18 verse 252 with *the fault of others is easily perceived, but that of oneself is difficult to perceive; a man winnows his neighbour's faults like chaff, but his own fault he hides, as a cheat hides the bad die from the gambler*. Both texts hold the same recognition — that one sees others' faults more easily than one's own, and that the work is to reverse the asymmetry.
