# Dhammapada, Chapter 21 (Miscellaneous)

Chapter 21 of the Dhammapada — the *Pakiṇṇaka-vagga*, "Miscellaneous" — is a chapter of sixteen verses given the title that says they are not held together by a single theme. The chapter does not, however, hold sixteen unrelated aphorisms; it holds several small patterned groups, set side by side. The opening pair turns on the trade between pleasures: *if by leaving a small pleasure one sees a great pleasure, let a wise man leave the small pleasure, and look to the great* (290); *he who, by causing pain to others, wishes to obtain pleasure for himself, he, entangled in the bonds of hatred, will never be free from hatred* (291). Verses 292 and 293 hold a paired contrast on action: what ought to be done is neglected and what ought not to be done is done by the unruly (292), but those whose watchfulness is on their body and who steadfastly do what ought to be done have their desires come to an end (293). Verses 294 and 295 hold the chapter's most arresting paired figure — *a true Brahmana goes scatheless, though he have killed father and mother, and two valiant kings, though he has destroyed a kingdom with all its subjects* (294); *a true Brahmana goes scatheless, though he have killed father and mother, and two holy kings, and an eminent man besides* (295). The chapter's central block is a six-fold parallel sequence (296–301), each verse opening with the same line and varying the object: *the disciples of Gotama (Buddha) are always well awake, and their thoughts day and night are always set on Buddha* (296); *on the law* (297); *on the church* (298); *on their body* (299); *on compassion* (300); *on meditation* (301). Verse 302 turns to the difficulty of every position — leaving the world, enjoying it, the monastery, the household, dwelling with equals, the itinerant life. Verse 303 holds the figure that the faithful and virtuous man is respected wherever he chooses to live. Verse 304 holds the simile that good people shine from afar like snowy mountains and bad people are unseen like arrows shot by night. Verse 305 closes the chapter on solitude: *he alone who, without ceasing, practises the duty of sitting alone and sleeping alone, he, subduing himself, will rejoice in the destruction of all desires alone, as if living in a forest.*

## Context

The Dhammapada is one of the books of the Khuddaka Nikaya, the fifth and shortest section of the Sutta Pitaka in the Pali Canon. It collects 423 verses arranged in twenty-six chapters by theme — pairs, heedfulness, mind, flowers, the sage, evil, and so on. The verses are attributed to the Buddha and were transmitted orally for several centuries before being committed to writing in Sri Lanka in the first century BCE. The collection took shape in roughly the third century BCE, in connection with the Theravada transmission of the canon.

Chapter 21 is the *Pakiṇṇaka-vagga*, "Miscellaneous." Its sixteen verses are numbered 290 through 305 in the standard continuous numbering of the Dhammapada — and in F. Max Müller's 1881 translation, the version held in the Archive library, the count and numbering match the standard exactly, with no merged or missing verse of the kind that occurs in Chapters 4, 6, 8, 11, 14, 17, and 19. The Pali word *pakiṇṇaka* — scattered, miscellaneous, mixed — names the chapter's principle of arrangement: it gathers verses that did not fit elsewhere. But "miscellaneous" is not the same as unrelated; the chapter still keeps small patterned groups side by side. Each verse is short — two or four lines in the Pali — and each completes one statement of the figure.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the chapter is genuinely several small groups rather than one sustained figure. The opening pair on pleasure and pain (290–291), the paired contrast on action (292–293), the paired *true Brahmana* figure (294–295), the six-fold Gotama-disciples sequence (296–301), the difficulty-of-every-position verse (302), the snowy-mountains and arrows-by-night simile (304), and the closing solitude verse (305) are not all about the same thing. Reading the chapter as several small groups, rather than as one extended figure, recovers the form the chapter has. The Dhammapada's compositional habit of putting groups beside each other — rather than fusing them into one — is on display here more than in most chapters.

Second, verses 294 and 295 hold a paired figure that has often been read out of context. The verses say that *a true Brahmana goes scatheless, though he have killed father and mother*, and the violent surface has unsettled readers since the chapter was first translated. The Dhammapada's commentarial tradition reads the verse figuratively — the "father" and "mother" stand for craving and ignorance, the "two kings" for the views of eternalism and annihilationism, the "kingdom with all its subjects" for the twelve sense bases and what arises from them. Reading verses 294 and 295 with the awareness that the violent surface is a deliberate paradox — not literal counsel — keeps the form the verses share. The pair is the chapter's most arresting figure, and its arrest is the figure's purpose.

## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Dhammapada, Chapter 20 (The Way) — the immediate predecessor on the shelf, with the same kind of doctrinal vocabulary clustered into a single chapter; Chapter 20 with its Eightfold Path declaration at verse 273 and its three-marks-of-existence parallel at 277–279, Chapter 21 with its six-fold *disciples-of-Gotama-always-well-awake* parallel at 296–301 turning the figure of attention to the Triple Refuge (Buddha, the law, the church), the body, compassion, and meditation. Chapter 20 holds the path; Chapter 21 holds what the disciples on the path are always set on.
- **structural-parallel** → Dhammapada, Chapter 14 (The Buddha) — the earlier chapter on the figure of the Awakened, with the central refuge-passage at verses 188–192 (the false refuges of mountains and forests against the true refuge of Buddha, Law, Church, Four Truths, Eightfold Way). Chapter 21 verses 296–298 return to the same Buddha-Law-Church Triple Refuge, but as the constant orientation of the disciples' minds rather than as the safe refuge they take. The figure is held twice with the centre of gravity differently placed.
- **structural-parallel** → Dhammapada, Chapter 15 (Happiness) — the earlier chapter with the same kind of opening-quartet patterned form; Chapter 15 verses 197–200 with the *let-us-live-happily-among-X* quartet, Chapter 21 verses 296–301 with the *disciples-of-Gotama-always-well-awake-on-X* sextet. The two chapters share the figure of repeating one form across substitutions, with Chapter 21 extending it to six.
- **structural-parallel** → Dhammapada, Chapter 19 (The Just) — the recent chapter on the *true* version of contested identities; Chapter 19 with verses on the true elder, the true Samana, the true Bhikshu, the true Muni, Chapter 21 verses 294–295 on the *true Brahmana* held through the violent paradox. The figure of the *true X* is held across the chapters with different content; Chapter 21's true Brahmana is the most provocative case.
- **structural-parallel** → Daodejing, Chapters 1–10 — the Chinese contemplative pair on the figure of paradoxical statement; the Daodejing's habit of stating that the way that can be walked is not the eternal way and that the sage acts without acting, Dhammapada 21 verses 294–295 with the figurative killing of father and mother as the figure of the true Brahmana. Both texts hold the figure that some teachings require the surface to arrest and the meaning to come second.
