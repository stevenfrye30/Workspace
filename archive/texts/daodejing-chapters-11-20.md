# Daodejing, Chapters 11–20

The second ten chapters of the Daodejing turn from the opening's statement of the Way to the practical orientation that follows from it. Chapters 11 through 20 take up the usefulness of what is empty (the hub at the wheel, the inside of a clay vessel, the doors and windows of a room), governance the people are barely aware of, and the return to simplicity. The closing chapter (20) sets the speaker apart from the busy world: while others have what to do, the speaker alone is ignorant and boorish, drifting like the wind.

## Context

Chapters 11 through 20 belong to the *Dao* section of the Daodejing — the first half of the eighty-one chapters that opens the standard text. The chapters in this stretch are short, gnomic, and thematically grouped rather than continuous: a small cluster on emptiness and use (chapters 11–13), a paragraph on the elusive Way (14), a portrait of the ancient sages (15), and a sequence on returning, simplicity, and the rejection of fame and learning (16–20). The order followed in the Archive's loaded text is Wang Bi's third-century CE recension, in which the *Dao* section opens the book; the older Mawangdui silk manuscripts (c. 200 BCE) place the *De* section first.

Chapter 17 introduces the political register that the rest of the Daodejing will return to several times: the ruler whose presence the people barely register, set against the ruler who is feared, and the ruler who is despised. Chapter 19 gathers several recurring themes, rejecting the cultivated virtues of sageliness, wisdom, humanity, and righteousness, and recommending in their place a return to simplicity, the *pu* or uncarved block.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, chapters 11–20 do not develop a single theme. They circle several: emptiness and its use, the elusiveness of the Way, the manner of the ancient sages, governance, simplicity, the speaker's withdrawal from the busy world. Reading the ten chapters as a series of short related angles, rather than as steps in an argument, is closer to the way the text is shaped.

Second, the political and the personal are not separated. The chapters on governance (17, 18) sit beside chapters on personal cultivation (15, 16) and on the speaker's own withdrawal (20). The Daodejing does not divide what a ruler does from what a person practices alone; the same orientation is recommended for both. Reading the political chapters with this in mind, rather than treating them as a separate doctrine, keeps them in proportion.
