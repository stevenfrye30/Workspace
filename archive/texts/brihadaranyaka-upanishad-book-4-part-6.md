# Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 4, Part 6

Book 4, Part 6 of the Brihadaranyaka Upanishad — the Sixth Brahmana of the Fourth Adhyaya — closes the Adhyaya in a form distinct from any of its prior five Brahmanas. The Adhyaya had run across the Janaka–Yajnavalkya audience (Brahmanas 1–4) and the Yajnavalkya–Maitreyi household-renunciation dialogue (Brahmana 5), with the Fifth Brahmana closing on the narrator's report *Having said so, Yagnavalkya went away (into the forest)*. The Sixth Brahmana opens directly into a wholly different procedure: not a dialogue, not a teaching, not a renewal of any prior named participant. The form is a *vamsha* — a stem, a lineage, a transmission-list — giving the names of the teachers through whom the Brihadaranyaka has been transmitted back to its source in Brahman Svayambhu.

The Brahmana opens with a single short line at verse 1: *Now follows the stem.* The line is followed by a colon — *Now follows the stem:* — and the lineage list begins on the next passage with the first-person *(We)*. The opening item of the chain is the recension's compilers, who hold the lineage's most recent point: *(We) from Pautimashya*. Pautimashya is the first named teacher; the lineage then runs back through sixty named teachers in unbroken succession until it reaches Brahman Svayambhu.

The lineage is given in three Müller paragraphs, marked verse 1, 2, and 3 in the digitized source. The verse-markers correspond to Müller's paragraph-divisions of the long list; the teacher-numbering runs continuously across all three paragraphs from 1 to 60, embedded inside each named transmission as Müller's editorial enumeration. Reading the lineage at full length is the only way to recover its form; the list is not flattened into *the lineage runs through sixty teachers* but is preserved as the substantive content of the Brahmana itself, since the lineage is what the Brahmana is.

**Paragraph 1 (verse 1)** runs across teachers 1 through 9, holding the dense early section of the lineage where named teachers recur in cycles: *(We) from Pautimashya, Pautimashya from Gaupavana, Gaupavana from Pautimashya, Pautimashya from Gaupavana, Gaupavana from Kausika, Kausika from Kaundinya, Kaundinya from Sandilya, Sandilya from Kausika and Gautama, Gautama*. The paragraph closes mid-line at the Gautama-from-Agnivesya transition; the next paragraph picks up the chain at Agnivesya.

**Paragraph 2 (verse 2)** runs across teachers 9 through 23: *from Agnivesya, Agnivesya from Gargya, Gargya from Gargya, Gargya from Gautama, Gautama from Saitava, Saitava from Parasaryayana, Parasaryayana from Gargyayana, Gargyayana from Uddalakayana, Uddalakayana from Gabalayana, Gabalayana from Madhyandanayana, Madhyandanayana from Saukarayana, Saukarayana from Kashayana, Kashayana from Sayakayana, Sayakayana from Kausikayani, Kausikayani*. The paragraph closes mid-line at Kausikayani's transition to Ghritakausika; footnote `[*1]` attaches at *Kausikayani* before the paragraph break. The paragraph holds the central section of the lineage with the double-Gargya succession and the long *-ayana* patronymic chain.

**Paragraph 3 (verse 3)** runs across teachers 24 through 60. The chain holds the most diverse named teachers of the lineage, including the closing run of mythological figures: *Ghritakausika from Parasaryayana, Parasaryayana from Parasarya, Parasarya from Gatukarnya, Gatukarnya from Asurayana and Yaska, Asurayana from Travani, Travani from Aupagandhani, Aupagandhani from Asuri, Asuri from Bharadvaga, Bharadvaga from Atreya, Atreya from Manti, Manti from Gautama, Gautama from Gautama, Gautama from Vatsya, Vatsya from Sandilya, Sandilya from Kaisorya Kapya, Kaisorya Kapya from Kumaraharita, Kumaraharita from Galava, Galava from Vidarbhi-kaundinya, Vidarbhi-kaundinya from Vatsanapat Babhrava, Vatsanapat Babhrava from Pathi Saubhara, Pathi Saubhara from Ayasya Angirasa, Ayasya Angirasa from Abhuti Tvashtra, Abhuti Tvashtra from Visvarupa Tvashtra, Visvarupa Tvashtra from Asvinau, Asvinau from Dadhyak Atharvana, Dadhyak Atharvana from Atharvan Daiva, Atharvan Daiva from Mrityu Pradhvamsana, Mrityu Pradhvamsana from Pradhvamsana, Pradhvamsana from Ekarshi, Ekarshi from Viprakitti, Viprakitti from Vyashti, Vyashti from Sanaru, Sanaru from Sanatana, Sanatana from Sanaga, Sanaga from Parameshthin, Parameshthin from Brahman, Brahman is Svayambhu, self-existent.* The closing line follows: *Adoration to Brahman.* Footnote `[*1]` attaches at *Asurayana and Yaska* (teacher 27), and footnote `[*2]` attaches at *Viprakitti* (teacher 53).

The named teachers of the closing run hold the lineage's transition from historical-named figures (the human teachers from Pautimashya through Galava) into the mythological-and-divine register: the Asvinau (the Asvins, divine twin physicians of the Vedic pantheon), Dadhyak Atharvana (the Atharvanic seer who was decapitated and rememberd), Atharvan Daiva, Mrityu Pradhvamsana (Death-Conquering), Pradhvamsana (the Conqueror), Ekarshi (the One Seer), Viprakitti, Vyashti, Sanaru, Sanatana (the Eternal), Sanaga, Parameshthin (the One in the Highest Place), and Brahman Svayambhu. The lineage ends at the source.

After the closing of the lineage at `5.1179`, a separate editorial note follows at `5.1180`. The note is not part of the sacred text. *The Madhyandina text begins with vayam, we, and proceeds to 1. Saurpanayya, 2. Gautama, 3. Vatsya, 4. Parasarya, &c., as in the Madhukanda, p. 118, except in 10, where it gives Gaivantayana for Atreya. Then after 12. Kaundinyayana, it gives 13. 14. the two Kaundinyas, 15. the Aurnavabhas, 16. Kaundinya, 17. Kaundinya, 18. Kaundinya and Agnivesya, 19. Saitava, 20. Parasarya, 2 1. Gatukarnya, 22. Bharadvaga, 2 3. Bharadvaga, Asurayana, and Gautama, 24. Bharadvaga, 25. Valakakausika, 26. Kashayana, 27. Saukarayana, 28. Traivani, 29. Aupagandhani, 30. Sayakayana, p. Kausikayani, &c., as in the Kanva text, from No. 22 to Brahman.* The note is Müller's editorial apparatus describing the variant lineage of the Madhyandina recension of the Brihadaranyaka, with cross-references to "the Madhukanda, p. 118" of his own edition. The Madhyandina recension is one of the two main branches of the Shukla Yajurveda (alongside the Kanva, which Müller's translation principally follows); the variant lineage gives different teachers at certain positions (notably Gaivantayana in place of Atreya at position 10) and a different sequence in the early chain. The note's preservation in the digitized source as part of the Brahmana's passages requires it to be marked as editorial apparatus rather than sacred text.

## Context

The Brihadaranyaka Upanishad is one of the two earliest of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads, sharing that position with the Chandogya. It belongs to the Shukla Yajurveda branch of the Vedic corpus and forms the closing portion of the Shatapatha Brahmana — the *Aranyaka* in its name marks it as a forest-text recited at the late stages of Vedic study. Modern scholarship places the Brihadaranyaka in roughly the eighth to sixth century BCE, prior to the Buddha; the text is anonymous, and the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship. The text is the longest of the principal Upanishads and is typically structured in six *adhyayas* (chapters), each containing several *brahmanas* (sections).

Book 4, Part 6 holds the Brihadaranyaka's second sustained *vamsha* (lineage) Brahmana. The Sixth Brahmana of the Second Adhyaya had given the first lineage at the close of the Madhukanda — the *honey* section that opens the Yajnavalkya cycle of the Upanishad — running from Pautimashya backward through fifty-eight named teachers to Brahman Svayambhu. The Sixth Brahmana of the Fourth Adhyaya gives a closely paired lineage at the close of the Yajnavalkya cycle's other major section, running from Pautimashya backward through sixty named teachers to Brahman Svayambhu. The two lineages share the opening point (Pautimashya), the closing point (Brahman Svayambhu), and the substantive form (a chain of *X from Y, Y from Z* transmissions), and they hold the same vamsha-form across the two Adhyayas of the Brihadaranyaka where the Yajnavalkya teaching has been transmitted. Reading the two lineages alongside each other recovers the Upanishad's transmission-form: the same teaching is held twice, with two paired vamshas attached to the two principal sections of the Yajnavalkya cycle.

The Brahmana also holds the Adhyaya's narrative-form closing in distinction from the Adhyaya's substantive-form closing. The Fourth Brahmana closed on the substantive seal *Fearless is Brahman, and he who knows this becomes verily the fearless Brahman* — the doctrine of fearlessness as the term of the Janaka–Yajnavalkya teaching. The Fifth Brahmana closed on the narrator's report *Having said so, Yagnavalkya went away (into the forest)* — the priest's physical departure for renunciation. The Sixth Brahmana closes on the line *Brahman is Svayambhu, self-existent. Adoration to Brahman.* — the lineage's source. The three closings hold three distinct closing-forms: substantive doctrine, renunciation narrative, and lineage transmission. The Adhyaya is closed by all three together, with the substantive teaching held in Brahmana 4, the renunciation event held in Brahmana 5, and the lineage continuation held in Brahmana 6.

The vamsha's form is not without doctrine. The closing line of the lineage — *Brahman is Svayambhu, self-existent. Adoration to Brahman.* — names the lineage's source as Brahman itself, with the predicate *Svayambhu* (self-existent, self-arisen) and the substantive *Adoration to Brahman* (*Brahmane namah*) given as the closing seal. The form holds the lineage as both historical record and substantive teaching: the chain of teachers is anchored in the doctrine that the lineage's source is the self-existent absolute. The Brahmana closes the Adhyaya, and through the Adhyaya's closing it closes the Brihadaranyaka's principal Yajnavalkya cycle, with the form of doctrine-as-lineage rather than doctrine-as-statement.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the lineage should be read at full length, not collapsed into a single phrase. The Brihadaranyaka holds the names of sixty teachers because those sixty teachers are the substance of the Brahmana. Reading the list verbatim — neither summarizing it as *the lineage runs through sixty teachers* nor abbreviating it as *Pautimashya through Brahman Svayambhu* — recovers the form the Upanishad has when it gives doctrine through the named transmission of doctrine. The teacher-numbers 1–60 hold the ordered chain; the Müller paragraph-markers (verses 1, 2, 3) hold the editorial division of the chain into three reading-units; the closing run from Asvinau through Brahman holds the lineage's transition from historical-named to mythological-named figures.

Second, the editorial note at `5.1180` should be read as Müller's apparatus rather than as sacred text. The note describes the variant Madhyandina recension's lineage, with cross-references to the page numbers of Müller's own edition. The note holds three pieces of substantive information: that the Madhyandina recension begins with the same first-person *vayam* (we) but proceeds through different early teachers (Saurpanayya, Gautama, Vatsya, Parasarya); that it diverges at position 10 (Gaivantayana for Atreya); and that it converges with the Kanva text at position 22 onward. Reading the note as editorial commentary on textual transmission rather than as part of the Brahmana's substance preserves the source's distinction between sacred-text lineage and apparatus-on-lineage. The OCR irregularities inside the note — the broken numbers *2 1.* (twenty-one) and *2 3.* (twenty-three) — are part of the digitized source rather than of the Müller original; the irregularities are preserved as found.

## The text

The text is held in the Archive library under `upanishads-muller-part2`, chapter 5 (Müller translation, 1884 — *The Sacred Books of the East*, Volume XV; via SacredTexts.com). Library chapter 5 is shared across sixty-four Archive entities — four parts of the Katha Upanishad, the six parts of the Mundaka Upanishad, twenty-eight anuvakas of the Taittiriyaka, the eleven Brahmanas of the Brihadaranyaka's First and Second Adhyayas, all nine Brahmanas of the Third Adhyaya, and the First through Fifth Brahmanas of the Fourth Adhyaya — and continues here into the Sixth Brahmana, the Adhyaya's closing. Passages `5.1171` through `5.1180` carry the body in Müller's prose presentation across ten short passages, with verse-numbered markers at `1.` (`5.1171`), `2.` (`5.1174`), and `3.` (`5.1176`). The Brahmana contains several boundary features that should be preserved honestly:

- **Verse-1 introductory line with footnote on the term *stem*.** Passage `5.1171` carries the introductory line *Now follows the stem* with footnote `[*2]` on *stem*. Müller's note glosses the underlying Sanskrit term *vamsha* — a lineage, a stem, a genealogical chain — and is the principal note on the form of the Brahmana itself.
- **Lineage-paragraph 1 across two passages.** Verse 1 runs across passages `5.1172` and `5.1173`, with teacher-numbers 1–9 across both. Passage `5.1172` carries teachers 1–3 (the (We)–Pautimashya–Gaupavana–Pautimashya cycle); passage `5.1173` carries teachers 4–9 (Gaupavana through Gautama). The paragraph closes mid-line at *9. Gautama* with the next teacher (Agnivesya) carried into the verse-2 passage.
- **Verse-2 paragraph-marker with mid-line continuation.** Passage `5.1174` carries the verse-2 marker *2.* and the closing of the prior teacher's transmission-clause: *2. from Agnivesya*. The verse-2 paragraph continues into passage `5.1175` with teachers 10–23. The paragraph closes mid-line at *23. Kausikayani* with footnote `[*1]` on *Kausikayani* and the next teacher (Ghritakausika) carried into the verse-3 passage.
- **Verse-3 paragraph-marker with mid-line continuation.** Passage `5.1176` carries the verse-3 marker *3.* and the closing of the prior teacher's transmission-clause: *3. from Ghritakausika*. The verse-3 paragraph continues into passage `5.1177` (teacher 24, Ghritakausika from Parasaryayana on its own short passage), passage `5.1178` (teachers 25–54, with footnote `[*1]` at *Asurayana and Yaska* and footnote `[*2]` at *Viprakitti*), and passage `5.1179` (teachers 55–60, with the closing line *Brahman is Svayambhu, self-existent. Adoration to Brahman*).
- **Closing line on Brahman Svayambhu at `5.1179`.** The closing of the lineage at teacher 60 carries the substantive seal *Brahman is Svayambhu, self-existent.* The line names the lineage's source with the predicate *Svayambhu* — self-existent, self-arisen — and is followed by the closing adoration *Adoration to Brahman.* The closing carries no verse-marker and no footnote; the substantive seal is held inside the lineage's last position rather than as a separate verse.
- **Editorial note at `5.1180` on the Madhyandina recension.** Passage `5.1180` carries Müller's editorial note describing the variant lineage of the Madhyandina recension of the Brihadaranyaka. The note begins *The Madhyandina text begins with vayam, we, and proceeds to* and runs through the variant lineage's early teachers, the substitution at position 10 (Gaivantayana for Atreya), and the convergence with the Kanva text at position 22 onward. Cross-references to *the Madhukanda, p. 118* point to Müller's own edition's prior lineage at the close of the Second Adhyaya. The note is editorial apparatus and is preserved as such rather than being merged with the sacred text.
- **OCR irregularities inside the editorial note.** Passage `5.1180` carries two OCR irregularities in the digitized source's transcription of the editorial note. The number 21 is rendered with a space — *2 1. Gatukarnya* — rather than as the unbroken number, and the number 23 is similarly rendered as *2 3. Bharadvaga, Asurayana, and Gautama*. The irregularities are transcription artifacts carried over from Müller's volume and are preserved as found in the digitized source. A further small irregularity at the same passage gives *p. Kausikayani* in place of the expected *31. Kausikayani* — the *p.* appears to be an OCR misreading of the digit *31.*; the substance of the note is unaffected.
- **Closing of the Adhyaya at `5.1180`.** The Brahmana closes with the editorial note rather than with a substantive seal. The Adhyaya's closing form is held in the substantive seal at the close of the lineage at `5.1179` (*Brahman is Svayambhu, self-existent. Adoration to Brahman*), with the editorial note on the variant recension following as Müller's apparatus on the lineage's textual transmission.

Passage `5.1181` is the "Fifth Adhyaya" header that opens Book 5 of the Brihadaranyaka and is not part of this entity. The library chapter continues past this entity into the Fifth Adhyaya and the remaining portions of the volume; the Brihadaranyaka extends well beyond the boundaries of library chapter 5. ## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 2, Part 6 — paired *vamsha* (lineage) Brahmanas closing the two principal sections of the Brihadaranyaka's Yajnavalkya cycle. The Sixth Brahmana of the Second Adhyaya closes the Adhyaya in pure transmission record across fifty-eight named teachers from Pautimashya backward to Brahman Svayambhu, the Sixth Brahmana of the Fourth Adhyaya closes the Fourth Adhyaya in the same transmission-form across sixty named teachers from Pautimashya backward to Brahman Svayambhu. Both lineages share the opening point — *(We) from Pautimashya* — and the closing point — *Brahman is Svayambhu, self-existent* — and both hold the substantive form of *X from Y, Y from Z* across all named teachers without further commentary. The two lineages are the Brihadaranyaka's two principal *vamsha* records, attached to the two principal Adhyayas where the Yajnavalkya teaching has been transmitted; the variant Madhyandina lineage at `5.1180` cross-references *the Madhukanda, p. 118* — the prior lineage at the close of the Second Adhyaya.
- **structural-parallel** → Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 4, Part 5 — paired immediate-predecessor Brahmanas of the Fourth Adhyaya holding the renunciation-narrative and the lineage-record as paired closing motifs of the Yajnavalkya cycle. The Fifth Brahmana closes with Yajnavalkya's physical departure for the forest — *Having said so, Yagnavalkya went away (into the forest)* — at the close of the second recension of the Maitreyi dialogue, the Sixth Brahmana opens directly into the lineage that continues the transmission of the teaching after the priest's departure. The Fifth Brahmana names the renunciation event; the Sixth Brahmana names the chain through which the teaching that the renouncer has passed on continues. The two Brahmanas hold the Adhyaya's two complementary closings: the priest leaving the household and the lineage continuing through the named teachers.
- **structural-parallel** → Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 4, Part 4 — paired Adhyaya-closing forms holding three distinct kinds of closure across the Adhyaya's last three Brahmanas. The Fourth Brahmana closes the Janaka–Yajnavalkya substantive teaching with the seal *Fearless is Brahman, and he who knows this becomes verily the fearless Brahman*, the Fifth closes the Yajnavalkya–Maitreyi household-renunciation with the narrator's report on Yajnavalkya's departure, the Sixth closes the Adhyaya with the lineage to Brahman Svayambhu. The three closings together hold the Adhyaya's whole closing-form: substantive doctrine, renunciation narrative, and lineage transmission. The Fourth Brahmana's seal is the substantive doctrine-form; the Sixth Brahmana's lineage is the transmission-form; reading the two together with the Fifth Brahmana's renunciation-narrative held between them recovers the Adhyaya's three-fold closing across its last three Brahmanas.
