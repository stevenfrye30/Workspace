# Dhammapada, Chapter 15 (Happiness)

Chapter 15 of the Dhammapada — the *Sukha-vagga*, "Happiness" — is a chapter of twelve verses. The opening four verses share a single form, varied through four substitutions: *let us live happily then, not hating those who hate us! among men who hate us let us dwell free from hatred!* (197); *free from ailments among the ailing* (198); *free from greed among the greedy* (199); *though we call nothing our own! we shall be like the bright gods, feeding on happiness* (200). The fifth verse holds the chapter's hinge: *victory breeds hatred, for the conquered is unhappy. he who has given up both victory and defeat, he, the contented, is happy* (201). Verses 202–204 hold three short lists in the same negative-superlative form. Verse 202: *there is no fire like passion; there is no losing throw like hatred; there is no pain like this body; there is no happiness higher than rest.* Verse 203: *hunger is the worst of diseases, the body the greatest of pains; if one knows this truly, that is Nirvana, the highest happiness.* Verse 204: *health is the greatest of gifts, contentedness the best riches; trust is the best of relationships, Nirvana the highest happiness.* The closing four verses turn the figure to companionship and solitude. Verse 205 holds the sweetness of solitude and tranquillity. Verse 206 holds the contrast between seeing the elect and not seeing fools. Verse 207 holds company with fools as long suffering and company with the wise as pleasure. Verse 208 closes the chapter on the figure that one should follow the wise *as the moon follows the path of the stars*.

## Context

The Dhammapada is one of the books of the Khuddaka Nikaya, the fifth and shortest section of the Sutta Pitaka in the Pali Canon. It collects 423 verses arranged in twenty-six chapters by theme — pairs, heedfulness, mind, flowers, the sage, evil, and so on. The verses are attributed to the Buddha and were transmitted orally for several centuries before being committed to writing in Sri Lanka in the first century BCE. The collection took shape in roughly the third century BCE, in connection with the Theravada transmission of the canon.

Chapter 15 is the *Sukha-vagga*, "Happiness." Its twelve verses are numbered 197 through 208 in the standard continuous numbering of the Dhammapada — and in F. Max Müller's 1881 translation, the version held in the Archive library, the count and numbering match the standard exactly, with no merged or missing verse of the kind that occurs at the close or middle of Chapters 4, 6, 8, 11, and 14. The Pali word *sukha* — happiness, ease, well-being, pleasure — is rendered uniformly by Müller as "happiness" throughout. Each verse is short — two or four lines in the Pali — and each completes one statement of the figure.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the chapter is built across two structural quartets and a central hinge. The opening quartet (197–200) shares one form four times — *let us live happily, free from X among men of X* — with hatred, ailment, greed, and possession as the four Xs. The closing quartet, more loosely, holds verses 205–208 on solitude and the company of the wise, against the company of fools. Between them, verses 201–204 hold the chapter's central negative-superlative form: *there is no X like Y*, used to rank passion as the worst fire, hatred as the worst losing throw, the body as the greatest pain, hunger as the worst disease, health as the best gift, contentedness as the best riches, trust as the best relationship, and Nirvana — twice — as the highest happiness. Reading the chapter as two quartets framing a central ranking, rather than as twelve separate aphorisms, recovers the form the chapter is built on.

Second, the *Nirvana the highest happiness* refrain at the close of verses 203 and 204 is the chapter's structural pillar. The chapter does not make Nirvana a doctrinal abstraction; it places it as the upper bound of the same scale on which health, contentment, trust, and the absence of passion, hatred, and bodily pain are also placed. Reading the chapter with that scale held visible, rather than as a contrast between worldly and ultimate goods, keeps the form the chapter has.

## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Dhammapada, Chapter 1 (The Twin Verses) — the opening pair on the shelf, with the same compositional principle of paired and patterned verses; Chapter 1 with ten paired verses contrasting wholesome and unwholesome states across different situations, Chapter 15 with an opening quartet of *let-us-live-happily-among-X* verses and a central ranking of negative-superlatives. Both chapters work by setting the same form against varied content.
- **structural-parallel** → Dhammapada, Chapter 5 (The Fool) and Chapter 6 (The Wise Man) — the earlier chapters whose fool/wise polarity is reused here in compressed form; Chapters 5 and 6 work the polarity as the substance of two whole chapters, Chapter 15 verses 206–208 work the same polarity in three verses on the company of the wise versus the company of fools. The figure is held across the chapters.
- **structural-parallel** → Dhammapada, Chapter 14 (The Buddha) — the immediate predecessor on the shelf; Chapter 14 with seventeen verses on the Awakened, his tracklessness, his teaching summarised at verse 183, and the refuge in him as the deliverance from all pain, Chapter 15 with twelve verses on the happiness that follows the deliverance — *Nirvana, the highest happiness* (203, 204). The two chapters move from the figure who delivers to the state of the delivered.
- **structural-parallel** → Epictetus, Enchiridion, Chapters 1–5 — the Stoic shelf-pair on the figure of inward freedom from external compulsion; the Enchiridion's distinction between what is and is not within one's power and the practices that follow, Dhammapada 15 verses 197–200 on living free of hatred among the hating, free of ailment among the ailing, free of greed among the greedy, and free of possession itself. Both texts hold the figure that one's state need not match the state of one's surroundings.
- **structural-parallel** → Plato, Apology — the Greek pair on the contented happiness that does not depend on victory or defeat; Socrates' refusal to plead for life or to weep at the verdict, Dhammapada 15 verse 201 on giving up both victory and defeat as the ground of contentment. Both texts hold contentment as a state achieved by removing the terms that ordinarily govern happiness.
