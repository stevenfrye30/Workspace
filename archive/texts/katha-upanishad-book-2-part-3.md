# Katha Upanishad, Book 2, Part 3

Book 2, Part 3 of the Katha Upanishad — the Sixth Valli — is the concluding valli of the dialogue between Yama and Nachiketas. The valli opens with the figure of an ancient tree whose roots grow upward and whose branches grow downward; that tree is named the Bright, Brahman, the Immortal. From the opening figure the valli moves through a sequence of summary verses that return earlier material in condensed form: the graded ordering of senses, mind, Great Self, Undeveloped, and Person first set out in Book 1, Part 3 returns here in two compact verses; Yoga is named as the firm holding back of the senses; the Self that cannot be reached by speech, mind, or eye is reached by the one who says "He is"; when all desires that dwell in the heart cease, the mortal becomes immortal; and at verse 15 the formula *iti vidyānuśāsanam* — here ends the teaching. The closing verses recall the Person not larger than a thumb seated in the heart, narrate that Nachiketas obtained Brahman, and end the Upanishad with a peace invocation: *Om! Peace! peace! peace!*

## Context

The Katha Upanishad is one of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads — a small group of texts traditionally received as the philosophical core of the late Vedic literature. It belongs to the Krishna Yajurveda branch of the Vedic corpus and is composed in Sanskrit verse, mostly in the *anushtubh* meter. Modern scholarship places its composition in roughly the fifth to third century BCE; the principal Upanishads as a group are dated to the centuries on either side of the Buddha. The text is anonymous; the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship.

Book 2, Part 3 closes the second *adhyaya* and the Upanishad as a whole. The dialogue continues uninterrupted from Book 2, Part 2: Yama is still answering, Nachiketas is still listening, and the valli does not introduce new framing material. What it does is gather. The opening figure of the inverted tree, the recapitulated faculty hierarchy, the formal definition of Yoga, the closing narrative of Nachiketas — these are not new doctrine but a drawing-together of what the preceding vallis have set out, ending in the sealed formula that names the close of the teaching. The Bhagavad Gita's Chapter 15 opens with the same inverted *ashvattha* tree and develops the figure into a chapter of its own; the textual line of descent between the closing of the Katha and the opening of Gita 15 is one of the clearest in the Upanishadic-epic relation.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the valli is built on return rather than novelty. The graded list of senses, mind, intellect, the Great, the Undeveloped, and the Person was set out at length in Book 1, Part 3 with the chariot image; here it returns in two compressed verses. The Person of the size of a thumb was named in Book 2, Part 1; here the figure returns at the close. The teaching that the Self is not reached by argument or by much learning was given in Book 2, Part 2 (and earlier in Book 1, Part 2); here the teaching is given again in shorter form, sealed by the formula that here ends the teaching. Reading the valli with attention to the return of earlier figures, and to the slight rotation of vocabulary that gives each return its place, recovers the closing form the Upanishad has.

Second, the verses move toward closure. The dialogue does not resolve into a thesis; it ends. The line *iti vidyānuśāsanam* at verse 15 is the formal close of the instruction; verses 16 and 17 add the practical figures of the arteries of the heart and the Self drawn from the body as one draws the pith from a reed; verse 18 narrates the outcome — Nachiketas, having received the teaching, became free from passion and death; verse 19 closes with the peace invocation that ends every Upanishad in this branch. Reading slowly enough to let the closing cadence register — first the formal seal, then the practical figures, then the narrative outcome, then the peace — keeps the form the Katha gives its ending.

## The text

The text is held in the Archive library under `upanishads-muller-part2`, chapter 5 (Müller translation, 1884 — *The Sacred Books of the East*, Volume XV; via SacredTexts.com). Library chapter 5 is shared across four Archive entities of the Katha: Book 1, Part 3 (the Third Valli, passages `5.1` through `5.19`); Book 2, Part 1 (the Fourth Valli, passages `5.20` through `5.37`); Book 2, Part 2 (the Fifth Valli, passages `5.38` through `5.54`); and Book 2, Part 3 (the Sixth Valli, the entity here). Book 2, Part 3 occupies passages `5.55` through `5.75`. Passage `5.55` is the "Sixth Valli" header; passages `5.56` through `5.75` carry the nineteen numbered verses of the valli, with verse 17 split across passages `5.72` and `5.73` where Müller's footnote interrupts the verse mid-line. Passage `5.71` carries verse 16 with a transcription artifact in the source: the verse number appears as "116." rather than "16." — the content is correct and the surrounding passages are correctly numbered. The library chapter does not end with the close of the Katha: passage `5.76` is a page-break artifact (page references in square brackets), and passage `5.77` opens the Mundaka Upanishad ("MUNDAKA-UPANISHAD."). Nineteen verses; about twelve minutes' reading.

## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 15 (Purushottama Yoga) — paired Sanskrit passages opening on the inverted *ashvattha* tree; the Katha's Sixth Valli opens with the ancient tree whose roots grow upward and whose branches grow downward (Katha 2.3.1), and the Gita's Chapter 15 opens with the same image (Gita 15.1) and develops it into a sustained teaching on the supreme Person — the Upanishadic figure carried forward into the epic in close textual relation.
- **structural-parallel** → Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 18 (Moksha Sanyasa Yoga) — paired closing dialogues; the Gita's Chapter 18 is Krishna's recapitulation of the whole teaching at the end of the conversation with Arjuna, ending in the disciple's accepted instruction and the narrator's closing frame, the Katha's Sixth Valli is Yama's recapitulation of the whole teaching at the end of the conversation with Nachiketas, ending in the disciple's accepted instruction and the closing peace invocation.
- **structural-parallel** → Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 2 (Sankhya Yoga) — paired Sanskrit passages on the cessation of desire and the imperishable Self; the Gita's verses on the *sthitaprajna* whose desires have ceased and who is firm in wisdom (Gita 2.55–72) close on the same recognition the Katha reaches in this valli — that when all desires that dwell in the heart cease, the mortal becomes immortal (Katha 2.3.14) — completing in compressed form the line of teaching the two texts hold together.
