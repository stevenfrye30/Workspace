# Taittiriyaka Upanishad, Book 1, Part 4

Book 1, Part 4 of the Taittiriyaka Upanishad — the Fourth Anuvaka of the Sikshavalli — is a teacher's prayer, recited inside the same school-form the preceding anuvakas have opened. The first verse calls on the strong bull of the Vedas, who has risen from the Vedas and from the Immortal, to strengthen the speaker with wisdom and to make him an upholder of the Immortal — and turns inward: *may my body be able, my tongue sweet, may I hear much with my ears; thou (Om) art the shrine of Brahman, covered by wisdom; guard what I have learnt*. The second verse asks that *Sri* — prosperity — come without delay, with garments and cattle and food and drink; and turns again, in the verse most often quoted from this Anuvaka, to ask in the *Svāhā* cadence that the Brahman-students come to the speaker, *Svāhā!* — that they come from all sides, *Svāhā!* — that they come forth, *Svāhā!* — that they practise restraint, *Svāhā!* — that they enjoy peace, *Svāhā!* The third verse continues in the same cadence — *may I be a glory among men, Svāhā! may I be better than the richest, Svāhā!* — and reaches the line that the speaker enters into the treasure (named here as Om) and the treasure enters into him, that as water runs downward and as months join the year, so let the students come into the speaker. The Anuvaka closes with the short formula *thou art a refuge; enlighten me; take possession of me*.

## Context

The Taittiriyaka Upanishad is one of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads — a small group of texts traditionally received as the philosophical core of the late Vedic literature. It belongs to the Krishna Yajurveda branch of the Vedic corpus and to the Taittiriya school of that branch; the Upanishad sits inside the Taittiriya Aranyaka. Modern scholarship places it among the older prose Upanishads, in roughly the sixth to fifth century BCE — earlier than the verse Upanishads of the Katha and Mundaka. The text is anonymous; the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship.

Book 1, Part 4 sits inside the Sikshavalli, the chapter on *śikṣā*. The First Anuvaka set the protective frame; the Second Anuvaka named the topic; the Third Anuvaka taught the *samhita* under five paired correspondences; the Fourth Anuvaka turns the same school-form into the form of a prayer that the teacher says — not for the pupil's benefit only, but for the teacher's own benefit and for the benefit of the school as a whole. The verses are sometimes called the *Medha-sūkta* — the prayer for retentive intelligence — and the line on the students coming from all sides has been kept in liturgical use ever since. The Anuvaka is held inside the Sikshavalli's wider concern with the conditions of recitation: the protection of the recitation, the disciplines that hold it, the correspondences that organize it, and now the prayer that staffs it.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the Anuvaka is built on patterned repetition. The *Svāhā* cadence carries the second and third verses; each clause is a single short petition closed by the same word. Reading the Anuvaka aloud, allowing the cadence to register before any attempt to read for content, recovers the form the prayer has and the place patterned repetition holds in the late-Vedic school-form: the words are arranged so that the speaker can say them in order without losing the order, and so that the order itself carries the weight of what is asked.

Second, the Anuvaka holds together what would otherwise be three different kinds of asking. The first verse asks for the speaker's own wisdom, ability, and protection of what has been learnt; the second verse asks for material prosperity (cattle, garments, food, drink) and for students; the third verse asks for the speaker's standing among men and for entry into the treasure that is named as Om. The Anuvaka does not separate the three; they are placed in a single sequence, in the same cadence, under the same protective frame. Reading the Anuvaka with the three kinds of asking held alongside one another, rather than ranking them, keeps the form the school-prayer has.

## The text

The text is held in the Archive library under `upanishads-muller-part2`, chapter 5 (Müller translation, 1884 — *The Sacred Books of the East*, Volume XV; via SacredTexts.com). Library chapter 5 is shared across thirteen Archive entities — four parts of the Katha Upanishad, the six parts of the Mundaka Upanishad, and Books 1, Parts 1 through 3 of the Taittiriyaka — and continues here into the Fourth Anuvaka without break in the chapter. Book 1, Part 4 of the Taittiriyaka — the entity here — occupies passages `5.180` through `5.187`. Passage `5.180` is the "Fourth Anuvaka" header. Passages `5.181` through `5.187` carry the body of the Anuvaka in Müller's prose presentation, with the verse-numbered markers visible at `1.` (start of `5.181`), `2.` (start of `5.184`), and `3.` (start of `5.186`). Each of the three numbered verses is split across two adjacent passages where Müller's editorial paragraphing interrupts the verse. Passage `5.187` opens with the editorial sub-marker `(1)` — a sub-numbering carried over from the source that signals a short closing line ("Thou art a refuge! Enlighten me! Take possession of me!") attached to the third verse rather than as a fourth verse of its own. Passage `5.188` is the "Fifth Anuvaka" header that opens Book 1, Part 5 of the Taittiriyaka and is not part of this entity. The library chapter continues past this entity into the rest of the Taittiriyaka and the remaining Upanishads of the volume. About four minutes' reading.

## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Taittiriyaka Upanishad, Book 1, Part 3 — paired immediately consecutive Anuvakas of the Sikshavalli; the Third Anuvaka named the teacher–pupil pair as one of the five fields of the *samhita*, joined by knowledge through the recitation of the Veda, the Fourth Anuvaka turns from the structural correspondence to the prayer that fills it — that the Brahman-students come to the teacher from all sides — the abstract pairing of the Third Anuvaka and the petitionary form of the Fourth held inside one continuous instructional sequence.
- **structural-parallel** → Analects, Book 1 (Xue Er) — paired short pedagogical openings on the joy of students arriving; the Analects opens with the line that it is not delightful to have friends (or fellow students) come from afar (1.1), the Taittiriyaka's Fourth Anuvaka holds the same recognition in the petitionary form — *may the Brahman-students come to me, Svāhā! may they come from all sides, Svāhā!* — both texts placing the arrival of those who would learn at the centre of the teacher's relation to learning, in two distinct civilizational vocabularies.
- **structural-parallel** → Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 4 (Jnana Karma Sanyasa Yoga) — paired Sanskrit passages on the disciplines that hold the transmission of teaching; the Gita's Chapter 4 names *yajña* in many forms — material sacrifice, sacrifice of breath, sacrifice of the senses, sacrifice of study, sacrifice of austerity, sacrifice of knowledge — as the practices by which the disciple is prepared to receive the teaching, the Taittiriyaka's Fourth Anuvaka holds a related form in the school-prayer that asks for ability, sweetness of tongue, attentiveness of ear, retentive memory, restraint, and peace — both texts placing the teaching in the field of practiced conditions that make it transmissible.
