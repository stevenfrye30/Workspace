# Katha Upanishad, Book 1, Part 3

Book 1, Part 3 of the Katha Upanishad — the Third Valli — continues Yama's instruction directly from the Second Valli's teaching on the Self. The valli opens with the figure of two drinking their reward in the cave of the heart and turns into the chariot image that has carried the teaching ever since: the Self seated in the chariot of the body, the intellect (*buddhi*) as the charioteer, the mind as the reins, the senses as the horses, and the objects of sense as their roads. From the chariot image the valli moves to a graded ordering of faculties — beyond the senses are the objects, beyond the objects the mind, beyond the mind the intellect, beyond the intellect the Great, beyond the Great the Undeveloped, beyond the Undeveloped the Person, and beyond the Person nothing — and closes with the line that the path is hard to traverse, sharp as the edge of a razor.

## Context

The Katha Upanishad is one of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads — a small group of texts traditionally received as the philosophical core of the late Vedic literature. It belongs to the Krishna Yajurveda branch of the Vedic corpus and is composed in Sanskrit verse, mostly in the *anushtubh* meter. Modern scholarship places its composition in roughly the fifth to third century BCE; the principal Upanishads as a group are dated to the centuries on either side of the Buddha. The text is anonymous; the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship.

Book 1, Part 3 closes the first *adhyaya* of the Katha. Part 1 set the situation; Part 2 began the instruction with the distinction between the good (*shreyas*) and the pleasant (*preyas*) and the first sustained teaching on the Self; Part 3 develops that teaching into more explicitly structured imagery. The chariot figure (Katha 1.3.3–9) and the verses ordering the faculties (1.3.10–11) are among the most cited passages of the Upanishadic corpus; the Bhagavad Gita's later teaching on the disciplined mind and on the field (*kshetra*) and the knower of the field (*kshetrajna*) draws on the framework this valli sets out. The valli closes Book 1; Book 2 will resume in the Fourth Valli with the senses turned outward by the Self-existent.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the valli proceeds by ordered relation rather than abstraction. The chariot is not a symbol to decode; it is a way of holding charioteer, reins, horses, and roads together in one figure so that their relation can be seen at once. The graded list that follows — senses, objects, mind, intellect, the Great, the Undeveloped, the Person — is a continuation of the same procedure: each term is identified by what is beyond it, and the ordering itself is the teaching. Reading the verses with attention to the order, and to the small word "beyond" returning through the sequence, rather than to the metaphysical content of the named terms, recovers the form the valli has.

Second, the valli moves consistently from outer to inner. The senses face outward; the objects of sense lie further out; behind them the mind, behind the mind the intellect, behind the intellect the unspoken hierarchy that ends in the Person. The closing imperative — rise, awake, the path is sharp as a razor's edge — turns the same direction inward into instruction. Reading Part 3 with the outward-to-inward movement held visible, and noticing where the same movement returns through different vocabulary, keeps the form the Upanishad gives the teaching.

## The text

The text is held in the Archive library under `upanishads-muller-part2`, chapter 5 (Müller translation, 1884 — *The Sacred Books of the East*, Volume XV; via SacredTexts.com). Library chapter 5 begins with the Third Valli but, unlike chapters 3 and 4 — which are dedicated to the First and Second Vallis — chapter 5 continues past the close of Book 1 into Book 2 and the remaining Upanishads of the volume. The Third Valli itself occupies passages `5.1` through `5.19` in the library: seventeen numbered verses, with verses 5 and 14 each split across two adjacent passages where Müller's editorial paragraphing or footnote interrupts the verse mid-line. Passage `5.20` is the "Second Adhyaya" header that opens Book 2 and is not part of Book 1, Part 3. Seventeen verses; about ten minutes' reading.

## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 6 (Dhyana Yoga) — paired Sanskrit passages on the disciplined mind; the Gita's Krishna teaches Arjuna that the mind is restless and hard to hold but can be brought under control by steady practice, the Katha's Third Valli sets the same teaching in the chariot image, with the intellect as the charioteer holding the reins of the mind and the horses of the senses.
- **structural-parallel** → Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 13 (Kshetra Kshetrajna Vibhaga Yoga) — paired passages on the layered ordering of what changes and what does not; the Gita's Chapter 13 distinguishes the field (*kshetra*) and the knower of the field (*kshetrajna*) and lists the contents of the field in graded order, the Katha's Third Valli sets out the graded order of senses, objects, mind, intellect, the Great, the Undeveloped, and the Person, with each term identified by what is beyond it.
- **structural-parallel** → Dhammapada, Chapter 1 (Yamakavagga, the Twin Verses) — paired short passages on the priority of the inner over the outer; the Dhammapada opens with the line that mind precedes all things, mind is their chief, they are mind-made, and the Katha's Third Valli holds the same priority in the figure of the chariot and the graded ordering of the faculties.
