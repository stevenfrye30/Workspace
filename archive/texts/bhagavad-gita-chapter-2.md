# Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 2 (Sankhya Yoga)

Chapter 2 of the Bhagavad Gita — the *Sankhya Yoga*, conventionally translated "The Yoga of Knowledge" — is the philosophical core of the Gita. In seventy-two verses, Krishna answers Arjuna's refusal to fight, beginning with rebuke and turning to the doctrine the rest of the Gita develops: the distinction between the imperishable self and the body, action performed without attachment to its fruits, and a portrait of the sage who has settled into steadiness of mind. The Bhagavad Gita itself is a dialogue spoken between two armies on the eve of the Kurukshetra War, embedded in the much larger Sanskrit epic, the Mahabharata.

## Context

The Bhagavad Gita is a 700-verse philosophical poem in eighteen chapters, set inside Book VI (the *Bhishma Parva*) of the Mahabharata. Most scholars place its present form between roughly the second century BCE and the second century CE — a layered composition that took shape during the long growth of the epic from an older bardic core. The traditional attribution is to Vyasa, the legendary compiler of the Mahabharata; the historical authorship is unknown.

Chapter 2 is the longest chapter of the Gita and is generally read as the place where Krishna first lays out, in compressed form, the whole of the teaching the later chapters elaborate. Arjuna, the warrior-prince, has refused to fight when he sees his teachers, kinsmen, and former friends arrayed in the opposing army. Chapter 2 is Krishna's first sustained reply. The argument moves from a defense of fighting on grounds of duty, to the position that the imperishable self is not killed when the body is killed, to the distinctive Gita teaching that action should be performed for its own sake rather than for its outcome, and finally to a portrait of the *sthitaprajna*, the person of steady wisdom.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, Chapter 2 is the chapter, not the book. It states the position the rest of the Gita develops; reading it as a complete short statement, then returning later to the chapters that elaborate each part, follows the way the text itself is structured. The portrait of the *sthitaprajna* in the closing verses (2.54–72) is the chapter's own summary.

Second, the chapter is a dialogue in a war camp, not a sermon. Arjuna's refusal is concrete: these are his teachers and cousins, and the war is about a kingdom. Krishna's reply works through the immediate question — whether Arjuna should fight — before turning to the philosophical foundation. Reading the chapter with the dramatic situation kept in view, rather than as a free-standing treatise on the self and action, keeps it in its native form.

## The text

The text is held in the Archive library under `bhagavad-gita`, chapter 2 (Edwin Arnold's verse translation, *The Song Celestial*, 1885 — Project Gutenberg edition). Arnold's verse translation compresses the chapter's seventy-two Sanskrit shlokas into nineteen English verse-paragraphs followed by a closing colophon line (passages `2.1`–`2.20` in the library's verse-level subdivision). A modern prose translation by the International Gita Society is held in the library as a parallel translation. About forty-five minutes' reading.
