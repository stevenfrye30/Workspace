# Dhammapada, Chapter 9 (Evil)

Chapter 9 of the Dhammapada — the *Pāpa-vagga*, "Evil" — is a chapter of thirteen verses worked through paired contrast. Verse 116 states the form: hasten toward good, keep thought away from evil. Verses 117 and 118 pair sin and good as opposite practices: sin should not be repeated, good should be repeated, with pain as the outcome of one and happiness of the other. Verses 119 and 120 pair the slow ripening of consequences: even the evil-doer sees happiness while the evil deed has not ripened, even the good man sees evil days while the good deed has not ripened — when each ripens, each sees its own outcome. Verses 121 and 122 pair the *water-drops-filling-a-pot* simile in opposite directions: the fool becomes full of evil little by little, the wise becomes full of good little by little. Verse 123 turns to avoidance: a merchant with few companions and much wealth, a man who loves life avoiding poison — so a man should avoid evil. Verse 124 holds the figure of the unwounded hand and the poison: as poison does not affect one without a wound, evil does not affect one who does not commit it. Verse 125 holds the figure of dust thrown against the wind: evil offered to the innocent falls back upon the fool. Verse 126 names four destinations — the womb, hell, heaven, Nirvana. The closing two verses share a single refrain — *not in the sky, not in the midst of the sea, not in the clefts of the mountains, is there known a spot in the whole world* — first to say that no such spot frees a man from his evil deed (127), then to say that no such spot frees a mortal from death (128). The chapter holds evil and its consequences across a series of paired statements; the closing pair turns the figure to its limit.

## Context

The Dhammapada is one of the books of the Khuddaka Nikaya, the fifth and shortest section of the Sutta Pitaka in the Pali Canon. It collects 423 verses arranged in twenty-six chapters by theme — pairs, heedfulness, mind, flowers, the sage, evil, and so on. The verses are attributed to the Buddha and were transmitted orally for several centuries before being committed to writing in Sri Lanka in the first century BCE. The collection took shape in roughly the third century BCE, in connection with the Theravada transmission of the canon.

Chapter 9 is the *Pāpa-vagga*, "Evil." Its thirteen verses are numbered 116 through 128 in the standard continuous numbering of the Dhammapada — and in F. Max Müller's 1881 translation, the version held in the Archive library, the count and numbering match the standard exactly, with no merged or missing verse of the kind that occurs at the close of Chapter 4, Chapter 6, and Chapter 8 (where Müller combines two conventional verses into a single passage). Each verse is short — two or four lines in the Pali — and each completes one statement of the contrast.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the chapter is built on a series of paired contrasts. Verses 117–118 pair sin and good. Verses 119–120 pair the delayed ripening of evil and good. Verses 121–122 pair the incremental accumulation of evil and good through the same water-drops simile. Verses 127–128 pair the inescapability of the evil deed and of death through the same "not-in-the-sky-not-in-the-sea-not-in-the-clefts" refrain. Reading the paired verses as one figure with two statements, rather than as separate aphorisms, recovers the form the chapter is built on.

Second, the chapter holds a doctrine of moral causation that is both gradual and inescapable. Verses 119–120 work the slow-ripening logic: a deed's outcome may not appear at once, and the evil-doer may see happiness while waiting and the good man may see evil days, but each ripens. Verses 121–122 work the gradual-accumulation logic: a single drop of water seems nothing, but a pot fills. Verse 124 works the no-vector-no-effect logic: poison cannot enter where there is no wound. Verses 127–128 close on the no-escape logic: there is no place in the world where the deed or death can be evaded. Reading the chapter with the gradual-and-inescapable double-figure held visible across the verses, rather than collapsing it into a single karmic principle, keeps the form the verses share.

## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Dhammapada, Chapter 5 (The Fool) — the earlier chapter that already worked the slow-ripening figure of evil; Chapter 5 verse 69 holds the figure that an evil deed seems sweet as honey to the fool until it ripens, and verse 71 holds it as smouldering fire under ashes, Chapter 9 verses 119–120 turn the same figure into a paired contrast — the evil-doer sees happiness until his evil ripens, the good man sees evil until his good ripens. The slow-ripening doctrine recurs across the two chapters.
- **structural-parallel** → Dhammapada, Chapter 1 (The Twin Verses) — the opening pair on the shelf, with the same compositional principle; Chapter 1 with ten paired verses contrasting wholesome and unwholesome states across different situations, Chapter 9 with thirteen verses including five paired contrasts (117–118, 119–120, 121–122, 127–128) that work the same paired-aphorism form.
- **structural-parallel** → Dhammapada, Chapter 8 (The Thousands) — the immediate predecessor on the shelf, with a related but distinct rhetorical figure; Chapter 8 sets a single instance of the better against a thousand or a hundred years of the lesser, Chapter 9 sets evil and good as gradual and inescapable forces. Both chapters work by terse comparative judgement, but Chapter 8's figure is *one-vs-many* while Chapter 9's figure is *paired-and-ripening*.
- **structural-parallel** → Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 6 — a Sanskrit philosophical pair on the gradual cultivation of mind; the sixth chapter of the Gita with its verses on the slow steadying of attention and the figure of one who has gathered virtue gradually, Dhammapada 9 with verses 121–122 on the gradual filling of the pot drop by drop. The figure of incremental moral cultivation is held in both texts.
