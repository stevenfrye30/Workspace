# Mundaka Upanishad, Book 1, Part 1

Book 1, Part 1 of the Mundaka Upanishad — the First Khanda of the First Mundaka — opens the text with a short lineage of teaching: Brahma told the knowledge of Brahman, the foundation of all knowledge, to his eldest son Atharvan; Atharvan told it to Angir; Angir to Satyavaha Bharadvaga; Bharadvaga to Angiras. Then the householder Saunaka approaches Angiras respectfully and asks the question that frames the rest of the Upanishad — *kasmin nu bhagavo vijñāte sarvam idam vijñātam bhavati*: "Sir, what is that through which, if it is known, everything else becomes known?" Angiras answers that two kinds of knowledge must be known: the lower (*apara*), which the Khanda lists as the four Vedas and their six ancillary disciplines, and the higher (*para*), by which the Indestructible is apprehended. The remainder of the Khanda turns to the Indestructible itself — that which has no family and no caste, no eyes and no ears, no hands and no feet, eternal and all-pervading — and to the figure of the world arising from it as threads from a spider, plants from the earth, hairs from a human body.

## Context

The Mundaka Upanishad is one of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads — a small group of texts traditionally received as the philosophical core of the late Vedic literature. It is associated with the Atharvaveda branch of the Vedic corpus and is composed in Sanskrit verse. Modern scholarship places its composition somewhat later than the Katha, in roughly the fourth to second century BCE; the principal Upanishads as a group are dated to the centuries on either side of the Buddha. The text is anonymous; the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship. The name *Mundaka* — "shaven" — is traditionally taken to refer to those who, having received the teaching, are shorn of error.

Within the Müller volume that holds this entity, the Mundaka begins immediately after the close of the Katha Upanishad. Where the Katha is built on the long dialogue between Yama and Nachiketas — six vallis arranged as two books — the Mundaka opens on a different dialogic frame: a teaching lineage descending from Brahma to Angiras, and a single inquiring householder, Saunaka, who comes with a single question. The text has three *mundakas* (books), each with two *khandas* (parts), for six parts in all. Book 1, Part 1 sets the inquiry; Book 1, Part 2 answers from inside the lower (sacrificial) knowledge before the higher knowledge is given in the second and third Mundakas.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the Khanda is built on a distinction the rest of the Upanishad will develop. The two kinds of knowledge — higher and lower — are not opposed in argument but laid down side by side, named in turn, and given each its proper field. The lower is listed in detail (the four Vedas, then the six *vedangas*: phonetics, ceremonial, grammar, etymology, metre, astronomy); the higher is given a single line — that by which the Indestructible is apprehended — and then unfolded through the verses on what cannot be seized by the senses. Reading the Khanda with attention to the asymmetry — the long list answering to the short line — recovers the form the teaching has.

Second, the Khanda is structured by genealogy and inquiry. The opening verses do not begin with doctrine; they begin with a chain of names, each transmitting the knowledge of Brahman to the next, ending with Saunaka's approach to Angiras with fuel in hand and a question on his lips. The form is the form of the Upanishadic encounter: knowledge passes from teacher to disciple in a chain, and the dialogue begins when the disciple asks well. Reading the Khanda with the genealogical preface held alongside the substantive answer that follows, rather than skipped over, keeps the form the Mundaka gives the opening of its inquiry.

## The text

The text is held in the Archive library under `upanishads-muller-part2`, chapter 5 (Müller translation, 1884 — *The Sacred Books of the East*, Volume XV; via SacredTexts.com). Library chapter 5 was already shared across four Archive entities for the Katha Upanishad (Book 1, Part 3 and Book 2, Parts 1–3); the Mundaka begins inside the same library chapter, immediately after the close of the Katha. Book 1, Part 1 of the Mundaka occupies passages `5.77` through `5.89`. Passage `5.77` is the "Mundaka-Upanishad" collection header, `5.78` is the "First Mundaka" book header, and `5.79` is the "First Khanda" part header. Passages `5.80` through `5.89` carry the nine numbered verses of the Khanda, with verse 5 split across passages `5.84` and `5.85` at the page break in Müller's 1884 print. Passage `5.90` is the "Second Khanda" header that opens Book 1, Part 2 and is not part of this entity. Passage `5.76`, immediately before the Mundaka begins, is a page-break artifact (page references in square brackets) carried over from the close of the Katha Upanishad. Nine verses; about eight minutes' reading.

## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 4 (Jnana Karma Sanyasa Yoga) — paired Sanskrit passages that open with a lineage of teaching; the Gita's Krishna opens Chapter 4 by naming the imperishable yoga he taught to Vivasvan, Vivasvan to Manu, Manu to Ikshvaku, descending through royal sages until lost and now told again, the Mundaka's First Khanda opens with the lineage descending from Brahma through Atharvan, Angir, Satyavaha Bharadvaga, and Angiras to Saunaka — the same form, the lineage of transmission preceding the substantive teaching, in the same Sanskrit philosophical line.
- **structural-parallel** → Katha Upanishad, Book 1, Part 2 — paired Upanishadic openings on a foundational distinction; the Katha's Second Valli opens with the distinction between the good (*shreyas*) and the pleasant (*preyas*), the wise choosing the good and the foolish the pleasant, the Mundaka's First Khanda opens with the distinction between the higher (*para*) and the lower (*apara*) knowledge — paired structures in which the rest of the text develops what the opening pair has set down.
- **structural-parallel** → Analects, Book 1 (Xue Er) — paired short openings that foreground learning and the relation of teacher to student; the Analects opens with the line that learning and timely practice is its own pleasure and with verses on the right disposition of the student, the Mundaka opens with Saunaka approaching Angiras with fuel in hand and the question that frames the rest of the text — both texts beginning by placing the inquirer in the right relation to the teacher before any substantive teaching is given.
