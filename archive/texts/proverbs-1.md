# Proverbs, Chapter 1

Chapter 1 of the Book of Proverbs is the opening of the principal wisdom book of the Hebrew Bible. After a seven-verse opening that names Solomon, states the book's purpose, and gives the book's motto, the chapter divides into two long speeches. The first is a father's address to his son, warning him not to be drawn in by violent companions. The second is the figure of Wisdom herself, personified as a woman, calling aloud in the public streets and at the city gates, summoning the simple to listen and naming what comes to those who refuse. The chapter sets the book's principal voices: the household teacher, and Wisdom in public speech.

## Context

Proverbs is one of the three principal wisdom books of the Hebrew Bible, alongside Job and Ecclesiastes. Of the three, it is the most direct and the most extensively instructional: thirty-one chapters of teaching, much of it in the two-line aphoristic form that has come to define the genre in English. The book carries Solomon's name in its opening verse, and tradition long associated it with him; modern scholarship treats the Solomonic attribution as a literary frame and dates the compilation across a long span — older proverbial material gathered, edited, and prefaced over several centuries — with the present book reaching its form during the Persian and early Hellenistic period (roughly fifth to third centuries BCE).

The book is composed of several distinct collections, marked off by their own headings (Proverbs 1:1, 10:1, 22:17, 24:23, 25:1, 30:1, 31:1). Chapters 1–9 form the first collection: a sustained prologue of long instructional speeches, distinct in form from the short paired aphorisms that fill chapters 10–29. Chapter 1 stands at the head of this prologue. After the seven-verse opening that names Solomon, states the book's purpose, and gives the book's motto ("the fear of the LORD is the beginning of knowledge," 1:7), the chapter contains two complete speeches: a father's instruction to his son (1:8–19), and the public address of personified Wisdom (1:20–33). The two voices recur, in alternation, through the rest of the prologue.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the chapter moves between two distinct voices in two distinct settings. Verses 8–19 are the father's voice, addressed to his son in the household, warning against being drawn in by men who lay in wait for blood. Verses 20–33 shift to Wisdom personified as a woman — no longer in the household, but outside in the streets and at the city gates, calling aloud to anyone who will listen. Reading the chapter with the change of setting visible — household instruction in the first speech, public address in the second — recovers a structural feature of the book's prologue.

Second, both speeches turn on what their hearers do or fail to do. The father instructs and warns; Wisdom invites, then names the consequences for those who have refused her — "they shall call upon me, but I will not answer; they shall seek me earnestly, but they shall not find me." Reading the closing verses with attention to the shift from invitation to consequence, rather than smoothing the two into a single moral exhortation, keeps the form the chapter has.
