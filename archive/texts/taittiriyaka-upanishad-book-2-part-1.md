# Taittiriyaka Upanishad, Book 2, Part 1

Book 2, Part 1 of the Taittiriyaka Upanishad — the First Anuvaka of the Brahmanandavalli, the Second Valli — opens the second of the three vallis of the Taittiriyaka. The Valli is named for *ānanda* (bliss) and is the most condensed of the three; where the Sikshavalli unfolded across twelve anuvakas of school-lesson and prayer, the Brahmanandavalli works in shorter prose passages punctuated by quoted slokas, and the literary mode shifts accordingly. The opening of the Anuvaka is set by a new peace mantra — *saha nāv avatu, saha nau bhunaktu, saha vīryaṁ karavāvahai, tejasvi nāv adhītam astu, mā vidviṣāvahai* — "may it protect us both, may it enjoy us both, may we acquire strength together, may our knowledge become bright, may we never quarrel" — sealed by the threefold *Peace*. The body of the Anuvaka then begins with the line *brahmavid āpnoti param* — he who knows Brahman attains the highest — and immediately gives the defining sloka the Anuvaka has been most cited for: *satyam jñānam anantam brahma* — Brahman is the real, the conscious, the infinite, hidden in the depth of the heart, in the highest ether; the one who knows it there enjoys all blessings, at one with the omniscient Brahman. The Anuvaka then turns to the emanation that frames the rest of the Valli. From that Self sprang ether; from ether, air; from air, fire; from fire, water; from water, earth; from earth, herbs; from herbs, food; from food, man — the cascade ending in the man consisting of the essence of food (*annamaya*). The Anuvaka maps this man: head, right arm, left arm, trunk, seat. The closing line is the pointer that the Brahmanandavalli will use throughout — *tad apy eṣa ślokaḥ* — "on this there is also the following sloka" — turning the reader toward the Second Anuvaka.

## Context

The Taittiriyaka Upanishad is one of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads — a small group of texts traditionally received as the philosophical core of the late Vedic literature. It belongs to the Krishna Yajurveda branch of the Vedic corpus and to the Taittiriya school of that branch; the Upanishad sits inside the Taittiriya Aranyaka. Modern scholarship places it among the older prose Upanishads, in roughly the sixth to fifth century BCE — earlier than the verse Upanishads of the Katha and Mundaka. The text is anonymous; the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship.

The Taittiriyaka is structured in three vallis, each given its own opening peace mantra and its own internal subdivisions. Book 1 — the Sikshavalli — was the chapter on *śikṣā*, on pronunciation and the school-lesson, and its twelve anuvakas closed in the previous Archive entity. Book 2 — the Brahmanandavalli — is the chapter on *ānanda*, on the bliss of Brahman, and is the section in which the *pañca-kośa* teaching unfolds: the cascading sequence of selves consisting of food, breath, mind, knowledge, and bliss. Book 3 — the Bhriguvalli — is the dialogue between Bhrigu and his father Varuna, in which the same five sheaths are traversed in a different mode. The First Anuvaka of the Brahmanandavalli sets the framing peace mantra, gives the defining sloka of Brahman, opens the emanation cascade, and arrives at the first of the five sheaths — the *annamaya*, the self consisting of food.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the Anuvaka is denser than the Sikshavalli's anuvakas. The Sikshavalli's instruction was given in lists, refrains, and short imperatives; the Brahmanandavalli's First Anuvaka is given in continuous prose with embedded slokas. The defining line — *satyam jñānam anantam brahma* — is set inside a longer prose paragraph rather than offered as a separate verse, and the emanation that follows runs as a single cascading sentence rather than as a numbered list. Reading the Anuvaka with attention to the change of density — the same Upanishad now working in a different rhythm — recovers the form the Brahmanandavalli has and the place its compression holds inside the wider text.

Second, the Anuvaka anchors its instruction in pointers rather than in arguments. The opening pointer is *brahmavid āpnoti param* — "the knower of Brahman attains the highest" — and the closing pointer is *tad apy eṣa ślokaḥ* — "on this there is also the following sloka." The Anuvaka does not develop a position; it places a defining sloka, opens an emanation, names the first of the sheaths, and points the reader on. Reading the Anuvaka with attention to where it points rather than to what it asserts keeps the form the Brahmanandavalli is preparing — a sequence in which each Anuvaka adds one more level of the inward unfolding the Valli traces from food through breath, mind, knowledge, and bliss.

## The text

The text is held in the Archive library under `upanishads-muller-part2`, chapter 5 (Müller translation, 1884 — *The Sacred Books of the East*, Volume XV; via SacredTexts.com). Library chapter 5 is shared across twenty-two Archive entities — four parts of the Katha Upanishad, the six parts of the Mundaka Upanishad, and the twelve anuvakas of the Sikshavalli (Book 1) of the Taittiriyaka — and continues here into the Brahmanandavalli (Book 2) without break in the chapter. Book 2, Part 1 of the Taittiriyaka — the entity here — occupies passages `5.225` through `5.233`. Passage `5.225` is the "Second Valli" book header; passage `5.226` is the subtitle of the Second Valli, "Or, the Chapter on Ananda (Bliss)"; passage `5.227` carries the *Saha navavatu* peace mantra that opens the Brahmanandavalli, with its own footnote reference `[*1]`; passage `5.228` is the "First Anuvaka" part header. Passages `5.229` through `5.233` carry the body of the Anuvaka in Müller's prose presentation, without verse-numbered markers — the Brahmanandavalli does not number its verses in the same way the Sikshavalli did. The defining sloka *satyam jñānam anantam brahma* sits inside passage `5.230` set off by Müller in single quotation marks; the emanation cascade and the anatomical mapping of the *annamaya* are split across passages `5.231` and `5.232` where Müller's editorial paragraphing interrupts the prose; passage `5.233` is the closing pointer "On this there is also the following Sloka:" — Müller's framing for the verse that opens the Second Anuvaka. Passage `5.234` is the "Second Anuvaka" header that opens Book 2, Part 2 of the Taittiriyaka and is not part of this entity. The library chapter continues past this entity into the rest of the Taittiriyaka and the remaining Upanishads of the volume. About four minutes' reading.

## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Taittiriyaka Upanishad, Book 1, Part 12 — paired closure-and-opening Anuvakas across the boundary between the two vallis; the Twelfth Anuvaka of the Sikshavalli closed the First Valli by returning to its opening peace recitation in fulfilled tense, the First Anuvaka of the Brahmanandavalli opens the Second Valli with a new peace mantra (*Saha navavatu*) and immediately enters a different literary register — denser prose, embedded slokas, the defining line on Brahman as real, conscious, and infinite. The transition is held inside one continuous Upanishadic body, with the form changing while the text remains the same.
- **structural-parallel** → Mundaka Upanishad, Book 2, Part 1 — paired Sanskrit passages on the emanation of the elements from the source; the Mundaka's First Khanda of the Second Mundaka holds the cascade *from him is born breath, mind, and all organs of sense, ether, air, light, water, and the earth, the support of all* (Mundaka 2.1.3), the Taittiriyaka's First Anuvaka of the Brahmanandavalli holds the closely related cascade *from that Self sprang ether; from ether, air; from air, fire; from fire, water; from water, earth; from earth, herbs; from herbs, food; from food, man* — paired moments in the late-Vedic literature where the same emanation is given in two close vocabularies, the Mundaka in a single sweep and the Taittiriyaka in step-by-step descent that arrives at the human body.
- **structural-parallel** → Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 15 (Purushottama Yoga) — paired Sanskrit passages on Brahman held image-rich and condensed; the Gita's Chapter 15 names the supreme Person seated in the heart of all beings as the source of memory, knowledge, and discrimination (Gita 15.15) and identifies him as the digestive fire that consumes the food of all that lives (Gita 15.14), the Taittiriyaka's First Anuvaka of the Brahmanandavalli names Brahman as *real, conscious, infinite, hidden in the depth of the heart* and traces the emanation that ends in the food-self of the body. Both texts hold the recognition of Brahman in compressed image rather than in extended argument, with the heart, the food, and the body placed at the centre of the recognition.
