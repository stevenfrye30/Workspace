# Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 18 (Moksha Sannyasa Yoga)

Chapter 18 of the Bhagavad Gita — the *Moksha Sannyasa Yoga*, conventionally translated "The Yoga of Liberation through Renunciation" (Arnold's translation gives the chapter the parallel name "The Book of Religion by Deliverance and Renunciation") — is the longest chapter of the Gita and the dialogue's concluding synthesis. Arjuna opens with a final question: what is the difference between renunciation (*sannyasa*) and relinquishment (*tyaga*)? Krishna's reply gathers the work of the previous seventeen chapters and recasts it. The relinquishment of the fruits of action is the form of renunciation that does not require withdrawal; the threefold pattern of the *gunas* is applied yet more widely — across knowledge, action, agent, understanding, firmness, and happiness; the duties of the four *varnas* are named with the principle that one's own duty imperfectly performed is better than another's well; the closing verses offer Krishna's "supreme word" — abandon all dharmas and take refuge in him alone — and Arjuna's response: his delusion is destroyed, he will act. The chapter ends in Sanjaya's voice, the frame narrator who opened the Gita in Chapter 1, returning the dialogue to the war camp where the rest of the Mahabharata waits.

## Context

The Bhagavad Gita is a 700-verse philosophical poem in eighteen chapters, set inside Book VI (the *Bhishma Parva*) of the Mahabharata. Most scholars place its present form between roughly the second century BCE and the second century CE — a layered composition that took shape during the long growth of the epic from an older bardic core. The traditional attribution is to Vyasa, the legendary compiler of the Mahabharata; the historical authorship is unknown.

Chapter 18 concludes the Gita. At seventy-eight verses, it is the longest chapter and the only one that recapitulates rather than introduces. The earlier classifications — *kshetra* and *kshetrajna* (Chapter 13), the three *gunas* (Chapter 14), the doctrine of the three persons (Chapter 15), the divine and demonic endowments (Chapter 16), the threefold treatment of faith and practice (Chapter 17) — return in shorter form, often applied to new domains. The chapter is structurally a long single discourse from Krishna with brief framing exchanges: Arjuna's opening question (18.1), Krishna's sustained answer (18.2–66), Krishna's closing verses on the teaching's transmission (18.67–71), Arjuna's brief reply confirming his resolve (18.72–73), and Sanjaya's closing report to the blind king Dhritirashtra (18.74–78), which returns the dialogue to the frame established in Chapter 1. The Gita ends with Sanjaya's declaration that wherever Krishna and Arjuna are, there is glory, victory, prosperity, and justice.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the chapter is recapitulation, not new teaching. Almost every classification or distinction returns: action and renunciation, the three *gunas* applied to many domains, the duties of one's place, the question of who is truly the agent, the disposition that holds steady. Reading Chapter 18 with the awareness that the chapter is gathering and re-stating rather than advancing, and noticing where each earlier theme returns in compressed form, recovers the chapter's particular work as the dialogue's close. The chapter is not a new discourse; it is the previous seventeen chapters held in summary.

Second, the chapter ends in a different voice than it began. The opening question is Arjuna's; the long middle is Krishna's; the close is Arjuna's brief acknowledgment that his delusion is gone. But the final five verses (18.74–78) are Sanjaya's — the frame narrator who spoke the Gita's first words at 1.1, addressed to Dhritirashtra, who has been listening to the report all along. Reading the closing verses with the awareness that the dialogue is returning to its frame — and that the Gita ends where it began, in Sanjaya's report to the blind king — recovers the work's closing form.

## The text

The text is held in the Archive library under `bhagavad-gita`, chapter 18 (Edwin Arnold's verse translation, *The Song Celestial*, 1885 — Project Gutenberg edition). Arnold's rendering of this chapter has a feature absent from the previous seventeen: he omits a number of shlokas he considers interpolated (a verse around the middle, and — per his own note — "the concluding shlokas, as of very doubtful authenticity"), and he appends a sequence of editorial footnotes after the chapter. The library's seventy-eight Sanskrit shlokas correspond, accordingly, to thirty-one English verse-paragraphs of translated text (passages `18.1`–`18.31`), a closing colophon line marking the end of the chapter and the Gita as a whole (`18.32`), and a sequence of thirty-seven editorial footnotes (`18.33`–`18.69`) that follow the colophon. Reading the translated text alone — passages `18.1` through `18.32` — gives the chapter as Arnold rendered it, with his omissions in place. The footnotes are bibliographically useful but are not part of the Gita itself. A modern prose translation by the International Gita Society is held in the library as a parallel translation. About forty minutes' reading.
