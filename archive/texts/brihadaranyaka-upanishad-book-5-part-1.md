# Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 5, Part 1

Book 5, Part 1 of the Brihadaranyaka Upanishad — the First Brahmana of the Fifth Adhyaya — opens the Adhyaya in a frame fully reset from the Fourth. The Fourth Adhyaya had run across the Janaka–Yajnavalkya audience, the Yajnavalkya–Maitreyi household-renunciation, and the closing *vamsha* lineage from Pautimashya back to Brahman Svayambhu. The Fifth Adhyaya opens with no king, no priest, no wife, no contest, no lineage, and no dialogue. The form is a substantive aphorism given in the third person, opening directly into the Brihadaranyaka's most-cited paradox of the relation between the invisible and the visible Brahman.

The Adhyaya's opening verse — verse 1 of the First Brahmana — is the **Purnam** mantra. *That (the invisible Brahman) is full, this (the visible Brahman) is full. This full (visible Brahman) proceeds from that full (invisible Brahman). On grasping the fulness of this full (visible Brahman) there is left that full (invisible Brahman).* The form holds the paradox of fullness across two terms — *that* (*adah*) and *this* (*idam*) — naming the invisible Brahman and the visible Brahman, holding both as full, holding the visible as proceeding from the invisible, and holding the invisible as remaining full when the visible's fulness has been grasped. The mantra is one of the Upanishadic literature's most-quoted threshold passages and has been received in the Vedanta tradition as the Brihadaranyaka's principal *śānti* (peace) mantra; the Archive preserves it here in Müller's translation in its place at the open of the Fifth Adhyaya rather than as a separate liturgical unit.

The closing passage of the Brahmana at `5.1184` holds the Om-as-ether-as-Brahman triple identification, the first named teacher of the new Adhyaya, and the substantive seal on knowledge. *Om (is) ether, (is) Brahman.* The line gives the three-fold equation in compressed form, with the syllable *Om* identified with *ether* (*akasa*) and with *Brahman* — the same identification that the Chandogya Upanishad and the Mundaka Upanishad will hold at their own opening passages. The named teacher follows: *'There is the old ether (the invisible), and the (visible) ether of the atmosphere,' thus said Kauravyayaniputra.* The naming of Kauravyayaniputra is the Adhyaya's first named voice, and the substantive content of his line — the distinction between the *old* (invisible, primordial) ether and the *visible* ether of the atmosphere — pairs with the verse-1 mantra's distinction between the invisible and the visible Brahman. The two distinctions hold the same two-fold form across the same two passages, with verse 1 in the third-person aphoristic voice and verse 2 in the named-teacher voice. The Brahmana closes on the Om-as-Veda identification: *This (the Om) is the Veda (the means of knowledge), thus the Brahmanas know. One knows through it all that has to be known.* The substantive close is the knowledge-sufficient form — *one knows through it all that has to be known* — held without further commentary.

## Context

The Brihadaranyaka Upanishad is one of the two earliest of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads, sharing that position with the Chandogya. It belongs to the Shukla Yajurveda branch of the Vedic corpus and forms the closing portion of the Shatapatha Brahmana — the *Aranyaka* in its name marks it as a forest-text recited at the late stages of Vedic study. Modern scholarship places the Brihadaranyaka in roughly the eighth to sixth century BCE, prior to the Buddha; the text is anonymous, and the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship. The text is the longest of the principal Upanishads and is typically structured in six *adhyayas* (chapters), each containing several *brahmanas* (sections).

Book 5, Part 1 holds three of the most distinctive features of the Brihadaranyaka's Adhyaya-opening forms in concentrated form. The first is the radical compression. The Brahmana is the shortest of the Brihadaranyaka so far — two body passages, one numbered verse — and it gives the Adhyaya's opening teaching not through dialogue, narrative, contest, or sustained exposition but through an aphoristic mantra and a single named-teacher attribution. The Adhyaya's prior closings — the death of Sakalya and the slokas closing Book 3, the kingdom-and-self surrender and lineage to Brahman closing Book 4 — had been long and richly worked. The Adhyaya's opening here is the opposite: a single mantric paradox followed by a compressed Om-as-ether-as-Brahman identification.

The second is the Purnam mantra itself. The verse — *purnam adah, purnam idam, purnat purnam udacyate, purnasya purnam adaya, purnam evavasishyate* in the underlying Sanskrit — is one of the Upanishadic literature's most-quoted statements of the relation between the unmanifest and manifest Brahman. Müller's translation gives it through the parenthetical glosses *(the invisible Brahman)* and *(the visible Brahman)*, with the verb *udacyate* rendered *proceeds from* and the closing *avasishyate* rendered *there is left*. The mantra holds the paradox: that the visible Brahman is full and proceeds from the invisible Brahman, and that when the fullness of the visible has been grasped, the fullness of the invisible still remains. The Vedanta tradition has read the verse as a mantric statement of the doctrine that no diminution of Brahman occurs in the production of the manifest world; the Archive preserves Müller's translation without smoothing the parentheticals into a single rendering.

The third is the named teacher Kauravyayaniputra. The Adhyaya's first named teacher is given on a single short line at `5.1184` — *thus said Kauravyayaniputra* — and the substantive content of his teaching is the distinction between the *old* invisible ether and the *visible* ether of the atmosphere. The naming-form is unusual: Kauravyayaniputra is given without a dialogic frame, without a question, without a substantive setting beyond the Brahmana's two-passage opening. The line is closer to a *iti vacanāt* (*thus has been said*) citation than to a full named-teacher dialogue, and the form sets the Adhyaya's procedure as compressed teaching held through brief named attributions rather than through sustained dialogues. The Adhyaya's later Brahmanas will continue in this compressed form across many short Brahmanas; the First Brahmana opens the Adhyaya's procedure with the shortest possible instance of it.

The closing form is the Adhyaya's first knowledge-sufficient seal. *One knows through it all that has to be known.* The line names the syllable *Om* — through which one knows — as the means by which all knowable things are known. The form holds the Adhyaya's opening doctrine as already complete: the threshold mantra and the Om-as-ether-as-Brahman triple have given the substantive teaching, and the closing seal names the teaching as already sufficient. No further dialogue is required; no challenger is silenced; no king bows; no priest departs. The Adhyaya opens with the form of the threshold complete and proceeds into the rest of its content from this point.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the Brahmana's compression is its form. Reading the four passages at full length, attending to the radical brevity rather than expanding the content into doctrinal statement, recovers the Adhyaya's literary procedure. The Adhyaya's prior Brahmanas of the Brihadaranyaka had worked their substance through long dialogues, sustained question-and-answer, multi-step ladders, and recurring templates; the First Brahmana of the Fifth Adhyaya gives the Adhyaya's opening through one mantric verse and one Om-as-ether-as-Brahman identification. Reading the Brahmana with its brevity preserved — neither expanded into a doctrine of two-forms-of-Brahman nor flattened into a generic Vedantic statement — recovers the form the Upanishad has when it opens an Adhyaya in its shortest possible threshold-mode.

Second, the relation between verse 1 and the closing passage should be read as parallel rather than sequential. Verse 1 holds the paradox of the invisible-and-visible Brahman; the closing passage holds the parallel distinction between the *old* (invisible) ether and the *visible* ether of the atmosphere, attributed to Kauravyayaniputra. The two distinctions sit on two consecutive passages with the same two-fold form, and reading them as paired — the Brahman-paradox and the ether-paradox held alongside each other — rather than as a single doctrinal sequence recovers the Brahmana's structure. The Adhyaya's first named teacher's saying functions as a paired commentary on the verse-1 mantra: the same two-fold form held in the substantive register of the ether and the Brahman.

## The text

The text is held in the Archive library under `upanishads-muller-part2`, chapter 5 (Müller translation, 1884 — *The Sacred Books of the East*, Volume XV; via SacredTexts.com). Library chapter 5 is shared across sixty-five Archive entities — four parts of the Katha Upanishad, the six parts of the Mundaka Upanishad, twenty-eight anuvakas of the Taittiriyaka, the eleven Brahmanas of the Brihadaranyaka's First and Second Adhyayas, all nine Brahmanas of the Third Adhyaya, and all six Brahmanas of the Fourth Adhyaya — and continues here into the First Brahmana of the Fifth Adhyaya. Book 5, Part 1 of the Brihadaranyaka — the entity here — occupies passages `5.1181` through `5.1184`. Two structural headers open the entity:

- **Passage `5.1181`:** "Fifth Adhyaya" header — the major structural break between the Fourth Adhyaya (which closed on the lineage to Brahman Svayambhu) and the Fifth.
- **Passage `5.1182`:** "First Brahmana" header carrying its own footnote reference `[*1]`. Müller's note glosses the substantive content of the Brahmana as the *Purnam* mantra of the Brihadaranyaka.

Passages `5.1183` through `5.1184` carry the body in Müller's prose presentation across two short passages, with the verse-numbered marker at `1.` (`5.1183`) only. The closing passage at `5.1184` does not carry a verse-marker; the substantive content of the Brahmana is held across two passages, the first numbered as verse 1 and the second running as unnumbered continuation.

The Brahmana contains several boundary features that should be preserved honestly:

- **Adhyaya-and-Brahmana double-header at the open.** Passages `5.1181` and `5.1182` carry the two structural headers in immediate succession. The "Fifth Adhyaya" header at `5.1181` carries no footnote and no preface; the "First Brahmana [*1]" header at `5.1182` carries footnote `[*1]` immediately on the marker. The two headers occupy two adjacent passages without intervening text; the substantive content of the Brahmana begins only at `5.1183`.
- **Verse-1 with two footnotes and OCR irregularity.** Passage `5.1183` carries verse 1 — the Purnam mantra — with footnote `[*2]` on the line *this (the visible Brahman) is full* and footnote `[*3]` on *there is left that full*. The two notes attach at the substantive turning-points of the mantra. A stray closing brace `}` is preserved in the digitized source between *full* and the footnote-marker `[*2]`: *this (the visible Brahman) is full} [*2]*. The brace is an OCR transcription artifact carried over from Müller's volume; the substance of the verse is unaffected and the irregularity is preserved as found.
- **Closing passage with Om-as-Brahman footnote and named teacher.** Passage `5.1184` carries the Om-as-ether-as-Brahman triple identification with footnote `[*4]` on *Brahman*. Müller's note glosses the three-fold identification at its third term. The named teacher Kauravyayaniputra is given on a single short line — *thus said Kauravyayaniputra* — with no further commentary or footnote on the name. The Brahmana closes on the substantive line *One knows through it all that has to be known* without a verse-marker, without a *held his peace* form, and without a closing seal.

Passage `5.1185` is the "Second Brahmana" header that opens Book 5, Part 2 of the Brihadaranyaka and is not part of this entity. The library chapter continues past this entity into the rest of the Fifth Adhyaya and the remaining portions of the volume; the Brihadaranyaka extends well beyond the boundaries of library chapter 5. ## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 4, Part 6 — paired closing-and-opening Brahmanas at the boundary between the Fourth and Fifth Adhyayas of the Brihadaranyaka, with the Adhyaya transition working two distinct closure-and-opening forms. The Sixth Brahmana of the Fourth Adhyaya closes the Adhyaya in a *vamsha* (lineage) record across sixty named teachers running from Pautimashya backward to Brahman Svayambhu — the form is enumerative, transmissional, and historical. The First Brahmana of the Fifth Adhyaya opens the Adhyaya with the Purnam mantra at verse 1 and the Om-as-ether-as-Brahman triple identification at the closing passage — the form is aphoristic, mantric, and substantive. The two consecutive Brahmanas hold two distinct registers of Vedic continuity at the Adhyaya transition: the human teachers' transmission record and the substantive threshold mantra of the new Adhyaya. The Adhyaya boundary works as a register-shift from lineage to mantra rather than as a thematic continuation.
- **structural-parallel** → Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 2, Part 3 — paired Brihadaranyaka loci on the two-forms doctrine of Brahman. The Third Brahmana of the Second Adhyaya opens with *There are two forms of Brahman, the material and the immaterial, the mortal and the immortal, the solid and the fluid, sat and tya — sat-tya, true*, and works the two-forms doctrine across cosmic and bodily mappings. The First Brahmana of the Fifth Adhyaya opens with the Purnam mantra holding the same two-fold form across *that (the invisible Brahman)* and *this (the visible Brahman)* — both held as full, with the visible proceeding from the invisible and the invisible remaining full when the visible's fulness has been grasped. The two Brahmanas hold the two-forms doctrine of Brahman in two different registers — the Second Adhyaya through extended cosmic-bodily mapping, the Fifth Adhyaya through compressed mantric paradox — and the closing of the Second Brahmana on the *neti neti* line (*the teaching of Brahman by "No, no!" — for there is nothing else higher than this*) sits in textual relation to the Fifth Brahmana's compressed close on knowledge sufficient through the syllable Om.
- **structural-parallel** → Mundaka Upanishad, Book 2, Part 2 — paired Sanskrit Upanishadic teachings on Om as the substantive identification of Brahman. The Second Khanda of the Second Mundaka holds Om as the bow of meditation through which the soul is shot like an arrow into the target of Brahman, and works the doctrine of Om as the means of meditation toward the immortal. The First Brahmana of the Fifth Adhyaya of the Brihadaranyaka holds Om as the triple identification *Om (is) ether, (is) Brahman* and as the Veda itself — *This (the Om) is the Veda (the means of knowledge), thus the Brahmanas know. One knows through it all that has to be known.* Both Upanishadic loci hold Om as the substantive identification of Brahman and as the means by which Brahman is known, with the Mundaka working the doctrine through the bow-and-arrow figure and the Brihadaranyaka working it through the Veda-and-knowledge identification.
