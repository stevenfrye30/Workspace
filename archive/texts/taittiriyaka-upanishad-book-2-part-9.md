# Taittiriyaka Upanishad, Book 2, Part 9

Book 2, Part 9 of the Taittiriyaka Upanishad — the Ninth Anuvaka of the Brahmanandavalli — closes the Valli. The Anuvaka is short and gathers what the rest of the Valli has prepared. The opening line is a near-repetition of the sloka that opened the Fourth Anuvaka, returning here in slightly altered form to bracket the body of the Valli between two appearances of the same recognition: *yato vāco nivartante, aprāpya manasā saha; ānandaṁ brahmaṇo vidvān, na bibheti kadācana* — *he who knows the bliss of that Brahman, from whence all speech, with the mind, turns away unable to reach it, he fears nothing.* The closing recognition then follows in compact prose. The knower *does not distress himself with the thought, why did I not do what is good? why did I do what is bad?* — *etad ubhe etam evam vid manute*, *he who thus knows these two, frees himself; he who knows both, frees himself.* The Anuvaka closes the Valli with the formal seal *ity upaniṣat* — *this is the Upanishad.*

## Context

The Taittiriyaka Upanishad is one of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads — a small group of texts traditionally received as the philosophical core of the late Vedic literature. It belongs to the Krishna Yajurveda branch of the Vedic corpus and to the Taittiriya school of that branch; the Upanishad sits inside the Taittiriya Aranyaka. Modern scholarship places it among the older prose Upanishads, in roughly the sixth to fifth century BCE — earlier than the verse Upanishads of the Katha and Mundaka. The text is anonymous; the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship.

Book 2, Part 9 closes the Brahmanandavalli, the Second Valli of the Taittiriyaka. The Valli unfolded across nine anuvakas: an opening peace mantra and the *satyam jñānam anantam brahma* sloka in the First Anuvaka; the recursive descent through the five sheaths from food through breath, mind, knowledge, and bliss in the Second through Fifth Anuvakas; the post-recursive turn to pupil-questions and the *so 'kāmayata* creation formula in the Sixth; the *raso vai saḥ* and *abhaya* recognitions in the Seventh; the *ānanda-mīmāṁsā* bliss-calculation in the Eighth; and now the Ninth Anuvaka closing the Valli with the return of the bracketing sloka, the recognition that the knower is freed from regret about deeds, and the formal seal. The line that follows the seal — the "Third Valli, or the Chapter of Bhrigu" header — opens Book 3 of the Taittiriyaka, where the same recognitions will be approached through the dialogue of Bhrigu Varuni and his father Varuna.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the Anuvaka closes the Valli through return rather than through new doctrine. The sloka of the Fourth Anuvaka — *yato vāco nivartante* — returns now in a slightly altered closing form. The five sheaths that the recursive descent unfolded and that the bliss-calculation gathered in its closing line are not named again; the recognition reached through them is held in two short statements about freedom from regret. The Anuvaka does not summarize; it brackets. Reading the Ninth Anuvaka with the Fourth held alongside it, attending to where the language of the opening sloka returns and where it shifts, recovers the closing form the Brahmanandavalli has — body of teaching framed between two appearances of the same line.

Second, the recognition the Anuvaka closes on is given as freedom rather than as knowledge. The knower does not analyze the past; he does not weigh his good deeds against his bad; he does not distress himself. The line *etad ubhe etam evam vid manute* — *he who thus knows these two, frees himself* — places the recognition in the absence of regret rather than in the presence of an account. Reading the Anuvaka with the recognition held as a release rather than as a thesis keeps the form the Brahmanandavalli has at its close.

## The text

The text is held in the Archive library under `upanishads-muller-part2`, chapter 5 (Müller translation, 1884 — *The Sacred Books of the East*, Volume XV; via SacredTexts.com). Library chapter 5 is shared across thirty Archive entities — four parts of the Katha Upanishad, the six parts of the Mundaka Upanishad, the twelve anuvakas of the Sikshavalli (Book 1) of the Taittiriyaka, and the First through Eighth Anuvakas of the Brahmanandavalli (Book 2, Parts 1 through 8). The Ninth Anuvaka does not follow the Eighth Anuvaka directly in the digitized library: between the close of the Eighth Anuvaka at passage `5.282` and the header of the Ninth at passage `5.326`, the library carries a contiguous run of editorial apparatus from Müller's printed volume — a comparison table aligning the bliss-cascade across the Taittiriya, the Satapatha-Brahmana, and the Brihadaranyaka, followed by Müller's prose commentary on the cosmography of the cascade — across passages `5.283` through `5.325`. The editorial apparatus is part of Müller's edition, not of the Upanishad text itself; the sacred text resumes at passage `5.326`. Book 2, Part 9 of the Taittiriyaka — the entity here — occupies passages `5.326` through `5.328`. Passage `5.326` is the "Ninth Anuvaka" header and carries its own footnote reference `[*1]`. Passage `5.327` carries the closing sloka in Müller's quotation marks, with footnote reference `[*2]`. Passage `5.328` carries the closing recognition on freedom from regret and the formal seal *this is the Upanishad*, with footnote references `[*3]` and `[*4]`. Passage `5.329` is the "Third Valli" header that opens Book 3 of the Taittiriyaka (the Bhriguvalli) and is not part of this entity. The library chapter continues past this entity into the rest of the Taittiriyaka and the remaining Upanishads of the volume. About one minute's reading.

## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Taittiriyaka Upanishad, Book 2, Part 8 — paired immediately consecutive Anuvakas of the Brahmanandavalli, separated in the digitized library by Müller's editorial apparatus; the Eighth Anuvaka built the *ānanda-mīmāṁsā* — the cascade calculation of bliss that ascended in factors of one hundred from human pleasure to the bliss of Brahman, with the great sage who is free from desires placed at every level — and closed with the recognition that the knower comprehends all five sheaths after departing, the Ninth Anuvaka closes the Valli with the return of the *yato vāco nivartante* sloka, the recognition of freedom from regret, and the formal seal *iti upaniṣat*. The calculation reaches the Brahman; the closing seals the Valli the calculation belongs to.
- **structural-parallel** → Mundaka Upanishad, Book 3, Part 1 — paired Upanishadic recognitions of freedom from good and evil for the seer; the Mundaka's First Khanda of the Third Mundaka holds the line that *when the seer sees the brilliant maker as the Person who has his source in Brahman, then he is wise, and shaking off good and evil, he reaches the highest oneness, free from passions* (Mundaka 3.1.3), the Taittiriyaka's Ninth Anuvaka holds the closely related recognition that the knower *does not distress himself with the thought, why did I not do what is good? why did I do what is bad? — he who thus knows these two, frees himself*. Paired moments where the late-Vedic literature names the same release in close vocabulary.
- **structural-parallel** → Katha Upanishad, Book 2, Part 3 — paired Upanishadic closing seals; the Katha's Sixth Valli closes its long dialogue with the formal seal *iti vidyānuśāsanam* — *here ends the teaching* (Katha 2.3.15) — and proceeds to the closing narrative and peace invocation, the Taittiriyaka's Ninth Anuvaka closes the Brahmanandavalli with the formal seal *iti upaniṣat* — *this is the Upanishad* — and proceeds to the next Valli's opening peace mantra. Both texts mark the close of a long instructional movement with a short formal naming of the section just completed.
