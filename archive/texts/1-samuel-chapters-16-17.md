# 1 Samuel, Chapters 16–17

Chapters 16 and 17 of the First Book of Samuel introduce David. In chapter 16, the prophet Samuel is sent to Bethlehem and, passing over the older sons of Jesse, anoints the youngest — David, brought in from the sheep — at his father's house. In chapter 17, David enters the Israelite camp and confronts the Philistine champion Goliath in single combat, killing him with a sling and a single stone. The two chapters move David from a hidden anointing to a public victory before the army.

## Context

The First Book of Samuel is part of the *Nevi'im*, the Prophets, specifically the section of Former Prophets that includes Joshua, Judges, Samuel, and Kings. In Hebrew tradition, 1 and 2 Samuel are a single book (*Sefer Shmuel*); the Greek Septuagint divided it into two, and that division has been retained in Christian Bibles. The narrative spans the end of the period of the judges, the rise of the monarchy under Saul, and the rise of David — beginning with the prophet Samuel's birth and ending with Saul's death.

Modern scholarship generally treats Samuel as part of a larger historical narrative extending from Joshua through Kings, assembled in the seventh and sixth centuries BCE, drawing on older sources and traditions. Chapters 16 and 17 belong to the David narrative that occupies the second half of the book; they introduce David from two different angles, and many scholars hold that the two chapters preserve originally separate accounts of his entry into Saul's court (the harp-player and the giant-killer).

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, chapters 16 and 17 do not entirely fit together. In chapter 16, David is brought to Saul's court as a musician and becomes his armor-bearer; Saul knows him well. In chapter 17, when David arrives at the camp, Saul does not recognize him, and at the end of the chapter Saul asks Abner whose son this young man is. The two chapters preserve two different traditions of how David first came before Saul, set side by side without harmonization. Reading them with this seam visible — rather than smoothing it into a single chronology — keeps the form the book has.

Second, the famous line at the heart of chapter 16 — "the LORD looks on the heart" (16:7) — is the narrator's framing for the rejection of the older brothers. The Hebrew word translated "heart" (*lev*) means roughly the seat of thought and intention rather than feeling alone. Reading the line with this in mind, rather than as a sentimental statement, recovers what the narrator is doing: contrasting outward stature with the inward criterion the rejection of Saul has just made into a question.
