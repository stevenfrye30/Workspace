# Book of Exodus, Chapters 1–3

The first three chapters of the Book of Exodus open the second book of the Hebrew Bible. The Israelites in Egypt are under forced labor, and a new pharaoh has ordered the killing of their male infants. A Hebrew boy named Moses is preserved, raised in the pharaoh's own household, and later driven into exile in Midian after killing an Egyptian overseer. In the wilderness, he meets the God of his ancestors in a burning bush and is commissioned to bring his people out of Egypt. Chapters 1–3 set the oppression, the protagonist, and the call.

## Context

The Book of Exodus is the second of the five books of the Torah, following Genesis and preceding Leviticus, Numbers, and Deuteronomy. Together these five books form the Pentateuch, the foundational corpus of the Hebrew Bible. Modern scholarship generally understands Exodus as a layered text assembled from multiple traditions, with final redaction in the Persian period (sixth to fifth century BCE), drawing on older materials. The narrative itself purports to describe events of the late second millennium BCE.

The book is in two halves of unequal style. The first half (chapters 1–18) tells the story of the Israelite departure from Egypt: oppression, the call of Moses, the plagues, the Passover, the crossing of the sea, and the journey to Sinai. The second half (chapters 19–40) is the giving of the Law at Sinai and the construction of the tabernacle. Chapters 1–3 belong to the opening narrative half — the situation in Egypt, the early life of Moses, and the call at the burning bush. The exodus event itself does not begin until later in the book.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the call at the burning bush in chapter 3 is the section's center, and the divine self-naming — *ehyeh asher ehyeh*, often translated "I AM THAT I AM" or "I will be what I will be" — is a four-word Hebrew phrase whose grammar is harder to pin down than any English translation can suggest. The phrase is, formally, a verb. Reading the call scene with the awareness that the most literal English renderings differ from the most familiar ones keeps the strangeness of the passage visible.

Second, chapters 1 and 2 are framed by women. The midwives Shiphrah and Puah refuse the pharaoh's order; Moses's mother and sister set him on the Nile and arrange for his nursing; the pharaoh's daughter draws him from the water; later in chapter 2, Zipporah becomes Moses's wife. Reading the chapters with attention to the named women whose actions preserve Moses across the first two chapters keeps visible a structure that summary retellings often pass over.
