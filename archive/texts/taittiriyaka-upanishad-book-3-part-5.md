# Taittiriyaka Upanishad, Book 3, Part 5

Book 3, Part 5 of the Taittiriyaka Upanishad — the Fifth Anuvaka of the Bhriguvalli — is the fourth iteration of the dialogic engine and the second-to-last. The sentence the Fourth Anuvaka left open with the em-dash *Having performed penance—* is completed here in the same three short clauses with one substitution: *vijñānaṁ brahmeti vyajānāt* — *he perceived that understanding was Brahman.* The reasoning clause repeats the Brahman-definition with understanding in the place of *that*: *for from understanding these beings are born; by understanding, when born, they live; into understanding they enter at their death.* Bhrigu returns to his father with the same opening line he has given at the threshold of the Valli and at the close of every iteration so far — *adhīhi bhagavo brahmeti* — *Sir, teach me Brahman.* Varuna's reply is identical to the reply he has given before: *try to know Brahman by penance, for penance is the means of knowing Brahman.* The Anuvaka closes on the same line and the same em-dash that have closed every previous iteration — *He performed penance. Having performed penance—* — and the dialogue carries directly across the Anuvaka boundary into the Sixth, where the last of the five recognitions will be reached.

## Context

The Taittiriyaka Upanishad is one of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads — a small group of texts traditionally received as the philosophical core of the late Vedic literature. It belongs to the Krishna Yajurveda branch of the Vedic corpus and to the Taittiriya school of that branch; the Upanishad sits inside the Taittiriya Aranyaka. Modern scholarship places it among the older prose Upanishads, in roughly the sixth to fifth century BCE — earlier than the verse Upanishads of the Katha and Mundaka. The text is anonymous; the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship.

Book 3, Part 5 is the fifth of the ten anuvakas of the Bhriguvalli. The dialogic engine has now moved through four of the five recognitions the Brahmanandavalli unfolded as recursive sheaths: food, breath, mind, and now understanding. One recognition remains — bliss — and the Sixth Anuvaka will reach it. The same sentence-template has held across all four iterations of the substitution; the order of the substitutions has matched the order of the *pañca-kośa* the two Vallis share. The form of the engine is now nearly complete; the Sixth Anuvaka will close the recognition cycle, and the anuvakas that follow will move into the closing material the Valli holds after Bhrigu's progress is finished.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the iteration is at the second-to-last substitution and the cumulative pressure of the form is now visible. Four iterations have made the same sentence-template — *X is Brahman, for from X these beings are born; by X, when born, they live; into X they enter at their death* — and four substitutions have filled it. The form does not vary; what varies is the term and, with it, the level at which the recognition sits. Reading the Anuvaka with the previous three iterations held alongside it, attending to the order of the substitutions and to the way the form has accumulated weight by exact repetition, recovers what the Bhriguvalli is doing as it approaches its last recognition.

Second, the Anuvaka holds *vijñāna* — *understanding*, sometimes rendered *knowledge* or *discernment* — as the next-to-inmost level of the recognition. In the Brahmanandavalli's Fifth Anuvaka, the *vijñānamaya* was named as the level at which Bhrigu's fourth recognition is also placed: *understanding performs the sacrifice; understanding performs all sacred acts; all Devas worship understanding as Brahman, as the oldest.* The Bhriguvalli does not extend that reasoning here; it places the same recognition in the same sentence-template the previous iterations have used. Reading the Anuvaka with the Brahmanandavalli's Fifth Anuvaka held alongside, attending to the same recognition given through different procedures, keeps the form the two Vallis hold together when their substance most closely converges.

## The text

The text is held in the Archive library under `upanishads-muller-part2`, chapter 5 (Müller translation, 1884 — *The Sacred Books of the East*, Volume XV; via SacredTexts.com). Library chapter 5 is shared across thirty-five Archive entities — four parts of the Katha Upanishad, the six parts of the Mundaka Upanishad, the twelve anuvakas of the Sikshavalli, the nine anuvakas of the Brahmanandavalli, and the First through Fourth Anuvakas of the Bhriguvalli — and continues here into the Fifth Anuvaka without break in the chapter. Book 3, Part 5 of the Taittiriyaka — the entity here — occupies passages `5.349` through `5.352`. Passage `5.349` is the "Fifth Anuvaka" header. Passage `5.350` carries Bhrigu's fourth recognition — *He perceived that understanding was Brahman* — completing the sentence that ended with the em-dash at the close of the Fourth Anuvaka. Passage `5.351` carries Bhrigu's return to Varuna, the renewed request *Sir, teach me Brahman*, and Varuna's reply on penance; the prose is substantively identical to the corresponding passages in the Second through Fourth Anuvakas, with a small variation in punctuation in Müller's source — a double-dash before the quoted *Sir, teach me Brahman* in place of the colon used in the earlier iterations — preserved here as a minor formatting variation in the digitized text rather than as a structural difference. Passage `5.352` carries the closing line *He performed penance. Having performed penance—*, ending again on the same em-dash that has closed every previous iteration of the Bhriguvalli. Passage `5.353` is the "Sixth Anuvaka" header that opens Book 3, Part 6 of the Taittiriyaka and is not part of this entity. The library chapter continues past this entity into the rest of the Taittiriyaka and the remaining Upanishads of the volume. About one minute's reading.

## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Taittiriyaka Upanishad, Book 3, Part 4 — paired immediately consecutive Anuvakas of the Bhriguvalli; the Fourth Anuvaka completed the open sentence with the recognition that mind is Brahman, the Fifth Anuvaka completes the same open sentence with the recognition that understanding is Brahman. The substitution moves one step further inward; the form of the iteration is unchanged.
- **structural-parallel** → Taittiriyaka Upanishad, Book 2, Part 5 — paired moments inside the same Upanishad on understanding as Brahman; the Brahmanandavalli's Fifth Anuvaka holds the framing sloka of the *vijñānamaya* — *understanding performs the sacrifice, it performs all sacred acts; all Devas worship understanding as Brahman, as the oldest; if a man knows understanding as Brahman, and if he does not swerve from it, he leaves all evils behind in the body and attains all his wishes* — the Bhriguvalli's Fifth Anuvaka holds the recognition in dialogic form: Bhrigu, after penance, perceives that understanding is Brahman because from understanding beings are born, by understanding they live, into understanding they enter at death. Two registers of the same recognition — recursive descent and progressive dialogue — at the same level inside the same Upanishad's two vallis.
- **structural-parallel** → Mundaka Upanishad, Book 2, Part 2 — paired Sanskrit passages on the disciplined inward approach by which the recognition is reached; the Mundaka's Second Khanda holds the figure of the Upanishad as the bow, of *Om* as the bow, of the Self as the arrow, of Brahman as the aim, with the arrow becoming one with the target as the seeker becomes one with Brahman (Mundaka 2.2.3–4) — the inward approach given in compact figure — the Bhriguvalli's Fifth Anuvaka holds the inward approach as iterative penance and progressive recognition: each substitution made and tested, the closer recognition reached only after the previous has been performed in penance. Both texts hold the inward approach as a form, the one in image and the other in repeated dialogue.
