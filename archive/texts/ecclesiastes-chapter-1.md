# Ecclesiastes, Chapter 1

The first chapter of Ecclesiastes — the book the Hebrew tradition calls *Qohelet*, "the Assembler" — opens the second of the Hebrew Bible's wisdom books to be received as a single voice. Eighteen verses set the speaker, the project, and the refrain: *vanity of vanities, all is vanity*. Qohelet, identified as son of David and king in Jerusalem, surveys the cycles of sun, wind, river, and human striving, and concludes that nothing under the sun is new and nothing is finally retained. The book that follows is the long working-out of this opening assertion.

## Context

Ecclesiastes is one of the three principal wisdom books of the Hebrew Bible, alongside Job and Proverbs. The Hebrew title is *Qohelet*, a participle from a verb meaning to gather or assemble; the speaker is one who addresses the assembly, sometimes rendered "the Preacher" or "the Teacher." The Greek translation in the Septuagint gives the book the name by which it has come to most modern readers: *Ekklesiastes*.

The book is in twelve chapters and has two voices. The body — chapter 1 through most of chapter 12 — is in Qohelet's first person. A short editorial frame at the very end (12:9–14) speaks of Qohelet in the third person and offers a closing summary. Modern scholarship places the book in the Persian or early Hellenistic period, somewhere between the fifth and third centuries BCE; the Hebrew is late, with vocabulary and Persian loanwords characteristic of that range. The traditional attribution to Solomon, suggested by the opening verse, does not match the language of the book, but the literary identification of Qohelet with Solomon was the frame the editors of the canon used.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the central word of the book is *hevel* — translated "vanity" since the King James Version, but literally meaning vapor, breath, or mist. Reading the refrain as *vapor of vapors, all is vapor* keeps closer to the Hebrew. Qohelet is not declaring a doctrine of meaninglessness; he is naming a quality — the briefness, the not-staying — that he has found everywhere he has looked.

Second, chapter 1 is the survey, not the verdict. Qohelet's account of the sun, the wind, the rivers, and the searching of the mind sets the field of observation. The chapters that follow press the survey against particular questions — labor, wealth, wisdom, pleasure, justice, time. Reading chapter 1 as the opening of an inquiry rather than its conclusion follows the form of the book.
