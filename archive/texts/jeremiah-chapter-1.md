# Jeremiah, Chapter 1

Chapter 1 of the Book of Jeremiah is the call of the prophet. It opens with a superscription naming Jeremiah son of Hilkiah, a priest of Anathoth in the territory of Benjamin, and the regnal years that frame his career — from the thirteenth year of king Josiah down to the fall of Jerusalem under Zedekiah. The word of the LORD comes to Jeremiah, who objects that he is too young to speak; the LORD touches his mouth, places his words in it, and appoints him over nations and kingdoms "to root out, and to pull down, and to destroy, and to throw down, to build, and to plant." Two short visions — a rod of an almond tree, and a seething pot tipping from the north — close the chapter, with a charge to gird up his loins, arise, and not be dismayed.

## Context

The Book of Jeremiah is one of the four major prophetic books of the Hebrew Bible — alongside Isaiah, Ezekiel, and Daniel — and is the longest of them by word count, in fifty-two chapters. The figure of Jeremiah son of Hilkiah, a priest of Anathoth a few miles north of Jerusalem, is dated by the superscription to the late seventh and early sixth centuries BCE: called in the thirteenth year of king Josiah (around 627 BCE), and active across the reigns of Josiah, Jehoiakim, and Zedekiah, into and beyond the destruction of Jerusalem in 587 BCE. Modern scholarship treats the book as a layered work compiled over a long period — oracles attributed to the prophet, biographical narratives associated with his scribe Baruch ben Neriah, and an extensive prose framework whose language has affinities with Deuteronomy — reaching its present form during and after the Babylonian exile. The Septuagint preserves a shorter recension of Jeremiah, about an eighth shorter than the Masoretic text and with several sections in different order; fragments matching both recensions have been found at Qumran.

Chapter 1 stands at the head of the book and is the call narrative. It opens with the superscription (verses 1–3) naming the prophet, his father, his town, and the regnal years that frame his career. The body of the chapter (verses 4–19) contains the call dialogue, the youth's objection, the touching of the mouth, the appointment over nations and kingdoms, two short vision-and-interpretation pairs (the almond rod, the seething pot from the north), and a closing commission to speak without fear. The longer collection of oracles begins in chapter 2.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the call dialogue is built on objection and overruling. Jeremiah answers, "Ah, Lord GOD! behold, I cannot speak: for I am a child" — the Hebrew term translated "child" or "youth" carries a wider age range than either English word suggests — and the LORD overrules the objection directly: "Say not, I am a child: for thou shalt go to all that I shall send thee, and whatsoever I command thee thou shalt speak." The exchange is followed by a gesture — the LORD touches Jeremiah's mouth — and a phrase: "Behold, I have put my words in thy mouth." Reading the call with attention to the shape of the dialogue — objection, overruling, gesture to the body, words placed in the mouth — keeps the form the chapter has.

Second, the two short visions in verses 11–16 follow the same question-and-interpretation form. "What seest thou, Jeremiah?" — "I see a rod of an almond tree" — "Thou hast well seen: for I will hasten my word to perform it." The Hebrew turns on a sound-pattern: *shaqed* (almond tree) and *shoqed* (one who watches), a wordplay that the English translation can flag in a footnote but cannot carry. The second vision — the seething pot tipping from the north — is interpreted within the chapter itself as the coming of evil from the northern kingdoms. Reading the vision pair with the awareness that the first turns on a Hebrew sound and the second on a geographical direction, rather than on a visual symbol to be decoded, recovers the form the text gives.
