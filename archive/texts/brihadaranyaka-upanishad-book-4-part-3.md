# Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 4, Part 3

Book 4, Part 3 of the Brihadaranyaka Upanishad — the Third Brahmana of the Fourth Adhyaya — is the longest Brahmana of the Adhyaya so far and the most sustained Upanishadic exposition of the three states of the Self. The Janaka–Yajnavalkya dialogue continues: the same king and the same priest the prior two Brahmanas had held in private audience, but the procedure is different. The First Brahmana had run the recurring six-fold instructional template, and the Second had run the compressed teaching ending on fearlessness and the king's surrender of kingdom and self. The Third opens on a narrative interruption that names a prior boon outside the present dialogue. *Yagnavalkya came to Ganaka Vaideha, and he did not mean to speak with him.* The line is one of the Brihadaranyaka's most distinctive openings: the priest enters the king's audience without an intention to teach. The narrator's clause that follows gives the reason: *when formerly Ganaka Vaideha and Yagnavalkya had a disputation on the Agnihotra, Yagnavalkya had granted him a boon, and he chose (for a boon) that he might be free to ask him any question he liked. Yagnavalkya granted it, and thus the King was the first to ask him a question.* The dialogue's procedure is held by the prior boon, not by the priest's voluntary teaching, and the king is the one to ask first.

The substantive opening is the light-cascade. Verse 2 puts the form of the question: *Yagnavalkya, what is the light of man?* Yajnavalkya answers: *The sun, O King; for, having the sun alone for his light, man sits, moves about, does his work, and returns.* Janaka's seal — *So indeed it is, O Yagnavalkya* — is given on its own short passage, and verse 3 then opens with the same form altered: *When the sun has set, O Yagnavalkya, what is then the light of man?* The moon. Verse 4: when the sun has set and the moon has set, fire. Verse 5: when sun, moon, and fire are gone, sound — *therefore, O King, when one cannot see even one's own hand, yet when a sound is raised, one goes towards it*. Verse 6: when sun, moon, fire, and sound are all gone, *the Self*. The form is substitutional ladder, working through five iterations of the same shape — *what is then the light of man?* — with one more light-source added to the extinguishment-list at every iteration. The cascade rests at the Self.

Verse 7 then opens the substantive teaching with the question *Who is that Self?* The answer is given in compressed form: *He who is within the heart, surrounded by the Pranas (senses), the person of light, consisting of knowledge. He, remaining the same, wanders along the two worlds, as if thinking, as if moving. During sleep (in dream) he transcends this world and all the forms of death.* Verses 8–10 work the doctrine of the three states. *There are two states for that person, the one here in this world, the other in the other world, and as a third an intermediate state, the state of sleep.* The dream-state is named as that in which *the person is self-illuminated*, and verse 10 gives the dream-state's creative power: *There are no (real) chariots in that state, no horses, no roads, but he himself sends forth (creates) chariots, horses, and roads. There are no blessings there, no happiness, no joys, but he himself sends forth (creates) blessings, happiness, and joys. There are no tanks there, no lakes, no rivers, but he himself sends forth (creates) tanks, lakes, and rivers. He indeed is the maker.* The doctrine is the dreamer-as-creator doctrine: in dream the person makes the world he dreams.

Verse 11 marks the Brahmana's first formal verse-form: *On this there are these verses.* Four slokas follow, numbered (1) through (4) inside the closing parentheses. Sloka (1): *After having subdued by sleep all that belongs to the body, he, not asleep himself, looks down upon the sleeping (senses). Having assumed light, he goes again to his place, the golden person, the lonely bird.* Sloka (2): *Guarding with the breath (prana, life) the lower nest, the immortal moves away from the nest; that immortal one goes wherever he likes, the golden person, the lonely bird.* Sloka (3): *Going up and down in his dream, the god makes manifold shapes for himself, either rejoicing together with women, or laughing (with his friends), or seeing terrible sights.* Sloka (4) is given at verse 14 and closes with the warning that has carried in the tradition: *People may see his playground but himself no one ever sees. Therefore they say, Let no one wake a man suddenly, for it is not easy to remedy, if he does not get back (rightly to his body).* The narrator's voice then introduces an objection: *Here some people (object and) say: "No, this (sleep) is the same as the place of waking, for what he sees while awake, that only he sees when asleep."* The answer: *No, here (in sleep) the person is self-illuminated (as we explained before).* The Brahmana absorbs the objection inside its own teaching rather than dismissing it.

Verses 15–17 walk the dreamer's recurring movement three times. Each follows the same shape: *That (person) having enjoyed himself in that state of X, having moved about and seen both good and evil, hastens back again as he came, to the place from which he started, to Y. And whatever he may have seen there, he is not followed (affected) by it, for that person is not attached to anything.* Verse 15: from the state of bliss (samprasada, deep sleep) back to dream. Verse 16: from sleep (dream) back to waking. Verse 17: from the state of waking back to dream. The three iterations are given in nearly verbatim form, with X and Y substituted at each cycle. After verse 15 and verse 16, Ganaka's reply is the same: *I give you, Sir, a thousand. Speak on for the sake of (my) emancipation.* The Brahmana's offer-and-acceptance form here reverses the First Brahmana's offer-and-refusal form. The First Brahmana had given six iterations of *a thousand cows with a bull as big as an elephant* offered and *one should not accept a reward without having fully instructed a pupil* refused. The Third Brahmana gives the offers as *a thousand* without specification of the cattle, and Yajnavalkya neither accepts nor refuses — he simply continues teaching at each cycle. The same offer-form will return at verse 33's close at `5.1087`, in the same wording.

Verses 18–19 give two similes for the dreamer's movement. *In fact, as a large fish moves along the two banks of a river, the right and the left, so does that person move along these two states, the state of sleeping and the state of waking.* The fish-and-banks figure is paired with the falcon-and-nest figure: *as a falcon, or any other (swift) bird, after he has roamed about here in the air, becomes tired, and folding his wings is carried to his nest, so does that person hasten to that state where, when asleep, he desires no more desires, and dreams no more dreams.* The two similes hold the same doctrine — the person moves between states without being held by any one of them — through two named animals at two scales of motion.

Verse 20 names the Hita veins and the dream-fears. *There are in his body the veins called Hita, which are as small as a hair divided a thousandfold, full of white, blue, yellow, green, and red.* The Hita-veins doctrine had been given in the Second Brahmana of the Fourth Adhyaya (the heart's anatomy with veins *like a hair divided into a thousand parts*) and in the First Brahmana of the Second Adhyaya (the seventy-two thousand arteries called Hitā that run from the heart through the body); the Third Brahmana now gives the veins the additional five-color naming. Verse 20 then catalogues the dream-fears and dream-elevations: *when, as it were, they kill him, when, as it were they overcome him, when, as it were, an elephant chases him, when, as it were, he falls into a well, he fancies, through ignorance, that danger which he (commonly) sees in waking. But when he fancies that he is, as it were, a god, or that he is, as it were, a king, or "I am this altogether," that is his highest world.* The repeated *as it were* — *iva* in the Sanskrit — is preserved across each clause; the dream's fears and elevations are given as conditional rather than substantive, and the *I am this altogether* (*aham evedam sarvaḥ*) is named as the dream's highest world.

Verses 21–22 turn to deep sleep (sushupti). *This indeed is his (true) form, free from desires, free from evil, free from fear.* The doctrine is given through the embraced-by-beloved-wife simile, which is one of the Upanishadic literature's most-cited erotic figures of the Self. *Now as a man, when embraced by a beloved wife, knows nothing that is without, nothing that is within, thus this person, when embraced by the intelligent (pragna) Self, knows nothing that is without, nothing that is within. This indeed is his (true) form, in which his wishes are fulfilled, in which the Self (only) is his wish, in which no wish is left, — free from any sorrow.* Verse 22 then gives the deep-sleep negation catalogue. *Then a father is not a father, a mother not a mother, the worlds not worlds, the gods not gods, the Vedas not Vedas. Then a thief is not a thief, a murderer not a murderer, a Kandala not a Kandala, a Paulkasa not a Paulkasa, a Sramana not a Sramana, a Tapasa not a Tapasa.* The catalogue holds twelve negations across two registers: the social-relational (father, mother, worlds, gods, Vedas) and the named outcastes-and-renouncers (thief, murderer, Kandala, Paulkasa, Sramana, Tapasa). The named figures the catalogue holds — Kandala, Paulkasa, Sramana, Tapasa — carry footnoted glosses across `5.1071` and are preserved with their Sanskrit terms intact. The closing line: *He is not followed by good, not followed by evil, for he has then overcome all the sorrows of the heart.*

Verses 23–31 give the inseparability of the eight senses from the seer/smeller/taster/speaker/hearer/thinker/toucher/knower in the sushupti state. The form is held verbatim across nine successive verses. Verse 23 (sight): *And when (it is said that) there (in the Sushupti) he does not see, yet he is seeing, though he does not see. For sight is inseparable from the seer, because it cannot perish. But there is then no second, nothing else different from him that he could see.* Verses 24 through 30 give the same form for smell, taste, speech, hearing, thinking, touch, and knowing. Verse 31 reverses to give the contrasting waking state: *When (in waking and dreaming) there is, as it were, another, then can one see the other, then can one smell the other, then can one speak to the other, then can one hear the other, then can one think the other, then can one touch the other, then can one know the other.* The contrast names the duality of the waking-and-dreaming worlds against the non-duality of deep sleep, with the eight senses preserved as paired across the boundary.

Verse 32 holds the closing line of the substantive answer: *An ocean is that one seer, without any duality; this is the Brahma-world, O King. Thus did Yagnavalkya teach him. This is his highest goal, this is his highest Success, this is his highest world, this is his highest bliss. All other creatures live on a small portion of that bliss.* The ocean-as-one-seer line is one of the Brihadaranyaka's most-cited statements of the Self's non-duality, and it sits in verse 32 with the *Brahma-world* (brahmaloka) named as the deep-sleep state itself.

Verse 33 walks the bliss-cascade through seven hundred-fold multiplications. The form: *A hundred X-blessings make one Y-blessing.* The cascade ascends from the highest human blessing — *if a man is healthy, wealthy, and lord of others, surrounded by all human enjoyments* — through the world of the fathers (those *who have conquered the world of the fathers*), through the Gandharva world, through the Devas-by-merit (*who obtain their godhead by merit*), through the Devas-by-birth (*also (of) a Srotriya who is without sin, and not overcome by desire*), through the world of Pragapati, to *the world of Brahman, also (of) a Srotriya who is without sin, and not overcome by desire*. The closing seal: *And this is the highest blessing. This is the Brahma-world, O king, thus spake Yagnavalkya.* Ganaka offers a thousand and asks Yajnavalkya to speak on for the sake of (his) emancipation a third time.

The narrator's interruption at `5.1088` is one of the most psychologically arresting lines in the Brihadaranyaka. *Then Yagnavalkya was afraid lest the King, having become full of understanding, should drive him from all his positions.* The priest's fear is named directly. The teaching the priest had refused to begin in the First Brahmana, had begun under boon-pressure at the open of the Third, and had given through three iterations of the dreamer's movement, four slokas, two similes, the Hita-veins, the deep-sleep doctrine, the eight senses' inseparability, the ocean-as-one-seer, and the bliss-cascade — that teaching is now named in the narrator's voice as something that may turn against the priest. The line stands without commentary, and the Brahmana continues directly into verses 34–38.

Verses 34–38 give the death-and-departure teaching. Verse 34 returns to the recurring movement-cycle — *That (person), having enjoyed himself in that state of sleeping (dream), having moved about and seen both good and bad, hastens back again as he came, to the place from which he started, to the state of waking* — and verse 35 turns the movement into the dying body's groaning: *as a heavy-laden carriage moves along groaning, thus does this corporeal Self, mounted by the intelligent Self, move along groaning, when a man is thus going to expire.* Verse 36 gives the body weakening through age or illness as a fruit-from-stalk separation: *as an Amra (mango), or Udumbara (fig), or Pippala-fruit is separated from the stalk, hastens back again as he came, to the place from which he started, to (new) life.* Verses 37–38 hold the king-and-attendants twin similes. Verse 37: *as policemen, magistrates, equerries, and governors wait for a king who is coming back, with food and drink, saying, "He comes back, he approaches," thus do all the elements wait on him who knows this, saying, "That Brahman comes, that Brahman approaches."* Verse 38 gives the inversion: *as policemen, magistrates, equerries, and governors gather round a king who is departing, thus do all the senses (pranas) gather round the Self at the time of death, when a man is thus going to expire.* The Brahmana closes on verse 38 without a *held his peace* form, without a kingdom-offer, without a closing seal. The teaching is suspended; the Fourth Brahmana will continue.

## Context

The Brihadaranyaka Upanishad is one of the two earliest of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads, sharing that position with the Chandogya. It belongs to the Shukla Yajurveda branch of the Vedic corpus and forms the closing portion of the Shatapatha Brahmana — the *Aranyaka* in its name marks it as a forest-text recited at the late stages of Vedic study. Modern scholarship places the Brihadaranyaka in roughly the eighth to sixth century BCE, prior to the Buddha; the text is anonymous, and the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship. The text is the longest of the principal Upanishads and is typically structured in six *adhyayas* (chapters), each containing several *brahmanas* (sections).

Book 4, Part 3 holds two of the most distinctive features of the Upanishadic literature in concentrated form. The first is the doctrine of the three states. The Brihadaranyaka had given the three-states framework in compact form at the close of the First Brahmana of the Second Adhyaya — Ajatasatru's three-state analysis at the sleeping man's bedside — and the Third Brahmana of the Fourth Adhyaya now gives it across thirty-eight verses with the dreamer-as-creator, the embraced-by-beloved-wife simile, the eight senses' inseparability in sushupti, the ocean-as-one-seer of the Brahma-world, and the death-and-departure teaching. The doctrine has its principal Upanishadic elaboration here: the Self at the three levels of waking, dream, and deep sleep, with the deep-sleep state named as the Brahma-world itself. Reading the Brahmana with the three states held alongside each other rather than collapsed into a single Atman-doctrine recovers the form the Upanishad has when it works the doctrine through the procedure of state-distinction.

The second is the narrator's interruption at `5.1088`. *Then Yagnavalkya was afraid lest the King, having become full of understanding, should drive him from all his positions.* The line names what no other Brahmana of the Brihadaranyaka has named in this form: the priest's fear of the pupil. The Adhyaya's prior Brahmanas had given the king as the named questioner and the priest as the answerer; the Fourth Adhyaya's First Brahmana had given the priest refusing the king's reward; the Second had given the king bowing and offering kingdom and self. The Third now gives the priest afraid of the king. The reversal of authority that the Adhyaya has been working since `5.1009` is given its narrative-voice acknowledgment here. The narrator's line stands without further commentary; the priest does not stop the teaching, and the king does not become priest. The teaching continues into verses 34–38 in the same voice as before, with the fear named once and not addressed in the substantive matter.

The Brahmana also holds the Adhyaya's first sustained verse-form. Verses 11–14 hold the four slokas on the golden person and the lonely bird in the formal sloka measure, with the warning *Let no one wake a man suddenly* given inside Sloka (4). The Adhyaya's prior Brahmanas had been entirely in prose, with the recurring template of the First Brahmana and the compressed teaching of the Second. The Third Brahmana sets four slokas inside its prose body and works the dream-state doctrine partly through them. The slokas' *golden person* (*purusha-hiranmaya*) and *lonely bird* are figures the later Vedanta literature will work at length, and the Brahmana holds them inside the same sustained dialogue as the prose teaching that surrounds them.

The closing form is suspended. The First Brahmana had closed on Yajnavalkya's sixth refusal of the reward; the Second had closed on Ganaka's offer of kingdom and self. The Third Brahmana has no closing line, no narrator's seal, no *held his peace* form. The Brahmana ends mid-teaching on verse 38's twin similes of the king-departing-with-attendants. The Fourth Brahmana will continue the discourse from this point.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the Brahmana is given through nested literary forms. The opening light-cascade is a substitutional ladder; the three-states doctrine is a definitional sequence; the four slokas are formal verse; the recurring movement-cycle is a tripled iteration; the two similes are paired animal-figures; the deep-sleep negation catalogue is an enumeration of relational and outcaste terms; the eight senses' inseparability is a verbatim-repeated formula across nine verses; the bliss-cascade is a hundred-fold multiplication ladder; the death-and-departure section is a sequence of three figures (the carriage, the fruit, the king-and-attendants twin). Reading the Brahmana with the literary form of each section held distinct from the substantive content recovers the form the Adhyaya has when its longest Brahmana is given as a sequence of formal procedures rather than a single sustained one. The Brahmana works the same doctrine through eight different rhetorical machines, and the procedure of switching between machines is one of its literary signatures.

Second, the Brahmana's relation to the prior two Brahmanas of the Adhyaya should be read across the offer-form. The First Brahmana had given six refused offers of *a thousand cows with a bull as big as an elephant* with the citation of Yajnavalkya's father at every refusal. The Second had given the king's offer of kingdom and self as the closing form — accepted by the priest implicitly through the seal *O Ganaka, you have indeed reached fearlessness*. The Third gives three offers of *a thousand* across the recurring movement-cycle (verses 15, 16, 17) with no explicit acceptance and no explicit refusal, and a fourth offer at verse 33's close at `5.1087` in the same form. Yajnavalkya neither cites his father nor refuses; he simply continues teaching. The Adhyaya's offer-economy is held through the Brahmana with the procedure of the offer altered three times across the Adhyaya's three Brahmanas: refused / surrendered / accepted-by-continuation.

- **Verse-2 footnote on *light of man*.** Passage `5.1029` carries verse 2 — Janaka's first substantive question — with footnote `[*1]` on *light of man*. The footnote attaches at the substantive form of the question and is the principal note on the light-cascade's open.

- **Objection-and-answer at `5.1056`–`5.1057`.** The closing of verse 14 holds an objection inserted in the text: *Here some people (object and) say: "No, this (sleep) is the same as the place of waking, for what he sees while awake, that only he sees when asleep."* Passage `5.1056` carries the objection with footnote `[*3]` on *that only he sees when asleep*. Passage `5.1057` carries Müller's translation of the answer: *No, here (in sleep) the person is self-illuminated (as we explained before).* The form is rare in the Brihadaranyaka — an objection-and-answer interpolation inside the main teaching — and Müller's footnote glosses the form.
- **Verses 15–17 recurring-cycle with three offer-passages.** Each of the three iterations of the dreamer's movement (verses 15, 16, 17) closes with Ganaka's offer-passage in nearly verbatim form: *Ganaka Vaideha said: 'I give you, Sir, a thousand. Speak on for the sake of (my) emancipation.'* Passage `5.1058` carries the first offer; passages `5.1060`–`5.1061` carry the second offer split across an editorial bracket *[paragraph continues]* (passage `5.1061` opens with the bracket); passage `5.1063` carries the third offer. The First Brahmana's *cows with a bull as big as an elephant* is here compressed to *a thousand* with no animal-specification, and Yajnavalkya's father-citation refusal is not given in any of the three offers; the priest continues teaching at each offer-cycle.
- **Verse-20 split across the Hita-veins clause.** Verse 20 is split across passages `5.1067` and `5.1068` at *full of white, blue, yellow, green, and red. Now / when, as it were, they kill him*. Passage `5.1067` carries footnote `[*1]` on the five-color list. Passage `5.1068` carries footnotes `[*1]` on *as it were, a king* and `[*2]` on *highest world*.
- **Verse-21 split across the embraced-by-beloved-wife simile.** Verse 21 is split across passages `5.1069` and `5.1070` at *in which the Self (only) is / his wish*. Passage `5.1069` carries footnote `[*3]` on *free from fear*; passage `5.1070` carries footnote `[*1]` on *any sorrow*.
- **Verse-22 six-footnote cluster on the deep-sleep negation catalogue.** Passage `5.1071` carries verse 22 — the deep-sleep negation catalogue — with six successive footnotes: `[*2]` on *murderer*, `[*3]` on *Kandala*, `[*4]` on *Paulkasa*, `[*5]` on *Sramana*, `[*6]` on *Tapasa*, and `[*7]` on *sorrows of the heart*. Müller's six notes attach at five of the six named outcaste-or-renouncer figures and at the closing-clause; the cluster is the densest footnote-grouping on a single passage in the Brahmana.
- **Verse-23 split mid-clause.** Verse 23 — the first of the eight senses' inseparability verses — is split across passages `5.1072` and `5.1073` at *yet he is seeing, though he does not see [*8]. For sight is inseparable from the / seer*. Passage `5.1072` carries footnote `[*8]` on the substantive line and the split falls inside the *inseparable from the seer* clause.
- **Verse-28 split with editorial bracket.** Verse 28 — the thinking-inseparability verse — is split across passages `5.1078` and `5.1079`; passage `5.1079` opens with the editorial bracket *[paragraph continues]* preserved in the digitized source.
- **OCR irregularity at verse 29 (`5.1080`).** Verse 29 — the touching-inseparability verse — closes at `5.1080` with the line *But there is then no second, nothing else different from him that he could think.* The closing word *think* is an OCR transcription artifact; the substantive content of the verse is the touching-inseparability formula, and the closing word should track the verse's substance (i.e. *touch*). Müller's underlying translation gives *touch*; the digitized source carries *think* without correction. The irregularity is preserved as found in the source; Müller's text will give the corrected reading.
- **Verse-32 footnote cluster on the Brahma-world.** Passage `5.1083` carries verse 32 — the ocean-as-one-seer line — with footnote `[*1]` on *ocean* and footnote `[*2]` on *Brahma-world*. The two notes gloss the substantive figures of the verse and are the principal apparatus on the closing line of the substantive answer.
- **Verse-33 split with footnote on the Srotriya.** Verse 33 — the bliss-cascade — is split across passages `5.1084` and `5.1085`. Passage `5.1085` carries footnotes `[*1]` on *Srotriya* (the *Srotriya who is without sin, and not overcome by desire* repeated three times in the cascade) and `[*2]` on *highest blessing*.
- **Narrator's interruption at `5.1088` with footnote.** Passage `5.1088` carries the narrator's line *Then Yagnavalkya was afraid lest the King, having become full of understanding, should drive him from all his positions* with footnote `[*3]` on *drive him from all his positions*. The line sits between verse 33's close and verse 34's open without a verse-number, and Müller's note glosses the underlying Sanskrit on which the priest's fear is named. The line is the only narrative interruption inside the substantive body of the Brahmana, and it is the only line in the Adhyaya that names the priest's fear of the pupil.
- **Verse-34 split.** Verse 34 — the dreamer's movement back to waking — is split across passages `5.1089` and `5.1090`. The split falls inside the recurring-cycle formula.
- **Brahmana ends without closing line.** Passage `5.1094` carries verse 38 — the king-departing-with-attendants simile applied to the senses gathering round the Self at death — with no closing seal, no *held his peace* narrator-line, no kingdom-offer, no Janaka-reply. The Brahmana closes on the substantive verse, and the Fourth Brahmana that follows continues the teaching directly.

Passage `5.1095` is the "Fourth Brahmana" header that opens Book 4, Part 4 of the Brihadaranyaka and is not part of this entity. The library chapter continues past this entity into the rest of the Fourth Adhyaya and the remaining portions of the volume; the Brihadaranyaka extends well beyond the boundaries of library chapter 5. About fifteen minutes' reading.

## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 4, Part 2 — paired immediate-predecessor Brahmanas of the Fourth Adhyaya holding the Janaka–Yajnavalkya teaching across radically different scales; the Second Brahmana opens with Ganaka's bow from the throne and works the doctrine of the Self through the right-eye (Indha) / left-eye (Virag) anatomy, the heart's interior (meeting-place, food, covering, road, Hita veins), the six-quarter pranas, and the *neti neti* close on fearlessness — eighteen passages, four numbered verses — the Third Brahmana works the same Self-doctrine across thirty-eight numbered verses, sixty-nine passages, eight literary forms (light-cascade, three-states definition, four slokas, recurring movement-cycle, two similes, Hita-veins-and-dream-fears, deep-sleep negation, eight senses' inseparability, ocean-as-one-seer, bliss-cascade, death-and-departure). The Second Brahmana closes with Ganaka offering kingdom and self; the Third Brahmana closes mid-teaching without a closing line, with the priest's fear of the pupil named at the narrative interruption between verses 33 and 34.
- **structural-parallel** → Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 4, Part 1 — paired Brahmanas of the Fourth Adhyaya holding the offer-and-acceptance economy of the Janaka–Yajnavalkya dialogue across opposite procedures; the First Brahmana gives six iterations of *a thousand cows with a bull as big as an elephant* offered by Ganaka and refused by Yajnavalkya with the citation *one should not accept a reward without having fully instructed a pupil*, the Third Brahmana gives three iterations of *a thousand* offered by Ganaka inside the recurring movement-cycle (verses 15, 16, 17) plus a fourth offer at verse 33's close, with no refusal-citation and no acceptance-line; Yajnavalkya simply continues teaching at each offer. The First Brahmana's offer-form names the cattle and the bull-as-elephant; the Third Brahmana's offer-form has neither animal-specification nor refusal. The Adhyaya's offer-economy carries from refused-six-times into accepted-by-continuation across the Brahmana boundary.
- **structural-parallel** → Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 2, Part 1 — paired Brihadaranyaka loci on the three states of the Self and the Hita-arteries / Hita-veins anatomy; the First Brahmana of the Second Adhyaya holds the Gargya Balaki / Ajatasatru dialogue at the king of Kasi's court, with Ajatasatru's three-states analysis given at the sleeping man's bedside (the intelligent person in deep sleep lying in the ether of the heart, in dream as a great king, in profound sleep moving through the seventy-two thousand arteries called Hitā that spread from the heart through the body), and the closing figure of the spider with its thread and the small sparks from fire as the form of how the senses, worlds, Devas, and beings come forth from the Self, the Third Brahmana of the Fourth Adhyaya holds the same three-states framework — waking, the other world, intermediate state of sleep — and the same Hita-veins anatomy with the additional five-color naming (*full of white, blue, yellow, green, and red*), worked across thirty-eight verses and closing on the bliss-cascade and the king-and-attendants twin similes for departure at death. Both Brahmanas hold the doctrine of the Self in three states inside a king-and-Brahmana frame, with the Second Adhyaya giving the doctrine through Ajatasatru as teacher and the Fourth Adhyaya giving it through Yajnavalkya as teacher.
