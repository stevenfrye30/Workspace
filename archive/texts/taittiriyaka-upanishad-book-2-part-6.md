# Taittiriyaka Upanishad, Book 2, Part 6

Book 2, Part 6 of the Taittiriyaka Upanishad — the Sixth Anuvaka of the Brahmanandavalli — is the moment at which the Valli leaves the recursive form behind. The Anuvaka opens with the framing sloka of the *ānandamaya* — *asanneva sa bhavati, asad brahmeti veda cet; asti brahmeti ced veda, santam enaṁ tato viduḥ* — *he who knows the Brahman as non-existing, becomes himself non-existing; he who knows the Brahman as existing, him we know himself as existing* — and immediately closes the recursive identity-line one final time: *the embodied Self of this (bliss) is the same as that of the former (understanding).* The recursion is finished. The next line is the literary turn the rest of the Valli will take: *atha ataḥ anupraśnāḥ* — *thereupon follow the questions of the pupil.* Two questions are given. *Does anyone who knows not, after he has departed this life, ever go to that world? Or does he who knows, after he has departed, go to that world?* The answer the Anuvaka gives is not an argument but a creation formula. *He wished — may I be many, may I grow forth. He brooded over himself, like a man performing penance. After he had thus brooded, he sent forth all, whatever there is. Having sent forth, he entered into it. Having entered it, he became* sat *(what is manifest) and* tyat *(what is not manifest), defined and undefined, supported and not supported.* The Anuvaka closes with the pointer *on this there is also this sloka* — turning the reader toward the Seventh Anuvaka, where the *sat/tyat* recognition will be developed further.

## Context

The Taittiriyaka Upanishad is one of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads — a small group of texts traditionally received as the philosophical core of the late Vedic literature. It belongs to the Krishna Yajurveda branch of the Vedic corpus and to the Taittiriya school of that branch; the Upanishad sits inside the Taittiriya Aranyaka. Modern scholarship places it among the older prose Upanishads, in roughly the sixth to fifth century BCE — earlier than the verse Upanishads of the Katha and Mundaka. The text is anonymous; the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship.

Book 2, Part 6 sits inside the Brahmanandavalli, the Second Valli of the Taittiriyaka. The Fifth Anuvaka closed the recursive descent at the *ānandamaya* with Brahman named as the support of the innermost sheath; the Sixth Anuvaka closes that recursion's loop and opens what the Brahmanandavalli will do for the rest of its anuvakas — turn from the recursive descent to a series of inquiries and creation formulas that develop the recognition the descent had reached. The pupil-question form that opens here will recur through the rest of the Valli; the *so 'kāmayata, bahu syām, prajāyeyeti* line — "he wished, may I be many, may I grow forth" — is one of the most-cited Upanishadic creation formulas, repeated in close form in the Brihadaranyaka (1.4.3) and elsewhere; the *sat/tyat* (manifest/not-manifest) distinction the Anuvaka introduces will return in the Seventh.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the Anuvaka holds two literary registers in close succession. The opening lines are the recursive form the Valli has been carrying — the existence/non-existence sloka in Müller's quotation marks, followed by the recursive identity-line closing the *ānandamaya* loop. The middle of the Anuvaka shifts: *thereupon follow the questions of the pupil*. The shift is small but decisive. From this point the Valli works in pupil-questions and creation formulas rather than in nested-sheath recursions. Reading the Anuvaka with attention to where the form changes — the closing of the recursion, the introduction of the pupil-question, the creation formula, the *sat/tyat* distinction — recovers the literary motion the Brahmanandavalli takes after its descent.

Second, the answer to the pupil's question is a wish rather than a position. The pupil asks whether the one-who-knows-not or the one-who-knows reaches that world; the answer the Anuvaka gives is *he wished — may I be many, may I grow forth.* The Anuvaka does not say which kind of person reaches Brahman; it gives the wish from which all that is exists. The form of the answer is characteristic of the late-Vedic prose Upanishads: a question about the destinies of persons is met by a recognition about the source from which all persons issue. Reading the answer as the form it has — a wish, a brooding, a sending-forth, an entering-into — rather than as a logical reply to the question asked, keeps the form the Brahmanandavalli has when it leaves the recursive descent.

## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Taittiriyaka Upanishad, Book 2, Part 5 — paired immediately consecutive Anuvakas of the Brahmanandavalli at the end of the recursive descent; the Fifth Anuvaka completes the recursive form by reaching the *ānandamaya* and naming Brahman as its support, the Sixth Anuvaka closes the bliss-loop, holds the existence/non-existence sloka, and then turns to the pupil-question and creation-formula form that the rest of the Valli will use. The recursion ends in the Fifth Anuvaka; the Sixth Anuvaka is what comes after.
- **structural-parallel** → Katha Upanishad, Book 2, Part 3 — paired post-climactic dialogic clarifications; the Katha's Sixth Valli, after Yama's long instruction, gathers the closing recognitions in a sequence of compressed statements and ends in the narrative outcome that Nachiketas, having received the teaching, became free from passion and death and obtained Brahman, the Taittiriyaka's Sixth Anuvaka, after the recursive descent of the Brahmanandavalli, turns to the pupil's questions about who reaches that world after death and gives the creation formula in answer. Both texts hold the same kind of post-climactic motion — questions and answers placed after the formal teaching has reached its inmost point.
- **structural-parallel** → Mundaka Upanishad, Book 2, Part 1 — paired Sanskrit creation formulas; the Mundaka's First Khanda of the Second Mundaka opens with the figure of sparks flying forth from a blazing fire and develops through a long unfolding of what comes from the Imperishable — breath, mind, the senses, the elements, the Vedas, the gods, men, the worlds — the Taittiriyaka's Sixth Anuvaka holds the closely related recognition in the compressed wish-formula *so 'kāmayata, bahu syām, prajāyeyeti* — *he wished, may I be many, may I grow forth* — followed by the *sat/tyat* distinction. Paired moments in the late-Vedic literature where the source's becoming-many is given as a wish or as a single sweep.
