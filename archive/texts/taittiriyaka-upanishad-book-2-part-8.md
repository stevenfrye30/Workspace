# Taittiriyaka Upanishad, Book 2, Part 8

Book 2, Part 8 of the Taittiriyaka Upanishad — the Eighth Anuvaka of the Brahmanandavalli — is the *ānanda-mīmāṁsā*, the examination of bliss. The Anuvaka opens with a sloka, marked in Müller's source by the sub-numeral `(1)`: *bhīṣāsmād vātaḥ pavate; bhīṣodeti sūryaḥ; bhīṣāsmād agniś cendraś ca; mṛtyur dhāvati pañcamaḥ* — *from terror of it the wind blows, from terror the sun rises; from terror of it Agni and Indra, yea Death runs as the fifth.* The next line introduces the calculation: *atha eṣānanda-mīmāṁsā bhavati* — *now this is an examination of what is meant by Bliss.* The Anuvaka then builds the cascade. *Let there be a noble young man, who is well read in the Veda, very swift, firm, and strong, and let the whole world be full of wealth for him: that is one measure of human bliss.* One hundred times that human bliss is one measure of the bliss of the human Gandharvas — *and likewise of a great sage learned in the Vedas who is free from desires.* The refrain on the great sage repeats at every level. One hundred times that bliss of human Gandharvas is the bliss of divine Gandharvas; one hundred times that, the bliss of the Fathers; one hundred times that, the bliss of the Devas born in the Agana heaven; one hundred times that, the bliss of the sacrificial Devas who go to the gods through their Vaidik sacrifices; one hundred times that, the bliss of the thirty-three Devas; one hundred times that, the bliss of Indra; one hundred times that, the bliss of Brihaspati; one hundred times that, the bliss of Pragapati; one hundred times that, the bliss of Brahman. *And likewise of a great sage who is free from desires* closes each step. The Anuvaka then turns. *He who is this Brahman in man, and he who is that Brahman in the sun, both are one.* And then the closing line, which gathers the Brahmanandavalli's recursive descent into the calculation just completed: *he who knows this, when he has departed this world, reaches and comprehends the Self which consists of food, the Self which consists of breath, the Self which consists of mind, the Self which consists of understanding, the Self which consists of bliss.* The closing pointer *on this there is also this Sloka* turns the reader toward the Ninth Anuvaka.

## Context

The Taittiriyaka Upanishad is one of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads — a small group of texts traditionally received as the philosophical core of the late Vedic literature. It belongs to the Krishna Yajurveda branch of the Vedic corpus and to the Taittiriya school of that branch; the Upanishad sits inside the Taittiriya Aranyaka. Modern scholarship places it among the older prose Upanishads, in roughly the sixth to fifth century BCE — earlier than the verse Upanishads of the Katha and Mundaka. The text is anonymous; the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship.

Book 2, Part 8 sits inside the Brahmanandavalli, the Second Valli of the Taittiriyaka. The Sixth and Seventh Anuvakas had left the recursive descent behind and turned to pupil-questions, the *so 'kāmayata* creation formula, the *raso vai saḥ* line, and the *abhaya* recognition; the Eighth Anuvaka builds out from those into the Valli's most striking literary structure — the bliss-calculation that ascends in factors of one hundred from a single measure of human bliss to the bliss of Brahman, with the great sage free from desires placed at every level. The calculation is paired in Müller's printed volume with a comparison table aligning the same bliss-cascade as it appears in the Taittiriya, the Satapatha-Brahmana, and the Brihadaranyaka — the line by which the Upanishadic literature carried the same calculation across multiple Vedic texts. The closing identity-line *yaś cāyam puruṣe yaś cāsāv āditye sa ekaḥ* — *he who is this Brahman in man, and he who is that Brahman in the sun, both are one* — is one of the most-cited Upanishadic identity statements; the closing recognition that the knower comprehends all five sheaths is the moment at which the Brahmanandavalli's recursive descent and its post-recursive examination are drawn together.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the Anuvaka is built as a single literary structure rather than as a sequence of small recognitions. The opening sloka places Brahman as the source of the elements' obedience; the calculation that follows uses the same structure of multiplication at each step (one hundred times the previous measure); the refrain *and likewise of a great sage who is free from desires* is repeated at every step without modification. The cumulative effect is the implicit recognition the Anuvaka does not state directly: the great sage is named alongside Brahman at every level of the cascade, and the bliss of the sage who is free from desires is therefore equal at every step to the bliss above. Reading the Anuvaka aloud and letting the cascade and the refrain build through repetition — rather than skipping ahead to the closing identity-line — recovers the form the *ānanda-mīmāṁsā* has when its argument is most thoroughly carried by structure.

Second, the closing returns to the recursive language the Brahmanandavalli left behind in the Sixth Anuvaka. The five sheaths — food, breath, mind, understanding, bliss — that were unfolded one by one in Anuvakas Two through Five, and that the recursion abandoned at the Sixth Anuvaka, return here in a single short sentence: *the Self which consists of food, the Self which consists of breath, the Self which consists of mind, the Self which consists of understanding, the Self which consists of bliss.* The Anuvaka does not develop the five sheaths again; it gathers them into the recognition the calculation has reached. Reading the closing line as the moment at which the Brahmanandavalli's two procedures — the recursive descent and the bliss-calculation — meet, rather than as a summary of the descent, keeps the form the Valli has at this point.

## The text

The text is held in the Archive library under `upanishads-muller-part2`, chapter 5 (Müller translation, 1884 — *The Sacred Books of the East*, Volume XV; via SacredTexts.com). Library chapter 5 is shared across twenty-nine Archive entities — four parts of the Katha Upanishad, the six parts of the Mundaka Upanishad, the twelve anuvakas of the Sikshavalli (Book 1) of the Taittiriyaka, and the First through Seventh Anuvakas of the Brahmanandavalli (Book 2, Parts 1 through 7) — and continues here into the Eighth Anuvaka without break in the chapter. Book 2, Part 8 of the Taittiriyaka — the entity here — occupies passages `5.264` through `5.282`. Passage `5.264` is the "Eighth Anuvaka" header. Passage `5.265` carries the opening sloka in Müller's quotation marks with the editorial sub-marker `(1)` and footnote `[*3]`; passage `5.266` carries the introductory line *Now this is an examination of (what is meant by) Bliss*. Passages `5.267` through `5.279` carry the bliss-calculation: passage `5.267` gives the base unit of human bliss; passage `5.268` opens the cascade with the sub-marker `(2)`; passages `5.269`–`5.279` work through the eleven multiplications of one hundred up to the bliss of Brahman, with sub-markers `(3)` at the Devas-in-Agana-heaven level (passage `5.272`) and `(4)` at the Indra level (passage `5.275`); passage `5.278` ends mid-line at "is one" with the continuation landing in passage `5.279`. Passage `5.280` carries the identity-line — *he who is this Brahman in man, and he who is that Brahman in the sun, both are one* — with the sub-marker `(5)` and footnotes `[*1]` and `[*2]`. Passage `5.281` carries the closing recognition that the knower comprehends the five sheaths after departing. Passage `5.282` is the closing pointer "On this there is also this Sloka:". Between this entity and the start of the Ninth Anuvaka at passage `5.326`, the library carries a substantial run of editorial apparatus from Müller's printed volume — passages `5.283` through `5.325` — consisting of a comparison table aligning the bliss-cascade across the Taittiriya, the Satapatha-Brahmana, and the Brihadaranyaka, followed by Müller's prose commentary on the cosmography of the bliss-cascade. The editorial apparatus is part of Müller's edition, not of the Upanishad text itself; it is preserved in the digitized library as a contiguous run of short passages between the close of the Eighth Anuvaka and the header of the Ninth. About six minutes' reading for the Anuvaka body itself.

## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Taittiriyaka Upanishad, Book 2, Part 7 — paired immediately consecutive Anuvakas of the Brahmanandavalli; the Seventh Anuvaka named Brahman as *rasa* — the source of all flavour and pleasure — and reached the *abhaya* recognition that fearlessness comes when one rests in the invisible, incorporeal, undefined, and unsupported, the Eighth Anuvaka builds the *ānanda-mīmāṁsā* — the examination of bliss by multiplication — that places that same recognition at every level of the cosmic cascade alongside the great sage who is free from desires. The recognitions of the Seventh and the calculation of the Eighth carry the same recognition through different literary structures.
- **structural-parallel** → Katha Upanishad, Book 1, Part 2 — paired Upanishadic distinctions between the pleasant and the good; the Katha's Second Valli opens with Yama's distinction between *śreyas* and *preyas* — the good and the pleasant — with the wise choosing the good and the foolish the pleasant, the Taittiriyaka's Eighth Anuvaka holds the related recognition through the cascade in which the great sage who is free from desires is placed beside each level of bliss from human pleasure to the bliss of Brahman, naming the same sage at every step. Both texts hold the recognition that the disciplined sage's bliss is not a small portion of cosmic bliss but the same bliss the source itself has.
- **structural-parallel** → Mundaka Upanishad, Book 2, Part 2 — paired Upanishadic identity-statements that Brahman within and Brahman without are one; the Mundaka's Second Khanda holds the recognition that the sun, the moon, the stars, and the lightnings do not shine there because by his light all is lit, and that Brahman is before, behind, right and left, below and above (Mundaka 2.2.10–11), the Taittiriyaka's Eighth Anuvaka holds the closely related line *he who is this Brahman in man, and he who is that Brahman in the sun, both are one*. Paired moments where the late-Vedic literature names the inner and the outer recognitions as the same recognition.
