# Dhammapada, Chapter 26 (The Brahmana)

Chapter 26 of the Dhammapada — the *Brāhmaṇa-vagga*, "The Brahmana" — is the closing chapter of the Dhammapada and at forty-one verses the longest chapter of the collection. It works through a single sustained inversion: the *Brahmana* is not defined by birth, family, platted hair, or sacrificial role, but by conduct, knowledge, and the destruction of attachment. The opening verse states the form as direct address: *stop the stream valiantly, drive away the desires, O Brahmana! when you have understood the destruction of all that was made, you will understand that which was not made* (383). Verses 384–387 hold the Brahmana on his arrival at the other shore, in his fearlessness, and in the simile that the sun is bright by day, the moon by night, the warrior in his armour, the Brahmana in meditation, but Buddha is bright with splendour day and night. Verse 388 holds the chapter's etymological inversion: *because a man is rid of evil, therefore he is called Brahmana; because he walks quietly, therefore he is called Samana; because he has sent away his own impurities, therefore he is called Pravragita (Pabbagita, a pilgrim)* — three identities given by what the words mean as Pali action-states, not by what they name as institutional roles. Verses 389–392 hold injunctions toward and from the Brahmana — that no one should attack him and that he should not strike back, that he should hold his mind from pleasures, that he should not offend by body, word, or thought, that he should worship the law as the Brahmana worships the sacrificial fire. Verses 393–396 hold the chapter's most explicit caste-rejection: *a man does not become a Brahmana by his platted hair, by his family, or by birth; in whom there is truth and righteousness, he is blessed, he is a Brahmana* (393); *what is the use of platted hair, O fool! what of the raiment of goat-skins? Within thee there is ravening, but the outside thou makest clean* (394); the true Brahmana wears dirty raiments, is emaciated, lives alone in the forest, meditates (395); *I do not call a man a Brahmana because of his origin or of his mother. He is indeed arrogant, and he is wealthy: but the poor, who is free from all attachments, him I call indeed a Brahmana* (396).

Verses 397–423 — the chapter's final twenty-seven verses — hold a sustained litany. Each verse opens with the same line, *him I call indeed a Brahmana who…*, and each names a different inward attribute or accomplishment. The Brahmana is the one who has cut all fetters and never trembles (397); who has cut the strap, the thong, and the chain, and is awakened (398); who endures reproach, bonds, and stripes (399); who is free from anger and has received his last body (400); who does not cling to pleasures, like water on a lotus leaf, like a mustard seed on the point of a needle (401); who knows the end of his suffering and has put down his burden (402); whose knowledge is deep (403); who keeps aloof from laymen and mendicants (404); who finds no fault with feeble or strong, and does not kill (405); who is tolerant with the intolerant, mild with fault-finders, free from passion among the passionate (406); from whom anger, hatred, pride, and envy have dropped like a mustard seed (407); who utters true speech, instructive and free from harshness (408); who takes nothing not given (409); who has no desires for this world or the next (410); who has no interests, has reached the depth of the Immortal (411); who is above good and evil (412); who is bright like the moon, pure, serene, undisturbed (413); who has traversed the miry road and reached the other shore (414); who travels without a home, all concupiscence extinct (415); the same with covetousness (416); who has risen above all bondage to men and gods (417); who has left pleasure and pain, has conquered all the worlds (418); who knows the destruction and return of beings, *welfaring (Sugata), and awakened (Buddha)* (419); whose path the gods, spirits, and men do not know, who is an Arhat (420); who calls nothing his own, before, behind, or between (421); the manly, the noble, the hero, the great sage, the conqueror, the impassible, the accomplished, the awakened (422). And the closing verse — and the closing verse of the entire Dhammapada — names the final criterion: *him I call indeed a Brahmana who knows his former abodes, who sees heaven and hell, has reached the end of births, is perfect in knowledge, a sage, and whose perfections are all perfect* (423).

## Context

The Dhammapada is one of the books of the Khuddaka Nikaya, the fifth and shortest section of the Sutta Pitaka in the Pali Canon. It collects 423 verses arranged in twenty-six chapters by theme — pairs, heedfulness, mind, flowers, the sage, evil, and so on. The verses are attributed to the Buddha and were transmitted orally for several centuries before being committed to writing in Sri Lanka in the first century BCE. The collection took shape in roughly the third century BCE, in connection with the Theravada transmission of the canon.

Chapter 26 is the *Brāhmaṇa-vagga*, "The Brahmana" — the closing chapter of the Dhammapada and the longest chapter of the collection at forty-one verses. Its verses are numbered 383 through 423 in the standard continuous numbering of the Dhammapada — and in F. Max Müller's 1881 translation, the version held in the Archive library, the count and numbering match the standard exactly, with no merged or missing verse of the kind that occurs in Chapters 4, 6, 8, 11, 14, 17, and 19. Verse 423 is the final verse of the entire Dhammapada; no chapter follows. The Pali word *brāhmaṇa* — Brahmin, brahman, member of the priestly varṇa — is rendered uniformly by Müller as "Brahmana" through the chapter, and the word is used in the chapter not in its caste sense but in the inverted sense the chapter elaborates: a person of any birth who has come to the conduct the chapter describes. The Pali words *Samana* (the quiet one), *Pravragita* (the one who has gone forth, with the Pali equivalent *Pabbagita* preserved parenthetically), *Sugata* (the well-gone, an epithet of the Buddha), and *Arhat* (venerable) are preserved in transliteration. *Gandharva* appears as "Gandharvas" at verse 420. Each verse is short — two or four lines in the Pali — and each completes one statement of the figure.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the chapter is built on a deliberate compositional inversion of the brahmanical caste-system. The chapter does not say that the Brahmana is a Buddhist or a non-Brahmin in disguise; it says that the word *Brahmana* names a person of certain conduct, and that the conduct is the criterion. The chapter's etymological verse 388 makes this explicit by giving three names — *Brahmana*, *Samana*, *Pravragita* — and then reading each as the Pali action-state the word's root names: *rid of evil*, *walks quietly*, *sent away his own impurities*. The chapter's caste-rejection verses (393–396) repeat the inversion as direct denial of the alternative: not by platted hair, not by family, not by birth, not by origin, not by mother. Reading the chapter as a sustained inversion of caste-based identity, rather than as a generic celebration of the holy person, recovers the form the chapter is built on.

Second, verses 397–423 are a single composition, not twenty-seven separate aphorisms. Each verse opens with *him I call indeed a Brahmana who…* and gives one further criterion. The litany is the longest sustained anaphoric form in the Dhammapada — twenty-seven repetitions of the same opening line. Reading the closing twenty-seven verses as one extended figure, with each verse adding one attribute to the cumulative picture of the true Brahmana, keeps the form the chapter has. The chapter does not summarise the criteria at the end; it ends with the criterion that brings the whole collection to its close — knowledge of former abodes, sight of heaven and hell, the end of births, perfect knowledge, perfect perfections.

## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Dhammapada, Chapter 19 (The Just) — the earlier chapter that already used the *true X / false X* compositional grammar across nine identities (the just, the learned, the supporter of the law, the elder, the respectable, the Samana, the Bhikshu, the Muni, the Ariya); Chapter 26 elaborates the same grammar at scale across forty-one verses focused on a single identity, *the Brahmana*. The two chapters are formal twins, with Chapter 19 distributing the grammar across nine identities and Chapter 26 concentrating it on one.
- **structural-parallel** → Dhammapada, Chapter 25 (The Mendicant) — the immediate predecessor on the shelf; Chapter 25 with twenty-three verses on the *Bhikshu* defined by conduct rather than role, Chapter 26 with forty-one verses on the *Brahmana* defined by conduct rather than birth. The two closing chapters work the same identity-by-conduct figure on the two contested social terms — the Buddhist mendicant and the brahmanical priest — and use them to close the collection.
- **structural-parallel** → Dhammapada, Chapter 14 (The Buddha) — the earlier chapter on the Awakened, which culminates at verse 195 in the figure of *those who have overcome the host (of evils), and crossed the flood of sorrow*; Chapter 26 verse 419 closes the litany on the same figure: the Brahmana *who knows the destruction and the return of beings everywhere, who is free from bondage, welfaring (Sugata), and awakened (Buddha)*. The figure of the true Brahmana at the chapter's close converges on the figure of the Buddha at the centre of the collection.
- **structural-parallel** → Dhammapada, Chapter 1 (The Twin Verses) — the opening pair on the shelf, which the closing chapter answers; Chapter 1 states the principle that mind precedes action and outcome, Chapter 26 verses 397–423 work out the criteria of the person whose mind, action, and outcome have all reached the consummation. The two chapters bookend the collection — Chapter 1 with the foundation, Chapter 26 with the description of those who have built on it.
- **structural-parallel** → Matthew 5 — the Christian shelf-pair on the litany of inward attributes; the Sermon on the Mount with its *blessed are the poor in spirit / blessed are they that mourn / blessed are the meek* anaphoric beatitudes, Dhammapada 26 verses 397–423 with its *him I call indeed a Brahmana who* anaphoric litany. Both texts use a sustained anaphora as their compositional form for the description of the inward person who has been turned in the direction the text is teaching, with twenty-seven verses in the Dhammapada and nine in Matthew 5.
- **structural-parallel** → Plato, Apology — the Greek pair on the inversion of social identity by inward conduct; Socrates' insistence that he is what the Athenians take him not to be — neither sophist, nor atheist, nor corruptor, but the gadfly of the city — Dhammapada 26 with its insistence that the Brahmana is what the brahmanical reader takes him not to be — neither caste-bearer, nor ritualist, nor wearer of the platted hair, but the one whose conduct is the criterion. Both texts hold the inversion of who the named person actually is.
