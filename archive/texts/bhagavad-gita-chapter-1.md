# Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 1 (Arjuna's Despair)

Chapter 1 of the Bhagavad Gita — *Arjuna-Vishada Yoga*, conventionally translated "Arjuna's Despair" or "The Distress of Arjuna" — opens the Sanskrit philosophical poem set inside the Mahabharata. The chapter is the dramatic frame from which the rest of the work unfolds: two armies stand drawn up for battle on the field of Kurukshetra; the warrior-prince Arjuna asks his charioteer Krishna to drive him between the two hosts; he recognizes on the opposing side his teachers, kinsmen, and former friends; and he refuses to fight. The chapter ends with Arjuna sinking onto his chariot-seat and letting fall his bow. The seventeen chapters that follow are Krishna's reply.

## Context

The Bhagavad Gita is a 700-verse philosophical poem in eighteen chapters, set inside Book VI (the *Bhishma Parva*) of the Mahabharata, the great Sanskrit epic of the Bhārata clan. Most scholars place its present form between roughly the second century BCE and the second century CE — a layered composition that took shape during the long growth of the epic from an older bardic core. The traditional attribution is to Vyasa, the legendary compiler of the Mahabharata; the historical authorship is unknown.

Chapter 1 is the dramatic prelude to the Gita's philosophical argument. The narrative is presented as a report within a report: the blind king Dhritirashtra asks his charioteer-narrator Sanjaya, who has been granted distant sight, to recount what is happening on the battlefield where his own sons (the Kauravas, led by Duryodhana) face their cousins (the Pandavas, led by Yudhishthira and including Arjuna) in the long-prepared war over the Bhārata kingdom. Sanjaya's report opens with the survey of the two armies, the blowing of the conches that signals battle, and Arjuna's request that Krishna drive his chariot between the two hosts so he may see the men he is about to fight. The chapter's last several verses are Arjuna's refusal — given as direct speech and ending in his collapse on the chariot-seat. Krishna's reply begins in Chapter 2.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, Chapter 1 is a dramatic frame, not philosophy yet. A reader who has heard of the Gita's teachings and reaches for it expecting philosophy meets instead a war camp, two armies, blown conches, and a man's refusal to act. Reading Chapter 1 as setting — placement, persons, situation — recovers the chapter's role in the work, and makes the philosophical reply that follows in the rest of the Gita answer a specific situation rather than stand as a free treatise.

Second, the Gita is a dialogue inside a dialogue. The frame narrator is Sanjaya, who reports what he sees on the battlefield to the blind king Dhritirashtra; inside Sanjaya's report, Arjuna and Krishna speak to each other. Chapter 1 makes the frame visible: the chapter opens with Dhritirashtra's question to Sanjaya, and Sanjaya's narration carries the rest. Reading the chapter with both speakers in view — the blind king receiving the report, the charioteer-narrator delivering it — keeps the form the Gita inherits from the larger epic.

## The text

The text is held in the Archive library under `bhagavad-gita`, chapter 1 (Edwin Arnold's verse translation, *The Song Celestial*, 1885 — Project Gutenberg edition). Arnold's verse translation compresses the chapter's forty-seven Sanskrit shlokas into nine English verse-paragraphs followed by a closing colophon line (passages `1.1`–`1.9` in the library's verse-level subdivision). A modern prose translation by the International Gita Society is held in the library as a parallel translation. About fifteen minutes' reading.
