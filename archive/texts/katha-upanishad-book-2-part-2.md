# Katha Upanishad, Book 2, Part 2

Book 2, Part 2 of the Katha Upanishad — the Fifth Valli — continues Yama's instruction directly from the Fourth Valli without break in the dialogue. The valli opens with the body as a town of eleven gates belonging to the Unborn, names the Self in a series of guises — swan in the bright heaven, Vasu in the sky, sacrificer on the hearth, guest in the house, dweller among men, in the gods, in truth, in the wide sky — and turns to the one Self that remains itself one while becoming many according to what it enters. The figures of the one fire and the one air that take the form of whatever they touch are placed beside the figure of the sun, the eye of the whole world, which is not stained by what the eyes see. The valli closes with the verse that the sun does not shine there, nor the moon and the stars, nor the lightnings, nor this fire — when he shines, everything shines after him.

## Context

The Katha Upanishad is one of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads — a small group of texts traditionally received as the philosophical core of the late Vedic literature. It belongs to the Krishna Yajurveda branch of the Vedic corpus and is composed in Sanskrit verse, mostly in the *anushtubh* meter. Modern scholarship places its composition in roughly the fifth to third century BCE; the principal Upanishads as a group are dated to the centuries on either side of the Buddha. The text is anonymous; the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship.

Book 2, Part 2 is the middle valli of the second *adhyaya*. It follows directly from Book 2, Part 1: where the Fourth Valli established the distinction between what the senses meet and the Self by which all perception is known, the Fifth Valli holds the same distinction inside the figure of the body itself — the town of eleven gates — and turns from the recognition of the Self behind the senses to the recognition of the one Self within the many forms of the world. The dialogue is uninterrupted; the Bhagavad Gita's later verses on the supreme Person, on the sun and the moon that do not shine in the highest abode, and on the one undivided that appears as if divided in beings, belong to the textual line this valli sets out.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the valli proceeds by repeated figures of the one and the many. The Self is named in a list of dwellings — heaven, sky, hearth, house, men, gods, truth, sky again — and then in a list of natural elements that take many forms while remaining one: the one fire that becomes the form of whatever it burns, the one air that becomes the form of whatever it enters, the one sun whose light is not stained by what the eyes see. Reading the verses with attention to the recurrence of the figure, and to the slight rotation of vocabulary it gathers each time, recovers the form the teaching has.

Second, the instruction moves inward. The opening verse names the body as a town of eleven gates with the Unborn at its center; the closing verses name the Self that the wise perceive within their own Self and to whom alone belongs eternal peace. The recognition the valli is preparing is not a metaphysical conclusion but a turn of attention: the one Self in the many forms of the world is the same Self present in the heart, and the recognition of one is the recognition of the other. Reading slowly enough to let each figure register before the next is given, rather than collapsing them into a single doctrine, keeps the form the valli has.

## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 15 (Purushottama Yoga) — paired Sanskrit passages on the supreme Person and on the one source by whose light the world is lit; the Gita's verse that the sun, the moon, and fire do not illumine that supreme abode (Gita 15.6) is the close parallel to the Katha's closing verse of this valli (Katha 2.2.15), and the Gita's wider teaching on the one Person who upholds the perishable and the imperishable develops the figure the Fifth Valli sets out in the one Self within the many forms.
- **structural-parallel** → Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 13 (Kshetra Kshetrajna Vibhaga Yoga) — paired passages on the hidden knower behind what is known; the Gita's Chapter 13 names the one undivided that appears as if divided in beings, the Katha's Fifth Valli holds the same recognition through the figures of the one fire that becomes many forms, the one air that becomes many forms, and the one Self within all things that remains itself one.
- **structural-parallel** → Daodejing, Chapters 1–10 — paired short passages on a continuity beneath the named forms of the world; the Daodejing's early chapters return to the contrast between the named and the nameless and to the source from which the ten thousand things issue, the Katha's Fifth Valli holds a comparable recognition in the figures of the one fire and the one air that become many according to what they enter while remaining themselves one.
