# Katha Upanishad, Book 1, Part 2

Book 1, Part 2 of the Katha Upanishad — the Second Valli — is the section in which Yama, having tested Nachiketas in Part 1 by offering boons of long life and worldly prosperity, begins the deeper instruction the boy has insisted on. The valli opens with the distinction the rest of the Upanishad will turn on: there is the good (*shreyas*) and there is the pleasant (*preyas*); each takes a person to a different end; the wise choose the good, and the foolish choose the pleasant. Yama praises Nachiketas for refusing the pleasant when offered, and turns to the teaching the boy has earned: the syllable OM as the goal of all the Vedas; the Self that is not born and does not die, smaller than the small and greater than the great, hidden in the heart of all creatures; the Self that cannot be reached by argument or by much learning, but only by the one whom the Self chooses. The valli closes with the famous question of the One whose food is the warriors and the priests, and whose condiment is death itself.

## Context

The Katha Upanishad is one of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads — a small group of texts traditionally received as the philosophical core of the late Vedic literature. It belongs to the Krishna Yajurveda branch of the Vedic corpus and is composed in Sanskrit verse, mostly in the *anushtubh* meter. Modern scholarship places its composition in roughly the fifth to third century BCE; the principal Upanishads as a group are dated to the centuries on either side of the Buddha. The text is anonymous; the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship.

Book 1, Part 2 follows the opening narrative of Part 1 directly. Where Part 1 set the situation — Nachiketas sent to Yama, the granting of the first two boons, the question about what survives death that Yama tries to deflect — Part 2 begins the instruction. Yama tests Nachiketas once more at the opening verses, distinguishing the good from the pleasant and praising the boy for refusing the second; the rest of the valli is the first sustained portion of Yama's teaching on the Self. The famous verses on the self that is not born nor dies (Katha 2.18–19) are echoed almost verbatim in the Bhagavad Gita's Chapter 2 (2.19–20), where Krishna quotes them in his reply to Arjuna's refusal to fight; the textual line of descent between the Upanishadic and the Gita's teaching on the imperishable self is most visible in this valli.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the valli is built on a sequence of paired distinctions. The good and the pleasant; the wise and the foolish; the path that takes a person to the goal and the path that misses the end; the deluded who do not even hear of the Self and the few who, having heard, attain. The valli proceeds by holding pairs in contrast rather than by argument. Reading Book 1, Part 2 with the distinction-pairs held visible — and noticing where the same distinction returns through different vocabulary — recovers the form the valli has.

Second, the closing third of the valli describes the Self in negations and reversals. The Self is not slain when the slayer thinks he slays; smaller than the small, greater than the great; not gained by Veda, by understanding, by much hearing; not reached by the unsubdued. Reading the section with attention to the negative phrasings — the Self defined by what cannot be said of it directly — keeps the form the Upanishad gives the teaching.

## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 2 (Sankhya Yoga) — paired Sanskrit philosophical passages on the imperishable self in close textual relation; the Gita's verses on the self that is not slain when the body is slain (2.19–20) are an almost verbatim echo of Katha 2.18–19 in this valli, and the Gita's wider argument on action without attachment develops material the Katha had been preparing.
- **structural-parallel** → Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 13 (Kshetra Kshetrajna Vibhaga Yoga) — paired passages on the distinction between the witness and what is witnessed; the Gita's Chapter 13 sets the field (*kshetra*) and the knower of the field (*kshetrajna*) as analytic terms, the Katha's Second Valli holds the same distinction in earlier Upanishadic form, with the Self present in the embodied without being bound by what changes around it.
- **structural-parallel** → Plato, Apology — paired short texts in which the seeker refuses the easier path; Socrates refusing to abandon his practice of questioning for an easier life or a quieter end, Nachiketas refusing the boons of long life and worldly prosperity Yama offers in order to press for the question that has brought him.
