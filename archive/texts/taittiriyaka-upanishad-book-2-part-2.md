# Taittiriyaka Upanishad, Book 2, Part 2

Book 2, Part 2 of the Taittiriyaka Upanishad — the Second Anuvaka of the Brahmanandavalli — completes the treatment of the *annamaya* (the self consisting of the essence of food) that the First Anuvaka introduced and begins the recursive nested structure that will carry the rest of the Valli. The opening sloka, set off in Müller's quotation marks, places food at the centre of the visible order: *from food are produced all creatures which dwell on earth; then they live by food, and in the end they return to food. For food is the oldest of all beings, and therefore it is called* sarvauṣadha — *panacea, that which consists of all herbs and quiets the heat of the body of all beings.* The prose continuation extends the recognition: those who worship food as Brahman obtain all food; from food all creatures are produced; by food, when born, they grow; and the etymological play on the word *anna* names food as that which is fed on and that which feeds on beings. The Anuvaka then introduces the formula that the Brahmanandavalli will repeat at each of the five sheaths: *anyontara ātmā prāṇamayaḥ* — *different from this, which consists of the essence of food, is the other, the inner Self, which consists of breath; the former is filled by this; it also has the shape of man; like the human shape of the former is the human shape of the latter.* The anatomical mapping of the next sheath then begins — Prana is its head, Vyana its right arm, Apana its left arm — and the closing pointer *on this there is also the following sloka* turns the reader to the Third Anuvaka.

## Context

The Taittiriyaka Upanishad is one of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads — a small group of texts traditionally received as the philosophical core of the late Vedic literature. It belongs to the Krishna Yajurveda branch of the Vedic corpus and to the Taittiriya school of that branch; the Upanishad sits inside the Taittiriya Aranyaka. Modern scholarship places it among the older prose Upanishads, in roughly the sixth to fifth century BCE — earlier than the verse Upanishads of the Katha and Mundaka. The text is anonymous; the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship.

Book 2, Part 2 sits inside the Brahmanandavalli, the Second Valli of the Taittiriyaka. The First Anuvaka opened the Valli with the *Saha navavatu* peace mantra, the defining sloka *satyam jñānam anantam brahma*, and the emanation cascade ending in food and the man consisting of food's essence; the Second Anuvaka completes that opening by placing the *annamaya* under its own sloka and inaugurating the formula by which the Valli will move inward. The pattern the Anuvaka establishes — *sloka, prose, anatomical mapping, formula introducing the next inner sheath, pointer to the next sloka* — will repeat through the next three anuvakas, with the inner self moving from food to breath to mind to knowledge to bliss. The Brahmanandavalli is the *locus classicus* for the *pañca-kośa* teaching that becomes central to the later Vedanta literature, and the Second Anuvaka is the moment at which the recursive structure of the teaching is set out.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the Anuvaka is built on a recursive formula. The same sentence — *different from this, which consists of [outer], is the other, the inner Self, which consists of [inner]; the former is filled by this; it also has the shape of man* — will return in the Third, Fourth, and Fifth Anuvakas with the next pair of sheaths in place of the present pair. Reading the Second Anuvaka with attention to the recursive form, and to the way the formula will pivot to introduce each successive sheath, recovers the procedure the Brahmanandavalli is preparing — a literary structure that nests selves inside selves the way the formula nests one phrase inside the next.

Second, the Anuvaka treats etymology and anatomy as instruction rather than ornament. The line that food is *anna* because it is fed on or because it feeds on beings is not a digression — it is the Valli's way of locating the recognition in the language the recognition will be carried in. The anatomical mapping of the *prāṇamaya* — head, right arm, left arm, trunk, support — is not a description of the body in any technical sense; it is the same shape the *annamaya* had, given again at the next level of inwardness. Reading the Anuvaka with the etymology and the anatomy held as part of the form, rather than as commentary on it, keeps the form the Brahmanandavalli has when its recursion is most visible.

## The text

The text is held in the Archive library under `upanishads-muller-part2`, chapter 5 (Müller translation, 1884 — *The Sacred Books of the East*, Volume XV; via SacredTexts.com). Library chapter 5 is shared across twenty-three Archive entities — four parts of the Katha Upanishad, the six parts of the Mundaka Upanishad, the twelve anuvakas of the Sikshavalli (Book 1) of the Taittiriyaka, and the First Anuvaka of the Brahmanandavalli (Book 2, Part 1) — and continues here into the Second Anuvaka without break in the chapter. Book 2, Part 2 of the Taittiriyaka — the entity here — occupies passages `5.234` through `5.238`. Passage `5.234` is the "Second Anuvaka" header. Passage `5.235` carries the opening sloka in Müller's quotation marks, with footnote reference `[*2]` carried over from the source — Müller's editorial gloss on the food-sloka. Passage `5.236` carries the prose continuation on the *annamaya*, with footnote reference `[*3]` on the worship-of-food line. Passage `5.237` carries the introduction of the *prāṇamaya* — the recursive formula and the beginning of the next anatomical mapping — and is split mid-line by Müller's editorial paragraphing (the passage ends with "Apana (down-breathin" and the continuation lands at the start of the Third Anuvaka). Passage `5.238` is the closing pointer "On this there is also the following Sloka:". The Brahmanandavalli does not number its anuvaka-internal verses in the same way the Sikshavalli did; Müller's prose runs continuously through each Anuvaka with embedded slokas in quotation marks. Passage `5.239` is the "Third Anuvaka" header that opens Book 2, Part 3 of the Taittiriyaka and is not part of this entity. The library chapter continues past this entity into the rest of the Taittiriyaka and the remaining Upanishads of the volume. About three minutes' reading.

## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Taittiriyaka Upanishad, Book 2, Part 1 — paired immediately consecutive Anuvakas of the Brahmanandavalli; the First Anuvaka introduced the emanation cascade ending in food and the man consisting of the essence of food, the Second Anuvaka places that food-self under its own framing sloka and inaugurates the recursive formula by which the Valli will descend through breath, mind, knowledge, and bliss. The opening of the recognition and the start of its recursive unfolding, given in two adjacent units.
- **structural-parallel** → Mundaka Upanishad, Book 2, Part 1 — paired Sanskrit passages on the layered constitution that follows from the cosmological emanation; the Mundaka's First Khanda of the Second Mundaka holds the cosmic Person whose head is the sky, eyes the sun and moon, ears the quarters, breath the wind, heart the universe (Mundaka 2.1.4) — emanation given as a single-figure anatomy — the Taittiriyaka's Second Anuvaka holds the same anatomical procedure but applies it nested: each inner self is given a head, a right arm, a left arm, a trunk, and a support, and the next inner self is introduced under the same form. Paired moments where the late-Vedic literature places the structure of the body at the heart of the recognition of Brahman.
- **structural-parallel** → Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 15 (Purushottama Yoga) — paired Sanskrit passages on food, body, and the Person who underlies them; the Gita's Chapter 15 names Krishna as the digestive fire (*vaiśvānara*) seated in the body of living beings, joined with the up-breath and the down-breath, who consumes the four kinds of food (Gita 15.14), the Taittiriyaka's Second Anuvaka holds the related recognition through the food-self (*annamaya*) which is then filled by the breath-self (*prāṇamaya*) — both texts placing food and breath at the threshold of the recognition of the Person within, in close vocabulary across the late-Vedic prose and the epic verse.
