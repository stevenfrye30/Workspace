# Dhammapada, Chapter 22 (The Downward Course)

Chapter 22 of the Dhammapada — the *Niraya-vagga*, "The Downward Course" — is a chapter of fourteen verses on hell as the destination of wrong-doing. The opening verse states the form: *he who says what is not, goes to hell; he also who, having done a thing, says I have not done it. After death both are equal, they are men with evil deeds in the next world* (306). The lie and the denial of one's deed both lead to the same place. Verse 307 turns the warning to the monastic: *many men whose shoulders are covered with the yellow gown are ill-conditioned and unrestrained; such evil-doers by their evil deeds go to hell.* Verse 308 holds the chapter's harshest image: *better it would be to swallow a heated iron ball, like flaring fire, than that a bad unrestrained fellow should live on the charity of the land.* Verses 309 and 310 hold a paired warning on adultery — four things gained by the man who covets his neighbour's wife (bad reputation, uncomfortable bed, punishment, hell), and the short pleasure of the frightened in the arms of the frightened. Verse 311 holds the grass-blade simile: *as a grass-blade, if badly grasped, cuts the arm, badly-practised asceticism leads to hell.* Verses 312–313 turn the figure to half-effort: a carelessly performed act, a broken vow, hesitating obedience — these bring no great reward; the careless pilgrim only scatters the dust of his passions more widely. Verse 314 holds the paired contrast on doing and not doing: an evil deed is better left undone (one repents); a good deed is better done (one does not repent). Verse 315 holds the well-guarded frontier fort simile: *like a well-guarded frontier fort, with defences within and without, so let a man guard himself. Not a moment should escape.* The chapter closes on a four-fold parallel sequence (316–319). Three negative verses share the same form: *they who are ashamed of what they ought not to be ashamed of, and are not ashamed of what they ought to be ashamed of, such men, embracing false doctrines enter the evil path* (316); the same form for *fear* (317); the same form for *forbid* (318). The fourth verse reverses the form: *they who know what is forbidden as forbidden, and what is not forbidden as not forbidden, such men, embracing the true doctrine, enter the good path* (319).

## Context

The Dhammapada is one of the books of the Khuddaka Nikaya, the fifth and shortest section of the Sutta Pitaka in the Pali Canon. It collects 423 verses arranged in twenty-six chapters by theme — pairs, heedfulness, mind, flowers, the sage, evil, and so on. The verses are attributed to the Buddha and were transmitted orally for several centuries before being committed to writing in Sri Lanka in the first century BCE. The collection took shape in roughly the third century BCE, in connection with the Theravada transmission of the canon.

Chapter 22 is the *Niraya-vagga*, "The Downward Course." Its fourteen verses are numbered 306 through 319 in the standard continuous numbering of the Dhammapada — and in F. Max Müller's 1881 translation, the version held in the Archive library, the count and numbering match the standard exactly, with no merged or missing verse of the kind that occurs in Chapters 4, 6, 8, 11, 14, 17, and 19. The Pali word *niraya* — hell, the downward course, the place of suffering — is rendered uniformly by Müller as "hell" or as "evil path" through the chapter, with the chapter title given as "The Downward Course." Each verse is short — two or four lines in the Pali — and each completes one statement of the figure.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the chapter is built on a single recurring premise: certain actions, attitudes, and habits lead to *niraya*, the downward course, and the chapter walks through several of them. Lying and concealment (306). Hypocritical wearing of the yellow gown (307). Living unrestrained on alms (308). Covetousness toward another's wife (309–310). Badly-practised asceticism (311). Half-effort, broken vows, hesitating obedience (312–313). Doing the evil deed and leaving the good undone (314). Letting the moment escape (315). And finally — embracing false doctrines about shame, fear, and what is to be forbidden (316–318). Reading the chapter as a chain of specific warnings, rather than as a single doctrine of hell, recovers the form the chapter is built on. The chapter does not develop a cosmology of *niraya*; it walks through the kinds of action that go there.

Second, the closing four-fold parallel (316–319) is the chapter's structural pillar. Three negative verses share the same form — the false reversal of what one ought to be ashamed of, what one ought to fear, what one ought to forbid — and each closes with *embracing false doctrines enter the evil path*. The fourth verse reverses the form: knowing what is forbidden as forbidden and what is not as not, *embracing the true doctrine, enter the good path*. The chapter does not close on the warning; it closes on the inverse of the warning, with three negatives setting up one positive. Reading the closing four verses as a single parallel form with a turn at the fourth, rather than as four separate aphorisms, keeps the form the chapter has.

- **structural-parallel** → Dhammapada, Chapter 9 (Evil) — the earlier chapter with the most direct doctrinal partner; Chapter 9 with thirteen verses on evil and good as gradual, ripening, and inescapable, Chapter 22 with fourteen verses on the specific kinds of wrong-doing that lead to hell. Chapter 9 holds the doctrine of consequence; Chapter 22 holds the catalogue of actions whose consequence is hell.
- **structural-parallel** → Dhammapada, Chapter 18 (Impurity) — the earlier chapter with the same kind of catalogue-and-summary structure; Chapter 18 with twenty-one verses on impurity culminating in *ignorance is the greatest taint* (243), Chapter 22 with fourteen verses on wrong-doing culminating in the four-fold parallel on false and true doctrine (316–319). Both chapters move from specific warnings to a closing summary that holds the chapter's deeper logic.
- **structural-parallel** → Dhammapada, Chapter 13 (The World) — the earlier chapter with the same combination of warning and counsel; Chapter 13 verse 167 holds a four-fold opening prohibition (do not follow the evil law, do not live in thoughtlessness, do not follow false doctrine, be not a friend of the world), Chapter 22 verses 316–319 hold a four-fold closing parallel on false doctrine and the evil path. The figure of *false doctrine leading to the evil path* recurs across the chapters.
- **structural-parallel** → Ezekiel 1 — the Hebrew shelf-pair on the unsparing visual confrontation; Ezekiel's wheels and creatures and fire-that-does-not-consume, Dhammapada 22 verse 308 with the heated iron ball that would be better swallowed than the alms eaten by the unrestrained. Both texts hold an unsparing image as the figure that is meant to arrest the reader and force a different orientation.
- **structural-parallel** → Plato, Apology — the Greek pair on the figure of false shame and false fear; Socrates' insistence that one should fear injustice rather than death, Dhammapada 22 verses 316 and 317 on those who are ashamed of what they ought not to be ashamed of and fear what they ought not to fear. Both texts hold the figure that the moral mistake often consists in misallocating these primary affects.
