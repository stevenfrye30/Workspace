# Dhammapada, Chapter 12 (Self)

Chapter 12 of the Dhammapada — the *Atta-vagga*, "Self" — is a chapter of ten verses turning on a single Pali word, *atta*. The opening verse states the form: *if a man hold himself dear, let him watch himself carefully; during one at least out of the three watches a wise man should be watchful* (157). The verses that follow keep the figure of the self in view across several positions. Self-direction comes before teaching others (158). The man who would teach must first make himself as he teaches others to be, for the self is the most difficult thing to subdue (159). The fourth verse holds the chapter's central declaration: *self is the lord of self, who else could be the lord? with self well subdued, a man finds a lord such as few can find* (160). The next four verses turn the figure to consequence — evil self-begotten crushes the foolish like a diamond breaks a precious stone (161); the wicked bring themselves to the state their enemy wishes (162); bad deeds are easy, good deeds are difficult (163); the foolish who scorn the venerable bear fruit to their own destruction, like the Katthaka reed (164). Verse 165 holds the chapter's other central declaration: *by oneself the evil is done, by oneself one suffers; by oneself evil is left undone, by oneself one is purified. Purity and impurity belong to oneself, no one can purify another.* The closing verse turns the figure to attention: let no one forget his own duty for another's, however great, but be always attentive to his own duty (166).

## Context

The Dhammapada is one of the books of the Khuddaka Nikaya, the fifth and shortest section of the Sutta Pitaka in the Pali Canon. It collects 423 verses arranged in twenty-six chapters by theme — pairs, heedfulness, mind, flowers, the sage, evil, and so on. The verses are attributed to the Buddha and were transmitted orally for several centuries before being committed to writing in Sri Lanka in the first century BCE. The collection took shape in roughly the third century BCE, in connection with the Theravada transmission of the canon.

Chapter 12 is the *Atta-vagga*, "Self." Its ten verses are numbered 157 through 166 in the standard continuous numbering of the Dhammapada — and in F. Max Müller's 1881 translation, the version held in the Archive library, the count and numbering match the standard exactly, with no merged or missing verse of the kind that occurs at the close or middle of Chapters 4, 6, 8, and 11. The Pali word *atta* — self, oneself — is the single thread through the chapter and is rendered uniformly by Müller as "self" or "oneself" throughout. Each verse is short — two or four lines in the Pali — and each completes one statement on the self.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the chapter is built on the figure of the *atta* — the self — taken across positions of watching, directing, teaching, mastering, suffering, purifying, and attending. The same word holds together verses that warn (the wicked are crushed by what they themselves have begotten) and verses that empower (one is one's own lord, one's own purifier). Reading the chapter as a sustained turn on a single Pali word, rather than as ten separate aphorisms, recovers the form the chapter is built on.

Second, two verses stand at the chapter's hinges. Verse 160 is the chapter's positive declaration: *self is the lord of self, who else could be the lord?* — the central Buddhist self-reliance line. Verse 165 is the chapter's reciprocal declaration: *Purity and impurity belong to oneself, no one can purify another* — the central Buddhist self-purification line. The two verses hold the chapter's logic in compressed form: there is no other lord, and there is no other purifier; one is one's own beginning and end. Reading the two declarations as the structural pillars of the chapter, with the warnings and instructions arranged around them, keeps the form the chapter has.

## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Dhammapada, Chapter 2 (Heedfulness) — the earlier chapter that already worked the figure of self-watchfulness as the path of the deathless; Chapter 2 with twelve verses turning the contrast between heedfulness and heedlessness, Chapter 12 with ten verses turning the self as the object that watchfulness is directed toward. The two chapters share the figure of attention to oneself, with Chapter 2 stressing the quality of the attention and Chapter 12 stressing what the attention is paid to.
- **structural-parallel** → Dhammapada, Chapter 5 (The Fool) — the earlier chapter that already held the verse that the fool is his own greatest enemy; Chapter 5 verse 66 holds the figure that fools of little understanding have themselves for their greatest enemies, Chapter 12 verses 161–162 hold the same figure as evil self-begotten that crushes the foolish and as wickedness that brings a man to the state his enemy wishes. The figure of the self as either lord or enemy is shared across the chapters.
- **structural-parallel** → Dhammapada, Chapter 11 (Old Age) — the immediate predecessor on the shelf, with the same kind of body-and-self imagery in different proportions; Chapter 11 with ten verses on the body's decay and the house-builder finally seen, Chapter 12 with ten verses on the self that one must watch, direct, and purify alone. Both chapters work the figure of inward responsibility, but Chapter 11 stresses what the body cannot keep, and Chapter 12 stresses what the self alone can do.
- **structural-parallel** → Epictetus, Enchiridion, Chapters 1–5 — the Stoic shelf-pair on self-government; the Enchiridion's opening with the distinction between what is and is not within one's power and the practices that follow from that distinction, Dhammapada 12 with verses 160 and 165 on the self as one's own lord and one's own purifier. Both texts hold the figure of inward responsibility as the ground of practice, articulated through different vocabulary.
- **structural-parallel** → Plato, Apology — the Greek pair on the duty to attend to oneself rather than to one's reputation or possessions; Socrates' charge to the Athenians that they care for their souls before their wealth, Dhammapada 12 verse 166 on attending to one's own duty rather than another's, however great. Both texts hold the figure that one's own work is the work that cannot be delegated.
