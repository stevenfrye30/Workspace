# Taittiriyaka Upanishad, Book 1, Part 1

Book 1, Part 1 of the Taittiriyaka Upanishad — the First Anuvaka of the First Valli, the Sikshavalli ("the chapter on pronunciation") — is the opening peace invocation that traditionally frames recitation of the text. The Anuvaka is short: six Vedic deities are called upon to be propitious — Mitra, Varuna, Aryaman, Indra, Brihaspati, and the wide-striding Vishnu; adoration is given to Brahman, and adoration to Vayu the air, who is named here as the visible Brahman; the speaker pledges to proclaim Brahman directly, to proclaim the right (*ṛta*), and to proclaim the true (*satya*); and the mantra closes with the protective formula — *avatu mām, avatu vaktāram* — "may it protect me, may it protect the speaker" — and the threefold sealing *Om! Peace! peace! peace!* The Anuvaka does not yet teach; it places the speaker, the teacher, and the recitation that is about to begin under the protection of the deities and of Brahman.

## Context

The Taittiriyaka Upanishad is one of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads — a small group of texts traditionally received as the philosophical core of the late Vedic literature. It belongs to the Krishna Yajurveda branch of the Vedic corpus and to the Taittiriya school of that branch; the Upanishad sits inside the Taittiriya Aranyaka, the larger Yajurvedic text from which the principal Upanishads of this school are drawn. The Taittiriyaka is composed in Sanskrit prose with embedded mantras, and modern scholarship places it among the older prose Upanishads, in roughly the sixth to fifth century BCE — earlier than the verse Upanishads of the Katha and Mundaka. The text is anonymous; the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship.

Within the Müller volume that holds this entity, the Taittiriyaka begins immediately after the close of the Mundaka. The literary mode shifts: the Katha and the Mundaka are dialogues in verse — Yama answering Nachiketas, Angiras answering Saunaka — and unfold their teaching through sustained instruction; the Taittiriyaka is structured as a recitation manual, organized into three *vallis* (books) and divided within each book into short *anuvakas* ("sections" or "lessons"). The First Valli is the Sikshavalli, the chapter on *śikṣā* — the discipline of pronunciation, the first of the six *vedāngas* (auxiliaries to the Veda); the Second Valli is the Brahmanandavalli, on the bliss of Brahman; the Third Valli is the Bhriguvalli, the dialogue between Bhrigu and his father Varuna. The First Anuvaka of the Sikshavalli is the *Saṁ no mitraḥ* peace mantra — the *śānti-mantra* traditionally recited at the opening (and at the close) of study of the Taittiriyaka.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the Anuvaka is built on rhythm rather than on argument. The verbs are imperatives or pledges — "may [the deities] be propitious," "adoration to Brahman," "I shall proclaim the right, I shall proclaim the true," "may it protect me, may it protect the teacher." The form is the form of recitation: short clauses, parallel openings, sealed by the threefold *Peace*. Reading the Anuvaka aloud, allowing the parallelism to register before any attempt to extract content, recovers the form the text has and the place the Anuvaka holds — preparation, not declaration.

Second, the Anuvaka is structured around the speaker, the teacher, and the act of recitation. The first lines call on the deities; the middle lines name what is to be said; the closing lines ask protection for the one who says it and for the one who has taught him. The Anuvaka is not addressed to a passive reader — it is the speech a reciter says before reciting, and the recitation that follows is held inside the protective frame the Anuvaka opens. Reading the Anuvaka with attention to who is speaking, who is being protected, and what is being prepared for, rather than as a self-contained statement, keeps the form the Taittiriyaka gives this opening.

## The text

The text is held in the Archive library under `upanishads-muller-part2`, chapter 5 (Müller translation, 1884 — *The Sacred Books of the East*, Volume XV; via SacredTexts.com). Library chapter 5 was already shared across ten Archive entities — four parts of the Katha Upanishad (Book 1, Part 3 and Book 2, Parts 1–3) and the six parts of the Mundaka Upanishad — and continues here into the Taittiriyaka without break in the chapter. Book 1, Part 1 of the Taittiriyaka — the entity here — occupies passages `5.160` through `5.166`. Passage `5.160` is the "Taittiriyaka-Upanishad" collection header; passage `5.161` is the "First Valli" book header; passage `5.162` is the subtitle of the First Valli, "Or, the Chapter on Siksha (Pronunciation)"; passage `5.163` is the "First Anuvaka" part header. Passages `5.164` through `5.166` carry the body of the Anuvaka, which Müller renders as a single numbered block opening with the verse marker `1.` rather than as separately numbered mantras; an editorial note "(1-5)" at the start of passage `5.166` signals that conventional Sanskrit editions divide the same block into five short mantras. Passage `5.167` is the "Second Anuvaka" header that opens Book 1, Part 2 of the Taittiriyaka and is not part of this entity. The library chapter continues past this entity into the rest of the Taittiriyaka and the remaining Upanishads of the volume. About three minutes' reading.

## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Mundaka Upanishad, Book 1, Part 1 — paired Upanishadic openings that place the speaker in relation to the teacher before any teaching is given; the Mundaka's First Khanda opens with the lineage descending from Brahma through Atharvan, Angir, Bharadvaga, and Angiras, and with Saunaka approaching Angiras with fuel in hand and a question, the Taittiriyaka's First Anuvaka opens with the peace mantra under which the speaker, the teacher, and the recitation are placed for protection — paired forms of the same care for the framing of the inquiry, before the substantive teaching of either text begins.
- **structural-parallel** → Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 4 (Jnana Karma Sanyasa Yoga) — paired Sanskrit openings that anchor the teaching in a chain of transmission; the Gita's Chapter 4 opens with Krishna naming the imperishable yoga as told to Vivasvan, Vivasvan to Manu, Manu to Ikshvaku, descending through royal sages until lost and now told again, the Taittiriyaka's First Anuvaka opens by calling on the Vedic deities, naming Vayu as the visible Brahman, and asking protection for both the speaker and the teacher — both texts placing the recitation that follows inside an explicitly framed line of transmission.
- **structural-parallel** → Analects, Book 1 (Xue Er) — paired short pedagogical openings that foreground the disposition of learning before any content is given; the Analects opens with the line that learning and timely practice is its own pleasure and with verses on the right disposition of the student and the teacher, the Taittiriyaka's First Anuvaka opens with the joint protective formula for the one who studies and the one who teaches and with the threefold *Peace* — both texts placing the conditions of learning before the learning itself.
