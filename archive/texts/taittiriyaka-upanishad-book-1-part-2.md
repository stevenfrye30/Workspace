# Taittiriyaka Upanishad, Book 1, Part 2

Book 1, Part 2 of the Taittiriyaka Upanishad — the Second Anuvaka of the First Valli, the Sikshavalli — is the briefest unit in the Archive so far: a single short verse that does the work of opening a lesson. *Om! Let us explain Siksha, the doctrine of pronunciation* — and then the six headings of *śikṣā* in order: letter (*varṇa*), accent (*svara*), quantity (*mātrā*), effort in the formation of letters (*bala*), modulation (*sāma*), and the union of letters or *sandhi* (*santāna*). The Anuvaka closes with the bare formula *iti śīkṣādhyāyaḥ* — "this is the lecture on Siksha." It does not yet teach pronunciation; it announces the discipline by name and lists the categories under which the rest of the Valli will work.

## Context

The Taittiriyaka Upanishad is one of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads — a small group of texts traditionally received as the philosophical core of the late Vedic literature. It belongs to the Krishna Yajurveda branch of the Vedic corpus and to the Taittiriya school of that branch; the Upanishad sits inside the Taittiriya Aranyaka. Modern scholarship places it among the older prose Upanishads, in roughly the sixth to fifth century BCE — earlier than the verse Upanishads of the Katha and Mundaka. The text is anonymous; the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship.

Book 1, Part 2 sits inside the Sikshavalli, the chapter on *śikṣā* — the discipline of pronunciation, the first of the six *vedāngas* (auxiliaries to the Veda). Where the First Anuvaka opened the recitation under the protection of the Vedic deities and the threefold *Peace*, the Second Anuvaka does what the First did not: it announces the lecture. The pattern is the school-form of the late-Vedic prose Upanishads — first the protective frame, then the topic-statement, then the body of the instruction. The substance of the Sikshavalli that follows the Second Anuvaka does not stay narrowly inside phonetics; the third anuvaka turns to the *upanishad* (the inner meaning) of the *samhita* (the union of letters) under five heads — the worlds, the heavenly lights, knowledge, offspring, and the self — and the rest of the Valli moves outward through teacher–pupil verses, meditation on *Om*, and the famous *brahmavid āpnoti param* line. The Second Anuvaka is the door through which the rest of the lesson enters.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the Anuvaka is built on the form of a topic-list. *Varṇa, svara, mātrā, balam, sāma, santānaḥ* — the six headings are given in order, with no expansion and no commentary. The form is the form of the *vedāṅga* manuals on which the late-Vedic schools were built: the topics are named first, the elaborations come later. Reading the Anuvaka with attention to the listing form — and to the small marker *iti* that closes it — recovers the place the Anuvaka holds in the structure of the Valli, and the place the school-form holds in the Upanishad as a whole.

Second, the Anuvaka holds the recitational frame the First Anuvaka opened. *Om* opens the verse; the formula *iti śīkṣādhyāyaḥ* closes it. The lecture is announced inside the protective space the peace mantra has already prepared. Reading the Anuvaka alongside the First — the protection given, then the topic named — rather than as a self-contained text, keeps the form the Sikshavalli has at its opening.

## The text

The text is held in the Archive library under `upanishads-muller-part2`, chapter 5 (Müller translation, 1884 — *The Sacred Books of the East*, Volume XV; via SacredTexts.com). Library chapter 5 is shared across eleven Archive entities — four parts of the Katha Upanishad, the six parts of the Mundaka Upanishad, and Book 1, Part 1 of the Taittiriyaka — and continues here into the Second Anuvaka without break in the chapter. Book 1, Part 2 of the Taittiriyaka — the entity here — occupies passages `5.167` and `5.168`. Passage `5.167` is the "Second Anuvaka" header; passage `5.168` carries the full content of the Anuvaka in a single verse-numbered passage. Müller renders the Anuvaka as a single short prose verse opening with the marker `1.`; there are no editorial footnotes inside the verse and no paragraph splits. Passage `5.169` is the "Third Anuvaka" header that opens Book 1, Part 3 of the Taittiriyaka and is not part of this entity. The library chapter continues past this entity into the rest of the Taittiriyaka and the remaining Upanishads of the volume. About one minute's reading.

## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Taittiriyaka Upanishad, Book 1, Part 1 — paired immediately consecutive Anuvakas of the Sikshavalli that together form the formal opening of the Upanishad; the First Anuvaka places the speaker, the teacher, and the recitation under the protection of the Vedic deities and the threefold *Peace*, the Second Anuvaka announces the topic of the lecture and lists its six headings — the protective frame and the topic-statement of the late-Vedic school-form, given in two short adjacent units.
- **structural-parallel** → Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 4 (Jnana Karma Sanyasa Yoga) — paired Sanskrit openings that name the teaching before unfolding it; the Gita's Chapter 4 names the imperishable yoga that Krishna formerly told to Vivasvan and that has now been declared again, the Taittiriyaka's Second Anuvaka names the discipline of *śikṣā* under its six headings as the lecture about to be given — both texts placing the announcement of the teaching above the teaching itself, in close formal vocabulary.
- **structural-parallel** → Analects, Book 1 (Xue Er) — paired short pedagogical openings that hold the form of disciplined learning before any content is given; the Analects opens with the line that learning and timely practice is its own pleasure, the Taittiriyaka's Second Anuvaka opens with the formal announcement of the lecture and the named headings of the discipline — paired forms of the same care for the structure of learning, in two distinct civilizational vocabularies.
