# Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 2, Part 3

## Primary Text

F. Max Müller, *The Upanishads, Part II*, Sacred Books of the East, Vol. XV (1884). Public domain via SacredTexts.com. Verbatim Müller fragments from Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 2, Part 3 (the Third Brahmana of the Second Adhyaya). The complete Müller translation of all six verses is not preserved in this archive entry; the lines below are the verbatim quotations available — the verse-1 two-forms formula, framing lines for the cosmic-to-bodily transition, the verse-6 color-figure description, and the closing prose at 2.3.6 containing the *neti neti* teaching and the *satyasya satyam* formula.

**Verse 1.** "There are two forms of Brahman, the material and the immaterial, the mortal and the immortal, the solid and the fluid, sat and tya" — *(i.e. sat-tya, true)*.

**Verse 3 (closing line).** "So far with regard to the Devas."

**Verse 4 (opening line).** "Now with regard to the body."

**Verse 6.** "Like a saffron-coloured raiment, like white wool, like cochineal, like the flame of fire, like the white lotus, like sudden lightning."

**Closing prose (2.3.6).** "Next follows the teaching of Brahman by No, no! — for there is nothing else higher than this (if one says): 'It is not so.'" — *atha ata ādeśo neti neti* — "now therefore the teaching is 'not this, not this.'" "Then comes the name 'the True of the True,' the senses being the True, and he (the Brahman) the True of them."

## Atlas notes

Book 2, Part 3 of the Brihadaranyaka Upanishad — the Third Brahmana of the Second Adhyaya — is a compact unit that introduces two of the most-cited formulas of the Upanishadic literature and works them out through the same kind of paired-mapping the Brihadaranyaka has been using throughout. The opening verse holds the two-forms doctrine: *dve vāva brahmaṇo rūpe, mūrtaṁ caivāmūrtaṁ ca, martyaṁ cāmṛtaṁ ca, sthitaṁ ca yacca, sad-tya cca* — *there are two forms of Brahman, the material and the immaterial, the mortal and the immortal, the solid and the fluid, sat and tya*. The closing parenthetical of the verse holds the etymology: *(i.e. sat-tya, true)* — the two forms are the two halves of *satya*, the true.

The verses that follow work the two-forms doctrine across two parallel fields. Verses 2 and 3 give the cosmic mapping. Everything except air and sky is material, mortal, solid, definite; the essence of that side is the sun that shines, for he is the essence of *sat*, the definite. Air and sky, by contrast, are immaterial, immortal, fluid, indefinite; the essence of that side is the person in the disk of the sun, for he is the essence of *tyad*, the indefinite. The verse closes with the framing line *so far with regard to the Devas.*

Verses 4 and 5 give the bodily mapping. Everything in the body except breath and the ether within the body is material, mortal, solid, definite; the essence of that side is the Eye, for it is the essence of *sat*. Breath and the ether within the body, by contrast, are immaterial, immortal, fluid, indefinite; the essence of that side is the person in the right eye, for he is the essence of *tyad*. The mapping is parallel to the cosmic mapping at every term — the same paired qualifiers, the same outer term and inner term — with sun and Eye as the outer essences and the person in the disk of the sun and the person in the right eye as the inner essences.

Verse 6 holds the appearance of that person — the inner essence of the body's bodily-cosmic correspondence. The appearance is given in a sequence of six color-figures: *like a saffron-coloured raiment, like white wool, like cochineal, like the flame of fire, like the white lotus, like sudden lightning*. The verse closes with the line that the one who knows this, his glory is like unto sudden lightning.

The Brahmana then closes with a short prose section that introduces the *neti neti* teaching: *Next follows the teaching of Brahman by* No, no! *— for there is nothing else higher than this (if one says): "It is not so."* The Sanskrit is *atha ata ādeśo neti neti* — *now therefore the teaching is "not this, not this."* The closing line returns to the formula that closed the First Brahmana of this Adhyaya: *Then comes the name "the True of the True," the senses being the True, and he (the Brahman) the True of them.* The First and the Third Brahmanas of the Second Adhyaya thus close on the same formula — *satyasya satyam* — bracketing the symbolic-mapping Brahmana between them.

## Context

The Brihadaranyaka Upanishad is one of the two earliest of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads, sharing that position with the Chandogya. It belongs to the Shukla Yajurveda branch of the Vedic corpus and forms the closing portion of the Shatapatha Brahmana — the *Aranyaka* in its name marks it as a forest-text recited at the late stages of Vedic study. Modern scholarship places the Brihadaranyaka in roughly the eighth to sixth century BCE, prior to the Buddha; the text is anonymous, and the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship. The text is the longest of the principal Upanishads and is typically structured in six *adhyayas* (chapters), each containing several *brahmanas* (sections).

Book 2, Part 3 is the Third Brahmana of the Second Adhyaya. The First Brahmana ran the long Gargya Balaki / Ajatasatru dialogue and closed on the *spider with its thread* figure for emergence and the formula *satyasya satyam* — *the True of the True*; the Second Brahmana shifted to compact symbolic prose and mapped the seven openings of the head onto the seven imperishable cosmic figures and the seven named Rishis; the Third Brahmana now holds the two-forms doctrine, the cosmic-bodily mapping, the color-figure description of the person in the right eye, and the *neti neti* teaching — closing again on *satyasya satyam*. The two formulas the Brahmana introduces — *neti neti* and *satyasya satyam* — are among the most-cited statements of the Upanishadic corpus and have continued to be drawn on across the later Vedanta tradition. The First Brahmana of this Adhyaya demonstrated the *neti neti* method without naming it: Ajatasatru's repeated *No, no! Do not speak to me on this* against each of Gargya's twelve identifications was the same procedure; the Third Brahmana now names the procedure as the *teaching of Brahman by "No, no!"*.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the Brahmana works the two-forms doctrine by parallel mapping, with each pair of verses giving the same form of identification in a different field. The pair *material / immaterial* receives the same paired qualifiers in each pair (mortal / immortal, solid / fluid, definite / indefinite), and the same form of essence-statement at the close (the sun for the cosmic *sat*, the person in the disk of the sun for the cosmic *tya*; the Eye for the bodily *sat*, the person in the right eye for the bodily *tya*). Reading the Brahmana with attention to the strict parallel between the cosmic and the bodily mappings — and to the way the same paired qualifiers are filled at every level — recovers the form the Brihadaranyaka is using.

Second, the Brahmana closes on two formulas that hold opposite forms but the same recognition. *Neti neti* — *not this, not this* — is the via negativa of the Upanishadic literature; *satyasya satyam* — *the True of the True* — is the positive identification by which the Brahman is named. The Brahmana places them in immediate succession at the close, with the line *for there is nothing else higher than this* sitting between them. Reading the closing prose with the negative and positive formulas held alongside one another, attending to the way they are not opposed but paired, keeps the form the Brihadaranyaka has when its closing recognition is given through both directions at once.

The Brahmana contains several boundary features that should be preserved honestly:

- **Verse-1 footnote.** Passage `5.556` carries verse 1 with footnote `[*2]` glossing the *sat-tya = satya* etymology; the parenthetical *(i.e. sat-tya, true)* in Müller's prose is part of the verse, not editorial intrusion.
- **Cosmic-to-bodily transition.** The transition between the cosmic mapping (verses 2–3) and the bodily mapping (verses 4–5) is held inside verse 3 with the framing line *so far with regard to the Devas* — Müller's English form for *iti adhidaivatam*. Verse 4 then opens with *now with regard to the body* — *atha adhyātmam*. The transition is not given a numbered marker of its own.
- **Verse-6 split.** Verse 6 — the appearance of the person in the right eye — is split across passages `5.561` and `5.562`. Passage `5.561` ends mid-line at *like white wool* and passage `5.562` resumes *like cochineal*, carrying the rest of the color-figures and the closing line on the knower's glory.
- **Closing prose with footnote.** Passage `5.563` carries the closing prose section — the *neti neti* teaching and the *satyasya satyam* recognition — without its own verse-number marker, with footnote `[*1]` at the open. The passage is structured as the Brahmana's closing rather than as a separately numbered verse.

Passage `5.564` is the "Fourth Brahmana" header that opens Book 2, Part 4 of the Brihadaranyaka and is not part of this entity.
