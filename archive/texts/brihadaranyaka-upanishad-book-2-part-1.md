# Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 2, Part 1

Book 2, Part 1 of the Brihadaranyaka Upanishad — the First Brahmana of the Second Adhyaya — opens the second of the six Adhyayas in a different literary mode from the first. The First Adhyaya worked through cosmogony, ritual mapping, identity-statements, and triple-mappings in long prose units; the Second Adhyaya opens directly into a sustained dialogue between two named persons: Gargya Balaki, a proud Brahmana described as *a man of great reading*, and Ajatasatru of Kasi, a king. The setup is given in verse 1: Gargya approaches the king and says *Shall I tell you Brahman?* Ajatasatru, observing that Gargya is the kind of teacher whom learned men flock to as one would flock to Janaka of Mithila for patronage, offers a thousand cows for the speech he is about to receive.

What Gargya offers, however, is not what Ajatasatru is willing to receive. Verses 2 through 13 hold a strict twelve-fold catalogue. Gargya offers, in turn, twelve identifications of Brahman: *the person that is in the sun*; *in the moon, and in the mind*; *in the lightning, and in the heart*; *in the ether, and in the ether of the heart*; *in the wind, and in the breath*; *in the fire, and in the heart*; *in the water, in seed, and in the heart*; *in the mirror*; the sound that follows a man while he moves; *in space*; the shadow; and finally, the person in the body. To each, Ajatasatru replies in the same form: *No, no! Do not speak to me on this. I adore him verily as…* — and gives the correct adoration with its proper consequence. The sun is rightly adored as *the supreme, the head of all beings, the king*; the moon as *the great, clad in white raiment, as Soma, the king*; the lightning as *the luminous*; the ether as *what is full, and quiescent*; the wind as *Indra Vaikuntha, as the unconquerable army of the Maruts*; the fire as *powerful*; the water as *likeness*; the mirror as *the brilliant*; the following sound as *life*; space as *the second who never leaves us*; the shadow as *death*; the body as *the embodied*. Each correct adoration has its reward — the adorer becomes supreme, becomes Soma, becomes luminous, becomes powerful, and so on through the catalogue.

When the twelve identifications have been given and twelve times rejected, the Brahmana places its narrative interruption: *Then Gargya became silent.* Ajatasatru asks, *Thus far only?* Gargya answers, *Thus far only.* Ajatasatru replies, *This does not suffice to know it (the true Brahman).* Gargya then offers what reverses the conventional caste-relation that the dialogue has been holding open: *Then let me come to you, as a pupil.* Ajatasatru's answer makes the reversal explicit: *Verily, it is unnatural that a Brahmana should come to a Kshatriya, hoping that he should tell him the Brahman. However, I shall make you know him clearly.* He takes Gargya by the hand and rises.

The teaching that follows is given by demonstration. Ajatasatru and Gargya come to a man who is asleep. Ajatasatru calls the sleeper by names — *Thou, great one, clad in white raiment, Soma, King* — names from the catalogue of correct adorations. The sleeper does not rise. Then, rubbing him with his hand, Ajatasatru wakes him, and the man arises. The question follows: *When this man was thus asleep, where was then the person (purusha), the intelligent? and from whence did he thus come back?* Gargya does not know.

Ajatasatru's answer (verses 17–19) gives the three-states analysis the later Vedanta tradition has continued to draw on. In deep sleep, the intelligent person — having absorbed within himself, through the intelligence of the senses, all intelligence — lies in the ether which is in the heart. In dream, he moves about; he is as it were a great king, as it were a great Brahmana, rising and falling, holding subjects within him as a king holds his subjects. In profound sleep, when he knows nothing, he moves through the seventy-two thousand arteries called *Hitā* that spread from the heart through the body, and rests in the surrounding body as a young man, a king, or a Brahmana might rest having reached the summit of happiness. The Brahmana closes with the figure of the spider and the sparks: *as the spider comes out with its thread, or as small sparks come forth from fire, thus do all senses, all worlds, all Devas, all beings come forth from that Self. The Upanishad (the true name and doctrine) of that Self is "the True of the True." Verily the senses are the true, and he is the true of the true.*

## Context

The Brihadaranyaka Upanishad is one of the two earliest of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads, sharing that position with the Chandogya. It belongs to the Shukla Yajurveda branch of the Vedic corpus and forms the closing portion of the Shatapatha Brahmana — the *Aranyaka* in its name marks it as a forest-text recited at the late stages of Vedic study. Modern scholarship places the Brihadaranyaka in roughly the eighth to sixth century BCE, prior to the Buddha; the text is anonymous, and the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship. The text is the longest of the principal Upanishads and is typically structured in six *adhyayas* (chapters), each containing several *brahmanas* (sections).

Book 2, Part 1 is the First Brahmana of the Second Adhyaya — and the major literary turn of the Upanishad up to this point. Where the First Adhyaya worked through accumulated cosmogonic, ritual, and analytic prose, the Second Adhyaya opens directly into named dialogue and will continue in that mode for several Brahmanas. The Gargya Balaki / Ajatasatru dialogue is one of three or four major dialogues in the Brihadaranyaka in which a Kshatriya — typically a king — teaches a Brahmana, reversing the conventional Brahmanical authority of teaching. The same dialogue is held in close form in the Kaushitaki Upanishad (Adhyaya 4), where Ajatasatru's role is similar; the Brihadaranyaka's version is the older, with the explicit narrative interruption at *Then Gargya became silent* and the explicit caste-reversal commentary. The closing formula *the Upanishad of that Self is "the True of the True"* — *satyasya satyam* — is one of the Brihadaranyaka's most condensed identity-statements and will return at later points in the Upanishad.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the Brahmana is built on strict template repetition for its first half. Twelve identifications are offered; each receives the same form of rejection and correction; each correction names the proper adoration and its reward. Reading the catalogue with attention to the unchanging form of Ajatasatru's reply — *No, no! Do not speak to me on this. I adore him verily as…* — recovers what the Brahmana is doing in this section: it is not refuting twelve doctrines one by one but holding the same procedure twelve times, with the substance changing inside the same shape, until the procedure runs out of material and Gargya falls silent.

Second, the Brahmana is structured around the narrative interruption that breaks the catalogue. *Then Gargya became silent* is given on its own short passage; what follows is the teaching that the catalogue could not provide. The interruption marks the place where Ajatasatru's teaching shifts from spoken correction to demonstrative action — taking Gargya by the hand, walking to the sleeping man, calling him by his names, rubbing him awake. Reading the Brahmana with the interruption held as a structural marker, attending to the way the dialogue moves from speech-and-rejection into walking-and-rubbing, keeps the form the Second Adhyaya has at its opening.

Paired moments where the late-Vedic literature places a careful catalogue beside its own insufficiency.
