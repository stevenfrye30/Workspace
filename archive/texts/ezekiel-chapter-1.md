# Ezekiel, Chapter 1

Chapter 1 of the Book of Ezekiel is the opening vision of the prophet — the chariot vision (the *merkavah*) seen by the priest Ezekiel in Babylonian exile, by the Chebar canal in the fifth year of his captivity. Out of a cloud of fire come four living creatures with four faces each, wheels within wheels, a crystal firmament, and at last a throne with a figure surrounded by amber. Ezekiel falls on his face. The chapter describes what he saw through accumulated qualifications — *the appearance of the likeness of the glory of the Lord* — without claiming to have seen God directly.

## Context

The Book of Ezekiel is one of the four major prophetic books of the Hebrew Bible — alongside Isaiah, Jeremiah, and Daniel — and runs to forty-eight chapters. Its narrator and protagonist, the priest Ezekiel son of Buzi, was taken into Babylonian captivity in the deportation of 597 BCE, eleven years before the destruction of Jerusalem. He prophesied from his settlement at the Chebar canal in Babylonia, with dated visions and oracles spanning roughly twenty-two years (c. 593–571 BCE). Modern scholarship generally accepts the bulk of the book as composed during or shortly after Ezekiel's own lifetime, with later editorial additions.

Chapter 1 is the opening of the book and the call vision of the prophet. It is dated precisely — "in the thirtieth year, in the fourth month, in the fifth day of the month, as I was among the captives by the river of Chebar" — and the vision occupies the chapter's twenty-eight verses. The commission narrative that follows in chapters 2–3 places Ezekiel's call in the response to the vision, rather than alongside it as in Isaiah 6.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the chapter narrates the vision through layered qualifications. The narrator does not say "Ezekiel saw God" but "the appearance of a throne," "the likeness as the appearance of a man," "the appearance of the likeness of the glory of the LORD." The chain of qualifying nouns — *appearance*, *likeness*, *form* — keeps distance between what Ezekiel saw and any direct claim about its object. Reading the vision with attention to this layering, rather than collapsing it into a more direct description, recovers the form the prophet was given.

Second, the imagery of the chapter does not arrange itself into a single coherent picture. Four creatures with four faces each; wheels with rims full of eyes; a firmament above the creatures; a throne above the firmament; fire, amber, sapphire, the bow in the cloud — the elements pile up faster than visual imagination can hold them. The accumulation itself is part of how the chapter proceeds. Reading the chapter as an accumulation that exceeds visual reconstruction, rather than as a description that should resolve into a clear image, tracks the way the text proceeds.
