# Aeneid, Book 6

Book 6 of the Aeneid is the underworld book of the Roman epic — Aeneas's descent into the realm of the dead at Cumae on the coast of Italy. Guided by the Sibyl of Cumae, Aeneas walks through the regions of the dead, encounters figures from his own past (among them Dido, who refuses to speak), and reaches his father Anchises in the Elysian Fields. There Anchises shows him the souls of future Romans waiting to be born — the parade of heroes that names Rome's coming history down to Augustus. Book 6 stands near the center of the poem.

## Context

The Aeneid is the foundational epic of Roman literature, composed by the poet Publius Vergilius Maro — Virgil — between roughly 29 and 19 BCE. It runs to twelve books in dactylic hexameter Latin and follows the Trojan prince Aeneas from the fall of Troy to his arrival in Italy and the wars he must fight to establish the Roman line. Virgil died in 19 BCE before completing his final revisions; the text was published posthumously on the orders of Augustus, against Virgil's expressed wish that it be burned. The poem became, almost immediately, the classical Roman text against which later Latin literature measured itself.

Book 6 sits at the center of the poem — the sixth of twelve books — and marks the hinge between the Odyssey-like first half (the wandering journey from Troy to Italy) and the Iliad-like second half (the wars in Latium that follow Aeneas's arrival). The book takes Aeneas first to the temple of Apollo at Cumae, then to the Sibyl, then through the underworld with the golden bough as his passage, and out again through the Gate of Ivory. The action covers a single day. Within the underworld itself, the book is structured as a sequence of regions — the borderlands of the unburied, the fields of mourning where Dido walks, Tartarus seen at a distance, and the Elysian Fields where Anchises waits — each marked off from the others by river, gate, or boundary.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, Book 6 is written in answer to *Odyssey* 11. Virgil knew Homer's underworld book intimately and rewrote it deliberately: where Odysseus stands at the trench and summons the dead to come up to him, Aeneas walks down through the underworld himself and moves through its regions; where Achilles laments that he would rather be a poor man's slave than king of the dead, Anchises shows Aeneas the future glory of Rome. Reading Book 6 with the Homeric original in view tracks both the imitation and the points where Virgil departs from it.

Second, Book 6 ends with the Gate of Ivory — through which, the text says, the dead send false dreams. Aeneas leaves the underworld through this gate rather than through the Gate of Horn, through which true dreams come. The detail has been debated since antiquity and resists easy explanation. Reading the closing lines as a deliberate puzzle, rather than supplying a settled meaning, leaves the book where the text leaves it.

## The text

The text is held in the Archive library under `aeneid`, chapter 6 (Dryden translation, 1697 — via classics.mit.edu). The library indexes Dryden's translation by book, with library chapter 6 corresponding to the Aeneid's Book 6. Roughly 901 lines in the Latin, rendered in Dryden's heroic couplets; about an hour and a half's reading in modern English.
