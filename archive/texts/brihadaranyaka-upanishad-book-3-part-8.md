# Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 3, Part 8

Book 3, Part 8 of the Brihadaranyaka Upanishad — the Eighth Brahmana of the Third Adhyaya, traditionally cited as the *Akshara Brahmana* — continues the Janaka Vaideha contest opened in the First Brahmana and continued through the Second, Third, Fourth, Fifth, Sixth, and Seventh. The court frame is not reset. The Seventh Brahmana closed on Uddalaka Aruni held in voluntary silence after Yajnavalkya's antaryamin-answer; the Eighth opens directly into the return of a prior challenger — *Then Vakaknavi said* — and the Brahmana works through the only return of a previously silenced challenger in the Adhyaya. Vakaknavi is the patronymic of the same Gargi Vakaknavi who was silenced in the Sixth Brahmana with the head-falling threat. The narrator names her here only by the patronymic; Yajnavalkya, when he speaks to her, addresses her by the personal name — *Ask, O Gargi* — and the two names sit on the same person across the Brahmana.

The Brahmana is the only one in the Adhyaya in which the named challenger addresses the assembly directly. Gargi opens with an address to the audience, not to Yajnavalkya: *Venerable Brahmanas, I shall ask him two questions. If he will answer them, none of you, I think, will defeat him in any argument concerning Brahman.* The contest's prior Brahmanas had been three-party in form — challenger, priest, narrator's frame — with the assembled Brahmanas of the Kurus and the Pankalas as the silent audience and the named participants speaking to each other. The Eighth opens with the audience addressed directly by the named challenger, and the contest's fourth wall is broken. The framing-question Gargi puts to the assembly — whether anyone will be able to defeat Yajnavalkya — names the contest's stake in public terms before the substantive question is asked. Verse 12 will close the Brahmana with the same audience addressed in the same form, with the framing-question's answer given by Gargi herself.

Verse 2 gives the warrior-imagery framing of the two questions. *O Yagnavalkya, as the son of a warrior from the Kasis or Videhas might string his loosened bow, take two pointed foe-piercing arrows in his hand and rise to do battle, I have risen to fight thee with two questions.* The simile carries the contest's substance into the field of armed combat — the Kasis and Videhas, the two warrior-clans of the same eastern kingdom that hosts the *aśvamedha* — and names Gargi's two questions as the two arrows. The Adhyaya's prior contest-language had used the priestly *brahmodya* form throughout; the Eighth Brahmana is the only one to translate the contest into warrior-imagery, with Gargi as the warrior and the two questions as the arrows.

The two questions are given in the same wording and receive the same answer. Verse 3 holds the question on its full substantive locus: *that of which they say that it is above the heavens, beneath the earth, embracing heaven and earth, past, present, and future, tell me in what is it woven, like warp and woof?* Verse 4 gives Yajnavalkya's answer: *That of which they say that it is above the heavens, beneath the earth, embracing heaven and earth, past, present, and future, that is woven, like warp and woof, in the ether (akasa).* The warp-and-woof formula is the same one Gargi had used in the Sixth Brahmana — the textile figure of *X is woven, like warp and woof, in Y* — but the substantive question has changed: in the Sixth Brahmana, Gargi had walked the ladder upward through cosmic levels until Yajnavalkya stopped her at the worlds of Brahman; in the Eighth, she asks the warp-and-woof question of the totality named in spatial-temporal terms, and Yajnavalkya answers with one term: the ether.

Verse 5 gives Gargi's bow. *I bow to thee, O Yagnavalkya, who hast solved me that question. Get thee ready for the second.* The bow is the formal acknowledgment of the first answer; the *get thee ready for the second* signals the second arrow. Yajnavalkya's invitation — *Ask, O Gargi* — is given a second time, with the verbatim form preserved. Verse 6 then asks the same question Gargi had asked in verse 3, in the same wording, without modification. Verse 7 gives the same answer Yajnavalkya had given in verse 4, in the same wording, without modification. The repetition is verbatim across the second cycle of question-and-answer; only after the second answer is given does Gargi continue with the substantive follow-up: *In what then is the ether woven, like warp and woof?* The two-arrow form holds the same question twice, with the substantive demand for the next support of the ladder placed only after the second answer.

The substantive body — verses 8 through 11 — gives the doctrine of the *Akshara* (the imperishable). Verse 8 is the catalogue of negations. *O Gargi, the Brahmanas call this the Akshara (the imperishable). It is neither coarse nor fine, neither short nor long, neither red (like fire) nor fluid (like water); it is without shadow, without darkness, without air, without ether, without attachment, without taste, without smell, without eyes, without ears, without speech, without mind, without light (vigour), without breath, without a mouth (or door), without measure, having no within and no without, it devours nothing, and no one devours it.* The catalogue runs through pairs (coarse/fine, short/long, red/fluid), through privatives at the level of the elements (air, ether), through privatives at the level of the senses (eyes, ears, speech, mind), and through the limit-condition (*having no within and no without*). The form is the negative form of the doctrine of Brahman that the Adhyaya has named in earlier Brahmanas — Ushasta's seer-of-sight in the Fourth, Uddalaka's antaryamin-negation in the Seventh — and the Eighth Brahmana now gives the negative form across an extended catalogue.

Verse 9 turns the doctrine to the Akshara's command. *By the command of that Akshara, O Gargi, sun and moon stand apart. By the command of that Akshara, O Gargi, heaven and earth stand apart. By the command of that Akshara, O Gargi, what are called moments (nimesha), hours (muhurta), days and nights, half-months, months, seasons, years, all stand apart. By the command of that Akshara, O Gargi, some rivers flow to the East from the white mountains, others to the West, or to any other quarter. By the command of that Akshara, O Gargi, men praise those who give, the gods follow the sacrificer, the fathers the Darvi-offering.* The five-fold *by the command of that Akshara* anchors the doctrine in the cosmic-temporal-ritual order: the celestial bodies hold their separation, the world's vertical division is held, the Sanskrit time-vocabulary names its full ladder, the rivers flow on their courses, and the social-ritual order — gift-giving, sacrifice, the offerings to the fathers — is sustained.

Verse 10 names the contrast. *Whosoever, O Gargi, without knowing that Akshara (the imperishable), offers oblations in this world, sacrifices, and performs penance for a thousand years, his work will have an end. Whosoever, O Gargi, without knowing this Akshara, departs this world, he is miserable (like a slave). But he, O Gargi, who departs this world, knowing this Akshara, he is a Brahmana.* The contrast is between the works of the one who has not known the Akshara — finite even at the scale of a thousand years' penance — and the standing of the one who has. The Brahmana's social term — *he is a Brahmana* — is given here as the standing of the one who departs in knowledge.

Verse 11 closes the substantive answer with the third form of the seer-of-sight negation. *That Brahman, O Gargi, is unseen, but seeing; unheard, but hearing; unperceived, but perceiving; unknown, but knowing. There is nothing that sees but it, nothing that hears but it, nothing that perceives but it, nothing that knows but it. In that Akshara then, O Gargi, the ether is woven, like warp and woof.* The four-clause negation in but-construction matches the Seventh Brahmana's verse-23 form; the four-clause uniqueness runs in *nothing that X but it* form, where the Seventh Brahmana's verse 23 had run in *no other X but he* form. The closing line of verse 11 returns to the warp-and-woof formula and gives Gargi's substantive follow-up its answer: the next support, beyond the ether, is the Akshara.

Verse 12 closes the Brahmana with Gargi's public concession. *Then said Gargi: 'Venerable Brahmans, you may consider it a great thing, if you get off by bowing before him. No one, I believe, will defeat him in any argument concerning Brahman.'* The address is again to the assembly, not to Yajnavalkya, and the framing-question of verse 1 is answered in the same form: no one will defeat him. The narrator's line then closes the Brahmana: *After that Vakaknavi held her peace.* The closing form returns to silence; the closing pronoun is again feminine; the narrator names her by the patronymic, as in the opening of the Brahmana. The contest's narrative has now produced the only voluntary public concession by a named challenger that the Adhyaya holds.

## Context

The Brihadaranyaka Upanishad is one of the two earliest of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads, sharing that position with the Chandogya. It belongs to the Shukla Yajurveda branch of the Vedic corpus and forms the closing portion of the Shatapatha Brahmana — the *Aranyaka* in its name marks it as a forest-text recited at the late stages of Vedic study. Modern scholarship places the Brihadaranyaka in roughly the eighth to sixth century BCE, prior to the Buddha; the text is anonymous, and the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship. The text is the longest of the principal Upanishads and is typically structured in six *adhyayas* (chapters), each containing several *brahmanas* (sections).

Book 3, Part 8 holds three of the Adhyaya's most distinctive features in concentrated form. The first is the return of Gargi. The Sixth Brahmana had introduced Gargi Vakaknavi as the first female challenger and the only challenger to be silenced by induced threat rather than by voluntary acceptance of an answer. The Eighth Brahmana brings her back. No other challenger in the Adhyaya returns; Asvala, Artabhaga, Bhugyu, Ushasta, Kahola, and Uddalaka Aruni each appear once and fall silent. Gargi alone has two appearances, and the form of her return — addressed by the narrator as *Vakaknavi*, by Yajnavalkya as *Gargi*, with the warrior-imagery and the two-arrow framing — places her on a different procedural footing from the prior challengers. The Brahmana works through the contest's only second appearance, and the contest's only voluntary public concession follows from it.

The second is the Akshara doctrine. The *Akshara* — *the imperishable*, *that which does not perish* — is one of the principal terms of the Upanishadic doctrine of Brahman, and the Eighth Brahmana is its earliest sustained statement in the Brihadaranyaka. The doctrine is given in three forms held inside the same Brahmana: the catalogue of negations of verse 8, the *by the command of* construction of verse 9, and the seer-of-sight negation of verse 11. The Mundaka Upanishad will give the term *Akshara* its principal Upanishadic theological place in the higher-knowledge / lower-knowledge distinction at its opening — *the higher knowledge is that by which the indestructible (akṣaram) is apprehended* — and the Eighth Brahmana sits in the Vedanta literature as one of the two earliest sources of the term and as the source of its negative-cataloguing form. Reading the Brahmana with the three forms of the Akshara-doctrine held alongside each other recovers the form the Upanishadic literature has when it works the doctrine of the imperishable through the contest's procedure.

The third is the audience-address. The Brahmana is the only one of the Adhyaya in which the named challenger speaks to the assembly directly. Verse 1 opens with *Venerable Brahmanas, I shall ask him two questions. If he will answer them, none of you, I think, will defeat him in any argument concerning Brahman*; verse 12 closes with *Venerable Brahmans, you may consider it a great thing, if you get off by bowing before him. No one, I believe, will defeat him in any argument concerning Brahman.* The contest's *brahmodya* form had been three-party in the prior Brahmanas — challenger, priest, narrator — with the assembled Brahmanas of the Kurus and the Pankalas as the silent audience that the contest is for; the Eighth Brahmana names the audience as a fourth party, with the named challenger speaking to it at both ends of the substantive exchange. The contest's stake — the one thousand cows enclosed by Janaka in the First Brahmana's opening — is named in public terms by the named challenger before the substantive question is asked, and the same address closes the Brahmana with the contest's outcome publicly declared.

The closing form is silence again. The contest's eight closings now sit in the pattern silence (voluntary) / private withdrawal / silence (voluntary) / silence (voluntary) / silence (voluntary) / silence (induced) / silence (voluntary) / silence (voluntary, with public concession). The Adhyaya's silence-closing predominates; the Second Brahmana's private withdrawal and the Sixth's induced silence remain the two deviations; the Eighth Brahmana's voluntary silence is held with the audience-addressed concession, which no other Brahmana of the Adhyaya carries.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the Brahmana's substantive body sits inside an unusual procedural frame. Verses 1–7 hold the entry-procedure: Gargi addresses the assembly, names the two-arrow form, asks the same question twice, receives the same answer twice, and bows to Yajnavalkya in between. The form of the entry is preserved at full length — same question verbatim, same answer verbatim, the bow inserted between the two cycles — and the substantive demand for the next support of the warp-and-woof ladder is given only after the second answer. Reading verses 1–7 with the verbatim repetition kept exactly as the source has it — neither smoothed away nor compressed into *Gargi asked twice* — recovers the form the Brahmana has when its substantive body is approached through a doubled cycle of question-and-answer with a bow inserted in the middle. The doctrine of the Akshara is not given as the answer to the first question; it is given as the answer to the substantive follow-up that comes only after the second cycle has closed.

Second, the Brahmana works the doctrine of Brahman in three forms across four verses. Verse 8 catalogues the privative negations — *neither X nor Y* and *without X* — at the level of the cosmos, the senses, and the limit. Verse 9 catalogues the affirmations of cosmic and ritual order under *by the command of*. Verse 10 catalogues the contrast of the ignorant and the knower at the moment of departure from the world. Verse 11 catalogues the four-clause negation in but-construction and uniqueness. The four verses sit in the substantive body as four panels of the same doctrine; reading them with the four forms held alongside each other — privative, command-affirmation, departure-contrast, sense-negation — recovers the form the Brahmana has when its substantive answer holds the doctrine of the imperishable across four distinct rhetorical procedures rather than through one.

## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 3, Part 6 — paired Brahmanas of the Janaka Vaideha contest with the same named challenger, Gargi Vakaknavi; the Sixth Brahmana introduced Gargi as the first female challenger and the first to be silenced by induced threat — the head-falling warning that closes Yajnavalkya's interruption of the warp-and-woof ladder at the worlds of Brahman — and the Eighth Brahmana brings her back as the only challenger of the Adhyaya to make a second appearance. The two Brahmanas hold the warp-and-woof formula across both — *X is woven, like warp and woof, in Y* — and the substantive distinction between them is the substantive locus the formula is asked of: in the Sixth, the eleven cosmic supports walked upward to the worlds of Brahman; in the Eighth, the totality named in spatial-temporal terms with the two-arrow framing and the Akshara as the next support beyond the ether. Gargi's two appearances together form the only multi-Brahmana sequence the Adhyaya gives to a single named challenger.
- **structural-parallel** → Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 3, Part 7 — paired immediate-predecessor Brahmanas of the Janaka Vaideha contest holding the four-clause but-construction negation of the witnessing Self at the close of the substantive answer; the Seventh Brahmana closed Uddalaka Aruni's antaryamin-cycle at verse 23 with *unseen, but seeing; unheard, but hearing; unperceived, but perceiving; unknown, but knowing. There is no other seer but he, there is no other hearer but he, there is no other perceiver but he, there is no other knower but he* — the Eighth Brahmana closes the Akshara-cycle at verse 11 with *unseen, but seeing; unheard, but hearing; unperceived, but perceiving; unknown, but knowing. There is nothing that sees but it, nothing that hears but it, nothing that perceives but it, nothing that knows but it.* The two Brahmanas hold the same four-clause but-construction in the same closing position with the uniqueness-clause given in *no other X but he* form in the Seventh and *nothing that X but it* form in the Eighth — the same doctrine across two consecutive Brahmanas with the variable substituted at the substantive locus.
- **structural-parallel** → Mundaka Upanishad, Book 1, Part 1 — paired Sanskrit Upanishadic teachings on the Akshara as the substantive object of the higher knowledge; the First Khanda of the First Mundaka opens with the higher/lower knowledge distinction and names the *akshara* as that which the higher knowledge apprehends — *the higher (knowledge) is that by which the Indestructible (akshara) is apprehended* — and gives the catalogue of the senses and faculties the Akshara is *not perceived by*: *that which cannot be seen nor seized, which has no family and no caste, no eyes nor ears, no hands nor feet, the eternal, the omnipresent, infinitesimal, that which is imperishable, that it is which the wise regard as the source of all beings.* The Brihadaranyaka's Eighth Brahmana of the Third Adhyaya gives the same Akshara doctrine in catalogue-of-negations form at verse 8: *neither coarse nor fine, neither short nor long, neither red nor fluid; without shadow, without darkness, without air, without ether, without attachment, without taste, without smell, without eyes, without ears, without speech, without mind, without light, without breath, without a mouth, without measure, having no within and no without.* Both Upanishadic loci hold the doctrine of the Akshara through the catalogue of what the Akshara is not.
