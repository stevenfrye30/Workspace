# Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 5, Part 12

Book 5, Part 12 of the Brihadaranyaka Upanishad — the Twelfth Brahmana of the Fifth Adhyaya — works the Adhyaya forward in a procedural form that combines several of the shapes the Adhyaya has used. The Brahmana is slightly more developed than the most-compact two-passage Brahmanas of the Adhyaya — the Third, Sixth, Seventh, Eighth, Ninth, and Tenth — at three passages and one verse split across two body passages. The substantive content combines a dialectical opening (paired partial views of Brahman and their substantive resolution), a named-figure dialogue (Pratrida and his father), a two-syllable *nirukti* etymology (Vi from *vishtani*, Ram from *ramante*), and a substantive benefit-seal (*All beings rest on him, all beings delight in him who knows this*).

The Brahmana opens with the food-and-life dialectic at `5.1216`. *Some say that food is Brahman, but this is not so, for food decays without life (prana).* The substantive form is the rejection of a partial view through its substantive insufficiency: food cannot be Brahman alone because food without life decays. *Others say that life (prana) is Brahman, but this is not so, for life dries up without food.* The same form is given for the second partial view: life cannot be Brahman alone because life without food dries up. The dialectical resolution follows: *Then these two deities (food and life), when they have become one, reach that highest state (i.e. are Brahman).* The substantive doctrine is that neither food nor life is Brahman alone; their substantive union — *when they have become one* — is the substantive Brahman. Müller's parenthetical *(i.e. are Brahman)* preserves the substantive identification at the close of the dialectical resolution.

The named-figure dialogue follows in the same passage. *Thereupon Pratrida said to his father: 'Shall I be able to do any good to one who knows this, or shall I be able to do him any harm?'* The named-figure Pratrida — whose father is unnamed — asks the substantive question whether the knower of the food-and-life-as-one doctrine can be affected by external good or harm. The father's answer is given with a substantive gestural detail: *The father said to him, beckoning with his hand: 'Not so, O Pratrida; for who could reach the highest state, if he has only got to the oneness of these two?'* The substantive answer is in the negative — *Not so* — and follows with a substantive question that names the substantive insufficiency: the substantive oneness of food and life is not yet the substantive highest state. The substantive procedure of the *vidyā* requires more than the substantive dialectical resolution; the substantive *Vi-Ram* etymological identification follows.

The two-syllable *nirukti* etymology is given on the close of the verse. The verse is split across passages `5.1216` and `5.1217` mid-clause at *He then said to him: 'Vi; / verily, food is Vi*. Passage `5.1217` resumes the substantive identification: *Vi; verily, food is Vi, for all these beings rest (vishtani) on food.* The substantive form is the *nirukti* derivation of the substantive name *Vi* from the verbal root *viś-* (to enter, to settle, to rest), with Müller's parenthetical *(vishtani)* preserving the underlying Sanskrit verbal form. *He then said: 'Ram; verily, life is Ram, for all these beings delight (ramante) in life.* The same form is given for the substantive name *Ram*, derived from the verbal root *ram-* (to delight), with Müller's parenthetical *(ramante)* preserving the underlying Sanskrit verbal form.

The substantive benefit-seal closes the Brahmana on the same passage at `5.1217`: *All beings rest on him, all beings delight in him who knows this.* The substantive doctrine is that the substantive identifications of food with *Vi* (*all beings rest on food*) and life with *Ram* (*all beings delight in life*) transfer to the knower of these substantive identifications: *All beings rest on him, all beings delight in him.* The substantive benefit is the substantive transfer of the substantive cosmic functions of food and life onto the knower of the substantive identifications. The Brahmana ends inside the substantive benefit-seal.

## Context

The Brihadaranyaka Upanishad is one of the two earliest of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads, sharing that position with the Chandogya. It belongs to the Shukla Yajurveda branch of the Vedic corpus and forms the closing portion of the Shatapatha Brahmana — the *Aranyaka* in its name marks it as a forest-text recited at the late stages of Vedic study. Modern scholarship places the Brihadaranyaka in roughly the eighth to sixth century BCE, prior to the Buddha; the text is anonymous, and the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship. The text is the longest of the principal Upanishads and is typically structured in six *adhyayas* (chapters), each containing several *brahmanas* (sections).

Book 5, Part 12 holds three of the Adhyaya's distinctive features in concentrated form. The first is the dialectical opening that pairs and rejects two partial views before giving the substantive resolution. *Some say food is Brahman, but this is not so, for food decays without life. Others say life is Brahman, but this is not so, for life dries up without food.* The substantive form is the *iti vai* citation-form (*some say... others say...*) followed by the substantive critique through the substantive insufficiency of each partial view. The form is rare in the Adhyaya — the prior Brahmanas had given substantive identifications without the dialectical critique of partial views — and it sits at the open of the Twelfth Brahmana as a substantive procedural shape that the Adhyaya has not used in its earlier compact Brahmanas.

The second is the named-figure dialogue with Pratrida. The Adhyaya's prior named figures — Kauravyayaniputra in Part 1, Pragapati in Parts 2 and 3 — had been given without dialogic frame (Kauravyayaniputra in *iti vacanāt* citation-form, Pragapati as the substantive subject of identification or as the father of the threefold descendants in parable). The Twelfth Brahmana gives a named son and his unnamed father in dialogic frame, with the son's substantive question and the father's substantive answer (with the substantive gestural detail of the hand-beckoning) preserved as part of the substantive teaching. The form is a compressed instance of the longer named-figure dialogues of the Brihadaranyaka's earlier Adhyayas (the Yajnavalkya cycle of Books 3 and 4, the Pragapati-and-three-classes parable of Part 2 of the Fifth Adhyaya); the Twelfth Brahmana gives the form in radically more compact scale.

The third is the two-syllable *Vi-Ram* etymological identification. The substantive procedure of *nirukti* derivation has been used in the Adhyaya through three prior Brahmanas: the Third Brahmana's *hridaya* etymology of *hri-da-yam*; the Fifth Brahmana's *satyam* etymology of *sa-t(i)-yam*; the Seventh Brahmana's *vidyut* etymology from *vidanat*. The Twelfth Brahmana gives the two-syllable *Vi-Ram* etymology with the substantive identifications of *food = Vi* (*viś-*, to rest) and *life = Ram* (*ram-*, to delight). The substantive form is paired-syllable *nirukti*, distinct from the prior three-syllable Brahmanas and from the prior single-word *vidyut* derivation. The two substantive verbal roots — *viś-* and *ram-* — are substantive Vedic-Sanskrit verbs of resting-in and delighting-in; the substantive identifications hold the substantive procedural relations of food and life to all beings.

The closing form is the Adhyaya's substantive benefit-seal in extended form. *All beings rest on him, all beings delight in him who knows this.* The substantive form holds the substantive transfer of the substantive cosmic functions onto the knower: just as all beings rest on food (= *Vi*) and delight in life (= *Ram*), all beings rest on the knower and delight in him. The substantive form is paired with the Eleventh Brahmana's substantive seal *He who knows this, conquers the highest world* — both seal substantive *vidyā* benefits with the substantive *who knows this* clause — but the Twelfth Brahmana's seal works the substantive transfer of cosmic functions rather than the substantive conquest of worlds.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the multi-stage substantive content should be read as four parallel procedures rather than as a single doctrinal statement. The dialectical opening, the named-figure dialogue, the *Vi-Ram* etymology, and the substantive benefit-seal are four distinct procedural shapes held in sequence on the single verse-1 body. Reading the four operations alongside each other — neither smoothed away into a single substantive teaching nor flattened into a generic statement of food-and-life-as-Brahman — recovers the Brahmana's literary form: four procedures held in sequence, each substantively distinct from the others.

Second, the substantive *Vi-Ram* identifications should be preserved at the level of the Sanskrit syllables and verbal roots. *Vi* is not a generic Sanskrit syllable but a substantive *upāsana-akshara* (meditation-syllable) derived from the substantive verbal root *viś-* (to rest, to enter); *Ram* is the same substantive *upāsana-akshara* derived from *ram-* (to delight). Müller's parentheticals *(vishtani)* and *(ramante)* preserve the underlying Sanskrit verbal forms. Reading the substantive identifications with the substantive Sanskrit verbal-root etymology preserved — neither smoothed into generic English equivalents nor expanded into Vedanta-doctrinal interpretations — recovers the substantive *nirukti* form of the Brahmana.

## The text

The text is held in the Archive library under `upanishads-muller-part2`, chapter 5 (Müller translation, 1884 — *The Sacred Books of the East*, Volume XV; via SacredTexts.com). Library chapter 5 is shared across seventy-six Archive entities — four parts of the Katha Upanishad, the six parts of the Mundaka Upanishad, twenty-eight anuvakas of the Taittiriyaka, the eleven Brahmanas of the Brihadaranyaka's First and Second Adhyayas, all nine Brahmanas of the Third Adhyaya, all six Brahmanas of the Fourth Adhyaya, and the First through Eleventh Brahmanas of the Fifth Adhyaya — and continues here into the Twelfth Brahmana. Book 5, Part 12 of the Brihadaranyaka — the entity here — occupies passages `5.1215` through `5.1217`. Passage `5.1215` is the "Twelfth Brahmana" header without footnote. Passages `5.1216` through `5.1217` carry the body in Müller's prose presentation across two short passages, with the verse-numbered marker at `1.` (`5.1216`) only.

The Brahmana contains several boundary features that should be preserved honestly:

- **Verse-1 multi-stage opening.** Passage `5.1216` carries verse 1 with three substantive operations on a single passage: the dialectical opening (food-and-life partial views), the substantive resolution (*these two deities, when they have become one, reach that highest state*), and the named-figure dialogue with Pratrida. The substantive content runs without internal verse-numbering or paragraph-break across the three operations.
- **Footnote irregularity.** Passage `5.1216` carries footnote `[*4]` on Pratrida's question. No footnotes `[*1]`, `[*2]`, or `[*3]` are given on any passage of the Brahmana. The numbering irregularity continues the Adhyaya's pattern in which Müller's footnote-sequence reflects the Adhyaya-level numbering rather than within each Brahmana; the substance of the verse is unaffected.
- **Verse-1 split mid-clause with semicolon.** Verse 1 is split across passages `5.1216` and `5.1217` mid-clause at *He then said to him: 'Vi; / verily, food is Vi*. Passage `5.1216` ends with a semicolon inside an open quotation — *Vi;* — and passage `5.1217` resumes *verily, food is Vi*. The semicolon end-punctuation inside the open quotation is preserved as found in the digitized source.
- **Two-syllable *Vi-Ram* identifications.** Passage `5.1217` carries the two substantive *nirukti* identifications: *Vi; verily, food is Vi, for all these beings rest (vishtani) on food*, and *Ram; verily, life is Ram, for all these beings delight (ramante) in life*. The substantive Sanskrit syllables *Vi* and *Ram* are preserved verbatim in transliteration; the substantive verbal roots *(vishtani)* and *(ramante)* are preserved in Müller's parenthetical glosses.
- **Substantive gestural detail in the dialogue.** Passage `5.1216` carries the substantive gestural detail *beckoning with his hand* in the father's answer to Pratrida. The substantive detail is preserved as part of the substantive content of the dialogue; the form is rare in the Brihadaranyaka — substantive gestural details inside named-figure dialogues are usually absent — and the irregularity is preserved as found.
- **Substantive benefit-seal at the close.** Passage `5.1217` ends with the substantive benefit-seal *All beings rest on him, all beings delight in him who knows this.* The seal is held in the substantive *who knows this* form, paired with the Eleventh Brahmana's substantive seal *He who knows this, conquers the highest world*. The Brahmana ends inside the seal without further commentary.

Passage `5.1218` is the "Thirteenth Brahmana" header that opens Book 5, Part 13 of the Brihadaranyaka and is not part of this entity. The library chapter continues past this entity into the rest of the Fifth Adhyaya and the remaining portions of the volume; the Brihadaranyaka extends well beyond the boundaries of library chapter 5. About one minute's reading.

The Twelfth Brahmana works the paired *Vi-Ram* etymology with the substantive identifications of *food = Vi* (from *viś-*, to rest) and *life = Ram* (from *ram-*, to delight). Both Brahmanas hold *upāsana-akshara* (meditation-syllable) procedures as substantive *nirukti* form, with the Third Brahmana giving three substantive syllables of one Sanskrit word and the Twelfth Brahmana giving two substantive syllables identified with two substantive principles. The substantive procedure of grounding the substantive *vidyā* in the substantive Sanskrit verbal-root etymology continues across both Brahmanas.
- **structural-parallel** → Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 5, Part 2 — paired Brahmanas of the Fifth Adhyaya with named-figure dialogues holding substantive teaching. Both Brahmanas hold named-figure dialogue as the procedural frame for substantive teaching, with the Second Brahmana giving the dialogue at three-class parallel scale and the Twelfth Brahmana giving it at the compressed father-and-son single-exchange scale.
