# Dhammapada, Chapter 17 (Anger)

Chapter 17 of the Dhammapada — the *Kodha-vagga*, "Anger" — is a chapter of thirteen verses. The opening verse states the form: *let a man leave anger, let him forsake pride, let him overcome all bondage! no sufferings befall the man who is not attached to name and form, and who calls nothing his own* (221). Verse 222 holds the chapter's first simile: *he who holds back rising anger like a rolling chariot, him I call a real driver; other people are but holding the reins.* Verse 223 holds the famous fourfold inversion: *let a man overcome anger by love, let him overcome evil by good; let him overcome the greedy by liberality, the liar by truth!* Verse 224 holds three steps to the gods — speak the truth, do not yield to anger, give. Verses 225 and 226 turn the figure to those whose passions end: the sages who injure nobody and control their body go to the unchangeable place; those who are ever watchful, study day and night, and strive after Nirvana have their passions end. Verses 227–229 turn the figure to praise and blame in a long passage that combines what is conventionally two Pali verses. *This is an old saying, O Atula, this is not only of to-day: `They blame him who sits silent, they blame him who speaks much, they also blame him who says little; there is no one on earth who is not blamed.'* (227). *There never was, there never will be, nor is there now, a man who is always blamed, or a man who is always praised* (228). *But he whom those who discriminate praise continually day after day, as without blemish, wise, rich in knowledge and virtue, who would dare to blame him, like a coin made of gold from the Gambu river? Even the gods praise him, he is praised even by Brahman* (229). The closing four verses hold a triple parallel and its summary: *beware of bodily anger, and control thy body* (231); *beware of the anger of the tongue, and control thy tongue* (232); *beware of the anger of the mind, and control thy mind* (233); *the wise who control their body, who control their tongue, the wise who control their mind, are indeed well controlled* (234).

## Context

The Dhammapada is one of the books of the Khuddaka Nikaya, the fifth and shortest section of the Sutta Pitaka in the Pali Canon. It collects 423 verses arranged in twenty-six chapters by theme — pairs, heedfulness, mind, flowers, the sage, evil, and so on. The verses are attributed to the Buddha and were transmitted orally for several centuries before being committed to writing in Sri Lanka in the first century BCE. The collection took shape in roughly the third century BCE, in connection with the Theravada transmission of the canon.

Chapter 17 is the *Kodha-vagga*, "Anger." Its verses are numbered 221 through 229 and 231 through 234 in F. Max Müller's 1881 translation, the version held in the Archive library — thirteen passages, where the standard continuous Pali numbering of the Dhammapada gives fourteen verses (221–234). Müller has combined what is conventionally verses 229 and 230 into a single English period at passage 17.229: the *praised-day-after-day-as-without-blemish* statement and the *who-would-dare-to-blame-him-like-a-coin-of-Gambu-gold* statement are run together as one sentence, with no passage numbered 17.230 in the source. Per Atlas-rule **OCR/transcription honesty**, the merged passage is preserved as Müller wrote it; the chapter therefore has thirteen passages here against the standard fourteen verses elsewhere — the sixth instance of the same kind of merging in Müller's translation, after Chapter 4 (verses 58–59), Chapter 6 (verses 87–88), Chapter 8 (verses 104–105), Chapter 11 (verses 153–154), and Chapter 14 (verses 195–196). The next chapter opens at passage 18.235. Each verse is short — two or four lines in the Pali — and each completes one statement of the figure, except the merged passage 17.229, which completes two.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the chapter is built across three groups. Verses 221–224 hold the opening on anger as a thing to be left, restrained like a rolling chariot, inverted by its opposite (love, good, liberality, truth), and replaced by truth-speaking, non-anger, and giving. Verses 225–229 turn the figure to those whose passions end — the sages who injure nobody, the watchful who strive after Nirvana — and to the *Atula* passage on the inevitability of being blamed and the rarity of being praised continually by those who discriminate. Verses 231–234 hold the closing triple parallel on the threefold control of body, tongue, and mind, with verse 234 as the summary. Reading the chapter as three groups, rather than as thirteen separate aphorisms, recovers the form the chapter is built on.

Second, verse 223 is the chapter's central inversion verse: anger overcome by love, evil by good, the greedy by liberality, the liar by truth. The verse holds four pairs in one verse, and each pair states the same compositional principle — the negative is overcome by its proper positive, not by another negative. Reading the chapter with this fourfold inversion held visible across the surrounding verses (the *real driver* who holds back anger at 222, the three steps of truth, non-anger, and giving at 224, the threefold control at 231–234) keeps the form the chapter has.

## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Dhammapada, Chapter 4 (Flowers), Chapter 6 (The Wise Man), Chapter 8 (The Thousands), Chapter 11 (Old Age), and Chapter 14 (The Buddha) — the earlier chapters with the same kind of Müller merging-irregularity at their close, middle, or hinge; Chapter 4 combines verses 58–59, Chapter 6 combines 87–88, Chapter 8 combines 104–105, Chapter 11 combines 153–154, Chapter 14 combines 195–196, Chapter 17 combines 229–230. The merging is a recurring feature of Müller's 1881 translation across the collection.
- **structural-parallel** → Dhammapada, Chapter 1 (The Twin Verses) — the opening pair on the shelf, with the same compositional principle that the unwholesome is overcome by its proper opposite; Chapter 1 verse 5 with the line *for hatred does not cease by hatred at any time: hatred ceases by love, this is an old rule*, Chapter 17 verse 223 with the fourfold extension of the same rule — anger by love, evil by good, the greedy by liberality, the liar by truth. The figure is held across the chapters with twelve-verse spacing.
- **structural-parallel** → Dhammapada, Chapter 16 (Pleasure) — the immediate predecessor on the shelf; Chapter 16 with twelve verses on attachment as the source of grief and fear, Chapter 17 with thirteen verses on anger as the bondage to be left and replaced by its opposites. The two chapters together hold the figure that the work is the inversion of two different emotional bindings — Chapter 16 the binding to what one loves, Chapter 17 the binding against what one hates.
- **structural-parallel** → Dhammapada, Chapter 10 (Punishment) — the earlier chapter that already worked the figure of speech-not-spoken-harshly and blows-for-blows; Chapter 10 verse 133 holds the figure that those spoken to harshly will answer the same way and that blows for blows will touch thee, Chapter 17 verses 222–223 hold the same recognition turned to the inversion form — restraint of rising anger like the chariot, replacement of anger by love. The two chapters share the figure of how the cycle of harm is broken.
- **structural-parallel** → Matthew 5 — the Christian shelf-pair on the inversion of evil by good; the Sermon on the Mount with its commands to love one's enemies, to turn the cheek, to bless those who curse, Dhammapada 17 verse 223 with the same inversion form — anger by love, evil by good. Both texts hold the figure that the unwholesome is not to be answered in kind but with its opposite, articulated through different theological vocabulary.
