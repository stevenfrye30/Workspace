# Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 1, Part 3

Book 1, Part 3 of the Brihadaranyaka Upanishad — the Third Brahmana of the First Adhyaya — works through twenty-eight numbered verses arranged in three connected movements: the contest of the Devas and the Asuras at the *udgītha*, the etymological chain by which the breath in the mouth is named, and the closing instruction on the Saman, the Stotras, and the *Pavamana* prayer. The Brahmana opens on the figure of two kinds of descendants of Pragapati, the Devas and the Asuras — the younger and the elder — and the Devas' resolve to overcome the Asuras at the sacrifices through the *udgītha*. The Devas ask Speech to sing the chant; she sings, and the Asuras pierce her with evil — the evil of speaking what is wrong. They ask Breath as smell, who sings; the Asuras pierce him with the evil of smelling what is bad. They ask the eye, then the ear, then the mind; each is overwhelmed in turn. Then they ask the breath in the mouth — *mukhya prāṇa* — and the Asuras rush at him and are scattered as a ball of earth scatters when it strikes a stone. The Devas turn to the deity that has remained un-struck, and from this turn the etymologies unfold. That deity is *Ayāsya* because it is *asye 'ntar* — within the mouth — and the *rasa* of the limbs (*aṅgānāṁ rasaḥ*); it is *Dur* because Death is far from it; it carries Speech across death and Speech becomes Agni, carries Breath as smell and it becomes Vayu, carries the eye and it becomes Aditya, the ear and it becomes the quarters, the mind and it becomes the moon. By singing, the same breath obtains eatable food; the Devas ask for a share; the breath says *enter into me*; the knower becomes the supporter of his relations, their chief leader, their strong ruler. The etymological chain continues: this breath is *Bṛhaspati* (lord of *Bṛhatī*, the Rig-veda); it is *Brahmaṇaspati* (lord of *Brahman*, the Yajur-veda); it is *Sāman* (because it is *sa* — speech — and *ama* — breath — together) and equal (*sama*) to a grub, a gnat, an elephant, the three worlds, the universe; it is *Udgītha* (*ut* because by it the universe is upheld, *gītha* because speech is song); the *Saman*'s property is tone, its gold is tone, its support is speech. The closing instruction places the *Pavamana* prayer at the moment when the Prastotri begins to sing the Saman: *asato mā sad gamaya, tamaso mā jyotir gamaya, mṛtyor māmṛtaṁ gamaya* — *Lead me from the unreal to the real! Lead me from darkness to light! Lead me from death to immortality!* — and the gloss reads each clause as a passage from death to immortality. The Brahmana closes on the Stotras during which the sacrificer asks for whatever desire he holds.

## Context

The Brihadaranyaka Upanishad is one of the two earliest of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads, sharing that position with the Chandogya. It belongs to the Shukla Yajurveda branch of the Vedic corpus and forms the closing portion of the Shatapatha Brahmana — the *Aranyaka* in its name marks it as a forest-text recited at the late stages of Vedic study. Modern scholarship places the Brihadaranyaka in roughly the eighth to sixth century BCE, prior to the Buddha; the text is anonymous, and the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship. The text is the longest of the principal Upanishads and is typically structured in six *adhyayas* (chapters), each containing several *brahmanas* (sections).

Book 1, Part 3 is the third Brahmana of the First Adhyaya. The First Brahmana opened the Upanishad with the sacrificial horse mapped onto the cosmos; the Second Brahmana extended that figure into the cosmogony of Death and Hunger; the Third Brahmana now turns to the form of recitation by which the priests overcome death — the *udgītha* — and grounds the recitation in the breath in the mouth. The Devas-and-Asuras contest is one of the most-told stories in the Upanishadic literature, with closely related versions in the Chandogya Upanishad (1.2). The etymological chain — *Ayāsya*, *Bṛhaspati*, *Brahmaṇaspati*, *Sāman*, *Udgītha* — works through the names by which the chant is held in the recitation tradition, and the *Pavamana* prayer at the close has been kept in liturgical use ever since: *asato mā sad gamaya, tamaso mā jyotir gamaya, mṛtyor māmṛtaṁ gamaya*, recited at countless openings of Vedic and post-Vedic study.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the Brahmana proceeds by repetition and substitution. The first six verses use the same template — the Devas ask the next sense to sing, the sense sings, the Asuras pierce it with evil corresponding to its function — and the seventh breaks the template, with the Asuras scattered against the breath in the mouth. The next six verses use a second template — that deity carries each sense across death, and each becomes a cosmic element after the crossing. The etymological chain that follows works through a third template: each name is given through the breath's relation to a Veda or to a property of the chant. Reading the Brahmana with attention to where the templates begin and end, and to the moment at which the contest's template breaks at the *mukhya prāṇa*, recovers the form the late-Vedic narrative has when it is most procedurally compact.

Second, the *Pavamana* prayer is held inside the structure of the recitation, not added to it. The instruction at verse 27 names the moment when the prayer is to be said — *when the Prastotri begins to sing the Saman, then let him recite these three verses* — and the gloss that follows reads each clause of the prayer as the same recognition: from the unreal to the real is from death to immortality; from darkness to light is from death to immortality. Reading the prayer with the gloss held alongside, attending to the way the three clauses are interpreted as one recognition, keeps the form the Brahmana has when it places its most-cited line inside its own commentary.

## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 1, Part 2 — paired immediately consecutive Brahmanas of the First Adhyaya; the Second Brahmana grounded the Upanishad in the cosmogony of Death and Hunger and the institution of the *aśvamedha*, the Third Brahmana now grounds the recitation of the *udgītha* in the contest of the Devas and the Asuras and in the breath in the mouth that scatters the Asuras. The cosmogonic narrative and the recitation that the cosmogony enables, in two adjacent Brahmanas of the same Adhyaya.
- **structural-parallel** → Taittiriyaka Upanishad, Book 1, Part 8 — paired Sanskrit etymological derivations of the central recitational principle; the Taittiriyaka's Eighth Anuvaka holds the *Omkara*-praise — *Om means Brahman, Om means all this, Om means obedience; after Om they sing Samans, after Om they recite hymns, after Om the Adhvaryu gives the response* — gathering the contexts in which the syllable opens recitation, the Brihadaranyaka's Third Brahmana works through a corresponding etymological chain in which breath is named *Ayāsya*, *Bṛhaspati*, *Brahmaṇaspati*, *Sāman*, and *Udgītha*. Both texts ground the form of the recitation in the etymological chain by which its central terms are received.
- **structural-parallel** → Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 16 (Daivasura Sampad Vibhaga Yoga) — paired Sanskrit passages on the divide between divine and demonic dispositions; the Gita's Chapter 16 holds the long catalogue of *daivī sampat* (divine endowment) and *āsurī sampat* (demonic endowment) as two contrasting orientations within the human field, the Brihadaranyaka's Third Brahmana holds the older mythic form of the same divide as the contest between the Devas and the Asuras at the *udgītha*. The Gita develops the typology into a teaching on dispositions; the Brihadaranyaka holds it in narrative form as the foundation of the recitation that overcomes evil.
