# Taittiriyaka Upanishad, Book 2, Part 3

Book 2, Part 3 of the Taittiriyaka Upanishad — the Third Anuvaka of the Brahmanandavalli — extends the recursive structure the Second Anuvaka opened. The pattern returns. The Anuvaka begins with a sloka, set off in Müller's quotation marks, that places the previously-introduced sheath under its own framing recognition: *prāṇaṁ devā anu prāṇanti, manuṣyāḥ paśavaś ca ye* — *the Devas breathe after breath, so do men and cattle; breath is the life of beings, therefore it is called* sarvāyuṣa, *all-enlivening.* The prose continuation gives the recognition that follows from the sloka — those who worship breath as Brahman obtain the full life — and seals the recognition with the line that the embodied Self of the breath-self is the same as that of the food-self that came before. Then the recursive formula returns and pivots inward: *anyontara ātmā manomayaḥ* — *different from this, which consists of breath, is the other, the inner Self, which consists of mind; the former is filled by this; it also has the shape of man; like the human shape of the former is the human shape of the latter.* The new anatomical mapping uses the language of scripture, not of the body: Yajus is its head, Rik its right arm, Saman its left arm, the *ādeśa* — the doctrine of the Brahmana — its trunk. The Anuvaka closes with the same pointer the previous anuvakas have used — *on this there is also the following sloka* — turning the reader toward the Fourth Anuvaka.

## Context

The Taittiriyaka Upanishad is one of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads — a small group of texts traditionally received as the philosophical core of the late Vedic literature. It belongs to the Krishna Yajurveda branch of the Vedic corpus and to the Taittiriya school of that branch; the Upanishad sits inside the Taittiriya Aranyaka. Modern scholarship places it among the older prose Upanishads, in roughly the sixth to fifth century BCE — earlier than the verse Upanishads of the Katha and Mundaka. The text is anonymous; the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship.

Book 2, Part 3 sits inside the Brahmanandavalli, the Second Valli of the Taittiriyaka. The First Anuvaka opened the Valli with the defining sloka and the emanation cascade ending in food and the *annamaya*; the Second Anuvaka placed the *annamaya* under its own sloka and introduced the *prāṇamaya*; the Third Anuvaka now does the same again — places the *prāṇamaya* under its own sloka and introduces the *manomaya*. The recursion is exact and predictable. Each Anuvaka frames the sheath that the previous Anuvaka introduced, gives the recognition that follows from worshipping that sheath as Brahman, links the embodied Self of the present sheath to the embodied Self of the previous, and then opens the next sheath under the same formula. The Brahmanandavalli will continue this procedure through the *vijñānamaya* (knowledge-self) in the Fourth Anuvaka and the *ānandamaya* (bliss-self) in the Fifth, before turning to the long instruction on Brahman as bliss that gives the Valli its name.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the Anuvaka is built on repetition with small variation. The recursive formula is the same as the one the Second Anuvaka inaugurated; what changes is the pair of sheaths. The framing sloka follows the same pattern — a short verse on the substance of the sheath, set off in quotation marks. The anatomical mapping follows the same pattern — head, right arm, left arm, trunk, support — but the vocabulary of the mapping changes with the sheath. The food-self had the parts of the body for digestion; the breath-self had the breaths in their proper places; the mind-self has the parts of the Veda. Reading the Anuvaka with attention to which terms repeat and which terms shift, recovers the form the Brahmanandavalli has when its recursion is most visible — the same procedure repeated, the substance moving inward.

Second, the Anuvaka places scripture inside the body. The mind-self is mapped to Yajus, Rik, Saman, and the Brahmana doctrine — not as figures of speech but as the parts of the *manomaya*. The procedure is consistent with the Sikshavalli's earlier work: the structures of the Veda are placed in the body of the speaker; the recitation is held inside the recitant. Reading the anatomical mapping of the *manomaya* alongside the mappings that came before, rather than as a separate doctrine of mind-as-scripture, keeps the form the Brahmanandavalli has when it is at its most procedurally compact.

## The text

The text is held in the Archive library under `upanishads-muller-part2`, chapter 5 (Müller translation, 1884 — *The Sacred Books of the East*, Volume XV; via SacredTexts.com). Library chapter 5 is shared across twenty-four Archive entities — four parts of the Katha Upanishad, the six parts of the Mundaka Upanishad, the twelve anuvakas of the Sikshavalli (Book 1) of the Taittiriyaka, and the First and Second Anuvakas of the Brahmanandavalli (Book 2, Parts 1 and 2) — and continues here into the Third Anuvaka without break in the chapter. Book 2, Part 3 of the Taittiriyaka — the entity here — occupies passages `5.239` through `5.243`. Passage `5.239` is the "Third Anuvaka" header. Passage `5.240` carries the opening sloka in Müller's quotation marks. Passage `5.241` carries the prose continuation on the *prāṇamaya* worship and the recursive identity-formula linking each sheath's embodied Self to the previous. Passage `5.242` introduces the next inner sheath — *manomaya* — with the recursive formula and the scripture-based anatomical mapping; the passage ends mid-mapping at "is its" where Müller's editorial paragraphing interrupts the prose, and the next passage in the source picks up the closing trunk and support of the *manomaya* at the start of the Fourth Anuvaka. Passage `5.243` is the closing pointer "On this there is also the following Sloka:". The Brahmanandavalli does not number its anuvaka-internal verses in the same way the Sikshavalli did; Müller's prose runs continuously through each Anuvaka with embedded slokas in quotation marks. Passage `5.244` is the "Fourth Anuvaka" header that opens Book 2, Part 4 of the Taittiriyaka and is not part of this entity. The library chapter continues past this entity into the rest of the Taittiriyaka and the remaining Upanishads of the volume. About three minutes' reading.

## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Taittiriyaka Upanishad, Book 2, Part 2 — paired immediately consecutive Anuvakas of the Brahmanandavalli; the Second Anuvaka placed the *annamaya* under its own sloka and inaugurated the recursive formula by introducing the *prāṇamaya*, the Third Anuvaka places the *prāṇamaya* under its own sloka and uses the same recursive formula to introduce the *manomaya*. The procedure is the same; the substance moves one step inward. The literary form holds two adjacent levels of the same nested structure.
- **structural-parallel** → Mundaka Upanishad, Book 2, Part 1 — paired Sanskrit passages on the layered constitution of beings; the Mundaka's First Khanda of the Second Mundaka holds the cascade *from him is born breath, mind, and all organs of sense, ether, air, light, water, and the earth* (Mundaka 2.1.3) — emanation as a single sweep — the Taittiriyaka's Third Anuvaka holds the closely related recognition through the recursive formula moving from food-self through breath-self to mind-self, with each level given its own anatomy and its identity to the level before. Both texts hold the same recognition that beings are constituted in nested layers, the Mundaka in a single emanation and the Taittiriyaka in step-by-step recursion.
- **structural-parallel** → Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 15 (Purushottama Yoga) — paired Sanskrit passages on the interior ordering of embodied being and the place of scripture within it; the Gita's Chapter 15 names Krishna as seated in the heart of all beings, as the source of memory, knowledge, and forgetfulness, and as that which is to be known by all the Vedas (Gita 15.15) — *vedaiś ca sarvair aham eva vedyaḥ* — the Taittiriyaka's Third Anuvaka places the same recognition through the *manomaya*: the mind-self whose head is Yajus, whose right arm is Rik, whose left arm is Saman, whose trunk is the Brahmana doctrine. Paired moments where the Veda is placed inside the body of the knower as the substance of the knowing.
