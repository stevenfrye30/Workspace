# Odyssey, Book 11 (Nekuia)

Book 11 of the Odyssey — the *Nekuia*, "the Book of the Dead" — is Odysseus's account of his visit to the borders of the underworld. At Circe's instruction he sails to the Cimmerian shore at the edge of the world, performs the rites she has prescribed, and the dead come up to drink the blood of his sacrifice and speak with him. Tiresias, his own dead mother Anticleia, the heroines of the older time, and his comrades from the Trojan War — Agamemnon, Achilles, Ajax — appear in succession. Book 11 is among the most read passages of Greek epic literature.

## Context

The Odyssey is one of the two surviving Homeric epics, alongside the Iliad. It is composed in dactylic hexameter in Homeric Greek — a literary register that mixes Ionic, Aeolic, and older forms — and runs to twenty-four books and roughly twelve thousand lines. Modern scholarship places the present text in the late eighth or seventh century BCE, drawing on a long oral tradition behind it; the traditional attribution to a single poet named Homer dates from antiquity itself and is debated. The poems were written down at some point in the early archaic period and stabilized in their received form by the Hellenistic editors of Alexandria.

Book 11 sits inside the *apologos*, the long first-person narrative Odysseus tells to the Phaeacian court (Books 9 through 12). It comes between the encounter with Circe in Book 10, who instructs him to seek the prophet Tiresias in the land of the dead, and the return to Circe's island in Book 12, where she advises him further. The book is structured by a sequence of encounters at the trench Odysseus has dug for the rites: the dead come forward in turn, each given a brief scene of speech with the living visitor.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, Book 11 is told in the first person. Odysseus is the narrator; the Phaeacian court is the audience inside the poem. Reading the book as Odysseus telling a Phaeacian banquet hall about his visit to the dead — a story spoken at a feast — keeps the layer of address visible. The famous lines spoken by Achilles or Agamemnon are spoken inside Odysseus's report of them and reach the reader through that frame.

Second, the book has two long sections that are not encounter-scenes: the catalogue of heroines (11.225–332) and the catalogue of damned figures at the end (11.568–600). They function differently from the encounters and have sometimes been read as later additions; reading them as catalogue-form rather than as story keeps their shape visible. The encounters with Tiresias, Anticleia, Agamemnon, Achilles, and Ajax sit on either side of these catalogues.
