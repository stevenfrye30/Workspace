# Plato, Apology

In 399 BCE, the philosopher Socrates — then seventy — stood before a jury of five hundred Athenian citizens to answer charges of impiety and of corrupting the city's young men. The *Apology* is the speech he gave that day, or the speech Plato, his student, recorded and composed for him to give. It is a forensic defense and a public account of a life, delivered under the threat of execution and ending in conviction by a narrow margin. Socrates was sentenced to death and executed within the month.

## Context

The trial took place during the political aftermath of Athens' defeat in the Peloponnesian War. Several of Socrates' associates had been involved in the brief oligarchic regime of the Thirty Tyrants in 404–403, and his long-running practice of public questioning had made him conspicuous and uncomfortable to rebuilt democratic Athens. The formal charges — introducing new gods and corrupting the young — were the legal frame for a deeper civic discomfort.

The *Apology* is forensic prose composed soon after the event, probably in the 390s BCE. It is one of the earliest surviving Greek courtroom speeches in any form, and one of a small group of texts (with Xenophon's *Apology*, his *Memorabilia*, and a lost *Apology* by Lysias) in which Socrates' defense was preserved or reconstructed. Plato's version is the fullest and the one through which Socrates entered Western literature. Whether it is a transcript, a reconstruction, or a literary composition is an open question; what survives is the text Plato wrote, not the words spoken in 399.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the *Apology* is a court speech, not a dialogue or a treatise. It runs as an extended monologue with a few brief exchanges during a section of cross-examination. The rhythm is rhetorical — Socrates' addresses to the jurors, his anticipation of their reactions, his refusal of the conventional appeals to pity — and reading the text as oratory rather than as philosophy gets it closer to its native form.

Second, Socrates' defense is also an account of his life. He explains why he questions, how the practice began, what the oracle at Delphi has to do with it, and why he will not stop. The autobiographical claim runs through the legal one. By the time he is sentenced, he has given Athens both a defense of his actions and the only kind of justification he can give: the shape of his life.

## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Augustine, Confessions, Book I — a self-account given by a public figure under judgment; both end in life-justification rather than refutation of charges.
- **structural-parallel** → Al-Ghazali, Munqidh — a philosopher's defense of his own mode of inquiry; both examine and defend the speaker's intellectual practice.
