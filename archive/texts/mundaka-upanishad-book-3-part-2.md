# Mundaka Upanishad, Book 3, Part 2

Book 3, Part 2 of the Mundaka Upanishad — the Second Khanda of the Third Mundaka — is the concluding Khanda of the Upanishad. The opening verse names the recognition of the highest home of Brahman in which all is contained and shines brightly; the second verse holds the line that the one whose desires are fulfilled and who is conscious of the true Self has all desires vanish even here on earth. The third verse — *nāyam ātmā pravacanena labhyo na medhayā na bahunā śrutena* — gives the line that the Self cannot be gained by the Veda or by understanding or by much learning; the Self is gained only by the one whom the Self chooses, and the Self chooses him as his own. The fourth verse names the means: by strength, by earnestness, by right meditation. The fifth verse names what the sages who have reached him become — satisfied, conscious of their Self, their passions passed away, tranquil. The verses that follow gather the closing figures: the anchorites who, having ascertained the object of the knowledge of the Vedanta and purified their nature by the Yoga of renunciation, become free at the great end; the fifteen parts that enter their elements and the deeds and the Self that become one in the highest Imperishable; the rivers that disappear in the sea, losing their name and their form, as the wise man freed from name and form goes to the divine Person who is greater than the great. The Khanda closes with the verse that he who knows that highest Brahman becomes Brahman, free from the fetters of the heart, and with the colophon — *Rishir Angirāḥ purovāca* — that the Rishi Angiras formerly told this true science, and the closing salutation: adoration to the highest Rishis, adoration to the highest Rishis.

## Context

The Mundaka Upanishad is one of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads — a small group of texts traditionally received as the philosophical core of the late Vedic literature. It is associated with the Atharvaveda branch of the Vedic corpus and is composed in Sanskrit verse. Modern scholarship places its composition somewhat later than the Katha, in roughly the fourth to second century BCE; the principal Upanishads as a group are dated to the centuries on either side of the Buddha. The text is anonymous; the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship.

Book 3, Part 2 closes the Upanishad. The dialogue does not return to its opening framing — Saunaka and Angiras are not named again as speakers — but the closing verses do return to Angiras: the colophon that the Rishi Angiras formerly told this true science folds the close of the text back to the opening of the First Khanda, where the lineage of teaching descended from Brahma through Atharvan, Angir, and Bharadvaga to Angiras. The literary movement is one of completion: the Khanda holds the recognition the preceding parts have prepared, names the means by which it is reached, gathers the closing figures of release, and signs the text with the name of its first teacher. The verbatim line that the Self is gained only by the one whom the Self chooses (Mundaka 3.2.3) is one of the closest cross-Upanishadic textual echoes — the same verse appearing in the Katha's Second Valli (Katha 1.2.23) — marking one of the older threads carried across the late-Vedic literature.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the Khanda is built on return rather than expansion. The line that the Self cannot be reached by argument or by much learning was given in the Katha's Second Valli and now returns in nearly identical form; the figure of the fetters of the heart that fall away was given in the Katha's Sixth Valli and in the Mundaka's own Second Mundaka, and now returns again at the close; the recognition that the wise man, having reached the Self, becomes himself one with the highest Imperishable returns the threads the Mundaka has been gathering through its three books. The Khanda does not introduce new teaching — it brings into compact form what the preceding parts have given, and seals it. Reading the verses with attention to which earlier figure each is gathering, and to the slight rotation of vocabulary that gives each return its place in the closing, recovers the form the teaching has.

Second, the Khanda moves toward closure through figures of dissolution and identity. The fifteen parts of the body enter their elements and their senses enter their gods; the deeds and the Self with all his knowledge become all one in the highest Imperishable; the rivers, flowing into the sea, lose their name and their form, and the wise man, freed from name and form, goes to the divine Person who is greater than the great. The closing verse — that he who knows that highest Brahman becomes Brahman — is the same recognition the verses of dissolution have prepared, given now in the simplest grammatical form. Reading the closing figures alongside the closing line, rather than as separate teachings, keeps the form the Mundaka gives its end.

## The text

The text is held in the Archive library under `upanishads-muller-part2`, chapter 5 (Müller translation, 1884 — *The Sacred Books of the East*, Volume XV; via SacredTexts.com). Library chapter 5 is shared across the Katha entries (Book 1, Part 3 and Book 2, Parts 1–3) and the Mundaka entries already in the Archive (Book 1, Parts 1 and 2 at passages `5.77` through `5.105`, Book 2, Parts 1 and 2 at passages `5.106` through `5.131`, and Book 3, Part 1 at passages `5.132` through `5.144`); Book 3, Part 2 of the Mundaka — the entity here — occupies passages `5.145` through `5.158`. Passage `5.145` is the "Second Khanda" header. Passages `5.146` through `5.158` carry the eleven numbered verses of the Khanda, with verse 5 split across passages `5.150` and `5.151` and verse 10 split across passages `5.156` and `5.157` where Müller's footnotes interrupt the verses mid-line. After this Khanda the Mundaka closes: passage `5.159` is a page-break artifact (page references in square brackets) carried over from the close of the Mundaka, and passage `5.160` opens the next Upanishad in the volume — the Taittiriyaka — with its own header structure ("TAITTIRIYAKA-UPANISHAD" → "FIRST VALLI" → "FIRST ANUVAKA"). The library chapter does not end with the close of the Mundaka but continues through the Taittiriyaka and the rest of the volume's Upanishads. Eleven verses; about ten minutes' reading.

## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 18 (Moksha Sanyasa Yoga) — paired Sanskrit closing chapters that gather the teaching into a final form; the Gita's Chapter 18 names *sannyasa* and *tyaga* — renunciation in two forms — and recapitulates action, knowledge, and devotion into a concluding instruction, the Mundaka's closing Khanda names *Vedānta-vijñāna* and the *Yoga* of renunciation as the means by which the anchorites become free at the great end and gathers the figures of dissolution and identity into the closing recognition that he who knows the highest Brahman becomes Brahman — the same culminating posture in the two texts, in close vocabulary.
- **structural-parallel** → Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 2 (Sankhya Yoga) — paired Sanskrit passages on the cessation of desire and the figure of the rivers entering the sea; the Gita's verses on the *sthitaprajna* close on the line that, as the waters enter the ocean which, though full, remains undisturbed, so all desires entering the wise man reach peace (Gita 2.70), the Mundaka's closing Khanda holds the closely related figure that, as the flowing rivers disappear in the sea, losing their name and their form, the wise man freed from name and form goes to the divine Person who is greater than the great (Mundaka 3.2.8) — paired moments in the same Sanskrit philosophical line, the figure of the river meeting the sea given in two close vocabularies.
- **structural-parallel** → Katha Upanishad, Book 2, Part 3 — paired Upanishadic dialogic closures; the Katha's Sixth Valli closes with the line that when all the ties of the heart are severed here on earth, then the mortal becomes immortal (Katha 2.3.15) and seals the dialogue with the formula that ends the teaching, the Mundaka's closing Khanda holds the related line that he who knows the highest Brahman becomes Brahman, free from the fetters of the heart, and seals the text with the colophon that the Rishi Angiras formerly told this true science. The Mundaka's closing Khanda also carries one of the closest verbatim Upanishadic echoes: the verse that the Self cannot be gained by the Veda, by understanding, or by much learning but only by the one whom the Self chooses (Mundaka 3.2.3) is held in nearly identical form at Katha 1.2.23.
