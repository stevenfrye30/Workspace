# Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 2, Part 5

Book 2, Part 5 of the Brihadaranyaka Upanishad — the Fifth Brahmana of the Second Adhyaya — is the *Madhu-Brahmana*, the Honey-Doctrine, one of the most-cited single units of the Upanishadic literature. The Brahmana is built almost entirely on a single repeated form. Verses 1 through 14 give the same sentence fourteen times, with one cosmic-bodily pair substituted at each iteration, and the same closing identification appended unchanged. The form is the form of the teaching.

The first verse sets the template: *this earth is the honey (madhu, the effect) of all beings, and all beings are the honey of this earth. Likewise this bright, immortal person in this earth, and that bright immortal person incorporated in the body — both are madhu. He indeed is the same as that Self, that Immortal, that Brahman, that All.* The second verse substitutes water and the seed in the body; the third, fire and speech in the body; the fourth, air and breath; the fifth, sun and eye; the sixth, space (the quarters) and ear; the seventh, moon and mind; the eighth, lightning and light in the body; the ninth, thunder and sound and voice in the body; the tenth, ether and the heart-ether in the body; the eleventh, law (*dharma*) and law in the body; the twelfth, the true (*satyam*) and the true in the body; the thirteenth, mankind and man in the body; the fourteenth and last, the Self itself, with the *bright, immortal person in this Self* and the *bright, immortal person, the Self* both named *madhu*. At every iteration the closing identification is identical: *He indeed is the same as that Self, that Immortal, that Brahman, that All.* The repetition is the work the Brahmana is doing. No iteration is preferred; no pair is subordinated to another; the same recognition is given fourteen times in fourteen vocabularies.

Verse 15 holds the unity-figure that gathers the fourteen iterations: *Verily this Self is the lord of all beings, the king of all beings. And as all spokes are contained in the axle and in the felly of a wheel, all beings, and all those selfs (of the earth, water, etc.) are contained in that Self.* The figure is the standard Upanishadic image of unity-in-multiplicity; the wheel here gathers the cosmic and the bodily mappings the preceding verses have made.

Verses 16 through 19 give the mythological lineage of the honey-doctrine itself. *Verily Dadhyak Atharvana proclaimed this honey (the madhu-vidya) to the two Asvins.* Each of the four verses opens with this same line, and each is followed by a quoted Rig-veda sloka embedded in the prose, set off in Müller's quotation marks. The first sloka (Rv. I.116.12) names *the fearful deed of yours which you performed for the sake of gain — the honey which Dadhyak Atharvana proclaimed to you through the head of a horse*; the second (Rv. I.117.22) names *you fixed a horse's head on Atharvana Dadhyak, and he, wishing to be true to his promise, proclaimed to you the honey, both that of Tvashtri and that which is to be your secret*; the third (unattributed) names *He made bodies with two feet, he made bodies with four feet. Having first become a bird, he entered the bodies as purusha*, with the etymology *purusha* as *purisaya, he who lies in the body*; the fourth (Rv. VI.47.18) names *He became like unto every form, and this is meant to reveal the true form of him. Indra appears multiform through the Mayas, for his horses (senses) are yoked, hundreds and ten.*

The Brahmana closes on a single passage that returns the Rig-veda's *hundreds and ten* and *thousands* into the recognition the whole Brahmana has been preparing: *This (Atman) is the horses, this (Atman) is the ten, and the thousands, many and endless. This is the Brahman, without cause and without effect, without anything inside or outside; this Self is Brahman, omnipresent and omniscient. This is the teaching (of the Upanishads).* The closing formula — *etat upaniṣadām* — names the recognition just given as *the teaching of the Upanishads*, sealing the Brahmana with the same kind of structural marker the Sikshavalli used at *eṣā vedopaniṣat* and the Brahmanandavalli at *iti upaniṣat*.

## Context

The Brihadaranyaka Upanishad is one of the two earliest of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads, sharing that position with the Chandogya. It belongs to the Shukla Yajurveda branch of the Vedic corpus and forms the closing portion of the Shatapatha Brahmana — the *Aranyaka* in its name marks it as a forest-text recited at the late stages of Vedic study. Modern scholarship places the Brihadaranyaka in roughly the eighth to sixth century BCE, prior to the Buddha; the text is anonymous, and the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship. The text is the longest of the principal Upanishads and is typically structured in six *adhyayas* (chapters), each containing several *brahmanas* (sections).

Book 2, Part 5 is the Fifth Brahmana of the Second Adhyaya. The First Brahmana ran the Gargya Balaki / Ajatasatru dialogue; the Second mapped the head onto cosmic figures and Rishis; the Third held the two-forms doctrine and the *neti neti* teaching; the Fourth held the long Yajnavalkya-Maitreyi conversation that closed on the question *How, O beloved, should he know himself, the Knower?* The Fifth Brahmana now answers in a different mode — not by dialogue, not by argument, but by fourteen iterations of the same identification, followed by the wheel-figure of unity, followed by the mythological lineage that names the doctrine itself. The closing-on-Knower question of the Fourth Brahmana is followed in the Fifth by the doctrine that all the cosmic and the bodily are *madhu* — sweet, effect, honey — to one another, and the Self is the lord of all beings. The *madhu-vidyā* is preserved in close form in other Upanishads and Brahmanas, and the mythological frame of Dadhyak Atharvana with the horse's head is preserved in the Rig-veda hymns the Brahmana quotes; the Brahmana brings the doctrine and the lineage together inside one Upanishadic unit.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the Brahmana is built on strict and audible repetition. Reading the fourteen verses aloud, allowing the same sentence to come fourteen times with the substitution as the only thing that changes, recovers the form the Brahmana has when its work is most carried by repetition. The recognition is not stated and then illustrated; it is given fourteen times, and the recognition is held in the cumulative force of the repetitions and in the unchanging closing formula. Reading the substitutions in their actual order — earth, water, fire, air, sun, space, moon, lightning, thunder, ether, law, the true, mankind, Self — and noticing where the cosmic terms shift to the abstract (law, the true, mankind, Self) keeps the form the Brahmana has at its centre.

Second, the Brahmana places its mythological lineage and its Rig-veda quotations after the doctrine, not before it. The doctrine is given first in fourteen iterations and the wheel-figure; only then does the Brahmana name Dadhyak Atharvana and the Asvins and embed the four Rig-veda slokas. The pattern is unusual for the Upanishadic literature, where the lineage typically opens the teaching; here the lineage closes it, and the closing turn from the slokas back into the recognition — *this Atman is the horses, this Atman is the ten, and the thousands* — uses the Rig-veda's own image of yoked horses to fold the Brahman-as-source recognition back into the cosmic-bodily identification the doctrine has made. Reading the Brahmana with the lineage held as the closing rather than the opening, attending to the way the slokas inherit the doctrine rather than ground it, keeps the form the Brihadaranyaka has when it places myth and recognition in this order.

Passages `5.595` through `5.621` carry the body in Müller's prose presentation across twenty-seven short passages, with verse-numbered markers running from `1.` through `19.`

The Brahmana contains several boundary features that should be preserved honestly:

- **Verse-1 footnote.** Passage `5.595` carries verse 1 with footnote `[*3]` glossing *honey (madhu, the effect)* — Müller's editorial gloss on the central term of the Brahmana, given on the first iteration only.
- **Verses 2–14 as repeated single-passage iterations.** Passages `5.596` through `5.610` carry the second through fourteenth iterations of the *madhu* identification, with each iteration on its own short passage. Two iterations are split across two passages where Müller's editorial paragraphing interrupts the prose: verse 8 across `5.602`–`5.603` (the lightning iteration, mid-line at *and*); verse 13 across `5.608`–`5.609` (the mankind iteration, mid-line at *Likewise*).
- **Footnotes inside the iteration sequence.** Footnote `[*1]` appears at `5.604` on *thunder* in verse 9; footnote `[*2]` appears at `5.607` on *true (satyam)* in verse 12. The other ten iterations carry no footnotes inside the body.
- **Verse 15 unity-figure.** Passage `5.611` carries verse 15 — the wheel/axle/spokes figure — as a single passage with no internal splits.
- **Verses 16–19 with embedded Rig-veda slokas.** Passages `5.612` through `5.620` carry the four mythological-lineage verses, each opening with the same line *Verily Dadhyak Atharvana proclaimed this honey to the two Asvins, and a Rishi, seeing this, said:* and followed by a quoted Rig-veda sloka in Müller's quotation marks. The Rig-veda references are given parenthetically in Müller's prose: Rv. I.116.12 (verse 16); Rv. I.117.22 (verse 17); unspecified (verse 18); Rv. VI.47.18 (verse 19). Verse 16 is split across `5.612`–`5.613` with footnotes `[*1]` (on *gain*) and `[*2]` (on *thunder*); verse 17 is split across `5.614`–`5.616` with footnotes `[*3]` (on *Dadhyak Atharvana*) and `[*1]` (on *Tvashtri*); verse 18 is split across `5.617`–`5.618`; verse 19 is split across `5.619`–`5.620` with footnote `[*2]` on *every form*.

The closing functions structurally as a coda to verse 19's sloka rather than as a numbered verse of its own.

## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 2, Part 4 — paired immediately consecutive Brahmanas of the Second Adhyaya at the boundary between two complementary forms; the Fourth Brahmana ran the Yajnavalkya-Maitreyi dialogue and closed on the question *How, O beloved, should he know himself, the Knower?* — the closing-on-Knower question without a settled formula — the Fifth Brahmana answers not by argument but by fourteen iterations of the *madhu* identification, the wheel-figure, and the closing line *this is the teaching of the Upanishads*. The dialogue's question and the doctrine's iteration sit in adjacent Brahmanas as two ways of arriving at the same recognition.
- **structural-parallel** → Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 1, Part 5 — paired Sanskrit accumulated-mapping pedagogies inside the same Upanishad; the First Adhyaya's Fifth Brahmana mapped the triad *speech, mind, breath* across five outward fields and the bodily-cosmic field, with the recognition reached through the cumulative force of the mappings, the Second Adhyaya's Fifth Brahmana now maps fourteen cosmic-bodily pairs as *madhu* under one identical sentence-template. Both Brahmanas trust the accumulated parallel mapping to do the work the doctrine itself does not state.
- **structural-parallel** → Mundaka Upanishad, Book 2, Part 1 — paired Sanskrit cosmic-bodily mappings and source-becoming-many; the Mundaka's First Khanda of the Second Mundaka holds the cascade *from him is born breath, mind, and all organs of sense, ether, air, light, water, and the earth* and the cosmic Person whose body is the elements (Mundaka 2.1.4), the Brihadaranyaka's Fifth Brahmana of the Second Adhyaya holds the closely related identification through the *madhu*-doctrine — every cosmic element and its bodily counterpart together as *madhu* — and closes with the figure of Indra appearing multiform through the Mayas. Both texts hold the cosmos and the body as the same recognition through different vehicles.
