# Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 4, Part 1

Book 4, Part 1 of the Brihadaranyaka Upanishad — the First Brahmana of the Fourth Adhyaya — opens the Adhyaya in a frame fully reset from the long Janaka Vaideha contest of the Third. The Third Adhyaya had run across nine Brahmanas and closed on the death of Vidagdha Sakalya at the *aśvamedha*, with Yajnavalkya inviting the assembled Brahmanas of the Kurus and the Pankalas to question him — *those Brahmanas durst not* — and the Adhyaya's last words on Brahman as knowledge and bliss given without a *held his peace* form. The Fourth Adhyaya opens directly into a different setting and a different procedure. *When Ganaka Vaideha was sitting (to give audience), Yagnavalkya approached.* The court is no longer the public sacrifice; it is the private royal audience. The challenger-succession of the Third Adhyaya is set aside, and the only named participants of the Brahmana are the king and the priest. The opening line of the Adhyaya gives the formal indication of the reset: not a renewal of the contest's substance but a new dialogic procedure between the two named figures who had stood on either side of the contest's whole length.

The Brahmana's opening verse names the two motives the contest had held in single thread. *Ganaka Vaideha said: 'Yagnavalkya, for what object did you come, wishing for cattle, or for subtle questions?'* The line names openly what the prior Adhyaya had only let stand: that Yajnavalkya's motives are double — the cattle (the contest's prize, the *aśvamedha*'s thousand cows with ten padas of gold tied to the horns) and the subtle questions (the *brahmodya*'s substance). Yajnavalkya's answer is direct: *For both, Your Majesty.* The opening exchange closes with the semicolon-pause held in Müller's prose at the open of `5.939` (*For both, Your Majesty;*) and the Brahmana's recurring six-fold template begins.

The template is held verbatim across verses 2 through 7. The shape, expanded fully:

1. *Yagnavalkya said: 'Let us hear what anybody may have told you.'*
2. *Ganaka Vaideha replied: 'X told me that Y is Brahman.'*
3. *Yagnavalkya said: 'As one who had (the benefit of a good) father, mother, and teacher might tell, so did X tell you that Y is Brahman; for what is the use of a person without Y? But did he tell you the body (ayatana) and the resting-place (pratishtha) of that Brahman?'*
4. *Ganaka Vaideha said: 'He did not tell me.'*
5. *Yagnavalkya said: 'Your Majesty, this (Brahman) stands on one leg only.'*
6. *Ganaka Vaideha said: 'Then tell me, Yagnavalkya.'*
7. *Yagnavalkya said: 'The Z is its body, ether its place, and one should worship it as W.'*
8. *Ganaka Vaideha said: 'What is the nature of that W?'*
9. *Yagnavalkya replied: 'Your Majesty, [substantive elaboration]. Y indeed, O King, is the Highest Brahman. Y does not desert him who worships that (Brahman) with such knowledge, all creatures approach him, and having become a god, he goes to the gods.'*
10. *Ganaka Vaideha said: 'I shall give you (for this) a thousand cows with a bull as big as an elephant.'*
11. *Yagnavalkya said: 'My father was of opinion that one should not accept a reward without having fully instructed a pupil.'*

Each iteration substitutes only X (the named prior teacher), Y (the prior teacher's Brahman-identification), Z (the body), W (the worship-as-X term), and the substantive elaboration that closes the verse before Ganaka's reward-offer. The shape of the dialogue holds verbatim across all six iterations, including the fixed phrase *as one who had (the benefit of a good) father, mother, and teacher might tell*, the *what is the use of a person without Y?* clause, the *body (ayatana) and the resting-place (pratishtha)* request, the *stands on one leg only* idiom for incompleteness, the *Then tell me, Yagnavalkya* invitation, and the closing *having become a god, he goes to the gods* formula. The reward-offer and its refusal close each iteration in the same wording; Ganaka's offer of *a thousand cows with a bull as big as an elephant* is given in identical phrasing six times, and Yajnavalkya's father-citation refusal is given in identical phrasing six times.

The six iterations across verses 2 through 7 give the six prior teachers, their Brahman-identifications, and their body / place / worship-triad correctives:

- **Verse 2 — Gitvan Sailini on speech (vak).** Body: the tongue. Place: ether. Worship as: knowledge. The substantive elaboration runs the full Vedic-textual catalogue: *speech itself (is knowledge). For through speech, Your Majesty, a friend is known (to be a friend), and likewise the Rig-Veda, Yagur-veda, Sama-veda, the Atharvangirasas, the Itihasa (tradition), Purana-vidya (knowledge of the past), the Upanishads, Slokas (verses), Sutras (rules), Anuvyakhyanas and Vyakhyanas (commentaries, &c.); what is sacrificed, what is poured out, what is (to be) eaten and drunk, this world and the other world, and all creatures.* The closing seal: *By speech alone, Your Majesty, Brahman is known, speech indeed, O King, is the Highest Brahman.*
- **Verse 3 — Udanka Saulbayana on life (prana).** Body: breath. Place: ether. Worship as: what is dear. The substantive elaboration: *for the sake of life, Your Majesty, a man sacrifices even for him who is unworthy of sacrifice, he accepts presents from him who is not worthy to bestow presents, nay, he goes to a country, even when there is fear of being hurt, for the sake of life. Life, O King, is the Highest Brahman.*
- **Verse 4 — Barku Varshna on sight (kakshus).** Body: the eye. Place: ether. Worship as: what is true. The substantive elaboration: *if they say to a man who sees with his eye, "Didst thou see?" and he says, "I saw," then it is true. Sight, O King, is the Highest Brahman.*
- **Verse 5 — Gardabhivibhita Bharadvaga on hearing (sruta).** Body: the ear. Place: ether. Worship as: what is endless. The substantive elaboration: *space (disah) itself (is that which is endless), and therefore to whatever space (quarter) he goes, he never comes to the end of it. For space is endless. Space indeed, O King, is hearing, and hearing indeed, O King, is the Highest Brahman.*
- **Verse 6 — Satyakama Gabala on mind (manas).** Body: mind itself. Place: ether. Worship as: bliss. The substantive elaboration: *with the mind does a man desire a woman, and a like son is born of her, and he is bliss. Mind indeed, O King, is the Highest Brahman.*
- **Verse 7 — Vidagdha Sakalya on the heart (hridaya).** Body: the heart itself. Place: ether. Worship as: certainty (sthiti). The substantive elaboration: *the heart indeed, O King, is the body of all things, the heart is the resting-place of all things, for in the heart, O King, all things rest. The heart indeed, O King, is the Highest Brahman.*

The Brahmana closes on the sixth iteration's reward-refusal at `5.1007`. *Yagnavalkya said: 'My father was of opinion that one should not accept a reward without having fully instructed a pupil.'* The narrator's *held his peace* form that had closed all eight prior Adhyaya's silence-Brahmanas is not given; the Brahmana ends on Yajnavalkya's refusal, with the instruction explicitly named as not yet fully complete.

## Context

The Brihadaranyaka Upanishad is one of the two earliest of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads, sharing that position with the Chandogya. It belongs to the Shukla Yajurveda branch of the Vedic corpus and forms the closing portion of the Shatapatha Brahmana — the *Aranyaka* in its name marks it as a forest-text recited at the late stages of Vedic study. Modern scholarship places the Brihadaranyaka in roughly the eighth to sixth century BCE, prior to the Buddha; the text is anonymous, and the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship. The text is the longest of the principal Upanishads and is typically structured in six *adhyayas* (chapters), each containing several *brahmanas* (sections).

Book 4, Part 1 holds three of the most distinctive features of the Adhyaya's opening in concentrated form. The first is the frame-reset. The Third Adhyaya had been one of the longest sustained sequences in the Upanishadic literature on a single literary procedure: the *brahmodya*, the formalized priestly debate at the great sacrifice, with eight named challengers, the contest's silence-closing form preserved across seven Brahmanas, the doctrine of Brahman worked through every angle the contest could give it, and the close of the Adhyaya in the death of the eighth challenger. The Fourth Adhyaya opens with no challenger, no *aśvamedha*, no assembly of Brahmanas of the Kurus and the Pankalas, no enclosing of the cattle. The setting is Ganaka's audience-chamber; the dialogue is between two named participants only; the procedure is the question-and-answer of a king who has already been instructed by other Brahmanas seeking the further instruction of Yajnavalkya. The contrast with the Third Adhyaya's opening — *Ganaka Vaideha sacrificed with a sacrifice at which many presents were offered to the priests of the Asvamedha* — sits inside the same Janaka Vaideha but in the opposite social mode.

The second is the recurring dialogue template. The Brihadaranyaka has used templated dialogue at multiple points — the warp-and-woof formula of Gargi in Book 3, the antaryamin formula of Uddalaka in Book 3, the eight-purusha formula of Sakalya in Book 3 — and the First Brahmana of the Fourth Adhyaya holds a template that is longer and more articulated than any of those. The eleven-step shape (invitation, citation, recognition, body-place-question, not-told, one-leg, *tell me*, body-place-worship, nature-question, substantive elaboration, reward-and-refusal) is held verbatim six times in succession, with only five substitutions per iteration. The form is one of the Upanishadic literature's most sustained instances of a verbatim-repeated multi-step dialogue procedure, and it works the doctrine of the Brahman through six prior teachers' partial identifications, with each correction held in the same shape.

The third is the named teacher Vidagdha Sakalya at verse 7. The Third Adhyaya had closed on Sakalya's death — *Sakalya did not know him, and his head fell, nay, thieves took away his bones, mistaking them for something else* — and the Fourth Adhyaya opens with Sakalya's prior teaching to Ganaka Vaideha named as the sixth and last in the Brahmana's iteration. The narrative does not reconcile the two appearances. Sakalya does not appear at the audience; the line from Ganaka is *Vidagdha Sakalya told me that the heart (hridaya) is Brahman* — a report of a prior teaching, not the presence of the named participant. The Upanishadic narrative is non-linear at the Adhyaya boundary, and the teaching the dead Sakalya had given is preserved through Ganaka's report as one of the six identifications corrected by Yajnavalkya. The form of the correction is the same as the form given for the other five: *the heart indeed, O King, is the body of all things, the heart is the resting-place of all things*. Reading the Brahmana with both Sakalya's death in 3.9 and Sakalya's prior teaching reported in 4.1 — neither smoothed into a single timeline nor treated as a contradiction — recovers the form the Upanishad has when it preserves multiple narrative-stages of a named participant inside the same text.

The closing form is altered. The Adhyaya's prior closing-form had been *held his peace* across seven of the nine Brahmanas of Book 3, with one private withdrawal and one death. The First Brahmana of the Fourth Adhyaya does not give a closing narrative line. The Brahmana closes on Yajnavalkya's sixth refusal of the reward — *one should not accept a reward without having fully instructed a pupil* — with the dialogue explicitly held as incomplete and the instruction explicitly held as not yet finished. The Adhyaya will continue in the Second Brahmana with the further instruction Yajnavalkya has refused to take payment for; the First Brahmana ends with the dialogue's openness preserved.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the Brahmana's verbatim template should be read at full length. The shape is held across six iterations with no abbreviation, no *and so on for the others*, and no narrator's interruption. Each cycle is given in the same eleven-step form, with only the named teacher, the Brahman-identification, the body, the worship-as-X term, and the substantive elaboration substituted. Reading the six iterations alongside each other rather than collapsed into one (*Yajnavalkya corrected six identifications*) recovers the form the Brahmana has when its substance is held through verbatim restatement of a long dialogue procedure. The reward-offer and its refusal at the close of each iteration are part of the form; the verbatim repetition of *a thousand cows with a bull as big as an elephant* and the verbatim repetition of *one should not accept a reward without having fully instructed a pupil* hold the Brahmana's economy of instruction inside the same shape across all six cycles.

Second, the Brahmana's relation to the contest of the Third Adhyaya is held by inversion, not by continuation. The Third Adhyaya's contest had given Yajnavalkya the priest answering challengers' questions in public; the Fourth Adhyaya's First Brahmana gives Yajnavalkya the priest correcting the king's prior teachers in private. The Third Adhyaya's prize had been the cattle Janaka enclosed at the *aśvamedha*; the Fourth Adhyaya's First Brahmana names the cattle openly as part of Yajnavalkya's motives — *wishing for cattle, or for subtle questions* — and the cattle are offered as reward at every cycle and refused at every cycle. The contest's exchange of substance for cattle is held in the open inside the new dialogue, with the offer made and refused six times. Reading the Brahmana with the contest's exchange-procedure visible inside the new private frame recovers the Adhyaya's literary procedure: the contest's economy is acknowledged inside the new Adhyaya rather than abandoned, and the cattle remain unaccepted across the entire First Brahmana.

## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 3, Part 1 — paired Janaka Vaideha–Yajnavalkya frames opening the two consecutive Adhyayas in opposite registers; the First Brahmana of the Third Adhyaya opened with *Ganaka Vaideha sacrificed with a sacrifice at which many presents were offered to the priests of the Asvamedha* and the public *brahmodya* contest with one thousand cows enclosed and ten padas of gold tied to the horns of each, the First Brahmana of the Fourth Adhyaya opens with *When Ganaka Vaideha was sitting (to give audience), Yagnavalkya approached* and the private dialogue with the same one thousand cows offered six times as reward, six times refused. Same king, same priest, same one-thousand-cows offering, in opposite social modes — the public sacrifice with assembled Brahmanas of the Kurus and the Pankalas in one Brahmana, and the audience-chamber with no other named participant in the other.
- **structural-parallel** → Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 2, Part 1 — paired royal-court Brahman-doctrine dialogues with named prior identifications corrected through the same recurring template; the First Brahmana of the Second Adhyaya holds the Gargya Balaki / Ajatasatru dialogue at the king of Kasi's court, with Gargya offering twelve identifications of Brahman — *the person in the sun, in the moon, in the lightning, in the ether, in the wind, in the fire, in the water, in the mirror, the sound that follows a man, in space, the shadow, in the body* — and Ajatasatru replying *No, no! Do not speak to me on this* and giving the corrective adoration each time, the First Brahmana of the Fourth Adhyaya holds Ganaka Vaideha reporting six prior teachers' Brahman-identifications — speech, life, sight, hearing, mind, the heart — and Yajnavalkya replying that *this (Brahman) stands on one leg only* and giving the body, place, and worship-as-X each time. The two Brahmanas hold paired king-and-Brahmana procedures for sequenced Brahman-identifications, with the king teaching the Brahmana in the Second Adhyaya and the Brahmana teaching the king in the Fourth.
- **structural-parallel** → Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 3, Part 9 — paired loci of the named teacher Vidagdha Sakalya inside the Brihadaranyaka; the Ninth Brahmana of the Third Adhyaya holds Sakalya as the eighth and final challenger of the Janaka Vaideha contest, killed at `5.923` after failing to answer Yajnavalkya's eight-fold summary, the First Brahmana of the Fourth Adhyaya holds Sakalya as the sixth and final prior teacher whose teaching to Ganaka Vaideha — *the heart (hridaya) is Brahman* — Yajnavalkya corrects with the body, place, and worship-as-certainty. The two Brahmanas hold the same named participant in two different narrative roles across consecutive Adhyayas, with the Third Adhyaya holding his death and the Fourth Adhyaya holding his prior teaching, and the Upanishadic narrative does not reconcile the two appearances.
