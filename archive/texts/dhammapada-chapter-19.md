# Dhammapada, Chapter 19 (The Just)

Chapter 19 of the Dhammapada — the *Dhammaṭṭha-vagga*, "The Just" or "Established in Dhamma" — is a chapter of fourteen passages turning on a single compositional pattern: a false version of an identity is named, then the true version is given by inward conduct rather than outward sign. The chapter walks through nine such identities. *A man is not just if he carries a matter by violence; no, he who distinguishes both right and wrong, who is learned and leads others, not by violence, but by law and equity, and who is guarded by the law and intelligent, he is called just* (256). *A man is not learned because he talks much; he who is patient, free from hatred and fear, he is called learned* (258). *A man is not a supporter of the law because he talks much; even if a man has learnt little, but sees the law bodily, he is a supporter of the law* (259). *A man is not an elder because his head is grey; his age may be ripe, but he is called `Old-in-vain'* (260); the true elder is one in whom there is *truth, virtue, love, restraint, moderation*, who is free from impurity and wise (261). The respectable (262–263), the *Samana* (264–265), the *Bhikshu* (266–267), the *Muni* (268), and the *Ariya* (270) are each given the same treatment — a paired or paired-and-merged contrast between the outward sign (much talking, beauty of complexion, tonsure, alms-begging, observing silence, ascetic violence) and the inward conduct (freedom from hatred, the quieting of evil, adopting the whole law, weighing good and evil, having pity on living creatures). The chapter closes (271) on a warning that even discipline, learning, trance, and sleeping alone are not enough — the *Bhikshu* should not be confident until the extinction of desires.

## Context

The Dhammapada is one of the books of the Khuddaka Nikaya, the fifth and shortest section of the Sutta Pitaka in the Pali Canon. It collects 423 verses arranged in twenty-six chapters by theme — pairs, heedfulness, mind, flowers, the sage, evil, and so on. The verses are attributed to the Buddha and were transmitted orally for several centuries before being committed to writing in Sri Lanka in the first century BCE. The collection took shape in roughly the third century BCE, in connection with the Theravada transmission of the canon.

Chapter 19 is the *Dhammaṭṭha-vagga*, "The Just." Its passages are numbered 256, 258 through 268, 270, and 271 in F. Max Müller's 1881 translation, the version held in the Archive library — fourteen passages, where the standard continuous Pali numbering of the Dhammapada gives seventeen verses (256–272). Müller has combined three pairs of conventional verses into single English periods: verses 256 and 257 at passage 19.256 (the negative-and-positive statement of the *just*); verses 268 and 269 at passage 19.268 (the negative-and-positive statement of the *Muni*); and verses 271 and 272 at passage 19.271 (the closing warning combined with the *extinction-of-desires* counsel). With no passages numbered 19.257, 19.269, or 19.272 in the source, this is the most merged chapter in the Müller Dhammapada — three internal merges where Chapters 4, 6, 8, 11, 14, and 17 each have one. Per Atlas-rule **OCR/transcription honesty**, the merged passages are preserved as Müller wrote them; the chapter therefore has fourteen passages here against the standard seventeen verses elsewhere. The next chapter opens at passage 20.273. Each verse is short — two or four lines in the Pali — and each completes one statement of the figure, except the three merged passages, which each complete two.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the chapter is built on a single repeated compositional move: state what an identity is *not* given by, then state what it *is* given by. The form is so consistent that the chapter can be read as a single grammar applied to nine identities. The grammar holds: *one is not X by Y* (the outward sign), *one is X by Z* (the inward conduct). Reading the chapter as one grammar applied nine times, rather than as fourteen separate aphorisms, recovers the form the chapter is built on. The chapter's value is partly in this repeated grammar — the figure that no identity is given by its outward sign alone, and that the test is always inward.

Second, Müller's three merges (256/257, 268/269, 271/272) all combine a *what-it-is-not* statement with its paired *what-it-is* statement into a single English period. The merges fall on three of the chapter's nine identity-pairs (the just, the Muni, and the closing warning). The other six identity-pairs are kept as two distinct verses (the learned at 258 — though the second half is implied; the supporter of the law at 259; the elder at 260–261; the respectable at 262–263; the Samana at 264–265; the Bhikshu at 266–267). Reading the chapter with the awareness that the grammar is the same throughout, even where Müller has merged or kept the pair distinct, keeps the form the verses share.

## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Dhammapada, Chapter 4 (Flowers), Chapter 6 (The Wise Man), Chapter 8 (The Thousands), Chapter 11 (Old Age), Chapter 14 (The Buddha), and Chapter 17 (Anger) — the earlier chapters with Müller merging-irregularities; each of those chapters has one internal merge, while Chapter 19 has three. The merging is most concentrated here because the chapter's compositional grammar is itself paired (*one is not X by Y, one is X by Z*), and Müller's English habit of using semicolons and colons to render the Pali paired-construction into one sentence falls on the points where the grammar is most evident.
- **structural-parallel** → Dhammapada, Chapter 6 (The Wise Man) — the earlier chapter that already worked the figure of the *paṇḍita* by inward conduct rather than outward sign; Chapter 6 with thirteen passages on the wise man as guide, teacher, self-fashioner, and rock-not-shaken-by-wind, Chapter 19 with verse 258 returning to the same identity in the chapter's *true-versus-false* form: *a man is not learned because he talks much; he who is patient, free from hatred and fear, he is called learned*. The same identity is held twice, with Chapter 6 elaborating it positively and Chapter 19 setting it against its outward counterfeit.
- **structural-parallel** → Dhammapada, Chapter 7 (The Venerable) — the earlier chapter on the *arahant* whose journey is finished and whose path cannot be tracked; Chapter 7 with ten verses on the venerable, Chapter 19 verse 270 returning to the *Ariya* in the chapter's compositional form: *a man is not an elect (Ariya) because he injures living creatures; because he has pity on all living creatures, therefore is a man called Ariya*. The same elect-figure is held twice, with Chapter 7 elaborating his finished state and Chapter 19 setting his identity by his conduct.
- **structural-parallel** → Matthew 5 — the Christian shelf-pair on the inversion of outward and inward criteria; the Sermon on the Mount with its line that not those who say *Lord, Lord* but those who do the will of the Father enter the kingdom, Dhammapada 19 with its repeated grammar that no identity is given by outward sign alone — not the tonsure, not the silence, not the talking, not the begging — but only by inward conduct. Both texts hold the figure that the test is inward.
- **structural-parallel** → Plato, Apology — the Greek pair on the inversion of reputation and reality; Socrates' insistence that those who are reputed wise are not, and that virtue is shown by what one does rather than what one says, Dhammapada 19 verse 258 with the same recognition that *a man is not learned because he talks much*. Both texts hold the same inversion of the outward sign of an identity and the test of its substance.
