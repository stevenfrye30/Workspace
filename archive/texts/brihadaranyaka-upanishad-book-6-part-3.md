# Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 6, Part 3

Book 6, Part 3 of the Brihadaranyaka Upanishad — the Third Brahmana of the Sixth Adhyaya — works a substantive procedural-ritual instruction distinct from the prior Brahmanas of Book 6. The First Brahmana had given six substantive parallel identifications (Breath/best, Speech/richest, Eye/firm rest, Ear/success, Mind/home, Seed/generation) followed by the substantive senses-quarrel narrative; the Second Brahmana had given the substantive Pancagni-vidya and the Two Paths through the named-figure dialogue with Pravahana Gaivali. The Third Brahmana works the substantive Mantha rite as substantive ritual procedure: a sacrificial procedure that the substantive performer enacts over twelve days with substantive ritual implements, substantive prayer-formulas, substantive eating, substantive recitation, and substantive transmission-restriction. The substantive form is the most substantively procedural Brahmana of the Brihadaranyaka so far — substantive operational instruction rather than substantive doctrinal exposition — and it preserves substantive content across substantive ritual sections.

The Brahmana opens at `5.1294` with the substantive preparation. *If a man wishes to reach greatness (wealth for performing sacrifices), he performs the upasad rule during twelve days (i.e. he lives on small quantities of milk), beginning on an auspicious day of the light half of the moon during the northern progress of the sun, collecting at the same time in a cup or a dish made of Udumbara wood all sorts of herbs, including fruits.* The substantive form is the substantive astronomical-calendrical specification (light half of moon, northern progress of sun), the substantive substance-restriction (small quantities of milk for twelve days), and the substantive material-specification (Udumbara-wood vessel for herbs and fruits). The substantive *upasad* rule is a substantive Vedic preparatory observance that the substantive Brahmana names as the substantive entry-condition for the substantive Mantha rite.

Verse 1 continues at `5.1295` with the substantive ritual setup: *He sweeps the floor (near the house-altar, avasathya), sprinkles it, lays the fire, spreads grass round it according to rule, prepares the clarified butter (agya), and on a day, presided over by a male star (nakshatra), after having properly mixed the Mantha (the herbs, fruits, milk, honey, &c.), he sacrifices (he pours agya into the fire), saying...* The substantive sequence is the substantive five-step ritual setup (sweeping, sprinkling, laying fire, spreading grass, preparing agya) followed by the substantive male-nakshatra-day Mantha-mixing. The substantive opening prayer addresses Gatavedas (Agni-as-knower-of-all): *O Gatavedas, whatever adverse gods there are in thee, who defeat the desires of men, to them I offer this portion; may they, being pleased, please me with all desires.* The substantive form is the substantive propitiation of substantive adverse gods, with *Svaha!* as the substantive ritual-call closing the prayer. Verse 2 at `5.1297`–`5.1304` holds the substantive seven-fold *Svaha* paired oblations. Each oblation runs the same shape: *He then says, Svaha to X, Svaha to Y, pours ghee into the fire, and throws what remains into the Mantha (mortar).* The substantive form is the substantive paired-oblation procedure, with X (substantive faculty) and Y (substantive quality) substituted at each iteration. Verse 3 at `5.1305`–`5.1317` runs thirteen further substantive *Svaha* oblations in the same form: Agni (fire), Soma, Bhuh (earth), Bhuvah (sky), Svah (heaven), the combined Bhur-Bhuvah-Svah, Brahman (the priesthood), Kshatra (the knighthood), the Past, the Future, the Universe, all things, and Pragapati. The substantive cosmological-and-Vedic catalogue runs through substantive elemental gods (Agni, Soma), the three Vyāhṛtis (Bhuh, Bhuvah, Svah) given individually and combined, the substantive social-classes (Brahman, Kshatra), the substantive temporal scope (Past, Future), the substantive universal scope (Universe, all things), and the substantive creator (Pragapati). Verse 4 at `5.1318` holds the substantive Touching-prayer. *Then he touches it (the Mantha, which is dedicated to Prana, breath), saying: 'Thou art fleet (as breath). Thou art burning (as fire). Thou art full (as Brahman). Thou art firm (as the sky). Thou art the abode of all (as the earth). Thou hast been saluted with Hin (at the beginning of the sacrifice by the prastotri). Thou art celebrated again (by the agnidhra in the middle of the sacrifice). Thou art bright in the wet (cloud). Thou art great. Thou art powerful. Thou art food (as Soma). Verse 6 at `5.1322`–`5.1325` holds the substantive Eating-with-Savitri-verse — one of the substantive most-cited Vedic-recitation procedures. The substantive form runs the substantive Rigvedic Savitri verse line by line, alternating with substantive honey-prayers and the substantive Vyāhṛti calls:

- **First Savitri-line and honey-prayer at `5.1322`**: *Tat savitur varenyam (We meditate on that adorable light) — The winds drop honey for the righteous, the rivers drop honey, may our plants be sweet as honey! Bhuh (earth) Svaha!* Müller's footnote `[*1]` attaches at *Tat savitur varenyam*.
- **Second Savitri-line and honey-prayer at `5.1323`**: *Bhargo devasya dhimahi (of the divine Savitri) — May the night be honey in the morning, may the air above the earth, may heaven, our father, be honey! Bhuvah (sky) Svaha!*
- **Third Savitri-line and honey-prayer at `5.1324`**: *Dhiyo yo nah prokodayat (who should rouse our thoughts) — May the tree be full of honey, may the sun be full of honey, may our cows be sweet like honey! Svah (heaven) Svaha!*

The substantive Savitri is the substantive Rigvedic Sandhya-mantra (Rigveda 3.62.10), and the substantive form here is the substantive line-by-line interleaving with substantive *madhu-vidyā* (honey-knowledge) verses and the three Vyāhṛti calls. Passage `5.1325` continues verse 6 with the substantive closing: *He repeats the whole Savitri verse, and all the verses about the honey, thinking, May I be all this! Bhur, Bhuvah, Svah, Svaha! Having thus swallowed all, he washes his hands, and sits down behind the altar, turning his head to the East. In the morning he worships Aditya (the sun), with the hymn, 'Thou art the best lotus of the four quarters, may I become the best lotus among men.' Then returning as he came, he sits down behind the altar and recites the genealogical list.* Müller's footnote `[*2]` attaches at *the genealogical list* — the substantive *vamsha* recitation that follows.

Verses 7–12 at `5.1326`–`5.1331` give the substantive transmission-*vamsha* of the Mantha-doctrine through six named teachers. Each verse holds the same shape: *X told this (Mantha-doctrine) to his pupil Y, and said: 'If a man were to pour it on a dry stick, branches would grow, and leaves spring forth.'* The substantive transmission-line repeats verbatim across the six iterations, with the substantive simile of the substantive dry-stick-and-branches preserved at each transmission-step.

The substantive six-teacher transmission:

1. **Verse 7 at `5.1326`** — Uddalaka Aruni → Vagasaneya Yagnavalkya. The substantive Uddalaka Aruni is the same substantive named figure as Svetaketu's father in the prior Brahmana of the Sixth Adhyaya (Book 6 Part 2 at `5.1271`–`5.1281`); the substantive Vagasaneya Yagnavalkya is the substantive Yajnavalkya of Books 3 and 4.
2. **Verse 8 at `5.1327`** — Vagasaneya Yagnavalkya → Madhuka Paingya.
3. **Verse 9 at `5.1328`** — Madhuka Paingya → Kula Bhagavitti.
4. **Verse 10 at `5.1329`** — Kula Bhagavitti → Ganaki Ayasthuna.
5. **Verse 11 at `5.1330`** — Ganaki Ayasthuna → Satyakama Gabala. The substantive Satyakama Gabala is the same substantive named teacher who appears in Book 4 Part 1 verse 6 at `5.986` as a prior teacher of Janaka teaching that *mind is Brahman*.
6. **Verse 12 at `5.1331`** — Satyakama Gabala → his pupils. The substantive transmission closes at the unspecified pupils of Satyakama Gabala.

The substantive transmission-restriction follows at `5.1332`: *Let no one tell this to any one, except to a son or to a pupil.* Müller's footnotes `[*1]` and `[*2]` attach at the substantive restriction-clause. The substantive form holds the substantive Mantha-doctrine as substantive esoteric teaching transmitted only through substantive son-or-pupil relations.

Verse 13 at `5.1333`–`5.1335` closes the Brahmana with substantive material-specifications. *Four things are made of the wood of the Udumbara tree, the sacrificial ladle (sruva), the cup (kamasa), the fuel, and the two churning sticks.* The substantive four-fold Udumbara-wood specification is given as a substantive ritual-implement catalogue. *There are ten kinds of village (cultivated) seeds, viz. rice and barley (brihiyavas), sesamum and kidney-beans (tilamashas), millet and panic seed (anupriyangavas), wheat (godhumas), lentils (masuras), pulse (khalvas), and vetches (khalakulas).* Müller's footnote `[*3]` attaches at *vetches (khalakulas)*. The closing instruction at `5.1335`: *After having ground these he sprinkles them with curds (dadhi), honey, and ghee, and then offers (the proper portions) of clarified butter (agya).* The substantive closing is the substantive grinding-and-sprinkling instruction with the substantive offering of agya — the substantive completion of the substantive Mantha-preparation.

The Brahmana ends inside the substantive closing instruction. No closing seal beyond the substantive ritual instruction; no narrator's voice; no *held his peace* form. The substantive content of the Brahmana is the substantive ritual procedure itself.

## Context

The Brihadaranyaka Upanishad is one of the two earliest of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads, sharing that position with the Chandogya. It belongs to the Shukla Yajurveda branch of the Vedic corpus and forms the closing portion of the Shatapatha Brahmana — the *Aranyaka* in its name marks it as a forest-text recited at the late stages of Vedic study. Modern scholarship places the Brihadaranyaka in roughly the eighth to sixth century BCE, prior to the Buddha; the text is anonymous, and the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship. The text is the longest of the principal Upanishads and is typically structured in six *adhyayas* (chapters), each containing several *brahmanas* (sections).

Book 6, Part 3 holds three of the most distinctive features of the Brihadaranyaka in concentrated form. The first is the substantive ritual-procedural form. The Brihadaranyaka has held substantive ritual content across multiple Brahmanas — the substantive *aśvamedha* horse-sacrifice frame of Book 1, the substantive Janaka-Vaideha contest of Book 3, the substantive *Pancagni-vidya* of Book 6 Part 2 — but the Third Brahmana of the Sixth Adhyaya is the substantive most-detailed substantive operational ritual instruction in the Upanishad. The substantive Mantha rite is given as substantive twelve-day preparation, substantive sequential oblations, substantive prayer-formulas, substantive eating-with-Savitri, substantive transmission, and substantive material-specifications. The substantive form holds the substantive *vidyā* tradition's substantive operational dimension: the substantive ritual that the substantive performer enacts to substantively obtain the substantive *vidyā*'s benefit.

The second is the substantive transmission-*vamsha* with cross-Brahmana named figures. The substantive six-teacher transmission at verses 7–12 names three figures who appear elsewhere in the Brihadaranyaka:
- **Uddalaka Aruni** appears at the prior Brahmana (Book 6 Part 2) as the substantive father of Svetaketu and the substantive Brahmana sage who becomes Pravahana Gaivali's pupil to receive the substantive *Pancagni-vidya*. The substantive transmission-*vamsha* of Book 6 Part 3 places the substantive Mantha-doctrine at the substantive open of the chain — Uddalaka teaches Yagnavalkya — paired with the substantive *Pancagni-vidya* that Uddalaka had received from Pravahana Gaivali.
- **Vagasaneya Yagnavalkya** (the substantive Yajnavalkya) appears across Books 3 and 4 as the substantive central named teacher of the Brihadaranyaka's Yajnavalkya cycle. The substantive transmission-*vamsha* places the substantive Mantha-doctrine in the substantive Yajnavalkya line of substantive teaching.
- **Satyakama Gabala** appears at Book 4 Part 1 verse 6 as a substantive prior teacher of Janaka who had taught that *mind (manas) is Brahman*. The substantive transmission-*vamsha* names Satyakama as the substantive fifth-step teacher of the Mantha-doctrine, and the substantive cross-reference holds Satyakama as a substantive recurring named teacher across the Brihadaranyaka.

The substantive cross-references substantively link the Third Brahmana of the Sixth Adhyaya to the substantive prior Brahmanas, with the substantive transmission-*vamsha* functioning as a substantive cross-Brahmana lineage rather than as an isolated substantive list.

The third is the substantive Savitri-verse-with-honey-prayers form at verse 6. The substantive Rigvedic Savitri verse (Rigveda 3.62.10) is the substantive most-cited Vedic mantra, and the substantive *madhu-vidyā* (honey-knowledge) tradition is one of the substantive principal substantive *vidyā* traditions of the Brihadaranyaka (with the Second Adhyaya's Madhu-Brahmanas as its principal substantive locus). The substantive Eating-with-Savitri form of verse 6 substantively combines the substantive Savitri-recitation with the substantive *madhu-vidyā* honey-prayers, with the substantive Savitri-line given first and the substantive honey-prayer given second at each of the three iterations. The substantive form holds the substantive Vedic-recitation-and-meditation tradition as the substantive procedure of the substantive Mantha-rite's substantive eating phase.

The closing form is the Adhyaya's substantive ritual-material specifications. The substantive four Udumbara-wood items and the substantive ten village seeds are given as substantive technical specifications without further substantive doctrinal content, and the substantive Brahmana ends inside the substantive grinding-and-sprinkling instruction. The substantive form holds the substantive ritual at the substantive material level as substantively complete content.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the substantive ritual-procedure should be read at full length without summarizing the substantive sequential oblations into a single substantive statement. The substantive seven faculty-quality oblations of verse 2 and the substantive thirteen cosmological-Vedic oblations of verse 3 are given on substantive twenty short passages, and the substantive form preserves the substantive verbatim repetition of the substantive *He then says, Svaha to X, [Svaha to Y,] pours ghee into the fire, and throws what remains into the Mantha (mortar)* clause across each iteration. Second, the substantive transmission-*vamsha* of verses 7–12 should be read with the substantive cross-Brahmana named figures preserved. Reading the substantive transmission-line *X told this (Mantha-doctrine) to his pupil Y, and said: 'If a man were to pour it on a dry stick, branches would grow, and leaves spring forth'* with the substantive named figures — Uddalaka Aruni from Book 6 Part 2, Vagasaneya Yagnavalkya from Books 3–4, Satyakama Gabala from Book 4 Part 1 — held in mind as substantive cross-Brahmana characters recovers the substantive form: substantive transmission-*vamsha* as substantive cross-Brahmana lineage rather than substantive isolated list of names. The substantive Brahmana places the substantive Mantha-doctrine inside the substantive teacher-lineage of the Brihadaranyaka rather than as substantive new teaching introduced ex novo.
