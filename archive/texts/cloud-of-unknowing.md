# The Cloud of Unknowing

## Primary Text

Anonymous fourteenth-century English author. Evelyn Underhill, *A Book of Contemplation the Which is Called The Cloud of Unknowing, in the Which a Soul is Oned with God*, London: John M. Watkins, 1922 (sixth edition). Public domain via Project Gutenberg / Internet Archive. Selections below are canonical apophatic passages from chapters 3, 4, 6, and 7 — the contemplative-method foundations and the doctrine that God is reached by love, not thought.

**Chapter 3.** Lift up thine heart unto God with a meek stirring of love; and mean Himself, and none of His goods. And thereto, look thee loath to think on aught but Himself. So that nought work in thy wit, nor in thy will, but only Himself.

**Chapter 4.** I had liefer have such a soothfast meek stirring of love unwitting, secretly springing in spirit, and that not stirred with any thought of any thing under God, than I had all the wits and special revelations that ever yet were seen or heard of in this life.

**Chapter 6.** But now thou askest me and sayest, "How shall I think on Himself, and what is He?" and to this I cannot answer thee but thus: "I wot not." For thou hast brought me with thy question into that same darkness, and into that same cloud of unknowing, that I would thou wert in thyself. For of all other creatures and their works — yea, and of the works of God's self — may a man through grace have fulhead of knowing, and well he can think of them: but of God Himself can no man think. And therefore I would leave all that thing that I can think, and choose to my love that thing that I cannot think. For why; He may well be loved, but not thought. By love may He be gotten and holden; but by thought never.

**Chapter 7.** And if any thought rise and will press continually above thee betwixt thee and that darkness, and ask thee saying, "What seekest thou, and what wouldest thou have?" say thou, that it is God that thou wouldest have. "Him I covet, Him I seek, and nought but Him." Smite upon that thick cloud of unknowing with a sharp dart of longing love, and go not thence for thing that befalleth.

## Atlas Notes

The Cloud of Unknowing is a foundational text of Western Christian apophatic contemplation, drawing on Pseudo-Dionysius's *Mystical Theology* and the Greek apophatic tradition. The four chapters excerpted above contain the work's central doctrinal moves: the orientation of the contemplative toward God by love-stirring rather than cognition (ch. 3); the preference of "meek stirring of love unwitting" over discursive understanding (ch. 4); the categorical denial that God can be reached by thought, paired with the affirmation that He is reached by love (ch. 6); and the imagery of "smiting" the cloud of unknowing with a "dart of longing love" (ch. 7).
