# Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 1, Part 2

Book 1, Part 2 of the Brihadaranyaka Upanishad — the Second Brahmana of the First Adhyaya — is a sustained creation narrative built on the figure of *mṛtyu* (Death) as the primordial reality. The opening line gives the figure: *naiveha kim cana agra āsīt; mṛtyunaivedam āvṛtam āsīt aśanāyayā; aśanāyā hi mṛtyuḥ* — *in the beginning there was nothing here whatsoever; by Death indeed all this was concealed — by hunger; for death is hunger.* Death wishes for a body — *so 'kāmayata, ātmanvī syām iti* — and worships; from his worship water arises, and the etymology gives the name *arka* to water. The froth of water hardens into earth. From Death resting on the earth, Agni emerges full of light; *prāṇa* divides threefold (Agni, Aditya, Vayu) and the cosmic body is mapped onto the directions: head as East, arms as the northeast and southeast, tail as West, legs as the northwest and southwest, sides as South and North, back as heaven, belly as sky, dust as earth. Then a second wish: *let a second body be born of me*. Death embraces Speech; the year is born from their union; Death's first impulse is to swallow him, but he resolves instead to eat everything. The etymology of *Aditi* is given here — *because he eats everything, Aditi is called Aditi*. Death performs penance; *glorious power* (*prāṇa*) goes out of him; the body swells; the body becomes a horse — *aśva* because it swelled (*aśvat*), *medhya* because it was fit for sacrifice — and the cosmic ritual is named: *aśva-medha*. The shining sun is the Asvamedha-sacrifice; its body is the year; Agni is the sacrificial fire; the worlds are its bodies; sacrifice and fire are one deity — Death. *He who knows this overcomes another death; death does not reach him; death is his Self.*

## Context

The Brihadaranyaka Upanishad is one of the two earliest of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads, sharing that position with the Chandogya. It belongs to the Shukla Yajurveda branch of the Vedic corpus and forms the closing portion of the Shatapatha Brahmana — the *Aranyaka* in its name marks it as a forest-text recited at the late stages of Vedic study. Modern scholarship places the Brihadaranyaka in roughly the eighth to sixth century BCE, prior to the Buddha; the text is anonymous, and the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship. The text is the longest of the principal Upanishads and is typically structured in six *adhyayas* (chapters), each containing several *brahmanas* (sections).

Book 1, Part 2 is the second Brahmana of the First Adhyaya. The First Brahmana — the previous Archive entry — opens the Upanishad with the figure of the sacrificial horse mapped onto the cosmos: dawn as head, sun as eye, wind as breath, year as body. The Second Brahmana extends and reframes that figure into a sustained cosmogonic narrative. The figure of Death/Hunger as the source — *aśanāyā hi mṛtyuḥ* — is one of the most striking openings of the Upanishadic corpus, and the Brahmana works the cosmogony, the etymological plays, and the institution of the *aśvamedha* into a single continuous story. The line *he who knows this overcomes another death* will recur through the Brihadaranyaka and is one of its signature formulas; the recognition is not that death is unreal but that the one who knows the form of the cosmic ritual passes beyond a second dying.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the Brahmana works through etymological pairs. *Arka* (water) is named from Death's worshipping (*arkate*); *Aditi* is named because Death eats everything (*atti* + *aditi*); *aśva* (horse) is named because the body *swelled* (*aśvat*); *aśva-medha* (horse-sacrifice) is named because the swollen body became *fit for sacrifice* (*medhya*). Each etymology is the Brahmana's way of binding the cosmogony to the language of the recitation; the names are not labels but the form by which the recognition is held. Reading the Brahmana with attention to where the etymological move occurs, and to the way each name follows from the action it traces, recovers what the late-Vedic narrative is doing when it works in this register.

Second, the Brahmana places ritual practice inside the same form as creation. The story of Death's two bodies — first the body that becomes the cosmos through the threefold *prāṇa*, second the body that becomes the year through union with Speech — closes on the institution of the *aśvamedha*: the horse-sacrifice the recitation knows is the same ritual the cosmogony has produced. Reading the Brahmana with cosmogony and ritual held as the same story rather than as separate teachings keeps the form the late-Vedic narrative gives its most ambitious unit. The closing recognition — that the shining sun, the year, the sacrificial fire, and the worlds are all one deity, Death — places the recognition at the level of the ritual that the recitation itself enacts.

## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 1, Part 1 — paired immediately consecutive Brahmanas of the First Adhyaya; the First Brahmana opens the Upanishad with the sacrificial horse mapped onto the cosmos — dawn as head, sun as eye, wind as breath, year as body — the Second Brahmana extends and reframes the figure as a sustained cosmogonic narrative built on Death and Hunger as the primordial source, ending in the institution of the *aśvamedha*. The opening figure and the cosmogony that grounds it, given in two adjacent units.
- **structural-parallel** → Taittiriyaka Upanishad, Book 2, Part 6 — paired Sanskrit creation formulas built on the source's wish for a second body or for many; the Taittiriyaka's Sixth Anuvaka of the Brahmanandavalli holds the formula *so 'kāmayata, bahu syām, prajāyeyeti* — *he wished, may I be many, may I grow forth* — followed by the *sat/tyat* distinction, the Brihadaranyaka's Second Brahmana opens with *so 'kāmayata, ātmanvī syām iti* — *he wished, let me have a body* — and develops the wish through the cosmogony of two successive bodies. Paired moments in the late-Vedic literature where the source's becoming-many is given as a wish.
- **structural-parallel** → Mundaka Upanishad, Book 2, Part 1 — paired Sanskrit emanation passages where the elements come forth from the source; the Mundaka's First Khanda of the Second Mundaka holds the cascade *from him is born breath, mind, and all organs of sense, ether, air, light, water, and the earth* (Mundaka 2.1.3), the Brihadaranyaka's Second Brahmana holds a closely related sequence — water arising from Death's worship, earth from the froth of water, fire from Death's resting on the earth, *prāṇa* dividing threefold into Agni, Aditya, and Vayu. Both texts hold the same kind of cosmogonic descent, the Mundaka in the form of emanation from the Imperishable and the Brihadaranyaka in the form of Death's wish.
