# Dhammapada, Chapter 25 (The Mendicant)

Chapter 25 of the Dhammapada — the *Bhikkhu-vagga*, "The Mendicant" — is a chapter of twenty-three verses on the *bhikkhu*, the mendicant or monk, defined throughout the chapter by inward conduct rather than by institutional role. The opening pair states the form across the senses: *restraint in the eye is good, good is restraint in the ear, in the nose restraint is good, good is restraint in the tongue* (360); *in the body restraint is good, good is restraint in speech, in thought restraint is good, good is restraint in all things. A Bhikshu, restrained in all things, is freed from all pain* (361). Verse 362 turns the figure to the conduct that defines the Bhikshu: he who controls his hand, his feet, his speech, who delights inwardly, is collected, solitary, and content — *him they call Bhikshu*. Verses 363–364 hold the Bhikshu in his speech and in his dwelling in the law. Verses 365–366 hold the figure of modesty: not despising what one has received and not envying others. Verse 367 holds the chapter's first identity-by-conduct line: *he who never identifies himself with name and form, and does not grieve over what is no more, he indeed is called a Bhikshu.* Verses 368–370 turn to liberation imagery: acting with kindness, reaching the quiet place (368); *empty this boat! if emptied, it will go quickly* (369); cutting off the five, leaving the five, rising above the five — *a Bhikshu, who has escaped from the five fetters, he is called Oghatinna, `saved from the flood'* (370). Verse 371 holds a warning: meditate and be not heedless, that you may not have to swallow the iron ball in hell. Verse 372 holds the chapter's reciprocal: *without knowledge there is no meditation, without meditation there is no knowledge: he who has knowledge and meditation is near unto Nirvana.* Verses 373–374 hold the Bhikshu in his empty house and the figure of seeing the origin and destruction of the *khandha*. Verses 375–376 give the beginning of the work — watchfulness, contentedness, restraint, noble friends, charity, perfect duties. Verse 377 holds the Vassika-plant simile: as it sheds withered flowers, so should men shed passion and hatred. Verse 378 holds the quiet Bhikshu — body, tongue, and mind quieted, baits of the world rejected. Verses 379 and 380 hold the chapter's self-discipline pillar: *rouse thyself by thyself, examine thyself by thyself, thus self-protected and attentive wilt thou live happily, O Bhikshu!* (379); *for self is the lord of self, self is the refuge of self; therefore curb thyself as the merchant curbs a good horse* (380). Verses 381–382 close the chapter on delight and the young Bhikshu: full of delight, calm in the doctrine of Buddha, reaching Nirvana (381); *he who, even as a young Bhikshu, applies himself to the doctrine of Buddha, brightens up this world, like the moon when free from clouds* (382).

## Context

The Dhammapada is one of the books of the Khuddaka Nikaya, the fifth and shortest section of the Sutta Pitaka in the Pali Canon. It collects 423 verses arranged in twenty-six chapters by theme — pairs, heedfulness, mind, flowers, the sage, evil, and so on. The verses are attributed to the Buddha and were transmitted orally for several centuries before being committed to writing in Sri Lanka in the first century BCE. The collection took shape in roughly the third century BCE, in connection with the Theravada transmission of the canon.

Chapter 25 is the *Bhikkhu-vagga*, "The Mendicant." Its twenty-three verses are numbered 360 through 382 in the standard continuous numbering of the Dhammapada — and in F. Max Müller's 1881 translation, the version held in the Archive library, the count and numbering match the standard exactly, with no merged or missing verse of the kind that occurs in Chapters 4, 6, 8, 11, 14, 17, and 19. The Pali word *bhikkhu* — mendicant, monk, almsman, beggar — is rendered uniformly by Müller as "Bhikshu" (the Sanskrit form) through the chapter, with "mendicant" used occasionally as a synonym. The *khandha* (aggregates that compose the person — form, sensation, perception, mental formations, consciousness) is preserved in transliteration at verse 374. The proper name *Vassika* (the jasmine flower) is preserved at verse 377; *Oghatinna* (one who has crossed the flood) is preserved at verse 370. Each verse is short — two or four lines in the Pali — and each completes one statement of the figure.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the chapter holds the *bhikkhu* through inward conduct rather than outward role. Verse 362 names the figure: he who controls hand, feet, speech, who delights inwardly, who is collected, solitary, and content — *him they call Bhikshu*. Verse 367 says the same: he who does not identify with name and form and does not grieve over what is no more — *he indeed is called a Bhikshu*. Verse 378 says the same: the Bhikshu whose body, tongue, and mind are quieted — *he is called quiet*. The chapter does not say the Bhikshu is the one who has been ordained or who wears the robe; it says the Bhikshu is the one who does these things. Reading the chapter with this identity-by-conduct figure held visible — paralleling Chapter 19's *true X / false X* compositional grammar — recovers the form the chapter is built on.

Second, verses 379 and 380 are the chapter's self-discipline pillar. *Rouse thyself by thyself, examine thyself by thyself, thus self-protected and attentive wilt thou live happily, O Bhikshu! For self is the lord of self, self is the refuge of self; therefore curb thyself as the merchant curbs a good horse.* The two verses repeat the central self-reliance line of Chapter 12 (*self is the lord of self, who else could be the lord?* at 12.160), here addressed directly to the Bhikshu. Reading the two verses as the chapter's structural pillar — surrounded by the practices the Bhikshu is to undertake — keeps the form the chapter has. The Bhikshu's discipline is on himself.

## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Dhammapada, Chapter 19 (The Just) — the earlier chapter that already worked the figure of the *true Bhikshu* defined by conduct rather than outward sign; Chapter 19 verses 266 and 267 hold *a man is not a mendicant (Bhikshu) simply because he asks others for alms; he who adopts the whole law is a Bhikshu, not he who only begs* and *he who is above good and evil, who is chaste, who with knowledge passes through the world, he indeed is called a Bhikshu*. Chapter 25 returns to the same identity-by-conduct figure as the chapter's organising principle, with three repetitions at verses 362, 367, and 378.
- **structural-parallel** → Dhammapada, Chapter 12 (Self) — the earlier chapter on self-government; Chapter 12 verse 160 holds *self is the lord of self, who else could be the lord? With self well subdued, a man finds a lord such as few can find*, Chapter 25 verses 379–380 hold the same line addressed directly to the Bhikshu — *for self is the lord of self, self is the refuge of self*. The Dhammapada repeats verses or near-verses across chapters as compositional layering; Chapter 25's repetition of Chapter 12's central self-reliance figure is one of the clearest such layerings.
- **structural-parallel** → Dhammapada, Chapter 17 (Anger) — the earlier chapter on the threefold control of body, tongue, and mind; Chapter 17 verses 231–234 hold the threefold parallel — beware of bodily anger, of the anger of the tongue, of the anger of the mind — and the closing summary that the wise who control these three are well controlled, Chapter 25 verse 378 holds the same threefold figure: *the Bhikshu whose body and tongue and mind are quieted, who is collected, and has rejected the baits of the world, he is called quiet*. The figure is held twice across the collection.
- **structural-parallel** → Dhammapada, Chapter 1 (The Twin Verses) — the opening pair on the shelf, with the figure of the moon-and-clouds at its closing; Chapter 1 already states the figure that mind precedes action and outcome, Chapter 25 verse 382 closes on the simile that the young Bhikshu who applies himself to the doctrine of Buddha *brightens up this world, like the moon when free from clouds* — the same moon-clouds figure used at Chapter 13 verses 172–173. The figure recurs across the collection as the standing simile for an apparent obstacle removed.
- **structural-parallel** → Plato, Apology — the Greek pair on the figure of the philosopher whose discipline is on himself; Socrates' charge that the unexamined life is not worth living, Dhammapada 25 verse 379 with *rouse thyself by thyself, examine thyself by thyself*. Both texts hold the figure that the work is one's own and cannot be done by another, articulated through different vocabulary.
