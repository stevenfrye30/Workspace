# Dhammapada, Chapter 13 (The World)

Chapter 13 of the Dhammapada — the *Loka-vagga*, "The World" — is a chapter of twelve verses on the world as something seen through. The opening verse states the form as a series of prohibitions: *do not follow the evil law! do not live on in thoughtlessness! do not follow false doctrine! be not a friend of the world* (167). Verses 168 and 169 pair the alternative — rouse thyself, follow the law of virtue, and the virtuous rests in bliss in this world and in the next, with verse 169 repeating the refrain. Verse 170 holds the chapter's central image: *look upon the world as a bubble, look upon it as a mirage: the king of death does not see him who thus looks down upon the world.* Verse 171 turns the figure to a royal chariot in which the foolish are immersed but which the wise do not touch. Verses 172 and 173 pair the moon-freed-from-clouds simile in two directions: the man who was reckless and became sober brightens up the world; he whose evil deeds are covered by good deeds brightens up the world. Verse 174 turns the figure to darkness and escape: the world is dark, few here can see, and a few only go to heaven, like birds escaped from the net. Verse 175 turns it to flight: swans go on the path of the sun, the wise are led out of the world after conquering Mara. Verse 176 returns to the warning form: a man who has transgressed one law, who speaks lies, and who scoffs at another world, there is no evil he will not do. Verse 177 turns the figure to liberality: the uncharitable do not go to the gods, fools do not praise liberality, the wise rejoice in it. The closing verse holds the chapter's valuation: *better than sovereignty over the earth, better than going to heaven, better than lordship over all worlds, is the reward of the first step in holiness* (178).

## Context

The Dhammapada is one of the books of the Khuddaka Nikaya, the fifth and shortest section of the Sutta Pitaka in the Pali Canon. It collects 423 verses arranged in twenty-six chapters by theme — pairs, heedfulness, mind, flowers, the sage, evil, and so on. The verses are attributed to the Buddha and were transmitted orally for several centuries before being committed to writing in Sri Lanka in the first century BCE. The collection took shape in roughly the third century BCE, in connection with the Theravada transmission of the canon.

Chapter 13 is the *Loka-vagga*, "The World." Its twelve verses are numbered 167 through 178 in the standard continuous numbering of the Dhammapada — and in F. Max Müller's 1881 translation, the version held in the Archive library, the count and numbering match the standard exactly, with no merged or missing verse of the kind that occurs at the close or middle of Chapters 4, 6, 8, and 11. The Pali word *loka* — world, realm, plane of existence — is rendered uniformly by Müller as "world" throughout. Each verse is short — two or four lines in the Pali — and each completes one statement of the figure.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the chapter is built on the figure of the world held in two registers at once. The world is an illusion to be looked through (the bubble at 170, the mirage at 170, the glittering royal chariot at 171, the darkness at 174); and it is also the field in which good and evil bear their consequence (the warning at 167, the bliss-this-world-and-next refrain at 168–169, the moon-freed-from-clouds at 172–173, the no-evil-he-will-not-do at 176, the gods who are or are not reached at 177). Reading the chapter with both registers held visible — the world as illusion and the world as moral field — recovers the form the chapter is built on. The chapter does not collapse one into the other.

Second, the closing verse (178) is the chapter's hinge: better than sovereignty, better than heaven, better than lordship over all worlds, is the reward of the first step in holiness. The closing verse measures all the worldly outcomes the chapter has named — heaven, the gods, lordship — against a single thing that is not in the world at all. Reading verse 178 as the chapter's structural close, rather than as one aphorism among others, keeps the form the chapter has.

## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Dhammapada, Chapter 11 (Old Age) — the earlier chapter that already worked the figure of the world burning and the body decaying; Chapter 11 verse 146 holds the world as always burning and asks why one does not seek a light, Chapter 13 verse 170 holds the world as a bubble and a mirage and as something looked down upon. Both chapters work the figure of the world as something to be seen through, but Chapter 11 stresses what is collapsing and Chapter 13 stresses what was never solid to begin with.
- **structural-parallel** → Dhammapada, Chapter 12 (Self) — the immediate predecessor on the shelf, with the *self* and the *world* as the two correlative figures the Dhammapada turns; Chapter 12 with ten verses on the self as one's own lord and one's own purifier, Chapter 13 with twelve verses on the world as bubble and mirage. The two chapters together hold the figure that the work is one's own (Chapter 12) and the world is not what it seems (Chapter 13).
- **structural-parallel** → Daodejing, Chapters 1–10 — the Chinese contemplative pair on the figure of the world as something to be seen through; the Daodejing's opening chapters on the Way that cannot be named and on the figure of yielding to what cannot be grasped, Dhammapada 13 with verses 170 and 171 on the world as bubble, mirage, and royal chariot. The two texts hold related figures of the world's apparent solidity and its actual transience.
- **structural-parallel** → Plato, Apology — the Greek pair on the valuation of the inner reward against worldly success; Socrates' position that the unexamined life is not worth living and that he prefers to suffer than to gain wealth or office through wrongdoing, Dhammapada 13 verse 178 on the first step in holiness as better than sovereignty, heaven, or lordship over all worlds. Both texts close on the same valuation form.
- **structural-parallel** → Ezekiel 1 — the Hebrew shelf-pair on the figure of the world made strange to the seer; Ezekiel's wheels within wheels and creatures with four faces, Dhammapada 13's bubble and mirage. Both texts hold the figure of an unfamiliar visual that displaces the assumption of the world's solidity, articulated through different theological frameworks.
