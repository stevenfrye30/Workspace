# Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 3, Part 3

Book 3, Part 3 of the Brihadaranyaka Upanishad — the Third Brahmana of the Third Adhyaya — continues the Janaka Vaideha contest opened in the First Brahmana and continued in the Second. The court frame is not reset. The Second Brahmana closed with Yajnavalkya and Garatkarava Artabhaga withdrawing in private from the assembly to argue and praise *karman*; the Third Brahmana opens directly into the next challenger — *Then Bhugyu Lahyayani asked* — without returning to the setup, and reverts the contest's closing form from private withdrawal back to silence in the assembly. *After that Bhugyu Lahyayani held his peace.*

The Brahmana is the most compressed of the contest so far. Only two verse-number markers — *1.* on the opening question and *2.* on Yajnavalkya's first response — span the five passages of the body. The form Asvala held across nine substitutional questions in the First Brahmana, and the form Artabhaga held across thirteen tightly-numbered verses in the Second, is here held in a single sustained question and a single sustained answer with one mid-exchange follow-up. The contest's structural unit — challenger, question, answer, closing — is preserved exactly; the volume of substance held inside it is shorter than at any prior point in the Adhyaya.

Verse 1 is Bhugyu's question, and the question is framed inside an embedded prior dialogue. Bhugyu does not ask in his own voice from the assembly. He reports a wandering: *Yagnavalkya, we wandered about as students, and came to the house of Patankala Kapya. He had a daughter who was possessed by a Gandharva. We asked him, 'Who art thou?' and he replied: 'I am Sudhanvan, the Angirasa.'* The students had asked the Gandharva *about the ends of the world*, putting the question *Where were the Parikshitas?* Bhugyu transposes that question — without paraphrase, in the same form — onto Yajnavalkya: *Where then were the Parikshitas, I ask thee, Yagnavalkya, where were the Parikshitas?* The repetition of the question across the line — first as report, then as direct address — is the literary form of the transposition: the question is held twice on the same passage so that it passes from the Gandharva-frame to the public-contest frame without losing its identity.

Verse 2 is Yajnavalkya's first response. He does not name the destination directly; he names it through what was said to Bhugyu by the Gandharva. *Yagnavalkya said: 'He said to thee, I suppose, that they went where those go who have performed a horse-sacrifice.'* The reply is given in the conditional — *I suppose* — which holds the Gandharva's report at one remove and lets Yajnavalkya answer Bhugyu's question without claiming first-person knowledge of the Gandharva's words. Bhugyu's follow-up presses for the substantive geography: *He said: 'And where do they go who have performed a horse-sacrifice?'*

The substantive answer is the Brahmana's only sustained passage. Yajnavalkya gives the cosmic geography of the destination across one continuous prose unit. *Thirty-two journeys of the car of the sun is this world. The earth surrounds it on every side, twice as large, and the ocean surrounds this earth on every side, twice as large. Now there is between them a space as large as the edge of a razor or the wing of a mosquito. Indra, having become a bird, handed them (through the space) to Vayu (the air), and Vayu (the air), holding them within himself, conveyed them to where they dwell who have performed a horse-sacrifice. Somewhat in this way did he praise Vayu indeed. Therefore Vayu (air) is everything by itself, and Vayu is all things together. He who knows this, conquers death.* The closing seal — *He who knows this, conquers death* — is the same form of knowledge-promise that the First Brahmana's closing formula carried (*This constitutes freedom, and perfect freedom (from death)*) and that the contest will hold across its later Brahmanas. The narrator's line then closes the Brahmana: *After that Bhugyu Lahyayani held his peace.*

## Context

The Brihadaranyaka Upanishad is one of the two earliest of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads, sharing that position with the Chandogya. It belongs to the Shukla Yajurveda branch of the Vedic corpus and forms the closing portion of the Shatapatha Brahmana — the *Aranyaka* in its name marks it as a forest-text recited at the late stages of Vedic study. Modern scholarship places the Brihadaranyaka in roughly the eighth to sixth century BCE, prior to the Buddha; the text is anonymous, and the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship. The text is the longest of the principal Upanishads and is typically structured in six *adhyayas* (chapters), each containing several *brahmanas* (sections).

Book 3, Part 3 holds two of the Adhyaya's distinctive features in concentrated form. The first is the Gandharva-frame. The named challenger is not the source of the question; the Gandharva possessing the daughter of Patankala Kapya is. The students who came to Kapya's house had heard the Gandharva name himself — *Sudhanvan, the Angirasa* — and had asked him *about the ends of the world*, with the specific case of the Parikshitas. Müller's footnote at *Parikshitas* glosses them as ancient kings or sages — the line is read as referring either to historical Pārikṣita kings or to a more remote semi-mythological lineage; the substantive question is unaffected. The narrative form embeds a Gandharva-encounter inside a public *brahmodya*, and Bhugyu performs the transposition. The form is not repeated elsewhere in the Adhyaya in this exact way, and the way Müller's prose holds the transposition — by putting the Gandharva's question directly into Bhugyu's own voice and then back again as a direct address — is the reason the question is rendered with the doubled framing on a single passage.

The second is the cosmic-geographical answer. Vayu — the air — is named twice in the Adhyaya so far. The First Brahmana identified Vayu with the Udgatri priest and with the breath in the body, as one of the four priest/element/faculty identifications of Asvala's first cycle. The Third Brahmana identifies Vayu with the cosmic conveyor of the souls of those who have performed the horse-sacrifice, and closes with the wider formula *Vayu is everything by itself, and Vayu is all things together.* The two registers — bodily and cosmological — are placed across the Adhyaya in the same form: the same name, with the cosmic and the bodily identifications held alongside each other and not collapsed. The horse-sacrifice (*aśvamedha*) is the rite that frames the whole contest — the First Brahmana opened with *Ganaka Vaideha sacrificed with a sacrifice at which many presents were offered to the priests of the Asvamedha* — and the Third Brahmana now puts the destination of those who have performed that rite as the substantive question of the Brahmana itself; the contest is held inside the rite that is also held inside the contest.

The closing form returns to silence. The contest's three closings so far are: *After that Asvala held his peace* (Part 1), *Take my hand, my friend... Then these two went out and argued, and what they said was karman* (Part 2), *After that Bhugyu Lahyayani held his peace* (Part 3). The Adhyaya does not settle into a single closing form. The First and Third Brahmanas close on the same line; the Second steps outside the assembly. Reading the Adhyaya with the closings held alongside each other — not smoothed into a uniform pattern — recovers the contest's form: the same *brahmodya* procedure with deliberately different ways of marking the end of each challenger's questioning.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the Brahmana is a compressed instance of the contest's form. Asvala held two cycles of four substitutional questions; Artabhaga held thirteen tightly-numbered verses across two cycles; Bhugyu holds one numbered question and one short follow-up. The contest's structural unit is preserved across the compression — challenger, question, answer, closing — and the compression is itself the Brahmana's form. Reading Part 3 with Parts 1 and 2 held alongside it, attending to the way the contest's procedure persists across the change of length, recovers the Adhyaya's literary form: the *brahmodya* held at three different scales without being abandoned at any of them.

Second, the Brahmana's question is given in an embedded frame and the answer is given in cosmic geography. Bhugyu does not ask the question in his own voice; he reports a Gandharva's words and transposes them onto Yajnavalkya. Yajnavalkya does not answer with priestly identifications, as Asvala had received, or with the catalogue of the *Grahas* and the dispersal of the body, as Artabhaga had received; he answers with the world-geography of the route — the thirty-two solar journeys, the earth-and-ocean, the razor-edge or mosquito-wing space, the Indra-as-bird transit, and Vayu the conveyor — and closes on the formula *Vayu is everything by itself, and Vayu is all things together. He who knows this, conquers death.* Reading the Brahmana with attention to the embedded-frame question and the cosmographic answer — the way the *brahmodya* form makes room for a Gandharva report on one side and a world-geography on the other — keeps the form the Adhyaya has when it varies the substance of the questioning without changing the procedure.

## The text

The text is held in the Archive library under `upanishads-muller-part2`, chapter 5 (Müller translation, 1884 — *The Sacred Books of the East*, Volume XV; via SacredTexts.com). Library chapter 5 is shared across fifty-two Archive entities — four parts of the Katha Upanishad, the six parts of the Mundaka Upanishad, twenty-eight anuvakas of the Taittiriyaka, the eleven Brahmanas of the Brihadaranyaka's First and Second Adhyayas, and the First and Second Brahmanas of the Third Adhyaya — and continues here into the Third Brahmana. Book 3, Part 3 of the Brihadaranyaka — the entity here — occupies passages `5.699` through `5.703`. Passage `5.699` is the "Third Brahmana" header carrying its own footnote reference `[*2]`. Passages `5.700` through `5.703` carry the body in Müller's prose presentation across four short passages, with verse-numbered markers at `1.` (`5.700`) and `2.` (`5.701`).

The Brahmana contains several boundary features that should be preserved honestly:

- **Verse-1 single-passage opening with footnote cluster.** Verse 1 — Bhugyu's full question, including the embedded Gandharva-narrative and the doubled framing of the Parikshitas-question — is held entirely on passage `5.700` without internal splits, with footnote `[*3]` on *students* and footnote `[*4]` on the first occurrence of *Parikshitas*. The single-passage span is unusual: Asvala's verse 1 and Artabhaga's verse 1 each ran across multiple passages, and the compression of Bhugyu's verse 1 onto a single passage — despite its length — is the most distinctive boundary feature of the Brahmana.
- **Verse-2 split into three passages without continuation markers.** Verse 2 — Yajnavalkya's first response, Bhugyu's follow-up question, and Yajnavalkya's substantive cosmic-geography answer — is split across passages `5.701` (Yajnavalkya's first reply, carrying the verse-marker *2.*), `5.702` (Bhugyu's follow-up question, with no verse-marker), and `5.703` (Yajnavalkya's substantive answer and the closing narrative line, with no verse-marker). The verse-2 marker carries across all three passages without being repeated; the absence of further verse-markers in the Brahmana means the substantive answer and the closing line are not separately numbered.
- **Footnote on the cosmic geography.** Passage `5.703` carries footnote `[*1]` on the line *Now there is between them a space as large as the edge of a razor or the wing of a mosquito*. Müller's note attaches at *between*; the footnote marker is at the open of the razor-edge clause, not at the close.
- **Closing narrative line at `5.703`.** *After that Bhugyu Lahyayani held his peace* is held on the same passage as Yajnavalkya's substantive answer rather than being given on its own short passage. The First Brahmana's closing line *After that Asvala held his peace* sat on its own short passage at `5.676`; the Third Brahmana's closing line is folded into the answer-passage. The literary form is the same — narrator marks the end of the challenger's questioning — and the boundary is given inside the larger passage rather than on its own.

Passage `5.704` is the "Fourth Brahmana" header that opens Book 3, Part 4 of the Brihadaranyaka and is not part of this entity. The library chapter continues past this entity into the rest of the Third Adhyaya and the remaining portions of the volume; the Brihadaranyaka extends well beyond the boundaries of library chapter 5. About four minutes' reading.

Then these two went out and argued, and what they said was karman* — the Third Brahmana opens directly into the next challenger without returning to the setup and works through the contest's procedure on a single sustained question with one short follow-up. The contest's form is preserved exactly across the Brahmana boundary; the closing forms differ — private withdrawal in Part 2, silence in Part 3 — and the variation is itself a feature of the Adhyaya's narrative method.
- **structural-parallel** → Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 3, Part 1 — paired Brahmanas of the Janaka Vaideha contest closing on the same form; the First Brahmana closed on *After that Asvala held his peace* and the Third Brahmana closes on *After that Bhugyu Lahyayani held his peace* — the same narrator's line with the named challenger substituted. The Brahmanas hold the contest at very different scales — Asvala received nine substitutional questions across two cycles, Bhugyu held one numbered question and one follow-up — and the matching closing form across the scale-difference is one of the Adhyaya's literary signatures: the *brahmodya* procedure made portable across the change of length.
- **structural-parallel** → Katha Upanishad, Book 1, Part 1 — paired Sanskrit dialogues placing the question of where the dead have gone at the center of the exchange; the First Valli of the Katha holds Nachiketas at the house of Yama with the third boon held back — *There is that doubt, when a man is dead — some saying, he is; others, he is not. This I should like to know, taught by thee* — the Third Brahmana of the Brihadaranyaka's Third Adhyaya holds Bhugyu Lahyayani at Janaka's *aśvamedha* asking *Where were the Parikshitas?* and receiving the cosmic-geographical answer of the route by Indra-as-bird and Vayu-as-conveyor. Both texts hold the question of post-death location inside a named-dialogue frame; the Katha makes the question the door to a long teaching, the Brihadaranyaka makes the answer the close of a short Brahmana.
