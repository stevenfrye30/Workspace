# Meditations, Book 2

Book 2 of the Meditations is one of the twelve short books of Marcus Aurelius's private philosophical journal, written in the third quarter of the second century while he was on campaign at the empire's northern frontier. Marcus, then in his late forties and a decade into a reign that would absorb the rest of his life, wrote in Greek and addressed himself. Book 2 opens with the famous passage on rising in the morning to meet difficult people — busybody, ungrateful, arrogant, deceitful, envious — and continues with short reflections on duty, the brevity of life, and the discipline of a person attending to what is in front of him. The Meditations was never circulated and was almost certainly never intended for any reader other than its author.

## Context

The Meditations (Greek title *Ta eis heauton*, "To Himself") is a collection of twelve short books of personal philosophical reflections by Marcus Aurelius (121–180 CE), Roman emperor from 161 until his death. He wrote them in Greek — the language of philosophy in his time, even for an emperor whose first language was Latin — during the Marcomannic Wars on the Danube frontier, where he commanded Roman forces against incursions from the north. The composition probably falls in the last decade of his life, the 170s.

The Meditations is not a treatise. The books are working journals, not laid out for publication, with passages that begin and end abruptly, return to the same themes, and address Marcus in the second person ("Thou must not..."). Marcus's Stoicism is the tradition of Epictetus — whom he names — together with the older Stoic line of Chrysippus and Cleanthes. Book 1 is a separate piece in form: an account of debts to family, teachers, and friends. The remaining books, including Book 2, are reflective and aphoristic. Book 2 is among the shortest, and is traditionally placed at Carnuntum on the Danube.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the Meditations is self-address. Marcus is not writing to a class, a successor, or a posterity; he is writing to himself, in the second person, as practice. Reading Book 2 with this in mind — as one half of a conversation Marcus is having with himself — is closer to the form of the text than reading it as essay or maxim collection.

Second, Book 2 is short and recursive. Its seventeen sections circle a few concerns: the company of difficult people, the shortness of life, the proper attention to the present action, the futility of acting for fame. The book does not develop a thesis. It returns. Reading the sections as repetitions of one practice rather than as separate teachings tracks the way Marcus is using the writing.
