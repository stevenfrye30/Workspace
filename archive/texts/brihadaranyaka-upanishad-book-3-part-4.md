# Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 3, Part 4

Book 3, Part 4 of the Brihadaranyaka Upanishad — the Fourth Brahmana of the Third Adhyaya — continues the Janaka Vaideha contest opened in the First Brahmana and continued through the Second and Third. The court frame is not reset. The Third Brahmana closed on silence — *After that Bhugyu Lahyayani held his peace* — and the Fourth opens directly into the next challenger: *Then Ushasta Kakrayana asked.* The closing form returns to silence again, with the same narrator's line and the new name in place: *After that Ushasta Kakrayana held his peace.*

The Brahmana works as a doubled Q-and-A cycle. The full frame — Ushasta's opening question *tell me the Brahman which is visible, not invisible, the Self (atman), who is within all*, Yajnavalkya's formulaic reply *This, thy Self, who is within all*, Ushasta's follow-up *Which Self, O Yagnavalkya, is within all?*, and Yajnavalkya's substantive answer — is held twice in succession. The opening question is restated by Ushasta verbatim across the two cycles; the formulaic reply is given verbatim by Yajnavalkya at the open of each; the follow-up is given verbatim each time. Only the substantive answer differs across the two cycles, and the substantive difference is the substance of the Brahmana.

Verse 1's substantive answer is the four-breaths identification. *He who breathes in the up-breathing, he is thy Self, and within all. He who breathes in the down-breathing, he is thy Self, and within all. He who breathes in the on-breathing, he is thy Self, and within all. He who breathes in the out-breathing, he is thy Self, and within all. This is thy Self, who is within all.* The four breaths — Prana, Apana, Vyana, Udana, named in the First Brahmana's verse 10 in connection with the Stotriya hymns — are not given here under their proper names but in Müller's participial rendering *He who breathes in the X-breathing*. The four-clause repetition runs across passages `5.708` and `5.709`, with the sentence split mid-clause: passage `5.708` ends at *He who breathes in* and passage `5.709` resumes *the out-breathing, he is thy Self, and within all*. The split falls between the third and the fourth clause of the four-clause formula, and the formulaic seal *This is thy Self, who is within all* sits at the close of `5.709`.

Verse 2 is Ushasta's rebuke. *As one might say, this is a cow, this is a horse, thus has this been explained by thee.* The cow-and-horse pointing — naming a visibly present object and explaining it ostensively — is named by Ushasta as the failure-mode of the first answer: the four breaths have been pointed at like a cow or a horse, and the question of the Self who is *within all* has not been answered. Ushasta repeats the substantive question verbatim — *Tell me the Brahman which is visible, not invisible, the Self, who is within all* — and the formal Q-and-A frame begins again: the same formulaic reply, the same follow-up.

The second substantive answer is the negative formula that the Brahmana is most-cited for. *Thou couldst not see the (true) seer of sight, thou couldst not hear the (true) hearer of hearing, nor perceive the perceiver of perception, nor know the knower of knowledge. This is thy Self, who is within all.* The Self is not the four breaths; the Self is the witness of the senses that cannot itself be made into the object of those senses. The seal *This is thy Self, who is within all* repeats verbatim from the first answer; the strange next line — *Everything also is of evil* — sits immediately after, on the same passage, without explanatory transition. The narrator's line closes the Brahmana on the same passage as the substantive answer: *After that Ushasta Kakrayana held his peace.*

## Context

The Brihadaranyaka Upanishad is one of the two earliest of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads, sharing that position with the Chandogya. It belongs to the Shukla Yajurveda branch of the Vedic corpus and forms the closing portion of the Shatapatha Brahmana — the *Aranyaka* in its name marks it as a forest-text recited at the late stages of Vedic study. Modern scholarship places the Brihadaranyaka in roughly the eighth to sixth century BCE, prior to the Buddha; the text is anonymous, and the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship. The text is the longest of the principal Upanishads and is typically structured in six *adhyayas* (chapters), each containing several *brahmanas* (sections).

Book 3, Part 4 holds two of the most distinctive features of the contest in concentrated form. The first is the doubled Q-and-A cycle. The Adhyaya's prior Brahmanas worked through their substance through accumulation — Asvala's two cycles of four substitutional questions, Artabhaga's catalogue of eight Grahas and the dispersal at death, Bhugyu's single sustained Gandharva-frame question. The Fourth Brahmana works through repetition: the entire Q-and-A frame is held twice with verbatim restatement of every component except the substantive answer. The challenger does not move on after the first answer; the challenger refuses the first answer and demands the same form again with different substance. The contest's procedure is preserved across the doubling — the challenger asks, the priest answers, the challenger presses for further specification — and the doubling is itself the Brahmana's literary form. Reading the second cycle alongside the first, with the verbatim repetition kept exactly where it stands, is the way the Brahmana works.

The second is the seer-of-sight formula. *Thou couldst not see the (true) seer of sight, thou couldst not hear the (true) hearer of hearing, nor perceive the perceiver of perception, nor know the knower of knowledge.* The four-clause negation is one of the Upanishadic literature's most-quoted statements of the witnessing Self that cannot itself be witnessed, and the Brahmana places it at the close of the second substantive answer of a doubled cycle in which the first answer had given the four breaths and been refused. The teaching is given as a correction to a positive identification — the four breaths — that has been named insufficient by the challenger. The Brahmana's closer *Everything also is of evil* sits on the same passage as the seer-of-sight formula and the closing narrative line; the line is not glossed by Müller and is given in the same prose unit. The line is part of the sacred text as Müller renders it; whatever its precise sense, it stands inside the Brahmana's closing and not outside it.

The closing form returns again to silence. The contest's four closings so far — *After that Asvala held his peace*, *Take my hand, my friend... what they said was karman*, *After that Bhugyu Lahyayani held his peace*, *After that Ushasta Kakrayana held his peace* — sit in the pattern silence / private withdrawal / silence / silence. The Adhyaya's silence-closing predominates, with the Second Brahmana's private withdrawal as the single deviation. Reading the Adhyaya with the closings held alongside each other recovers the contest's form: the same *brahmodya* procedure with one deliberate deviation in the way an ending is marked.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the Brahmana's repetition is not redundancy. The opening question is given twice, the formulaic reply is given twice, the follow-up is given twice — and each repetition is verbatim. The Brahmana refuses to abbreviate the second cycle into *and again, and again*; it gives the second cycle in full. Reading Part 4 with the verbatim repetition held exactly as the source has it — neither smoothed away nor compressed into summary — recovers the Adhyaya's procedure when a challenger refuses an answer and demands further specification. The form the Brahmana has when an answer is rejected is the form of full restatement.

Second, the substantive movement is from positive identification to negative formula. The first substantive answer identifies the Self with the four breaths — a determinate, ostensible, body-located answer of the kind Asvala and Artabhaga had received. The challenger names that kind of answer as cow-and-horse pointing. The second substantive answer turns to the negative form: the Self is the seer that cannot be seen, the hearer that cannot be heard, the perceiver that cannot be perceived, the knower that cannot be known. Reading the Brahmana with attention to the order of the two substantive answers — first the positive identification, then the negative formula, with the rebuke between them — recovers the literary procedure of the Brahmana: the negative formula is not given as the Brahmana's first word; it is given as the answer that follows the rejection of the positive answer. The Brahmana works the doctrine through the contest's procedure rather than around it.

Passages `5.705` through `5.713` carry the body in Müller's prose presentation across nine short passages, with verse-numbered markers at `1.` (`5.705`) and `2.` (`5.710`).

The Brahmana contains several boundary features that should be preserved honestly:

- **Verse-1 footnote.** Passage `5.705` carries verse 1 — Ushasta's opening question — with footnote `[*3]` on the phrase *visible, not invisible*. The footnote attaches inside the question; Müller's note renders the underlying Sanskrit term as *sākṣād aparokṣād* with the gloss *immediate, not mediated*.
- **Verse-1 doubled formulaic exchange.** Passages `5.706` and `5.707` carry Yajnavalkya's formulaic reply *This, thy Self, who is within all* and Ushasta's follow-up *Which Self, O Yagnavalkya, is within all?* — each on its own short passage, with no verse-marker. The same two passages will be given verbatim a second time at `5.711` and `5.712`.
- **Verse-1 substantive answer split mid-clause.** The four-breaths identification — *He who breathes in the up-breathing... down-breathing... on-breathing... out-breathing* — is split across passages `5.708` and `5.709`. Passage `5.708` ends mid-clause at *He who breathes in*; passage `5.709` resumes *the out-breathing, he is thy Self, and within all*. The split falls between the third and the fourth of the four-clause formula, and the formulaic seal *This is thy Self, who is within all* sits at the close of `5.709`.
- **Verse-2 verbatim restatement.** Passage `5.710` carries verse 2 — Ushasta's rebuke and the verbatim restatement of the opening question. The cow/horse line *As one might say, this is a cow, this is a horse, thus has this been explained by thee* sits inside the verse-2 passage. The substantive question is given in the same wording as verse 1 with one word omitted — *atman* is named in verse 1's parenthesis (*the Self (atman), who is within all*) and rendered as *the Self, who is within all* in verse 2 — and the rest of the question is verbatim.
- **Doubled formulaic exchange at `5.711` and `5.712`.** The two short passages give Yajnavalkya's formulaic reply and Ushasta's follow-up verbatim from `5.706` and `5.707`. The doubling is preserved exactly across passage boundaries; the second cycle is not compressed.
- **Closing answer-and-narrative-line at `5.713`.** Passage `5.713` carries Yajnavalkya's second substantive answer — the four-clause negation *Thou couldst not see the (true) seer of sight, thou couldst not hear the (true) hearer of hearing, nor perceive the perceiver of perception, nor know the knower of knowledge* — with the formulaic seal *This is thy Self, who is within all* and the unexplained closer *Everything also is of evil* on the same passage. The narrator's closing line *After that Ushasta Kakrayana held his peace* is folded into the same passage, as it was folded into the closing passage of the Third Brahmana at `5.703`. The First Brahmana's closing line had sat on its own short passage at `5.676`; the Third and Fourth Brahmanas' closing lines are held together with their final answer-passages.

Passage `5.714` is the "Fifth Brahmana" header that opens Book 3, Part 5 of the Brihadaranyaka and is not part of this entity. ## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 3, Part 3 — paired immediate-predecessor Brahmanas of the Janaka Vaideha contest closing on the same form; the Third Brahmana closed on *After that Bhugyu Lahyayani held his peace* and the Fourth Brahmana closes on *After that Ushasta Kakrayana held his peace* — the same narrator's line with the named challenger substituted, with the closing line folded into the final answer-passage in both Brahmanas. The Brahmanas hold the contest at very different scales — Bhugyu held one sustained question and one short follow-up, Ushasta holds a doubled Q-and-A cycle with verbatim restatement — and the same closing form across the scale-difference is one of the Adhyaya's literary signatures.
- **structural-parallel** → Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 2, Part 3 — paired Brihadaranyaka Brahmanas holding the negative-theology formula of the Self that cannot be made an object of the senses; the Third Brahmana of the Second Adhyaya closes on the *neti neti* doctrine — *the teaching of Brahman by "No, no!" — for there is nothing else higher than this* — the Fourth Brahmana of the Third Adhyaya closes on the four-clause seer-of-sight formula — *Thou couldst not see the (true) seer of sight, thou couldst not hear the (true) hearer of hearing, nor perceive the perceiver of perception, nor know the knower of knowledge.* Both Brahmanas hold the negative form of the doctrine of Brahman in the same Upanishad, with one giving the formula in the abstract Sanskrit phrase *neti neti* and the other giving it across the four senses of sight, hearing, perception, and knowledge.
- **structural-parallel** → Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 2, Part 4 — paired Yajnavalkya teachings on the Knower that cannot itself be known; the Fourth Brahmana of the Second Adhyaya holds the domestic Yajnavalkya-Maitreyi conversation closing on the question *How, O beloved, should he know himself, the Knower?* — the Fourth Brahmana of the Third Adhyaya holds the public Yajnavalkya-Ushasta exchange answering with the formula *Thou couldst not... know the knower of knowledge.* Yajnavalkya appears across both — domestic teacher of his wife in the Second Adhyaya, public answerer of his fourth challenger in the Third — with the same teaching of the un-knowable Knower held in opposite social settings.
