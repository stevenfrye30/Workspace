# Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 6, Part 2

Book 6, Part 2 of the Brihadaranyaka Upanishad — the Second Brahmana of the Sixth Adhyaya — is the substantive *Pancagni-vidya* (Five Fires Knowledge) and the substantive Two Paths doctrine. The Brahmana is one of the most-cited substantive units of the Upanishadic literature, and it appears in nearly verbatim form at Chandogya Upanishad 5.3–5.10. The substantive narrative and the substantive doctrine together form the substantive cosmological-eschatological foundation of the substantive *devayāna* and *pitryāna* (paths of the gods and the fathers) tradition that the Vedanta literature works at length.

The Brahmana opens with the substantive narrative frame at `5.1263`. *Svetaketu Aruneya went to the settlement of the Pankalas. He came near to Pravahana Gaivali, who was walking about (surrounded by his men). As soon as he (the king) saw him, he said: 'My boy!' Svetaketu replied: 'Sir!' Then the king said: 'Have you been taught by your father?' 'Yes,' he replied.* The substantive named figure Svetaketu Aruneya is the same Svetaketu of the substantive Chandogya Upanishad's Sixth Prapathaka — the substantive recipient of his father's *Tat Tvam Asi* (*That art thou*) instruction. The substantive king Pravahana Gaivali is a substantive Raganya (Kshatriya) of the substantive Pankala kingdom, and Müller's footnote `[*4]` glosses the substantive name. The substantive interaction-form is the king addressing the Brahmana boy as *My boy!* — a substantive social form that Svetaketu accepts with *Sir!*

Verse 2 at `5.1265`–`5.1270` holds the substantive five questions the king asks Svetaketu. The substantive form is verbatim: *Do you know X?* / *No*, he replied. The five questions, in the order they are asked:
1. *Do you know how men, when they depart from here, separate from each other?*
2. *Do you know how they come back to this world?*
3. *Do you know how that world does never become full with the many who again and again depart thither?*
4. *Do you know at the offering of which libation the waters become endowed with a human voice and rise and speak?*
5. *Do you know the access to the path leading to the Devas and to the path leading to the Fathers, i.e. by what deeds men gain access to the path leading to the Devas or to that leading to the Fathers? For we have heard even the saying of a Rishi: "I heard of two paths for men, one leading to the Fathers, the other leading to the Devas. On those paths all that lives moves on, whatever there is between father (sky) and mother (earth)."*

Verse 2 closes at `5.1270`: *Svetaketu said: 'I do not know even one of all these questions.'* The substantive form is the substantive five-question test, and Svetaketu fails it.

Verses 3–7 hold the substantive narrative continuation. Verse 3 at `5.1271`: Svetaketu runs back to his father Gautama (Aruni) and reports that *That fellow of a Raganya asked me five questions, and I did not know one of them*. Verse 4 at `5.1274`: Gautama replies *You know me, child, that whatever I know, I told you. But come, we shall go thither, and dwell there as students.* Verse 5 at `5.1277`: Gautama arrives at the king's court, the king offers him a boon, Gautama asks for the substantive teaching the king gave Svetaketu. Verse 6 at `5.1278`: the king demurs — *That belongs to divine boons, name one of the human boons.* Verse 7 at `5.1279`: Gautama refuses any worldly boon — *You know well that I have plenty of gold, plenty of cows, horses, slaves, attendants, and apparel; do not heap on me what I have already in plenty, in abundance, and superabundance.*

Passage `5.1282` carries an editorial-narrative comment that names openly the substantive social-inversion of the substantive teaching: *In word only have former sages (though Brahmans) come as pupils (to people of lower rank), but Gautama actually dwelt as a pupil (of Pravahana, who was a Raganya) in order to obtain the fame of having respectfully served his master.* The substantive comment is preserved as part of the digitized source text with footnote `[*2]`, and it explicitly names the substantive Kshatriya-teaches-Brahmana inversion as the substantive procedural form of the Brahmana.

Verse 8 at `5.1283` opens the substantive doctrine: *Do not be offended with us, neither you nor your forefathers, because this knowledge has before now never dwelt with any Brahmana. But I shall tell it to you, for who could refuse you when you speak thus?* Müller's footnote `[*1]` glosses the substantive Kshatriya-only-knowledge claim. The substantive doctrine that follows is given as a knowledge that has *never dwelt with any Brahmana* — a substantive claim that the substantive *Pancagni-vidya* is substantively a Kshatriya tradition transmitted to a Brahmana for the first time in this teaching.

The substantive Five Fires doctrine runs verses 9–13 at `5.1284`–`5.1288`. Each verse holds the same verbatim shape: *The altar, O Gautama, is X; the fuel is Y, the smoke Z, the light W, the coals V, the sparks U. On that altar the Devas offer T. From that oblation rises S.* The substantive form is the substantive ritual fire-altar mapped successively onto five substantive cosmic-and-bodily seats, with the substantive Devas offering substantive successive substances at each level:

- **Verse 9 — First Altar = the world (heaven)** at `5.1284`: fuel = sun itself, smoke = his rays, light = the day, coals = the quarters, sparks = the intermediate quarters. Devas offer the *sraddha* libation (water). From that oblation rises **Soma, the king (the moon)**. Müller's footnotes `[*2]` on *that world (heaven)* and `[*3]` on *water*.
- **Verse 10 — Second Altar = Parganya (the god of rain)** at `5.1285`: fuel = the year itself, smoke = the clouds, light = the lightning, coals = the thunderbolt, sparks = the thunderings. Devas offer Soma. From that oblation rises **rain**.
- **Verse 11 — Third Altar = this world** at `5.1286`: fuel = the earth itself, smoke = the fire, light = the night, coals = the moon, sparks = the stars. Devas offer rain. From that oblation rises **food**. Müller's footnote `[*4]` on *this world*.
- **Verse 12 — Fourth Altar = man** at `5.1287`: fuel = the opened mouth, smoke = the breath, light = the tongue, coals = the eye, sparks = the ear. Devas offer food. From that oblation rises **seed**.
- **Verse 13 — Fifth Altar = woman** at `5.1288`: Devas offer seed. From that oblation rises **man**. Müller's footnote `[*1]` on *woman*. Verse 13 is given in compressed form — the substantive fuel-smoke-light-coals-sparks substitutional series is not given for the woman-altar; only the substantive offering and the substantive product are given.

The five substantive altars trace the substantive cosmic-to-bodily descent through which **Soma → rain → food → seed → man** is generated. Each altar is the substantive *yajna* (sacrifice) at which the substantive Devas perform the substantive offering, and the substantive product of each oblation becomes the substantive offering at the next altar. The substantive form is the cosmic-ritual chain that produces the human person through five substantive successive offerings.

Verse 14 at `5.1289` adds the substantive sixth altar at the moment of death. *They take him to the fire (the funeral pile), and then the altar-fire is indeed fire, the fuel fuel, the smoke smoke, the light light, the coals coals, the sparks sparks. In that very altar-fire the Devas offer man, and from that oblation man rises, brilliant in colour.* The substantive form is the substantive funeral-pile as the substantive sixth altar, with the substantive substitutions held verbatim as identities (*the altar-fire is indeed fire, the fuel fuel, the smoke smoke...*) — the substantive ordinary fire functioning as the substantive ritual altar at death. The substantive product of the death-oblation is the substantive *man brilliant in colour* who rises from the funeral pile.

Verses 15 and 16 give the substantive Two Paths.

**Verse 15 at `5.1290` — The Path of the Gods (*devayāna*)**: *Those who thus know this (even Grihasthas), and those who in the forest worship faith and the True (Brahman Hiranyagarbha), go to light (arkis), from light to day, from day to the increasing half, from the increasing half to the six months when the sun goes to the north, from those six months to the world of the Devas (Devaloka), from the world of the Devas to the sun, from the sun to the place of lightning. When they have thus reached the place of lightning a spirit comes near them, and leads them to the worlds of the (conditioned) Brahman. In these worlds of Brahman they dwell exalted for ages. There is no returning for them.* The substantive *devayāna* is the substantive eight-stage upward ascent (light → day → increasing half → six northern months → Devaloka → sun → place of lightning → worlds of Brahman) that closes with the substantive *no returning* form. Müller's footnotes `[*2]` on *the True (Brahman Hiranyagarbha)* and `[*3]` on *a spirit*.

**Verse 16 at `5.1291` — The Path of the Fathers (*pitryāna*)**: *But they who conquer the worlds (future states) by means of sacrifice, charity, and austerity, go to smoke, from smoke to night, from night to the decreasing half of the moon, from the decreasing half of the moon to the six months when the sun goes to the south, from these months to the world of the fathers, from the world of the fathers to the moon. Having reached the moon, they become food, and then the Devas feed on them there, as sacrificers feed on Soma, as it increases and decreases. But when this (the result of their good works on earth) ceases, they return again to that ether, from ether to the air, from the air to rain, from rain to the earth. And when they have reached the earth, they become food, they are offered again in the altar-fire, which is man (see section 11), and thence are born in the fire of woman. Thus they rise up towards the worlds, and go the same round as before.* The substantive *pitryāna* is the substantive seven-stage upward ascent (smoke → night → decreasing half → six southern months → world of fathers → moon, where they become substantive food for the Devas) followed by the substantive five-stage return (ether → air → rain → earth → food → altar-fire of man → fire of woman → birth) closing with the substantive *go the same round as before* form. Müller's footnote `[*1]` on *as it increases and decreases*. The substantive cross-reference *(see section 11)* — Müller's editorial gloss — preserves the substantive parallel with verse 11's substantive altar-of-man identification.

The Brahmana closes at `5.1292` on the substantive third path: *'Those, however, who know neither of these two paths, become worms, birds, and creeping things.'* The substantive third path is the substantive *samsāra* of the substantive ignorant — the substantive unguided continuous rebirth into the substantive lower forms. The closing line is given on its own short passage with no verse-marker; it functions as the substantive closing-clause of verse 16 and of the Brahmana itself. The OCR of the underlying source preserves a doubled comma after *birds* — *worms, birds,, and creeping things* — a transcription artifact preserved as found.

## Context

The Brihadaranyaka Upanishad is one of the two earliest of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads, sharing that position with the Chandogya. It belongs to the Shukla Yajurveda branch of the Vedic corpus and forms the closing portion of the Shatapatha Brahmana — the *Aranyaka* in its name marks it as a forest-text recited at the late stages of Vedic study. Modern scholarship places the Brihadaranyaka in roughly the eighth to sixth century BCE, prior to the Buddha; the text is anonymous, and the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship. The text is the longest of the principal Upanishads and is typically structured in six *adhyayas* (chapters), each containing several *brahmanas* (sections).

Book 6, Part 2 holds three of the most distinctive features of the Upanishadic literature in concentrated form. The first is the substantive *Pancagni-vidya* (Five Fires Knowledge). The substantive doctrine of the five substantive cosmic-ritual fire-altars and their substantive sequential offerings — heaven (rises Soma), Parganya (rises rain), this world (rises food), man (rises seed), woman (rises man) — is one of the substantive cosmological foundations of the later Vedantic karma-and-rebirth tradition, and the substantive form holds the substantive fire-altar as the substantive cosmic procedural unit through which the substantive descent from cosmic Soma to human person is given. The substantive doctrine has its principal extended elaboration at Chandogya Upanishad 5.3–5.10, in nearly verbatim form, and the cross-textual relation between the two Upanishads is one of the strongest in the substantive Vedic-Upanishadic literature. The substantive Brihadaranyaka and Chandogya parallel here gives the substantive *Pancagni-vidya* the substantive status of a substantive shared doctrinal resource of the substantive early Upanishadic tradition.

The second is the substantive Two Paths doctrine — *devayāna* (the path of the gods) and *pitryāna* (the path of the fathers). The substantive *devayāna* runs through the substantive cosmic stages of light, day, the increasing half-month, the six months of the northward sun, Devaloka, the sun, and the place of lightning, with the substantive close on the worlds of (conditioned) Brahman and the substantive *no returning* doctrine. The substantive *pitryāna* runs through the substantive complementary stages of smoke, night, the decreasing half-month, the six months of the southward sun, the world of the fathers, and the moon, where the substantive souls become substantive food for the Devas before substantive descent through ether, air, rain, earth, food, the altar-fire of man, and the fire of woman to substantive rebirth. The substantive Two Paths doctrine is one of the substantive central doctrines of the substantive Vedanta tradition, with the Bhagavad Gita 8.23–27 working it through the substantive *uttarāyaṇa-dakṣiṇāyana* form (the substantive northern and southern paths of the sun) and the Brahma-Sutras working it across multiple substantive passages.

The third is the substantive Kshatriya-teaches-Brahmana inversion. The substantive narrative frame holds Svetaketu Aruneya (a substantive Brahmana boy) and his father Gautama (Aruni, a substantive Brahmana sage) as the substantive recipients of teaching from Pravahana Gaivali (a substantive Raganya / Kshatriya king). The substantive social-inversion is named openly at the editorial-narrative comment at `5.1282`: *In word only have former sages (though Brahmans) come as pupils (to people of lower rank), but Gautama actually dwelt as a pupil*. The substantive Brahmana literature holds the substantive Kshatriya-as-teacher form rarely, and the present substantive instance is one of the most explicit — substantively reinforced by the king's substantive claim at verse 8 that *this knowledge has before now never dwelt with any Brahmana*. The substantive doctrine of the *Pancagni-vidya* and the Two Paths is named substantively as Kshatriya-tradition transmitted to a Brahmana for the first time in this teaching, and the substantive narrative form preserves the substantive social-procedural inversion as part of the substantive teaching.

The closing form is the Adhyaya's substantive third-path-of-the-ignorant. The substantive close — *Those, however, who know neither of these two paths, become worms, birds, and creeping things* — names the substantive *samsāra* of the substantive ignorant in compressed form. The substantive doctrine is that the substantive *vidyā* of the *Pancagni-vidya* is the substantive condition for the substantive *devayāna*; the substantive ritual works of sacrifice, charity, and austerity are the substantive condition for the substantive *pitryāna*; and the substantive absence of both substantively places the substantive person in the substantive lower-form rebirth-cycle.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the substantive Five Fires doctrine should be read at full length with the substantive verbatim parallel form preserved across all five iterations. The substantive form is the substantive ritual-altar substitution-list (fuel, smoke, light, coals, sparks) repeated verbatim across each substantive altar, with only the substantive substitutions changing. Verses 9, 10, 11, and 12 give the substantive substitution-list at full length; verse 13 (woman as the fifth altar) gives only the substantive offering and the substantive product without the substantive five-substitution list. Reading the substantive five iterations alongside each other — neither summarized as *the Brahmana gives five fires* nor flattened into a generic statement of cosmic-bodily fire-symbolism — recovers the substantive ritual-altar form as the substantive procedural shape of the *Pancagni-vidya*.

Second, the substantive narrative frame should be read as substantive content rather than as a generic introduction to the doctrine. The substantive narrative of Svetaketu's failure, Gautama's substantive pupilship, the king's substantive Kshatriya-as-teacher claim, and the substantive editorial comment at `5.1282` are part of the substantive teaching itself. The substantive doctrine of the *Pancagni-vidya* and the Two Paths is given in the substantive form of a teaching transmitted across substantive social boundaries (Kshatriya to Brahmana), and the substantive form is named openly in the text. Reading the substantive narrative frame with the substantive social-inversion preserved — neither smoothed away into a generic teacher-pupil exchange nor expanded into a separate doctrinal statement — recovers the substantive form of the Brahmana: a substantive teaching whose substantive transmission is itself substantive content.

Müller's *Upanishads, Part I* (Volume I of the SBE, 1879) is also held under `upanishads-muller-part1`.
- **Critical edition.** Patrick Olivelle, ed. and trans., *The Early Upanishads: Annotated Text and Translation* (Oxford University Press, 1998) — Sanskrit text with modern English translation and critical apparatus; the standard scholarly bilingual edition.
- **Recommended translation upgrade.** Patrick Olivelle, *Upaniṣads*, Oxford World's Classics (Oxford University Press, 1996) — clear modern English with introduction and notes; the standard contemporary teaching translation.
- **Comparative reference.** Chandogya Upanishad 5.3–5.10 — the parallel locus of the substantive *Pancagni-vidya* and Two Paths doctrine in the early Upanishadic literature, in nearly verbatim form. The substantive Chandogya version holds the same substantive narrative frame (Svetaketu and Pravahana), the same substantive Five Fires (with the same substantive substitutions of fuel, smoke, light, coals, sparks at each altar), the same substantive Two Paths (*devayāna* and *pitryāna*), and the same substantive third-path of the substantive ignorant. The Brihadaranyaka and Chandogya parallel here is one of the strongest cross-textual relations in the substantive Upanishadic literature. Available in Müller's *Upanishads, Part I* (Volume I of the SBE, 1879) and in Olivelle's *Early Upanishads* (Oxford 1998).
- **Karma-rebirth doctrine.** Wendy Doniger, ed., *Karma and Rebirth in Classical Indian Traditions* (University of California Press, 1980) — sustained collection on the doctrinal history of karma in Indian thought, with the substantive *Pancagni-vidya* and Two Paths doctrine of the Brihadaranyaka's Second Brahmana of the Sixth Adhyaya as one of the principal early-Upanishadic source-loci of the substantive devayāna-pitryāna distinction that the substantive Vedanta tradition works at length.
