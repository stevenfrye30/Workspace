# Ramayana, Ayodhya Kanda, Sections 1–10

The opening sections of the Ayodhya Kanda — the second book of the Ramayana, set in the capital city of Ayodhya — narrate the announcement of Prince Rama's consecration as crown prince, the city's preparations for the ceremony, and the palace conspiracy that will divert him into exile. King Dasharatha, aged and wishing to retire, names his eldest son Rama as *yuvaraja*; the people receive the news with celebration; the hunchbacked maidservant Manthara persuades Queen Kaikeyi to demand instead that her own son Bharata be made king and Rama be banished. Sections 1 through 10 take the narrative from the king's first announcement to the moment Dasharatha enters Kaikeyi's anger chamber and finds her transformed into a stranger. The Ramayana is one of the two great Sanskrit epics; the Ayodhya Kanda is its longest book.

## Context

The Ramayana is the older of the two great Sanskrit epics, alongside the Mahabharata. Modern scholarship places the core books — the Ayodhya Kanda among them — in roughly the fifth to fourth century BCE, with the framing books (the *Bala Kanda* at the start, the *Uttara Kanda* at the end) added somewhat later. The traditional attribution is to the sage-poet Valmiki, who is figured in the text itself as both author and minor character; the historical authorship is unknown.

The seven books, or *kandas*, follow the life of Prince Rama: childhood and marriage (*Bala*), the events at Ayodhya leading to his exile (*Ayodhya*), his fourteen years in the forest (*Aranya*), the alliance with the monkey-king Sugriva (*Kishkindha*), Hanuman's mission to find the captive Sita (*Sundara*), the war against Ravana to recover her (*Yuddha*), and an epilogue (*Uttara*). The Ayodhya Kanda is the second and longest book — about 119 short sections, or *sargas* — and it concerns the political and domestic crisis at the royal court that drives Rama and Sita out of the city.

Sections 1 through 10 cover the events before the exile is demanded. The book opens with Dasharatha's decision to consecrate Rama (sections 1–6), the citizens' preparations for the ceremony (sections 6–7), and Manthara's persuasion of Kaikeyi to use the two boons Dasharatha once owed her to demand Bharata's kingship and Rama's banishment (sections 7–9). Section 10 is the king's confused arrival at the anger chamber where Kaikeyi waits to make her demand.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the Ramayana is poetry. The text is in Sanskrit *shloka* — a verse form that runs in couplets of sixteen syllables — and the stories are told slowly, with formal repetitions and digressions. Reading the opening sections at the pace of a poem rather than the pace of a novel keeps closer to the form. The most important moments — Manthara's monologue, Kaikeyi's anger, Dasharatha's grief — are extended verse-speeches, not summary paragraphs.

Second, the Ayodhya Kanda is a domestic and political book, not a battle book. The crisis that drives the rest of the epic begins not with an enemy king but with a maidservant talking to a queen across a private room. Reading sections 1–10 with this scale in view — palace, family, ceremony — keeps the events in proportion before the larger arc of the epic absorbs them.
