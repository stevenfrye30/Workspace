# Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 3, Part 2

Book 3, Part 2 of the Brihadaranyaka Upanishad — the Second Brahmana of the Third Adhyaya — continues the Janaka Vaideha contest opened in the First Brahmana. The court setting is not reset; the prize of one thousand cows is already in Yajnavalkya's hand from the previous Brahmana; and the closing line of the previous Brahmana — *After that Asvala held his peace* — has cleared the floor for the next challenger. The narrator's opening line names him: *Then Garatkarava Artabhaga asked.* Like Asvala, Artabhaga begins each of his questions with the same address — *Yajnavalkya, he said* — and the form of the dialogue persists.

The Brahmana works in two cycles. The first cycle (verses 1–9) is the catalogue of the *Grahas* and *Atigrahas* — *seizers* and *that which seizes beyond*. Artabhaga asks how many of each there are; Yajnavalkya answers *eight Grahas and eight Atigrahas*; Artabhaga asks for the list; Yajnavalkya gives it across eight verses, each holding the same shape: *X is one Graha, and that is seized by Y as the Atigraha, for with X one Z's Z's*. The eight pairs in order: *Prana / Apana* (one smells with the down-breathing); *Speech / name* (with speech one pronounces names); *the tongue / taste* (with the tongue one perceives tastes); *the eye / form* (with the eye one sees forms); *the ear / sound* (with the ear one hears sounds); *the mind / desire* (with the mind one desires desires); *the arms / work* (with the arms one works work); *the skin / touch* (with the skin one perceives touch). The closing line of verse 9 marks the catalogue's end: *These are the eight Grahas and the eight Atigrahas.*

The second cycle (verses 10–13) turns to death. Artabhaga asks: *Everything is the food of death. What then is the deity to whom death is food?* Yajnavalkya answers: *Fire is death, and that is the food of water. Death is conquered again.* Verse 11 asks whether the vital breaths move out of the sage at death; Yajnavalkya answers *No — they are gathered up in him, he swells, he is inflated, and thus inflated the dead lies at rest.* Verse 12 asks what does not leave the sage; Yajnavalkya answers *The name. For the name is endless, the Visvedevas are endless, and by it he gains the endless world.*

The thirteenth verse asks the question for which there is no public answer. Artabhaga gives the dispersal: *When the speech of this dead person enters into the fire, breath into the air, the eye into the sun, the mind into the moon, the hearing into space, into the earth the body, into the ether the self, into the shrubs the hairs of the body, into the trees the hairs of the head, blood and the seed are deposited in the water — where is then that person?* The catalogue is exhaustive; the question is precise; the answer does not come.

What comes instead is one of the most distinctive moments in the Brihadaranyaka: Yajnavalkya does not answer in public. He says: *Take my hand, my friend. We two alone shall know of this; let this question of ours not be (discussed) in public.* The Brahmana then closes with the narrator's report: *Then these two went out and argued, and what they said was karman (work), what they praised was karman, viz. that a man becomes good by good work, and bad by bad work.*

## Context

The Brihadaranyaka Upanishad is one of the two earliest of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads, sharing that position with the Chandogya. It belongs to the Shukla Yajurveda branch of the Vedic corpus and forms the closing portion of the Shatapatha Brahmana — the *Aranyaka* in its name marks it as a forest-text recited at the late stages of Vedic study. Modern scholarship places the Brihadaranyaka in roughly the eighth to sixth century BCE, prior to the Buddha; the text is anonymous, and the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship. The text is the longest of the principal Upanishads and is typically structured in six *adhyayas* (chapters), each containing several *brahmanas* (sections).

Book 3, Part 2 holds one of the earliest explicit references to *karman* in the Upanishadic literature, and the manner in which the reference is given has been kept in the tradition's discussion of the Brihadaranyaka ever since. The dispersal-of-the-body question Artabhaga asks at verse 13 is a precise and complete catalogue: every visible part of the dying person is given a destination. The question Yajnavalkya does not answer in public — *where is then that person?* — is the question of what survives all those dispersals. The narrator's report does not state the answer Yajnavalkya gave Artabhaga in private; it gives only the names by which the answer was held — *karman, karman* — and the closing summary that *a man becomes good by good work and bad by bad work*. The early form of the karma-and-rebirth doctrine is held in this brief report; the full development of the doctrine across the later Vedanta literature has its source partly here. The procedure of holding the doctrine outside the public *brahmodya* — the formal priestly debate at the great sacrifice — is itself a feature of the Brihadaranyaka's narrative method.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the Brahmana extends the contest's procedure but changes the kind of question. Asvala's questions in Part 1 had concerned the priestly vocabulary of the sacrifice — Hotri, Adhvaryu, Udgatri, Brahman; oblations, hymns, deities. Artabhaga's questions concern the body and its dispersal — the senses as seizers, the breaths at death, the dispersal at death. The form remains the same; the ground shifts. Reading Part 2 with Part 1 held alongside it, attending to the way the contest's structure persists across the change of substance, recovers what the Janaka contest is doing as a whole: each challenger holds the form of the *brahmodya* but tests Yajnavalkya in a different field.

Second, the Brahmana ends not on silence but on privacy. Asvala had been silenced (*Asvala held his peace*); Artabhaga is taken aside (*Take my hand, my friend*). The closing report tells what they said outside the assembly — *karman, karman* — without giving the substantive answer to verse 13's question. Reading the close with attention to the deliberate withholding — the question precisely framed in public, the answer placed precisely outside the public — keeps the form the Brihadaranyaka has when it marks a teaching as private. The narrative gesture is not concealment in the modern sense; it is the formal acknowledgment that some teachings belong to the relation of two and not to the assembly.

- **Verses 3–6 each on a single passage.** Passages `5.682`, `5.683`, `5.684`, `5.685` each carry one verse of the Graha/Atigraha catalogue without internal splits.
- **Verse-7 split.** Verse 7 (mind/desire) is split across passages `5.686` and `5.687`. Passage `5.686` ends mid-line at *that is seized by* and passage `5.687` resumes *desire as the Atigraha*.
- **Verses 8–9 each on a single passage.** Passages `5.688` and `5.689` each carry one verse, with `5.689` carrying the closing summary line *These are the eight Grahas and the eight Atigrahas*.
- **Verses 10–12 each as question-and-answer pairs.** Passages `5.690`–`5.691` carry verse 10 (death's deity); passages `5.692`–`5.693` carry verse 11 (the vital breaths at death); passages `5.694`–`5.695` carry verse 12 (the name as what does not leave). - **Verse-13 split with footnote.** Verse 13 — the dispersal-at-death catalogue — is split across passages `5.696` and `5.697`. Passage `5.696` carries the catalogue-question with footnote `[*1]` on the line *speech enters into the fire*. The split is mid-sentence at *the hairs of the head into the trees, blood and the seed* / *are deposited in the water* — Müller's editorial paragraphing breaking the dispersal-list across two passages.
- **Closing private-conference line at `5.698` with footnote.** The closing passage carries Yajnavalkya's *Take my hand, my friend* speech and the narrator's report on the karma-doctrine, with footnote `[*1]` at the close on the karma-doctrine summary. The closing is unusually structured — it is not a numbered verse but a narrator's report — and it stands alongside the closing line *Asvala held his peace* of the previous Brahmana as the second of the contest's two distinct closing forms.

Passage `5.699` is the "Third Brahmana" header that opens Book 3, Part 3 of the Brihadaranyaka and is not part of this entity. ## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 3, Part 1 — paired immediately consecutive Brahmanas of the Janaka Vaideha contest; the First Brahmana opened the contest with Asvala the Hotri priest as the first challenger and closed with the line *After that Asvala held his peace*, the Second Brahmana opens directly into the next challenger — *Then Garatkarava Artabhaga asked* — and works through the same form of public question-and-answer dialogue with a new field of substance. The contest's procedure is preserved exactly across the Brahmana boundary; the closing forms differ — silence in Part 1, private withdrawal in Part 2 — and mark the contest's two distinct ways of ending a challenger's questioning.
- **structural-parallel** → Katha Upanishad, Book 2, Part 3 — paired Upanishadic teachings on what does not leave the dying person; the Katha's Sixth Valli holds the closing recognition that *when all the ties of the heart are severed here on earth, then the mortal becomes immortal* (Katha 2.3.15) and the closing narrative that Nachiketas, having received the teaching, became free from passion and death and obtained Brahman, the Brihadaranyaka's Second Brahmana of the Third Adhyaya holds the closely related teaching that *the name does not leave* the dying sage — *for the name is endless, the Visvedevas are endless, and by it he gains the endless world* — and the privately-held doctrine of *karman* that closes the Brahmana. Both texts hold the question of what survives the dispersal of the body and place the answer at the boundary of public discourse.
- **structural-parallel** → Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 8 (Akshara Brahma Yoga) — paired Sanskrit cosmologies of departure at death; the Gita's Chapter 8 holds the teaching that the yogi who at the time of death fixes his mind on the Self, restraining the breath between the eyebrows, departing through the syllable Om reaches the supreme abode (Gita 8.10–13), the Brihadaranyaka's Second Brahmana of the Third Adhyaya holds the catalogue of the dispersal of the dying body — *speech into fire, breath into air, eye into sun, mind into moon, hearing into space, the body into earth, the self into the ether* — as the question Artabhaga asks Yajnavalkya in public and that Yajnavalkya will answer only in private. Paired moments where the late-Vedic and the epic literature work the question of the death-departure through closely related cosmic-bodily inventories.
