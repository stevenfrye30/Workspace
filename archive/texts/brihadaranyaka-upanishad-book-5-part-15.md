# Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 5, Part 15

Book 5, Part 15 of the Brihadaranyaka Upanishad — the Fifteenth Brahmana of the Fifth Adhyaya — closes the Fifth Adhyaya. The Adhyaya's prior fourteen Brahmanas have run through compact substantive teachings — the Purnam mantra and Om-as-ether-as-Brahman triple identification of the First Brahmana, the Pragapati-and-three-classes parable on the syllable Da of the Second Brahmana, the *hridaya* etymology of the Third Brahmana, the chained substantive identification of the heart with the true Brahman of the Fourth Brahmana, the cosmogony plus *satyam* etymology plus paired body-anatomies of the Fifth Brahmana, the *manomaya purusha* within the heart of the Sixth Brahmana, the lightning-as-Brahman *vidyut* etymology of the Seventh Brahmana, the speech-as-cow *upāsana* meditation of the Eighth Brahmana, the Agni-Vaisvanara digestive-fire identification of the Ninth Brahmana, the post-mortem ascent through wind-sun-moon of the Tenth Brahmana, the threefold "highest penance" of the Eleventh Brahmana, the food-and-life dialectic with *Vi-Ram* etymology of the Twelfth Brahmana, the four-fold breath-identification with Vedic-and-political categories of the Thirteenth Brahmana, and the substantial Gayatri-doctrine of the Fourteenth Brahmana. The Fifteenth Brahmana works the Adhyaya's close in a procedural form distinct from any of the prior fourteen: a sequence of four substantive Vedic prayers held in succession, with no exposition between them.

The substantive content of the Brahmana is the four substantive prayers, each on its own short passage (with verse 2 split across two passages). The four are widely recited in the substantive Vedic-Hindu tradition as the *aṅti-vacanāṁsi* — the prayers of the moment of death — and three of them appear in nearly identical form at the close of the Isha Upanishad (the Vajasaneyi Samhita Upanishad verses 15–18). The Brahmana gives them in Müller's English translation with the substantive Sanskrit retained at the close.

**Verse 1 at `5.1238` — the Pushan-prayer on the golden disk**: *The face of the True (the Brahman) is covered with a golden disk. Open that, O Pushan, that we may see the nature of the True.* The substantive form is the address to the Vedic deity Pushan — the substantive solar-pastoral guide of souls — asking that the substantive golden disk (*hiranmaya pātra*) covering the substantive face of Truth be removed so that the substantive nature of the True may be seen. The substantive doctrine is that the substantive solar disk is the substantive obstacle to the substantive vision of Brahman, and that the substantive prayer is the substantive request for its removal. Müller's footnotes `[*2]`, `[*3]`, `[*4]`, and `[*5]` cluster on this verse, glossing the substantive elements of the substantive prayer.

**Verse 2 at `5.1239`–`5.1240` — the Pushan-as-Surya identification**: *O Pushan, only seer, Yama (judge), Surya (sun), son of Pragapati, spread thy rays and gather them! The light which is thy fairest form, I see it. I am what he is (viz. the person in the sun).* The substantive form is the substantive multiple-identification of Pushan with *only seer*, with Yama (the judge of the dead), with Surya (the sun), and as the *son of Pragapati* — substantively gathering the substantive solar-and-judicial-and-sole-seer figures into one substantive prayer. The substantive request follows: *spread thy rays and gather them* — the substantive solar-radiance is asked to be substantively contracted so that the substantive form behind the rays may be seen. The substantive identification at the close is one of the most-cited Upanishadic statements: *I am what he is (viz. the person in the sun)*. Müller's parenthetical *(viz. the person in the sun)* preserves the substantive identification of *he* with the substantive person in the solar disk. The substantive *so'ham asmi* form — *I am he* — is the substantive identity-claim at the moment of substantive contemplation. Müller's footnote `[*6]` attaches at *Pragapati*. The verse is split across two passages mid-clause at *son of Pragapati, spread thy rays / and gather them!* — the split falls inside the substantive imperative.

**Verse 3 at `5.1241` — the death-moment prayer**: *Breath to air and to the immortal! Then this my body ends in ashes. Om! Mind, remember! Remember thy deeds! Mind, remember! Remember thy deeds!* The substantive form is the substantive death-moment prayer with three substantive operations: (1) the substantive dissolution-prayer *Breath to air and to the immortal* — the substantive return of breath to the substantive cosmic air and to the substantive immortal; (2) the substantive bodily-fate-clause *Then this my body ends in ashes* — the substantive recognition of cremation as the substantive end of the body; (3) the substantive Om-and-imperative *Om! Mind, remember! Remember thy deeds! Mind, remember! Remember thy deeds!* — the substantive doubled imperative to the mind to remember the deeds. The substantive doubling — *Mind, remember! Remember thy deeds! Mind, remember! Remember thy deeds!* — preserves the substantive Sanskrit *kṛto smara, kṛtaṃ smara, kṛto smara, kṛtaṃ smara* repetition that the substantive Vedic recitation tradition holds at the moment of death. Müller's footnote `[*1]` attaches at the substantive doubled imperative.

**Verse 4 at `5.1242` — the Agni-prayer with Rigveda citation**: *Agni, lead us on to wealth (beatitude) by a good path, thou, O God, who knowest all things! Keep far from us crooked evil, and we shall offer thee the fullest praise! (Rv. I, 189, 1.)* The substantive form is the substantive Agni-prayer for the substantive good path and the substantive removal of crooked evil. Müller's parenthetical *(beatitude)* at *wealth* preserves the substantive double-meaning of the substantive Sanskrit *rāyim* — both substantive material wealth and substantive spiritual beatitude. Müller's editorial citation *(Rv. Müller's footnote `[*2]` attaches at *by a good path*.

The Brahmana ends inside the Agni-prayer with the substantive Rigvedic citation. No closing seal beyond the substantive prayer; no narrator's voice; no further commentary. The Fifth Adhyaya closes here, and the Sixth Adhyaya begins at the next passage with its own header.

## Context

The Brihadaranyaka Upanishad is one of the two earliest of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads, sharing that position with the Chandogya. It belongs to the Shukla Yajurveda branch of the Vedic corpus and forms the closing portion of the Shatapatha Brahmana — the *Aranyaka* in its name marks it as a forest-text recited at the late stages of Vedic study. Modern scholarship places the Brihadaranyaka in roughly the eighth to sixth century BCE, prior to the Buddha; the text is anonymous, and the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship. The text is the longest of the principal Upanishads and is typically structured in six *adhyayas* (chapters), each containing several *brahmanas* (sections).

Book 5, Part 15 holds three of the Adhyaya's most distinctive features in concentrated form. The first is the substantive Adhyaya-closing-by-mantra-recitation form. The Adhyaya's prior fourteen Brahmanas have closed inside their substantive content — substantive identifications, substantive *vidyā* benefits, substantive seals — with no formal closure-marker for the Adhyaya as a whole. The Fifteenth Brahmana works the Adhyaya's closure through the substantive recitation of four substantive Vedic prayers, with no expository content between them. The substantive form holds the Adhyaya's closure as the substantive moment of substantive prayer rather than substantive doctrine: the substantive teaching of the Adhyaya's first fourteen Brahmanas is here followed by the substantive prayers that the substantive teaching has prepared.

The second is the substantive overlap with the Isha Upanishad's closing. The Isha Upanishad — one of the shortest and most-cited Upanishads, traditionally placed at the head of the Sanskrit Upanishadic corpus — closes with four substantive verses (15–18) that are nearly identical to the four substantive verses of the Brihadaranyaka's Fifteenth Brahmana of the Fifth Adhyaya. The substantive overlap is one of the principal textual relations between the Brihadaranyaka and the Isha Upanishad, and the Vedic-Hindu substantive tradition has read the four verses across both texts as the substantive *aṅti-vacanāṁsi* (final words) recited at the moment of death. The substantive form preserves the substantive verses across two distinct substantive Upanishadic loci, and the substantive doctrine of the *I am what he is* identification, the substantive death-moment prayer, and the substantive Agni-prayer is held in both texts.

The third is the substantive double footnote-sequence. The Brahmana carries two distinct substantive footnote-sequences in the digitized source: footnotes `[*2]`–`[*6]` on passages `5.1238`–`5.1239` (covering verse 1 and the open of verse 2), and footnotes `[*1]`–`[*2]` on passages `5.1241`–`5.1242` (covering verses 3 and 4). The reset-to-`[*1]` between the two sequences suggests a substantive page-break or a substantive Adhyaya-internal structural division in Müller's published edition that the digitized source has carried over. The substantive irregularity is preserved as found in the source rather than smoothed into a single continuous footnote-sequence.

The closing form is the Adhyaya's first formal closing-mantra-cluster. The Brahmana's substantive content is the four substantive Vedic prayers, and the substantive doctrine is that the substantive Adhyaya's closure is the substantive recitation. The Brahmana ends inside the substantive Agni-prayer with Müller's substantive Rigvedic citation as the closing line.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the four substantive prayers should be read as the Adhyaya's substantive closing-recitation rather than as four independent substantive teachings. The Brahmana works no exposition between the prayers; the substantive form is the substantive sequence of four substantive prayers, each on its own short passage (with verse 2 split across two passages). Reading the four prayers alongside each other as a substantive unit — neither summarized as *the Brahmana closes the Adhyaya with four prayers* nor expanded into four independent substantive teachings — recovers the substantive form of the Adhyaya's substantive closing-by-recitation.

Second, the substantive parallel with the Isha Upanishad's closing should be preserved as a substantive textual relation rather than a generic doctrinal similarity. The Brihadaranyaka and the Isha Upanishad hold the same four substantive verses at their substantive closing positions, and the substantive Vedic-Hindu tradition has read the four verses as the substantive *aṅti-vacanāṁsi* (final words) recited at the substantive moment of death. The substantive textual relation is one of substantive verbatim overlap rather than substantive thematic resonance, and reading the substantive prayers with the substantive Isha-Upanishadic pairing held alongside them recovers the substantive form of the substantive verses as the substantive Vedic-recitation tradition's substantive death-moment cluster.
