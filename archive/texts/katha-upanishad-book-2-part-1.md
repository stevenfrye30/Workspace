# Katha Upanishad, Book 2, Part 1

Book 2, Part 1 of the Katha Upanishad — the Fourth Valli — opens the second *adhyaya* but continues Yama's instruction directly from the close of Book 1, Part 3. The valli begins with the line that the Self-existent pierced the openings of the senses so that they turn outward; therefore the ordinary person looks forward and not backward into himself, and only the rare seeker, with eyes closed and wishing for immortality, sees the Self looking back. From that opening the valli develops the same distinction in a series of verses each closed by the refrain *etad vai tat* — "this is that" — pointing past form, taste, smell, sound, and touch to the Self by which all of these are known. The Person of the size of a thumb is named twice; the closing simile compares the knower's Self to pure water poured into pure water.

## Context

The Katha Upanishad is one of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads — a small group of texts traditionally received as the philosophical core of the late Vedic literature. It belongs to the Krishna Yajurveda branch of the Vedic corpus and is composed in Sanskrit verse, mostly in the *anushtubh* meter. Modern scholarship places its composition in roughly the fifth to third century BCE; the principal Upanishads as a group are dated to the centuries on either side of the Buddha. The text is anonymous; the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship.

Book 2, Part 1 opens the second *adhyaya* of the Katha. The division into two books is a structural division of the manuscript tradition, not a break in the dialogue: Yama is still answering Nachiketas, and the teaching simply continues. Where Book 1, Part 3 closed with the chariot image, the graded ordering of faculties, and the imperative to rise and awake, Book 2, Part 1 turns to the question the chariot prepared — what perceives the objects the senses bring in — and answers it through a sequence of verses that name the Self by way of the refrain "this is that." The Bhagavad Gita's later language of the field (*kshetra*) and the knower of the field (*kshetrajna*) develops the same distinction this valli sets out.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the valli is built on a contrast between the visible and the subtle. The senses face outward; the objects of sense are what they meet; what perceives both is not itself among the objects perceived. The valli does not argue this distinction — it states it, repeats it through different vocabulary (the great omnipresent Self, the lord of the past and the future, the Person of the size of a thumb), and closes each verse with the refrain "this is that." Reading the valli with attention to the recurrence of the refrain, and to the slight shift of vocabulary it gathers each time, recovers the form the teaching has.

Second, the instruction is gradual rather than argumentative. The Fourth Valli does not build a case; it gives the same recognition again and again, each time through a different image. The Self-existent piercing the senses outward; the wise closing the eyes to see; the soul that eats honey; the one born first from the brooding heat; the one that is here the same as there; the Person no larger than a thumb; pure water poured into pure water. Reading slowly enough to let each image settle before the next is given, rather than collating them into a single doctrine, keeps the form the valli has.

## The text

The text is held in the Archive library under `upanishads-muller-part2`, chapter 5 (Müller translation, 1884 — *The Sacred Books of the East*, Volume XV; via SacredTexts.com). Library chapter 5 is shared between Book 1, Part 3 (the Third Valli, passages `5.1` through `5.19`) and Book 2, Part 1 (the Fourth Valli, the entity here): the library does not give the Fourth Valli its own chapter, but continues consecutively after the close of Book 1 inside the same chapter. Book 2, Part 1 occupies passages `5.20` through `5.37`. Passage `5.20` is the "Second Adhyaya" header and `5.21` is the "Fourth Valli" header; passages `5.22` through `5.37` carry the fifteen numbered verses of the valli, with verse 6 split across passages `5.27` and `5.28` where Müller's footnote interrupts the verse mid-line. Passage `5.38` is the "Fifth Valli" header that opens Book 2, Part 2 and is not part of this entity. Fifteen verses; about ten minutes' reading.

## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 13 (Kshetra Kshetrajna Vibhaga Yoga) — paired Sanskrit passages on the distinction between what is perceived and what perceives; the Gita's Chapter 13 sets the field (*kshetra*) and the knower of the field (*kshetrajna*) as analytic terms, the Katha's Fourth Valli holds the same distinction through the refrain "this is that," each verse pointing past the named object — form, taste, smell, sound, touch — to the Self by which it is known.
- **structural-parallel** → Daodejing, Chapters 1–10 — paired short passages on a source not immediately visible; the Daodejing opens with the line that the Dao that can be named is not the constant Dao and develops through the early chapters the recurring contrast between the named and the nameless, the visible and the subtle, the Katha's Fourth Valli holds the same contrast through the refrain "this is that," each verse pointing past what the senses meet to what perceives them.
- **structural-parallel** → Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 6 (Dhyana Yoga) — paired passages on turning the senses inward; the Gita's Krishna teaches Arjuna the practice of withdrawing the mind and the senses from outward objects, the Katha's Fourth Valli opens with the same gesture — the rare seeker, with eyes closed and wishing for immortality, sees the Self looking back — and continues by naming the Self through what cannot be reached by the outward-facing senses.
