# Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 13 (Kshetra Kshetrajna Vibhaga Yoga)

Chapter 13 of the Bhagavad Gita — the *Kshetra Kshetrajna Vibhaga Yoga*, conventionally translated "The Yoga of the Distinction Between the Field and the Knower of the Field" (Arnold's translation gives the chapter the parallel name "The Book of Religion by Separation of Matter and Spirit") — opens the Gita's late, more classificatory movement. The chapter is given to a single sustained distinction. Krishna names the body the field (*kshetra*) and the consciousness that knows the body the knower of the field (*kshetrajna*); he goes on to describe the constituents of the field (the elements, the senses, intellect, ego, desire, pleasure, pain, and so on), the qualities that constitute knowledge (humility, harmlessness, patience, equanimity, and many others), and what is to be known (the supreme present in all and across all). The chapter's central pair — the distinction between *prakriti* (nature) and *purusha* (the conscious witness) — frames the analysis the closing chapters of the Gita will develop further.

## Context

The Bhagavad Gita is a 700-verse philosophical poem in eighteen chapters, set inside Book VI (the *Bhishma Parva*) of the Mahabharata. Most scholars place its present form between roughly the second century BCE and the second century CE — a layered composition that took shape during the long growth of the epic from an older bardic core. The traditional attribution is to Vyasa, the legendary compiler of the Mahabharata; the historical authorship is unknown.

Chapter 13 opens the Gita's late movement. The early chapters (2–6) answered the question Arjuna's refusal raised through successive accounts of action and discipline; the middle chapters (7–11) widened the frame into Krishna's nature, devotion, and the vision of the universal form; Chapter 12 contracted the frame back to the lived character of the devotee. Chapter 13 turns to analytic distinction — the field and the knower of the field, *prakriti* and *purusha*, the body and the consciousness that knows it, knowledge and what is known — and the closing chapters (14–18) take up further classifications: the three qualities (*gunas*) of nature, the divine and the demoniac, the three kinds of faith, the three kinds of action, and so on. Chapter 13 is the hinge between the relational portrait of Chapter 12 and the classificatory work that closes the Gita.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the chapter is built on paired concepts. *Kshetra* and *kshetrajna* (field and knower of the field); *prakriti* and *purusha* (nature and consciousness); knowledge and what is to be known; the seer and the seen. The chapter does not develop a single argument; it sets the pair, names the constituents of each side, and then returns to the pair. Reading the chapter with the principal distinction held visible — the body and the one who knows the body, the changing field and the witnessing consciousness — recovers the form the chapter has.

Second, the chapter contains two lists of qualities, each treated as a definition rather than a description. The list at 13.7–11, which Krishna calls "knowledge," names humility, harmlessness, patience, service to the teacher, purity, steadiness, self-restraint, equanimity, dwelling in solitude, devotion, and the perception of birth, death, old age, and disease as evil. The list at 13.13–18, which Krishna calls "what is to be known," describes the supreme in negative and contradictory terms — without hands, with hands; without senses, perceiving all; far and near. Reading the lists as definitions Krishna is offering, rather than as descriptive aside, follows the way the chapter is composed.

## The text

The text is held in the Archive library under `bhagavad-gita`, chapter 13 (Edwin Arnold's verse translation, *The Song Celestial*, 1885 — Project Gutenberg edition). Arnold's verse translation here returns to a moderate compression, between the heavier compression of Chapter 12 and the near-line-by-line rendering of Chapter 11: the chapter's thirty-four Sanskrit shlokas (some editions count thirty-five, with a variant opening verse) are gathered into twelve English verse-paragraphs followed by a closing colophon line (passages `13.1`–`13.13` in the library's verse-level subdivision). A modern prose translation by the International Gita Society is held in the library as a parallel translation. About twenty-five minutes' reading.

## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 12 (Bhakti Yoga) — the immediate Gita-internal pair; Chapter 12 gives the lived portrait of the devotee in a small-scale relational register, Chapter 13 turns from that portrait to the analytic distinction — body and knower, *prakriti* and *purusha* — that the closing chapters will develop.
- **structural-parallel** → Katha Upanishad, Book 1, Part 1 — the Hindu-shelf companion on the same distinction in earlier Upanishadic form; the Katha Upanishad with the boy Nachiketa pressing Yama, the lord of death, on what survives the body, Chapter 13 of the Gita with Krishna's analytic statement of the field and the knower of the field that the Upanishadic tradition had been developing for several centuries.
- **structural-parallel** → Dhammapada, Chapter 1 (The Twin Verses) — paired chapters that draw a line between an inner state and the visible world that follows from it; the Twin Verses with the mind preceding action and outcome, Chapter 13 with the conscious witness present in the field of the body without being identical to it.
