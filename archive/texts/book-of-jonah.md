# Book of Jonah

The Book of Jonah is a short prose narrative in the Hebrew Bible — four chapters, forty-eight verses — that stands among the twelve books of the Minor Prophets but tells a story rather than recording oracles. The prophet Jonah is sent by God to cry out against Nineveh; he flees by sea, is swallowed by a great fish, and only after being returned to land does he deliver the warning. The city of Nineveh repents and is spared. Jonah is angry that destruction has been averted, and the book ends with God's unanswered question about whether such a city should not be spared.

## Context

The Book of Jonah is one of the twelve books of the Minor Prophets (*Trei Asar*, "the Twelve" in Hebrew tradition), gathered in a single scroll in the Hebrew canon. Among the twelve, Jonah is unusual: where the other prophetic books preserve oracles attributed to a named prophet, Jonah is almost entirely narrative — a story about a prophet rather than the prophet's words. Modern scholarship generally places its composition in the post-exilic period, the fifth or fourth century BCE; the linguistic features of the book — late Biblical Hebrew, occasional Aramaic influence — point to that range. The narrative itself is set centuries earlier, in the eighth century BCE; a prophet named Jonah son of Amittai is mentioned briefly in 2 Kings 14:25 as active under Jeroboam II.

The book is in four short chapters. Chapter 1 is the call and the flight by sea; chapter 2 is Jonah's prayer from inside the fish, in psalm-form; chapter 3 is the prophecy at Nineveh and the city's repentance; chapter 4 is the closing dialogue between Jonah and God outside the spared city. The book ends on a question put by God to Jonah; the question is not answered.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the book repeatedly reverses expected roles and reactions. The prophet flees from God; the pagan sailors pray and offer sacrifice; the foreign king of Nineveh repents in sackcloth and orders even the cattle to fast; the prophet, after God spares the city, sulks under a plant. The narrator's eye for these inversions — Jonah behaves worst, the gentiles behave best — is part of the form. Reading the book with attention to its irony, rather than as a straightforward prophetic exhortation, recovers the tone the text is given in.

Second, the book ends on a question. God's closing words to Jonah — about the hundred and twenty thousand people of Nineveh "who cannot discern between their right hand and their left hand," and the cattle — are not followed by Jonah's reply, or by any narrator's resolution. The question is left open. Reading the book with attention to where the text stops, rather than supplying the answer, keeps the form the book has.
