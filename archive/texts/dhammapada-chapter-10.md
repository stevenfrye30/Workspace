# Dhammapada, Chapter 10 (Punishment)

Chapter 10 of the Dhammapada — the *Daṇḍa-vagga*, "Punishment" — is a chapter of seventeen verses opening with one of the Dhammapada's most quoted passages: *all men tremble at punishment, all men fear death; remember that you are like unto them, and do not kill, nor cause slaughter* (129). The opening twin verse repeats the line with a single word changed — *all men love life* in place of *all men fear death* — and shifts the address from *you* to *thou* (130). The next pair turns the form to the consequence of acting otherwise: he who, seeking his own happiness, punishes those who also long for happiness will not find happiness after death; he who does not, will (131–132). Verse 133 turns the figure to speech: do not speak harshly, for those spoken to will answer in the same way, and blows for blows will touch thee. Verse 134 turns it to silence: like a shattered metal plate that does not sound, the one who does not utter has reached Nirvana. Verse 135 turns it to the figure of the cowherd whose staff drives the cows: so Age and Death drive the life of men. Verse 136 holds the fool burning by his own deeds. Verses 137–140 hold a single sentence broken across four passages that enumerates the ten states a man comes to who inflicts pain on the innocent. Verse 141 holds a list of ascetic practices that do not purify — nakedness, platted hair, dirt, fasting, lying on the earth, rubbing with dust, sitting motionless. Verse 142 turns the figure to its other side: the man in fine apparel who is tranquil, subdued, and chaste is the true Brahmana, sramana, and bhikshu. Verses 143–144 turn it to the well-trained horse and the whip — a man should be active and lively under reproof. The closing verse (145) repeats verse 80 with a single word changed: *Well-makers lead the water; fletchers bend the arrow; carpenters bend a log of wood; good people fashion themselves* — where Chapter 6 verse 80 had said *wise people*, this chapter closes the figure as *good people*.

## Context

The Dhammapada is one of the books of the Khuddaka Nikaya, the fifth and shortest section of the Sutta Pitaka in the Pali Canon. It collects 423 verses arranged in twenty-six chapters by theme — pairs, heedfulness, mind, flowers, the sage, evil, and so on. The verses are attributed to the Buddha and were transmitted orally for several centuries before being committed to writing in Sri Lanka in the first century BCE. The collection took shape in roughly the third century BCE, in connection with the Theravada transmission of the canon.

Chapter 10 is the *Daṇḍa-vagga*, "Punishment." Its seventeen verses are numbered 129 through 145 in the standard continuous numbering of the Dhammapada — and in F. Max Müller's 1881 translation, the version held in the Archive library, the count and numbering match the standard exactly, with no merged or missing verse of the kind that occurs at the close of Chapter 4, Chapter 6, and Chapter 8. The Pali word *daṇḍa* — punishment, rod, staff, violence, force — is rendered by Müller as "punishment" in the chapter title, and the verses turn its sense between the punishment that a person fears (verses 129–130) and the rod-like force of Age and Death (verse 135). Each verse is short — two or four lines in the Pali — and each completes one statement of the figure.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the chapter is built on the recognition that the punishment one would inflict on another is what oneself fears. Verses 129 and 130 state this twice, with one word changed: all tremble at punishment, all fear death (or all love life); remember that you are like unto them, and do not kill. Verses 131 and 132 turn the same recognition into a paired moral consequence — those who punish others who seek happiness will not find happiness after death, those who do not will. Verse 133 extends the recognition to speech — angry words come back, blows for blows. The chapter's opening figure is reciprocal identification: the other is like oneself, and what oneself fears one must not inflict. Reading the chapter with the figure of identification-with-the-other held visible across the verses, rather than as a series of separate ethical injunctions, recovers the form the chapter is built on.

Second, verse 145 is a near-repetition of verse 80 from Chapter 6 — the *well-makers / fletchers / carpenters / X-fashion-themselves* craft simile. Chapter 6 closed it as *wise people fashion themselves*; Chapter 10 closes it as *good people fashion themselves*. The Dhammapada repeats some verses with small variations across chapters, which is a feature of its compositional history; the same craft simile is being used here to close the chapter on punishment as it was used in Chapter 6 to characterise the wise. Reading the closing verse as a deliberate repetition with a single-word shift, rather than as a fresh aphorism, keeps the form the verses share.

## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Dhammapada, Chapter 6 (The Wise Man) — the earlier chapter whose verse 80 is repeated nearly verbatim at this chapter's verse 145; both verses use the *well-makers / fletchers / carpenters / X-fashion-themselves* craft simile, with Chapter 6 closing it as *wise people fashion themselves* and Chapter 10 closing it as *good people fashion themselves*. The repetition with a single-word shift is a feature of Dhammapada compositional layering.
- **structural-parallel** → Dhammapada, Chapter 9 (Evil) — the immediate predecessor on the shelf, with the same kind of moral causation logic; Chapter 9 with thirteen verses on evil and good as gradual, ripening, and inescapable, Chapter 10 with seventeen verses on punishment and its reciprocal logic. The chapters share the figure of consequences that follow harm — Chapter 9 verses 119–120 on the slow ripening of evil, Chapter 10 verses 137–140 on the ten states a violent man comes to.
- **structural-parallel** → Matthew 5 — the Christian shelf-pair for the Buddhist non-retaliation injunction; the Sermon on the Mount with its turning of the cheek, its love-thine-enemies, its do-not-resist-evil, Dhammapada 10 with its all-tremble-at-punishment opening, its recognition of identification with the other, and its do-not-speak-harshly verse. Both texts hold the same identification-with-the-victim logic, articulated through different vocabulary.
- **structural-parallel** → Analects, Books 1–2 — the Confucian pair on reciprocity; the Analects' opening books with statements on what one would not have done to oneself and what one should not do to others, Dhammapada 10 verses 129–130 with the Buddhist parallel formulation that the other trembles and fears as oneself does. Reciprocity is held across both texts as the ground of moral practice.
- **structural-parallel** → Plato, Apology — the Greek pair on suffering wrong rather than doing it; Socrates' position that it is better to suffer wrong than to commit it, Dhammapada 10 verses 131–132 with the contrast between punishing those who long for happiness and not punishing them, with happiness or its absence as the consequence after death.
