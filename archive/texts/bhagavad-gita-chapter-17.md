# Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 17 (Shraddha Traya Vibhaga Yoga)

Chapter 17 of the Bhagavad Gita — the *Shraddha Traya Vibhaga Yoga*, conventionally translated "The Yoga of the Distinction Between the Three Kinds of Faith" (Arnold's translation gives the chapter the parallel name "The Book of Religion by the Threefold Kinds of Faith") — extends the threefold classification of Chapter 14 across multiple domains of practice. Arjuna opens with a question about those who worship with faith but without regard to scripture: what is their state? Krishna answers that faith itself is of three kinds, conforming to one's nature, and that what a person is corresponds to their faith. The chapter then moves through several domains in turn, applying the same threefold pattern to each: the kinds of worship (*sattvic*, *rajasic*, and *tamasic* persons turn to different beings); food (which kinds promote vitality, which produce restlessness, which produce dullness); sacrifice (*yajna*); austerity (*tapas*) of body, speech, and mind; and gift (*dana*). The chapter closes with a teaching on the formula "OM TAT SAT" as the designation of sacred speech and on faith as the condition without which any offering is unreal.

## Context

The Bhagavad Gita is a 700-verse philosophical poem in eighteen chapters, set inside Book VI (the *Bhishma Parva*) of the Mahabharata. Most scholars place its present form between roughly the second century BCE and the second century CE — a layered composition that took shape during the long growth of the epic from an older bardic core. The traditional attribution is to Vyasa, the legendary compiler of the Mahabharata; the historical authorship is unknown.

Chapter 17 belongs to the Gita's late classificatory movement and is the chapter in which the threefold *guna* typology of Chapter 14 is applied most extensively. Where Chapter 16 had set the binary of divine and demonic endowments, Chapter 17 returns to the threefold pattern and runs it across several domains of human practice. The chapter is twenty-eight verses long in the standard Sanskrit numbering and highly regular in structure: a brief opening question and reply on the three kinds of faith (17.1–4); then, in succession, the three kinds of food (17.7–10), the three kinds of sacrifice (17.11–13), the three kinds of austerity in body, speech, and mind (17.14–19), and the three kinds of gift (17.20–22); and a closing section on "OM TAT SAT" as the designation of sacred speech and on faith as the condition of any real offering (17.23–28). Chapter 17 prepares the final synthesis of Chapter 18, where the same threefold pattern is applied yet more broadly.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the chapter is built on the same triadic comparison Chapter 14 introduced, but now run across many domains. Each domain gets the same treatment in turn — *sattvic*, *rajasic*, *tamasic* — and the chapter moves from one domain to the next without doctrinal exposition between them. Reading the chapter as a sequence of parallel triadic treatments — faith, food, sacrifice, austerity, gift — rather than as a single argument, recovers the form the chapter has and prepares the reader for Chapter 18, which extends the pattern further.

Second, the chapter is concerned with patterned conduct rather than with abstract definition. The categories are named in their effects: which food gives vitality, which produces restlessness; which sacrifice is offered without expectation, which with desire for fruit; which gift is offered to one who has done no service, which is offered with disrespect. Reading the chapter with attention to the practical level at which the classifications are made, rather than reading the categories as metaphysical types, follows the way the chapter is composed.

## The text

The text is held in the Archive library under `bhagavad-gita`, chapter 17 (Edwin Arnold's verse translation, *The Song Celestial*, 1885 — Project Gutenberg edition). Arnold's verse translation here remains at moderate compression: the chapter's twenty-eight Sanskrit shlokas are gathered into thirteen English verse-paragraphs followed by a closing colophon line (passages `17.1`–`17.14` in the library's verse-level subdivision). The repeated triadic structure Krishna develops at verse-by-verse pace in the Sanskrit is held across the English verse-paragraphs, with each domain treated in its own paragraph and the threefold comparison preserved within each. A modern prose translation by the International Gita Society is held in the library as a parallel translation. About twenty minutes' reading.
