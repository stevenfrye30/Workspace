# Taittiriyaka Upanishad, Book 2, Part 4

Book 2, Part 4 of the Taittiriyaka Upanishad — the Fourth Anuvaka of the Brahmanandavalli — extends the recursive structure one further step inward. The Anuvaka opens with the framing sloka of the *manomaya*, the sheath the previous Anuvaka had introduced. The sloka is one of the most-quoted lines of the Upanishadic corpus: *yato vāco nivartante, aprāpya manasā saha; ānandaṁ brahmaṇo vidvān, na bibheti kutaścana* — *he who knows the bliss of that Brahman, from whence all speech, with the mind, turns away unable to reach it, he never fears.* The line that follows the sloka is the recursive identity-formula the Valli has been using throughout — *the embodied Self of this (consisting of mind) is the same as that of the former (consisting of breath)* — closing the mind-self loop just as the breath-self loop and the food-self loop were closed before. The Anuvaka then turns the recursive formula on the next sheath: *different from this, which consists of mind, is the other, the inner Self, which consists of understanding* — the *vijñānamaya*, the knowledge-self. The anatomy of the *vijñānamaya* is given in the language of inward virtue rather than of body or of breath or of scripture. Faith (*śraddhā*) is its head; the right (*ṛta*) is its right arm; the true (*satya*) is its left arm; yoga (*yogaḥ*) is its trunk; the great (*mahaḥ*) is its support. The Anuvaka closes with the same pointer the previous anuvakas have used — *on this there is also the following sloka* — turning the reader toward the Fifth Anuvaka.

## Context

The Taittiriyaka Upanishad is one of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads — a small group of texts traditionally received as the philosophical core of the late Vedic literature. It belongs to the Krishna Yajurveda branch of the Vedic corpus and to the Taittiriya school of that branch; the Upanishad sits inside the Taittiriya Aranyaka. Modern scholarship places it among the older prose Upanishads, in roughly the sixth to fifth century BCE — earlier than the verse Upanishads of the Katha and Mundaka. The text is anonymous; the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship.

Book 2, Part 4 sits inside the Brahmanandavalli, the Second Valli of the Taittiriyaka. The recursive structure the Second Anuvaka inaugurated continues here without a break in procedure: each Anuvaka places the previously-introduced sheath under its own framing sloka, closes the loop with the recursive identity-formula, and introduces the next inner sheath under the same recursive form. The Fourth Anuvaka does this for the *manomaya* and the *vijñānamaya*; the next Anuvaka will do it again for the *vijñānamaya* and the *ānandamaya*, the bliss-self the whole Valli is named for. The Anuvaka's opening sloka — *yato vāco nivartante* — is one of the most-cited Upanishadic lines, repeated in close form across several principal Upanishads and in the later Vedanta literature; its placement here, as the framing verse of the *manomaya*, identifies the mind-self as the level at which the recognition that the source is beyond speech and mind first becomes available.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the Anuvaka is built on the same recursive procedure the Brahmanandavalli has been using since the Second Anuvaka, with a single difference of substance — the pair of sheaths in question has shifted from breath/mind to mind/understanding. Reading the Anuvaka with attention to which terms repeat (the recursive formula, the closing identity-line, the pointer to the next sloka) and which terms shift (the framing sloka, the contents of the next sheath's anatomy) recovers the form the Brahmanandavalli has when its recursion is most deeply nested. The form is doing the work of the inward descent; the substance is what changes.

Second, the anatomy of the *vijñānamaya* is built from inward virtues rather than from external materials. The food-self had digestive organs; the breath-self had the breaths; the mind-self had parts of the Veda; the knowledge-self has faith, the right, the true, yoga, and the great. The mappings move further from the visible body with each level. Reading the inward shift of vocabulary alongside the recursive formula's stable structure, rather than treating the changing vocabulary as a separate doctrine, keeps the form the Anuvaka has — the same procedure, the substance moving toward the inwardness the Valli is approaching.

## The text

The text is held in the Archive library under `upanishads-muller-part2`, chapter 5 (Müller translation, 1884 — *The Sacred Books of the East*, Volume XV; via SacredTexts.com). Library chapter 5 is shared across twenty-five Archive entities — four parts of the Katha Upanishad, the six parts of the Mundaka Upanishad, the twelve anuvakas of the Sikshavalli (Book 1) of the Taittiriyaka, and the First through Third Anuvakas of the Brahmanandavalli (Book 2, Parts 1 through 3) — and continues here into the Fourth Anuvaka without break in the chapter. Book 2, Part 4 of the Taittiriyaka — the entity here — occupies passages `5.244` through `5.248`. Passage `5.244` is the "Fourth Anuvaka" header and carries its own footnote reference `[*1]` — Müller's editorial annotation on the Anuvaka, likely concerning the famous sloka that opens it. Passage `5.245` carries the opening sloka in Müller's quotation marks together with the closing identity-line that completes the *manomaya* loop. Passage `5.246` carries the introduction of the *vijñānamaya* with the recursive formula and the first three terms of its anatomical mapping (faith = head, right = right arm, true = left arm). Passage `5.247` carries the closing two terms (yoga = trunk, the great = support). Passage `5.248` is the closing pointer "On this there is also the following Sloka:". A small irregularity sits at the boundary between this Anuvaka and the previous: the *manomaya* anatomical mapping that began at the end of Book 2, Part 3 (Yajus = head, Rik = right arm, Saman = left arm, the Brahmana doctrine = trunk) was cut off mid-line at "is its" in passage `5.242`, and Müller's English in the Fourth Anuvaka does not explicitly complete the trunk and support of the *manomaya* before turning to its framing sloka — either a translation compression in Müller's edition or a digitization artifact carried over at the Anuvaka boundary; the surrounding text is otherwise complete. Passage `5.249` is the "Fifth Anuvaka" header that opens Book 2, Part 5 of the Taittiriyaka and is not part of this entity. The library chapter continues past this entity into the rest of the Taittiriyaka and the remaining Upanishads of the volume. About three minutes' reading.

## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Taittiriyaka Upanishad, Book 2, Part 3 — paired immediately consecutive Anuvakas of the Brahmanandavalli; the Third Anuvaka placed the *prāṇamaya* under its own sloka and used the recursive formula to introduce the *manomaya*, the Fourth Anuvaka places the *manomaya* under its own sloka and uses the same recursive formula to introduce the *vijñānamaya*. The recursion is exact in form and uniform in pace; the inward descent continues without break across the Anuvaka boundary.
- **structural-parallel** → Katha Upanishad, Book 2, Part 3 — paired Sanskrit passages on Brahman as that which speech and mind cannot reach; the Katha's Sixth Valli holds the line *naiva vācā na manasā prāptuṁ śakyo na cakṣuṣā* — *he cannot be reached by speech, by mind, or by the eye; how can it be apprehended except by him who says "He is?"* (Katha 2.3.12) — the Taittiriyaka's Fourth Anuvaka holds the closely related line *yato vāco nivartante aprāpya manasā saha* — *from whence all speech, with the mind, turns away unable to reach it.* Paired moments in the late-Vedic literature where the recognition that the source lies beyond the reach of speech and mind is given in two close vocabularies.
- **structural-parallel** → Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 15 (Purushottama Yoga) — paired Sanskrit passages on the interior ordering of embodied being and on Brahman as that which the senses do not seize; the Gita's Chapter 15 names the supreme Person seated in the heart of all beings, the source of memory and knowledge, and the ground beyond what is perishable and what is imperishable (Gita 15.16–17), the Taittiriyaka's Fourth Anuvaka holds the closely related recognition through the *vijñānamaya* — the knowledge-self whose substance is faith, the right, the true, yoga, and the great — and through the framing sloka that places the bliss of Brahman beyond what speech and mind can reach. Both texts hold the recognition of the supreme inwardness in close vocabulary across the late-Vedic prose and the epic verse.
