# Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 5, Part 10

Book 5, Part 10 of the Brihadaranyaka Upanishad — the Tenth Brahmana of the Fifth Adhyaya — works the Adhyaya forward in a procedural form distinct from the Adhyaya's prior nine Brahmanas. The Adhyaya has worked through aphoristic mantras (Part 1), parabolic dialogue (Part 2), *nirukti* etymologies (Parts 3, 5, 7), substantive identifications with chained equations (Parts 4, 5, 6, 9), the *upāsana* meditation-imperative (Part 8), and the dying-state observation (Parts 5, 9). The Tenth Brahmana introduces a substantive post-mortem journey description: a narrative of the person's ascent after death through three cosmic stages to the eternal world. The form is distinct from any prior Brahmana of the Adhyaya, and it picks up directly from the dying-moment of the Ninth Brahmana (*When he is on the point of departing this life, he does not hear that noise*) by naming what follows the moment of death.

The substance of the Brahmana is held in a single sentence at `5.1210`, with three substantive iterations and a closing destination-clause:

1. **Departure**: *When the person goes away from this world, he comes to the wind.* The substantive subject is *the person* (*purusha*) who has departed this life; the first stage of the post-mortem journey is the wind (*vāyu*).
2. **First "hole" simile**: *Then the wind makes room for him, like the hole of a carriage wheel, and through it he mounts higher.* The substantive procedure of the journey is given through a "hole" simile — the wind opens a passage like the hole of a carriage wheel — and the person mounts higher through the opening.
3. **Second stage**: *He comes to the sun. Then the sun makes room for him, like the hole of a Lambara, and through it he mounts higher.* The same form is given for the sun (*āditya*), with a *Lambara* (a kind of drum) as the "hole" simile. Müller's footnote `[*2]` glosses *Lambara*.
4. **Third stage**: *He comes to the moon. Then the moon makes room for him, like the hole of a drum, and through it he mounts higher.* The same form is given for the moon (*candramas*), with a drum as the "hole" simile.
5. **Destination**: *and arrives at the world where there is no sorrow, no snow. There he dwells eternal years.* The substantive destination is named by negation (no sorrow, no snow — Müller's footnote `[*3]` glosses *no snow*) and by substantive duration (*eternal years*).

The form holds three substantive iterations of the same shape — *He comes to X. Then X makes room for him, like the hole of Y, and through it he mounts higher* — with only X (the cosmic stage) and Y (the simile-object) substituted at each iteration. The three "hole" similes — the hole of a carriage wheel, the hole of a Lambara, the hole of a drum — are all named after artifacts with substantive holes through which something passes; the substantive procedure of the journey is the substantive procedure of passing through artifact-shaped openings in the cosmic medium. The closing destination — *the world where there is no sorrow, no snow* — is named in negation, and the substantive duration is *eternal years* — a substantive promise of permanence that closes the Brahmana.

The Brahmana ends inside the destination-clause. No closing seal beyond *There he dwells eternal years*; no narrator's voice; no *held his peace* form. The substantive content is complete.

## Context

The Brihadaranyaka Upanishad is one of the two earliest of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads, sharing that position with the Chandogya. It belongs to the Shukla Yajurveda branch of the Vedic corpus and forms the closing portion of the Shatapatha Brahmana — the *Aranyaka* in its name marks it as a forest-text recited at the late stages of Vedic study. Modern scholarship places the Brihadaranyaka in roughly the eighth to sixth century BCE, prior to the Buddha; the text is anonymous, and the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship. The text is the longest of the principal Upanishads and is typically structured in six *adhyayas* (chapters), each containing several *brahmanas* (sections).

Book 5, Part 10 holds three of the Adhyaya's distinctive features in concentrated form. The first is the post-mortem journey form. The Brihadaranyaka's earlier Brahmanas had given the post-mortem journey at extended length: the Third Brahmana of the Third Adhyaya holds Yajnavalkya's answer to Bhugyu's Parikshita-question with the cosmic-geographical answer of the route by Indra-as-bird and Vayu-as-conveyor; the Fourth Brahmana of the Fourth Adhyaya holds the karma-and-rebirth doctrine with the two paths of the desirer (who returns) and the non-desirer (who *being Brahman, goes to Brahman*). The Tenth Brahmana of the Fifth Adhyaya gives the post-mortem journey in radically compressed form: a three-stage ascent through wind, sun, and moon with three "hole" similes, closing on a substantive destination of no-sorrow-no-snow and eternal years. The form holds the same substantive doctrine — the post-mortem journey of the person — across radically different scales, with the present Brahmana giving the most compact instance.

The second is the *Lambara* substantive name. The substantive name *Lambara* — a kind of large drum used in Vedic ritual percussion — is one of the early Sanskrit references to the instrument, and it sits inside the Brahmana as the substantive simile for the sun's "hole." Müller's footnote `[*2]` glosses the substantive content of the term and is the principal note on the second of the three "hole" similes. The substantive choice of three artifact-shaped openings (carriage wheel, Lambara, drum) gives the Brahmana its distinctive substantive form: the cosmic stages of the post-mortem journey are given through substantive everyday-and-ritual objects with holes in them, and the substantive procedure is the procedure of passage through the openings.

The third is the closing destination. *The world where there is no sorrow, no snow. There he dwells eternal years.* The substantive destination is named by two negations (no sorrow, no snow) and one substantive duration (eternal years). The negation-of-snow is one of the Upanishadic literature's distinct substantive geographic-ethical figures: the absence of *snow* (*hima*) names the substantive absence of cold-and-suffering rather than weather, and Müller's footnote `[*3]` glosses the substantive content. The substantive duration *eternal years* — *śāśvatī samāḥ* in the underlying Sanskrit — is one of the principal Upanishadic substantive promises of permanence, and it sits at the close of the Brahmana as the substantive seal.

The closing form is the Adhyaya's standard compact close. *There he dwells eternal years.* No closing seal beyond the substantive duration; no narrator's voice. The Brahmana ends inside the substantive content.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the three iterations of the journey-form should be read at full length rather than collapsed into a single statement. The Brahmana works the three-fold ascent through verbatim repetition of the same shape — *He comes to X. Then X makes room for him, like the hole of Y, and through it he mounts higher* — with only X (the cosmic stage) and Y (the simile-object) substituted. Reading the three iterations alongside each other rather than summarized as *he ascends through wind, sun, and moon* recovers the substantive procedure of the journey: the person passes through three artifact-shaped openings in the cosmic medium, with each "hole" simile holding the substantive procedure of passage.

Second, the destination should be preserved through its substantive negations rather than smoothed into a generic *paradise* or *heaven*. *The world where there is no sorrow, no snow* names the substantive destination through its substantive absences — *no sorrow* (*aśoka*) and *no snow* (*ahima*) — and the substantive duration *eternal years* names the substantive permanence rather than the substantive happiness. The substantive form holds the post-mortem destination as the substantive absence-of-suffering-and-cold rather than as the substantive presence-of-something. Reading the destination with the negations preserved recovers the Brahmana's substantive form: the destination is named by what is not there rather than by what is there.

Book 5, Part 10 of the Brihadaranyaka — the entity here — occupies passages `5.1209` and `5.1210`. Passage `5.1209` is the "Tenth Brahmana" header without footnote. Passage `5.1210` carries the body in Müller's prose presentation across one short passage with the verse-numbered marker at `1.` (`5.1210`).

The Brahmana contains a small set of boundary features that should be preserved honestly:

- **Single-passage body.** The Brahmana's entire substantive content sits on one passage, `5.1210`. No multi-passage span, no internal verse-divisions beyond the verse-1 marker, no narrative interruptions. The form matches Parts 3, 6, 7, 8, and 9 of the Adhyaya as among the most compact in the Brihadaranyaka.
- **Footnote numbering irregularity.** Passage `5.1210` carries footnote `[*2]` on *Lambara* (the substantive name of the second "hole" simile-object) and footnote `[*3]` on *no snow* (the substantive negation of the destination). No footnote `[*1]` is given on any passage of the Brahmana. The numbering irregularity continues the Adhyaya's pattern in which Müller's footnote-sequence reflects the Adhyaya-level numbering rather than within each Brahmana; the substance of the verse is unaffected.
- **Three-fold journey form with verbatim repetition.** The verse-1 body holds three substantive iterations of the journey-form on one continuous sentence. Each iteration uses the same shape *He comes to X. Then X makes room for him, like the hole of Y, and through it he mounts higher*, with X (wind, sun, moon) and Y (carriage wheel, Lambara, drum) substituted at each iteration. The three iterations sit on the same passage without internal verse-numbering or paragraph-break.
- **Three substantive simile-objects.** The three "hole" similes — *the hole of a carriage wheel*, *the hole of a Lambara*, *the hole of a drum* — are given in Müller's English with the substantive Sanskrit *Lambara* preserved verbatim in transliteration, with footnote `[*2]` glossing the term. The first and third similes (carriage wheel and drum) are translated; the second (Lambara) is left in transliteration.
- **Closing destination by negation.** The Brahmana closes with the substantive destination given through two negations — *no sorrow, no snow* — and one substantive duration — *eternal years*. Müller's footnote `[*3]` attaches at *no snow* and is the second principal note in the Brahmana.

Passage `5.1211` is the "Eleventh Brahmana" header that opens Book 5, Part 11 of the Brihadaranyaka and is not part of this entity. The library chapter continues past this entity into the rest of the Fifth Adhyaya and the remaining portions of the volume; the Brihadaranyaka extends well beyond the boundaries of library chapter 5.
