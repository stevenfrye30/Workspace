# Taittiriyaka Upanishad, Book 1, Part 7

Book 1, Part 7 of the Taittiriyaka Upanishad — the Seventh Anuvaka of the Sikshavalli — is one of the most compact units of the Valli: a lesson made entirely of fivefold lists. The first verse holds three fivefolds on the side of *bhūta* (material objects). The earth, the sky, heaven, the four quarters, and the intermediate quarters — the regions. Agni (fire), Vayu (air), Aditya (sun), Chandramas (moon), and the stars — the lights. Water, herbs, trees, ether, and the *virāj* (the universal Self) — the ground. *So much*, the verse closes, *with reference to material objects.* The Anuvaka then turns to the side of *ātman* (the self, the body), and gives three more fivefolds. *Prāṇa*, *apāna*, *vyāna*, *udāna*, and *samāna* — the five breathings. The eye, the ear, the mind, speech, and touch — the senses. The skin, the flesh, the muscle, the bone, and the marrow — the bodily layers. The closing line is the lesson the patterning is for: by means of the one fivefold set — the set that refers to the body — the other fivefold set is completed.

## Context

The Taittiriyaka Upanishad is one of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads — a small group of texts traditionally received as the philosophical core of the late Vedic literature. It belongs to the Krishna Yajurveda branch of the Vedic corpus and to the Taittiriya school of that branch; the Upanishad sits inside the Taittiriya Aranyaka. Modern scholarship places it among the older prose Upanishads, in roughly the sixth to fifth century BCE — earlier than the verse Upanishads of the Katha and Mundaka. The text is anonymous; the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship.

Book 1, Part 7 sits inside the Sikshavalli, the chapter on *śikṣā*. The Anuvaka returns to the procedure the Third Anuvaka opened — paired correspondence by parallel listing — and gives one of the most condensed examples of it. Where the Third Anuvaka taught the *samhita* under five paired heads, and the Fifth and Sixth Anuvakas taught the *vyāhṛti* across four parallel fields and through the body, the Seventh Anuvaka holds three pairs of fivefold sets — the regions and the breathings, the lights and the senses, the ground and the layers — and closes with the rule that lets the student carry one side from the other. The Anuvaka is short; the lesson it gives is the school's procedure in compressed form.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the Anuvaka is built almost entirely of lists. There are no figures, no contemplative imagery, no narrative — three fivefolds outside, three fivefolds inside, and the rule that lets the inside complete the outside. Reading the Anuvaka with attention to the list-form alone, allowing the parallelism between cosmos and body to register without forcing it into a single doctrine, recovers the form the Sikshavalli has when it is most compact.

Second, the Anuvaka treats memorized correspondence as a method rather than an end. The closing line — that by the bodily fivefold the cosmic fivefold is completed — does not say that the body is the cosmos or that the cosmos is the body. It says that the student who has the inside set in order can use it to recover the outside set: the body is the way to the regions, the breathings to the lights, the senses to the ground. Reading the Anuvaka as an instruction in how to carry the cosmic ordering by carrying the bodily ordering, rather than as a metaphysical claim about correspondence, keeps the form the Sikshavalli is teaching.

## The text

The text is held in the Archive library under `upanishads-muller-part2`, chapter 5 (Müller translation, 1884 — *The Sacred Books of the East*, Volume XV; via SacredTexts.com). Library chapter 5 is shared across sixteen Archive entities — four parts of the Katha Upanishad, the six parts of the Mundaka Upanishad, and Books 1, Parts 1 through 6 of the Taittiriyaka — and continues here into the Seventh Anuvaka without break in the chapter. Book 1, Part 7 of the Taittiriyaka — the entity here — occupies passages `5.202` through `5.205`. Passage `5.202` is the "Seventh Anuvaka" header. Passage `5.203` carries the body of verse 1 with the verse marker `1.` — the three outward fivefolds and the closing phrase *so much with reference to material objects*. Passage `5.204` continues verse 1 across the outward/inward boundary into the three inward fivefolds (breathings, senses, layers). Passage `5.205` opens with the editorial sub-marker `(1)` carried over from the source — Müller's bracketed grouping that signals the closing summary line ("By means of the one fivefold set he completes the other fivefold set") as a continuation of verse 1 rather than as a separate verse. Passage `5.206` is the "Eighth Anuvaka" header that opens Book 1, Part 8 of the Taittiriyaka and is not part of this entity. The library chapter continues past this entity into the rest of the Taittiriyaka and the remaining Upanishads of the volume. About two minutes' reading.

## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Taittiriyaka Upanishad, Book 1, Part 6 — paired immediately consecutive Anuvakas of the Sikshavalli; the Sixth Anuvaka traced the four sacred interjections along an inward path through the body of the speaker, the Seventh Anuvaka returns from the contemplative tracing to the parallel-listing procedure of the earlier anuvakas, holding the same outward-and-inward structure now as a paired catalogue rather than a contemplative ascent — two adjacent registrations of the same Sikshavalli procedure.
- **structural-parallel** → Mundaka Upanishad, Book 2, Part 1 — paired Sanskrit passages on the parallel between cosmic body and bodily organs; the Mundaka's First Khanda of the Second Mundaka holds the cosmic Person whose head is the sky, whose eyes are the sun and the moon, whose ears are the quarters, whose speech is the Vedas disclosed, whose breath is the wind, whose heart is the universe (Mundaka 2.1.4), the Taittiriyaka's Seventh Anuvaka holds the closely related correspondence as paired fivefold lists — the regions, the lights, and the ground on the outward side; the breathings, the senses, and the layers on the inward side. Both texts hold the same recognition that the cosmos and the body are arrayed in the same order, the Mundaka in a single figure, the Taittiriyaka in a paired catalogue.
- **structural-parallel** → Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 4 (Jnana Karma Sanyasa Yoga) — paired Sanskrit passages on the disciplined memorized order by which the teaching is held; the Gita's Chapter 4 develops the long catalogue of *yajñas* — material, breath, sense-restraint, study, austerity, knowledge — by which the recipient is prepared for the teaching, the Taittiriyaka's Seventh Anuvaka gives the same care for memorized order in the form of the six paired fivefolds, with the closing rule that the inside set completes the outside set. Both texts trust the order of the lesson to carry the lesson's content.
