## Context

The Brihadaranyaka Upanishad is one of the two earliest of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads, sharing that position with the Chandogya. It belongs to the Shukla Yajurveda branch of the Vedic corpus and forms the closing portion of the Shatapatha Brahmana — the *Aranyaka* in its name marks it as a forest-text recited at the late stages of Vedic study. Modern scholarship places the Brihadaranyaka in roughly the eighth to sixth century BCE, prior to the Buddha; the text is anonymous, and the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship. The text is the longest of the principal Upanishads and is typically structured in six *adhyayas* (chapters), each containing several *brahmanas* (sections).

Book 6, Part 4 holds three of the Brihadaranyaka's most distinctive features in concentrated form. The first is the substantive sexual-rite-as-Vajapeya-sacrifice identification at verse 3. The substantive doctrine that the substantive woman's body is substantive sacrificial altar — *Her lap is a sacrificial altar; her hairs, the sacrificial grass; her skin, the soma-press. The two labia of the vulva are the fire in the middle* — and that substantive sexual intercourse with the substantive knowledge of the substantive identification is substantively equivalent to the substantive Vajapeya sacrifice is one of the substantive most-cited substantive instances of the Vedic-Upanishadic tradition's substantive identification of substantive sexual rite with substantive ritual sacrifice. The substantive doctrine is preserved across the substantive cross-cultural Vedic literature (Atharvaveda 14, Aitareya Brahmana 8.27, Chandogya Upanishad 5.8) and forms the substantive textual ground of the substantive *garbhādhāna* sacrament that the substantive Hindu *grihya-sūtra* literature works as the substantive first of the substantive *samskāras* (life-cycle rites).

The second is the substantive Müller-redaction-with-Hume-supplement structure. The substantive Brahmana is one of the substantive few substantive instances in Müller's Sacred Books of the East series where the substantive translator declined to render substantive content into English. The substantive consequence is that the substantive digital archive holds substantive editorial apparatus — the substantive *Note by Redactor* at `5.1381`–`5.1382` and the substantive complete Hume translation at chapter 6 of the source JSON — as substantive supplementary content to compensate for Müller's substantive Victorian-modesty redaction. The substantive form is one of the substantive clearest substantive instances of substantive editorial-tradition history visible in the substantive digital source: the substantive Müller text and the substantive Hume text are preserved alongside each other, with the substantive *Note by Redactor* explicitly naming the substantive redaction-cause. Reading the substantive Brahmana with both translations held alongside each other recovers the substantive form the substantive textual transmission has when substantive translator-decisions become substantive part of the substantive text's substantive history.

The third is the substantive specific-child-begetting rituals at verses 14–18. The substantive five-fold parallel substantive procedure for begetting children of substantive specific qualities (white son knowing one Veda; reddish son with tawny eyes knowing two Vedas; dark son with red eyes knowing three Vedas; learned daughter; learned son knowing all Vedas) is the substantive earliest substantive Indian source for the substantive procreation-ritual tradition that holds substantive ingredient-correspondences with substantive desired offspring. The substantive doctrine that substantive boiled rice with substantive specific dairy-and-meat ingredients substantively conditions the substantive child's substantive complexion, substantive number-of-Vedas-mastered, and substantive longevity is the substantive first substantive instance of the substantive *garbhopanishad* / *garbhādhāna* dietary-prescription tradition that the substantive later Hindu medical-and-ritual literature works at length.

The closing form is the substantive *jātakarma* (birth-ritual) at verses 24–28. The substantive form holds substantive birth-ritual through substantive *prishadagya*-offering, substantive whisper-of-Speech-three-times, substantive feeding-with-gold-ladle, substantive secret-name-as-Veda, substantive Sarasvati-breast-prayer, and substantive Ila-Maitravaruni-mother-address. The substantive form is the substantive Brihadaranyaka's substantive earliest extant substantive *jātakarma*-procedure and forms the substantive textual ground of the substantive Hindu birth-ritual sacrament.
