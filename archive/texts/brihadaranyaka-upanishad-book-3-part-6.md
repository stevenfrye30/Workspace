# Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 3, Part 6

Book 3, Part 6 of the Brihadaranyaka Upanishad — the Sixth Brahmana of the Third Adhyaya — continues the Janaka Vaideha contest opened in the First Brahmana and continued through the Second, Third, Fourth, and Fifth. The court frame is not reset. The Fifth Brahmana closed on silence — *After that Kahola Kaushitakeya held his peace* — and the Sixth opens directly into the next challenger: *Then Gargi Vakaknavi asked. 'Yagnavalkya,' she said.* The narrator's pronoun is the first signal of the Brahmana's break with the prior pattern. Asvala, Artabhaga, Bhugyu, Ushasta, and Kahola had each been introduced with the masculine *he said*; Gargi Vakaknavi is the sixth challenger and the first to be introduced with *she said*. The contest's procedure persists across the Brahmana boundary, and the named participant who carries it across this boundary is a woman.

The form of the Brahmana also breaks. Where the Fourth and Fifth Brahmanas had given a single sustained question with a formulaic reply, a follow-up, and a substantive answer, the Sixth Brahmana gives a single-formula ladder. *X is woven, like warp and woof, in Y. What then is that in which Y is woven, like warp and woof?* The formula is held verbatim across each step of the ladder, with the variable X moving up by one level at every iteration; Yajnavalkya's reply gives one short clause at each step, naming the next support — *In air, O Gargi*; *In the worlds of the sky, O Gargi*; *In the worlds of the Gandharvas, O Gargi* — without elaboration. The form is rapid-fire, compressed, and almost entirely repetition; the substance is the cosmic ladder.

Gargi opens at water. *Everything here is woven, like warp and woof, in water. What then is that in which water is woven, like warp and woof?* Yajnavalkya answers *In air*. Gargi asks the same form-question of air; he answers *In the worlds of the sky*. The ladder ascends across eleven steps. The named supports, in order of their appearance: water (the opening); then **air**, **the worlds of the sky**, **the worlds of the Gandharvas**, **the worlds of Aditya (sun)**, **the worlds of Kandra (moon)**, **the worlds of the Nakshatras (stars)**, **the worlds of the Devas (gods)**, **the worlds of Indra**, **the worlds of Pragapati**, and **the worlds of Brahman**. The cascade walks the cosmic order from the watery foundation through the atmospheric, celestial, divine, and creator-worlds, and rests at the worlds of Brahman.

When Gargi asks for what supports the worlds of Brahman — *In what then are the worlds of Brahman woven, like warp and woof?* — the form of the dialogue breaks. Yajnavalkya does not answer the question with the next support. He interrupts the ladder. *O Gargi, do not ask too much, lest thy head should fall off. Thou askest too much about a deity about which we are not to ask too much. Do not ask too much, O Gargi.* The triple repetition of *do not ask too much* is the form of the warning. The threat — that Gargi's head will fall off — is the form of the prohibition. The narrator's line then closes the Brahmana: *After that Gargi Vakaknavi held her peace.* The challenger's silence is, for the first time in the contest, induced by the priest's intervention rather than chosen by the challenger after an answer has been given. The closing line preserves the *held peace* form of the prior Brahmanas with the feminine pronoun substituted; the substance of the closing — silence at the request of Yajnavalkya — is new to the contest.

## Context

The Brihadaranyaka Upanishad is one of the two earliest of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads, sharing that position with the Chandogya. It belongs to the Shukla Yajurveda branch of the Vedic corpus and forms the closing portion of the Shatapatha Brahmana — the *Aranyaka* in its name marks it as a forest-text recited at the late stages of Vedic study. Modern scholarship places the Brihadaranyaka in roughly the eighth to sixth century BCE, prior to the Buddha; the text is anonymous, and the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship. The text is the longest of the principal Upanishads and is typically structured in six *adhyayas* (chapters), each containing several *brahmanas* (sections).

Book 3, Part 6 holds three of the most distinctive features of the contest in concentrated form. The first is the introduction of Gargi Vakaknavi. Gargi is one of the named women of the principal Upanishads — the others being Maitreyi, Yajnavalkya's wife in the Fourth Brahmana of the Second Adhyaya — and the form of her presence in the Brihadaranyaka is the public-contest form of the *brahmodya*, not the domestic-conversation form. Gargi will return to the contest in the Eighth Brahmana of the Third Adhyaya with two more questions, and her two appearances together form one of the most-cited sequences of the Upanishadic literature on the form of the doctrine of Brahman. The Sixth Brahmana is her first, and her stepping forward as the sixth challenger after five male Brahmanas places her inside the same procedure of the contest with no narrator's interruption distinguishing her appearance from the prior five.

The second is the warp-and-woof ladder. The form *X is woven, like warp and woof, in Y* is a textile figure — the warp and the woof are the two perpendicular sets of threads of the woven cloth, with the warp held under tension and the woof passed across — and the figure carries the question of *what supports what* through the structure of weaving. Each level of the cosmic ladder is the cloth woven on the level above; the level above is the loom on which the level below is woven. The ladder ascends through eleven steps, and the eleven supports are given in the form of *worlds of X* — worlds of the sky, of the Gandharvas, of Aditya, of Kandra, of the Nakshatras, of the Devas, of Indra, of Pragapati, of Brahman. The two opening supports — air, and water (the support held by the question itself) — are not given as *worlds of*; the next nine ascend in the *worlds of* form. The ladder rests at the worlds of Brahman.

The third is the prohibition. *Do not ask too much, lest thy head should fall off.* The Brahmana refuses the next step of the ladder. The silencing is not a refusal of Gargi specifically — Müller's footnote at the warning indicates that the prohibition holds for the deity asked about, not for the asker — but a refusal of the next question in the form Gargi has given. The contest's procedure has answered every question put to Yajnavalkya since the First Brahmana with a substantive reply; the Sixth Brahmana is the first in which Yajnavalkya stops the procedure with a refusal. The form of the silencing — three repetitions of *do not ask too much* and the threat of decapitation — is preserved in Müller's translation in compact prose, and the form is one of the contest's most-cited literary features.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the Brahmana's repetition is verbatim, not approximate. The formula *X is woven, like warp and woof, in Y. What then is that in which Y is woven, like warp and woof?* is held with the same wording across each level of the ladder, and Yajnavalkya's reply *In Y, O Gargi* is given in the same compressed clause each time. Reading the ladder with the eleven repetitions kept exactly as the source has them — neither smoothed away nor abbreviated into *and so on through the worlds* — recovers the form the Brahmana has when its substance is a chain of supports given through verbatim restatement of a single textile figure. The form the contest has in this Brahmana is the form of the rapid-fire ladder, and the ladder is held at full length.

Second, the closing is induced, not voluntary. The First, Third, Fourth, and Fifth Brahmanas closed on the named challenger holding his peace after a substantive answer had been given. The Sixth Brahmana closes on the named challenger holding her peace after the priest has stopped her with a refusal and a threat. The contest's silence-closing form is preserved exactly — *held her peace* — but the procedure that produces the silence is altered. Reading the closing with attention to the difference between voluntary silence after a substantive answer and induced silence after a refusal recovers the literary procedure of the Brahmana: the Brahmana works the same closing form across a different procedure of dialogue, and the difference is held inside the same surface phrasing.

## The text

The text is held in the Archive library under `upanishads-muller-part2`, chapter 5 (Müller translation, 1884 — *The Sacred Books of the East*, Volume XV; via SacredTexts.com). Library chapter 5 is shared across fifty-five Archive entities — four parts of the Katha Upanishad, the six parts of the Mundaka Upanishad, twenty-eight anuvakas of the Taittiriyaka, the eleven Brahmanas of the Brihadaranyaka's First and Second Adhyayas, and the First, Second, Third, Fourth, and Fifth Brahmanas of the Third Adhyaya — and continues here into the Sixth Brahmana. Book 3, Part 6 of the Brihadaranyaka — the entity here — occupies passages `5.720` through `5.742`. Passage `5.720` is the "Sixth Brahmana" header carrying its own footnote reference `[*3]`. Passages `5.721` through `5.742` carry the body in Müller's prose presentation across twenty-two short passages, with the verse-numbered marker at `1.` (`5.721`) only. The rest of the dialogue is unnumbered.

The Brahmana contains several boundary features that should be preserved honestly:

- **Verse-1 single-passage opening with feminine pronoun.** Passage `5.721` carries Gargi's opening — *Then Gargi Vakaknavi asked. 'Yagnavalkya,' she said* — and the first iteration of the warp-and-woof ladder on a single passage without internal splits. The narrator's *she said* is the first feminine pronoun in the Adhyaya's challenger-introductions, and it sits inside the verse-1 passage rather than on a separate narrator's line.
- **Eleven-step ladder across `5.722` through `5.741`.** Twenty short passages carry the ladder's reply-and-question pairs in alternation: each odd-numbered passage in the run carries a reply (*In air, O Gargi*; *In the worlds of the sky, O Gargi*; etc.), and each even-numbered passage carries Gargi's next question in the verbatim form *In what then is X woven, like warp and woof?* No verse-numbered markers appear across these twenty passages. The ladder's eleven supports — air, the worlds of the sky, of the Gandharvas, of Aditya (sun), of Kandra (moon), of the Nakshatras (stars), of the Devas (gods), of Indra, of Pragapati, of Brahman — are given each on its own short reply-passage.
- **OCR irregularity at `5.732`.** Passage `5.732` opens with a leading comma — *", In the worlds of the Nakshatras (stars), O Gargi,' he replied."* — preserved as found in the digitized source. The leading comma is a transcription artifact carried over from Müller's volume; the substance of the passage is correctly the seventh of the ladder's eleven replies. Müller's prose continues the formula at this passage in the same form as the prior replies; the leading-comma irregularity does not alter the content.
- **Variant transliteration at `5.730`.** Passage `5.730` carries the reply *In the worlds of Kandra (moon), O Gargi*. Müller's transliteration of the Sanskrit name for the moon — *Kandra* — is the form he uses across his Sacred Books of the East volumes; the reading note at `5.732` corresponds to the same moon-and-stars compound the digital edition uses elsewhere as *Kandra-tarakam*.
- **Closing interruption at `5.742` with footnote.** The closing passage carries Yajnavalkya's full warning — *O Gargi, Do not ask too much, lest thy head should fall off. Thou askest too much about a deity about which we are not to ask too much. Do not ask too much, O Gargi* — with footnote `[*1]` on the line *about which we are not to ask too much*. Müller's note glosses the prohibition as a doctrinal limit on the form of the question, not on the asker. The narrator's closing line *After that Gargi Vakaknavi held her peace* sits on the same passage, in the compression seen in the closing passages of the Third, Fourth, and Fifth Brahmanas. The closing pronoun — *her peace* — is preserved as the feminine inflection of the same *held his peace* / *held her peace* form the contest's silence-closings have used since the First Brahmana.

Passage `5.743` is the "Seventh Brahmana" header that opens Book 3, Part 7 of the Brihadaranyaka and is not part of this entity. The library chapter continues past this entity into the rest of the Third Adhyaya and the remaining portions of the volume; the Brihadaranyaka extends well beyond the boundaries of library chapter 5. About four minutes' reading.

## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 3, Part 5 — paired immediate-predecessor Brahmanas of the Janaka Vaideha contest with the same closing form altered by one feature; the Fifth Brahmana closed on Kahola Kaushitakeya holding his peace after Yajnavalkya's substantive answer on the form of life of the renouncing Brahmana, the Sixth Brahmana closes on Gargi Vakaknavi holding her peace after Yajnavalkya's threat that her head will fall off if she asks one more question. The contest's silence-closing form is preserved exactly across the Brahmana boundary; the procedure that produces the silence is altered — voluntary silence after a substantive answer in the Fifth, induced silence after a refusal in the Sixth — and the change of pronoun (*his* / *her*) marks the entry of the first female challenger into the contest.
- **structural-parallel** → Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 2, Part 4 — paired Brihadaranyaka Yajnavalkya dialogues with named female interlocutors; the Fourth Brahmana of the Second Adhyaya holds the domestic Yajnavalkya-Maitreyi conversation at the moment of Yajnavalkya's departure for the forest, with the closing question *How, O beloved, should he know himself, the Knower?* — the Sixth Brahmana of the Third Adhyaya holds Gargi Vakaknavi at Janaka Vaideha's *aśvamedha* asking the warp-and-woof question across the cosmic ladder until Yajnavalkya stops her at the worlds of Brahman. Yajnavalkya appears across both — domestic teacher of his wife, public answerer of his sixth challenger — with named women as his interlocutors in opposite social settings.
- **structural-parallel** → Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 3, Part 1 — paired Brahmanas of the Janaka Vaideha contest working their substance through a verbatim repeated formula; the First Brahmana opened the contest with Asvala the Hotri priest holding the substitutional formula *Yagnavalkya, everything here is reached by X, everything is overcome by X. By what means then is the sacrificer freed beyond the reach of X?* across four substitutions on freedom, the Sixth Brahmana works through the warp-and-woof formula *X is woven, like warp and woof, in Y. What then is that in which Y is woven, like warp and woof?* across eleven substitutions on cosmic support. Both Brahmanas hold the contest's procedure through verbatim formula-repetition with one variable substituted at each iteration; the difference between substitution-on-freedom and ascent-on-support is the difference between the contest's first formal challenger and its sixth.
