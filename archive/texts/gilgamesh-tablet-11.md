# Epic of Gilgamesh, Tablet XI (The Flood)

In the eleventh tablet of the Standard Babylonian *Epic of Gilgamesh*, the long-lived Utnapishtim recounts to Gilgamesh how he and his family alone survived a worldwide flood. The story, ancient by the time it was set down, is the Mesopotamian counterpart of the Genesis flood narrative — older, partly cognate, and from a different theological world. Tablet XI is read both as the climax of Gilgamesh's quest for immortality and as the most direct point of contact between Mesopotamian literature and the Hebrew Bible.

## Context

The Standard Babylonian recension was assembled in Akkadian around 1300–1000 BCE, drawing on older Sumerian and Old Babylonian material — particularly the *Atrahasis* epic and an earlier flood story attached to the figure of Ziusudra. The fullest surviving copies come from the seventh-century BCE library of Ashurbanipal at Nineveh, which the Assyriologist George Smith examined in the British Museum. Smith's 1872 announcement that he had read a Babylonian flood story closely paralleling Genesis was one of the foundational moments of modern comparative scholarship.

Tablet XI is part of a longer epic. Eleven tablets carry the story: Gilgamesh's friendship with the wild man Enkidu, their exploits against Humbaba and the Bull of Heaven, Enkidu's death, and Gilgamesh's failed search for the immortality that Enkidu's death has made unbearable. The flood narrative is told by the one human who has crossed that boundary — Utnapishtim, granted eternal life after surviving the deluge — to the king who has come to ask him how it was done.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, Tablet XI is best read on its own terms before being read alongside Genesis. The cosmology is not Israelite. The gods quarrel; the flood is a council decision; humanity is preserved not by moral reckoning but by the god Ea warning Utnapishtim because the destruction has been over-decided. Reading the tablet first as Mesopotamian, second as a Genesis cognate, produces a clearer view than the reverse.

Second, the cognate elements are concrete and structural — the boat's dimensions and pitching, the release of birds (a dove, a swallow, a raven) to test for dry land, the post-flood sacrifice and its smoke that the gods smell. These sit inside a different theological frame. The text is sharpest where the structures match and the conclusions diverge.

## The text

The Archive library holds two partial sources. George Smith's 1876 *Chaldean Account of Genesis* contains, in chapter 3, Smith's pioneering English translation of the deluge tablets — set among related Mesopotamian material rather than presented as a clean Tablet XI. Stephen Langdon's 1917 *Epic of Gilgamesh* covers Old Babylonian fragments, not the Standard Babylonian recension that contains Tablet XI as we now read it. For Tablet XI as a clean reconstructed unit, Andrew George's 2003 Oxford edition is the recommended modern reference; Tablet XI itself is roughly 325 lines, about an hour's reading.
