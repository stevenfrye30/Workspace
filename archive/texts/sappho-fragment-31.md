# Sappho, Fragment 31

Fragment 31 is one of the best-preserved poems attributed to Sappho of Lesbos. Sixteen lines survive in four stanzas of what was likely a slightly longer poem; the seventeenth line breaks off mid-word. The speaker, in the first person, describes a man seated near a woman she loves and the bodily responses that come over her as she watches: heart shuddering, voice failing, tongue broken, a fire under the skin, dimmed sight, ringing ears, sweat, trembling, paleness "greener than grass." The poem proceeds as accumulation — perception, then symptom, then symptom — in compressed first-person speech.

## Context

Sappho was an archaic Greek poet, active around the late seventh and early sixth centuries BCE on the island of Lesbos in the eastern Aegean. She composed lyric poetry in the local Aeolic dialect, set to musical accompaniment — likely the lyre — and her work was widely known across the Greek world by the fifth century BCE. Ancient sources credit her with nine books of poems collected by Alexandrian scholars in the third century BCE; almost none of that body has survived intact. One nearly complete poem (Fragment 1, the *Hymn to Aphrodite*) and a small number of substantial fragments are the basis of the surviving corpus.

Fragment 31 survives because the first-century treatise *On the Sublime* — long attributed to a writer called Longinus — quotes the four stanzas in full to illustrate Sappho's selection of bodily detail. The poem has not been recovered on papyrus; the citation in *On the Sublime* is its substantial surviving source. The fragment is conventionally numbered 31 in both Edgar Lobel and Denys Page's edition (1955) and Eva-Maria Voigt's standard 1971 edition of Sappho and Alcaeus.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the poem proceeds by accumulation rather than narration. After the opening that fixes the scene — the man seated near the woman, the speaker watching — the lines begin to list: heart, voice, tongue, fire under the skin, sight, hearing, sweat, trembling, color. There is no event between the symptoms, only the order in which they arrive. Reading the poem with attention to the pacing of the list, rather than skipping to its end, follows the form.

Second, the text we have breaks off at a turn. The four extant stanzas stay at the body. The seventeenth line — the surviving start of what was probably a fifth stanza — opens with "But all must be endured, since…" and stops. What followed is lost. Reading the poem with the awareness that the close we have is mid-utterance, not a designed ending, keeps the form the text has come down in.

## The text

Fragment 31 is not currently held in the Archive library. The library's Greek-shelf holdings are concentrated in the Iliad, the Odyssey, the Sophoclean tragedies, and selected Greek philosophy and historiography, and do not yet include a Sappho collection. The poem is short — sixteen surviving lines in four stanzas of Sapphic meter, with the start of a fifth — and is most reliably read in the parallel Greek-and-English text of David Campbell's Loeb edition or in Anne Carson's verse translation, listed below. Two to three minutes' reading.

## Resources

- **Standard Greek text.** Eva-Maria Voigt, ed., *Sappho et Alcaeus: Fragmenta* (Athenaeum, 1971) — the standard modern critical edition of Sappho and Alcaeus; supersedes Lobel-Page (1955) on most points of text.
- **Parallel Greek/English edition.** David A. Campbell, ed. and trans., *Greek Lyric I: Sappho and Alcaeus*, Loeb Classical Library 142 (Harvard University Press, 1982) — facing-page Greek and English; the field-standard parallel edition for English readers.
- **Recommended literary translation.** Anne Carson, *If Not, Winter: Fragments of Sappho* (Knopf, 2002) — verse translation with the Greek text on facing pages and brief notes; the standard contemporary literary translation.
- **Commentary.** G. O. Hutchinson, *Greek Lyric Poetry: A Commentary on Selected Larger Pieces* (Oxford University Press, 2001) — scholarly commentary in English including a chapter on Fragment 31.
