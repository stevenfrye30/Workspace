# Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 6, Part 1

Book 6, Part 1 of the Brihadaranyaka Upanishad — the First Brahmana of the Sixth Adhyaya — opens the Brihadaranyaka's final Adhyaya. The Sixth Adhyaya marks a substantial procedural shift from the rapidly compact Brahmanas of the Fifth Adhyaya. Where the Fifth had run fifteen Brahmanas across mostly two-or-three-passage compact units, the First Brahmana of the Sixth Adhyaya gives a substantial nineteen-passage composite teaching across two distinct procedural sections, and the substantive content returns to a more extended scale that the Brihadaranyaka had used in Books 3 and 4.

The Brahmana opens with the substantive recitation-marker *Harih, Om* at the open of verse 1. The marker is the first explicit *Harih, Om* (*hariḥ oṁ* in the underlying Sanskrit) opening-invocation of the Brihadaranyaka so far. The substantive form is the Vedic-recitation invocation that opens substantive Vedic texts and substantive recitation-units, and it sits at the open of the Sixth Adhyaya as the substantive recitation-frame of the entire Adhyaya.

The Brahmana's first section runs verses 1 through 6 across passages `5.1245` through `5.1251`. The substantive form is the parallel substantive identification of bodily faculties with substantive qualities. Each verse holds the same shape:

*He who knows the X, becomes himself the X among his people. Y is the X. He who knows this, becomes the X among his people, and among whomsoever he wishes to be so.*

The substantive form holds six substitutional pairs of substantive quality and substantive bodily faculty:

1. **Verse 1 at `5.1245`**: *first and best* = **Breath** (*prāṇa*). The substantive quality is the substantive priority/superiority; the substantive faculty is the substantive breath.
2. **Verse 2 at `5.1246`**: *richest* = **Speech** (*vāc*). Müller's footnote `[*2]` attaches at *richest*.
3. **Verse 3 at `5.1247`**: *firm rest* = **Eye**. The substantive form holds the substantive eye as the substantive ground of standing-firm-on-uneven-ground.
4. **Verse 4 at `5.1248`**: *success* = **Ear**. The substantive form runs the substantive doctrine that *in the ear are all these Vedas successful*.
5. **Verse 5 at `5.1249`–`5.1250`**: *home* = **Mind**. The verse is split mid-clause across two passages at *The mind / indeed is the home*.
6. **Verse 6 at `5.1251`**: *generation* = **Seed**. Müller's footnote `[*1]` attaches at *generation*. The substantive doctrine of the seed-as-generation closes the first section.

The Brahmana's second section runs verses 7 through 14 across passages `5.1252` through `5.1261`. The substantive form is the substantive narrative of the senses' quarrel — one of the Brihadaranyaka's most-cited substantive narratives, paired with the gods-and-Asuras contest of the Third Brahmana of the First Adhyaya (`upanishads-muller-part2`'s `brihadaranyaka-upanishad-book-1-part-3`).

**Verse 7 at `5.1252`** opens the narrative: *These Pranas (senses), when quarrelling together as to who was the best, went to Brahman and said: 'Who is the richest of us?' He replied: 'He by whose departure this body seems worst, he is the richest.'* The substantive form is the substantive deity-arbitration — Brahman is consulted to settle the quarrel, and Brahman gives the test: substantive priority is judged by the substantive worst-bodily-state when each sense departs. Müller's footnote `[*2]` attaches at *Brahman*.

**Verses 8 through 12** each give one sense's year-long departure and return. The substantive form is held verbatim across the five iterations: *The X (sense) departed, and having been absent for a year, it came back and said: 'How have you been able to live without me?' They replied: 'Like [substantive metaphor], not [substantive function]ing with X, but [substantive functions of all the others]. Thus we have lived.' Then the X entered in.*

The five iterations:
- **Verse 8 at `5.1253`** — Tongue (speech): *Like unto people, not speaking with the tongue, but breathing with breath, seeing with the eye, hearing with the ear, knowing with the mind, generating with seed.*
- **Verse 9 at `5.1254`** — Eye (sight): *Like blind people, not seeing with the eye, but breathing with the breath, speaking with the tongue, hearing with the ear, knowing with the mind, generating with seed.*
- **Verse 10 at `5.1255`–`5.1256`** — Ear (hearing), split mid-clause across two passages at *Like deaf people, not hearing with the ear, / but breathing with the breath, speaking with the tongue...*: *Like deaf people.*
- **Verse 11 at `5.1257`** — Mind: *Like fools, not knowing with their mind.*
- **Verse 12 at `5.1258`** — Seed: *Like impotent people, not generating with seed.*

The five substantive metaphors — *people / blind people / deaf people / fools / impotent people* — name the substantive defective-state that the body remains in despite the absence of each sense. The substantive doctrine is that the body lived through each absence; no single sense is the substantive priority.

**Verse 13 at `5.1259`** turns the narrative. *The (vital) breath, when on the point of departing, tore up these senses, as a great, excellent horse of the Sindhu country might tare up the pegs to which he is tethered.* The substantive simile of the *horse of the Sindhu country* (the substantive Vedic-region known for excellent horses) tearing up the *pegs to which he is tethered* names the substantive uprooting that breath performs at its substantive departure. The senses' substantive response: *Sir, do not depart. We shall not be able to live without thee.* The substantive contrast with verses 8–12 is the substantive absolute dependence: where the body lived through each sense's absence, the body cannot live without breath. Breath demands an offering: *Then make me an offering.* The senses agree.

**Verse 14 at `5.1260`–`5.1261`** holds the substantive offering. Each of the five senses gives up its substantive quality to breath in the same shape *If I am [the X], then thou art [the X] by it*. The five offerings:
- The tongue: *If I am the richest, then thou art the richest by it.*
- The eye: *If I am the firm rest, then thou art possessed of firm rest by it.*
- The ear: *If I am success, then thou art possessed of success by it.*
- The mind: *If I am the home, thou art the home by it.*
- The seed: *If I am generation, thou art possessed of generation by it.*

The substantive substantive qualities of the first section — richest, firm rest, success, home, generation — return here as the substantive offerings to breath. The substantive doctrine is that breath holds all five substantive qualities through the substantive offerings of the substantive senses.

Breath then asks: *What shall be food, what shall be dress for me?* The substantive reply at `5.1261`: *Whatever there is, even unto dogs, worms, insects, and birds, that is thy food, and water thy dress.* The substantive catalogue extends from substantive named animals — dogs, worms, insects, birds — through Müller's footnote `[*1]` glossing the substantive scope. The substantive doctrine of food-of-Ana (the breath) follows: *He who thus knows the food of Ana (the breath), by him nothing is eaten that is not (proper) food, nothing is received that is not (proper) food.* Müller's footnote `[*2]` glosses the substantive name *Ana* (one of the substantive names of the breath in the Vedic tradition). The substantive doctrine closes with the substantive Srotriya practice: *Srotriyas (Vedic theologians) who know this, rinse the mouth with water when they are going to eat, and rinse the mouth with water after they have eaten, thinking that thereby they make the breath dressed (with water).*

The Brahmana ends inside the Srotriya-rinsing doctrine. No closing seal beyond the substantive practice; no narrator's voice; no *held his peace* form. The substantive content of the Brahmana is the two sections together — the parallel identifications and the senses-quarrel narrative — closing on the substantive ritual practice.

## Context

The Brihadaranyaka Upanishad is one of the two earliest of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads, sharing that position with the Chandogya. It belongs to the Shukla Yajurveda branch of the Vedic corpus and forms the closing portion of the Shatapatha Brahmana — the *Aranyaka* in its name marks it as a forest-text recited at the late stages of Vedic study. Modern scholarship places the Brihadaranyaka in roughly the eighth to sixth century BCE, prior to the Buddha; the text is anonymous, and the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship. The text is the longest of the principal Upanishads and is typically structured in six *adhyayas* (chapters), each containing several *brahmanas* (sections).

Book 6, Part 1 holds three of the Brihadaranyaka's distinctive features in concentrated form. The first is the substantive *Harih, Om* recitation-marker. The Adhyaya's open at `5.1245` — *Harih, Om. He who knows the first and the best...* — names the substantive Vedic-recitation invocation that opens substantive Vedic texts and substantive Upanishadic compositions. The substantive *Harih, Om* form is rare in the Brihadaranyaka — the prior Adhyayas have not opened with this specific recitation-marker — and its appearance at the open of the Sixth Adhyaya signals the substantive recitation-frame of the Adhyaya as a whole.

The second is the substantive senses-quarrel narrative. The Brihadaranyaka holds the substantive narrative of the senses' quarrel and breath-as-supreme across two distinct loci: the Third Brahmana of the First Adhyaya (the gods-and-Asuras contest at the *udgītha*, where each sense is pierced with evil by the Asuras and overwhelmed, only the breath in the mouth — the *mukhya prāṇa* — scattering the Asuras like a ball of earth striking a stone), and the First Brahmana of the Sixth Adhyaya (the year-long departure-and-return of each sense, the breath-as-uprooter, the substantive offering of qualities). The two narratives hold the same substantive doctrine — breath is the substantive priority among the senses — through two distinct procedural shapes: the gods-and-Asuras combat-narrative in the First Adhyaya and the year-long-test-and-offering narrative in the Sixth Adhyaya. The substantive figures that recur across both narratives are the same six bodily faculties (speech, breath, eye, ear, mind, seed) and the substantive doctrine that breath is the substantive supreme.

The third is the substantive horse-of-the-Sindhu-country simile. The substantive simile *The (vital) breath, when on the point of departing, tore up these senses, as a great, excellent horse of the Sindhu country might tear up the pegs to which he is tethered* names breath's substantive priority through the substantive figure of the substantive Sindhu-region's horses. The substantive Vedic literature holds the substantive Sindhu-country horses as the substantive type of the most powerful horses, and the substantive simile gives breath the same substantive uprooting force that the substantive horse uses against its substantive tether-pegs. The substantive form closes the substantive year-long-test sequence and turns the substantive narrative into the substantive offering scene that follows.

The closing form is the Adhyaya's first substantive ritual-practice closing. The Brahmana ends on the substantive Srotriya-rinsing-with-water practice, which the substantive Brihadaranyaka holds as the substantive form of substantive recognition of the substantive food-and-dress of breath. The substantive doctrine that *Srotriyas who know this, rinse the mouth with water when they are going to eat, and rinse the mouth with water after they have eaten, thinking that thereby they make the breath dressed (with water)* preserves the substantive Vedic ritual practice of substantive *ācamana* (sipping water) at the open and close of substantive eating, and gives the substantive practice the substantive metaphysical grounding in the substantive offering-to-breath narrative.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the two sections of the Brahmana should be read as one continuous substantive procedure rather than as two independent teachings. The first section's six substantive identifications (best, richest, firm rest, success, home, generation = breath, speech, eye, ear, mind, seed) are the substantive prelude to the second section's senses-quarrel narrative, and the substantive offerings at verse 14 return the substantive qualities of the first section to breath. Reading the two sections together — the substantive identifications as substantive prelude and the substantive narrative as substantive enactment — recovers the substantive form: the substantive doctrine of breath-as-supreme is given through both the substantive parallel identifications and the substantive narrative-of-departure-and-offering.

Second, the substantive senses-quarrel narrative should be preserved at the substantive year-long-departure-and-return level. The substantive form is not a generic sense-priority statement but a substantive narrative: each sense departs, the body waits a year, the sense returns to find the body still lived. The substantive metaphors of *like unto people, like blind people, like deaf people, like fools, like impotent people* preserve the substantive consequence of each sense's substantive absence, and the substantive contrast at verse 13 — when breath departs, the body cannot live — names breath's substantive priority through the substantive narrative-test rather than through the substantive abstract identification. Reading the substantive narrative with the substantive year-long-departure preserved — neither summarized as *the senses tested each other and breath won* nor flattened into a generic statement of breath-as-life — recovers the substantive form of the substantive narrative.
