# Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 4, Part 4

Book 4, Part 4 of the Brihadaranyaka Upanishad — the Fourth Brahmana of the Fourth Adhyaya — is the resumption of the Yajnavalkya–Janaka discourse suspended at the close of the Third Brahmana. The Third Brahmana had ended mid-teaching on verse 38, the king-departing-with-attendants simile applied to *all the senses (pranas) gather round the Self at the time of death*; the Fourth Brahmana opens directly into the next clause of the same discourse with the narrator's word: *Yagnavalkya continued.* The Brahmana boundary is here a paragraph-break, not a literary reset. The same king is still seated in the same audience, the same priest is still speaking, and the same teaching on the Self at the time of death continues without interruption. The Fourth Brahmana works what the Third had begun: the Self's departure at death, the rebirth that follows, the karma doctrine that conditions the rebirth, the two paths of the desirer and the non-desirer, the slokas on the path of those who know Brahman, the great unborn Self, and the closing on the fearless Brahman.

The Brahmana's first section — verses 1 and 2 — gives the Self's departure. *Now when that Self, having sunk into weakness, sinks, as it were, into unconsciousness, then gather those senses (pranas) around him, and he, taking with him those elements of light, descends into the heart. When that person in the eye turns away, then he ceases to know any forms.* The eight-fold litany follows on a single passage at `5.1098`: *"He has become one," they say, "he does not see." "He has become one," they say, "he does not smell." "He has become one," they say, "he does not taste." "He has become one," they say, "he does not speak." "He has become one," they say, "he does not hear." "He has become one," they say, "he does not think." "He has become one," they say, "he does not touch." "He has become one," they say, "he does not know."* The eight verbs — see, smell, taste, speak, hear, think, touch, know — match the Third Brahmana's eight-senses-inseparability cycle (verses 23–30) in reverse register: in the Third Brahmana the senses had been held inseparable from their faculties in deep sleep, in the Fourth they are held inseparable from the dying Self at the moment of departure. The closing of verse 2 gives the exit: *The point of his heart becomes lighted up, and by that light the Self departs, either through the eye, or through the skull, or through other places of the body.* The senses follow; the chief prana follows; consciousness follows. The closing line of verse 2: *He is conscious, and being conscious he follows and departs. Then both his knowledge and his work take hold of him, and his acquaintance with former things.*

Verses 3 and 4 give the two famous similes for rebirth. The caterpillar at verse 3: *as a caterpillar, after having reached the end of a blade of grass, and after having made another approach (to another blade), draws itself together towards it, thus does this Self, after having thrown off this body and dispelled all ignorance, and after making another approach (to another body), draw himself together towards it.* The goldsmith at verse 4: *as a goldsmith, taking a piece of gold, turns it into another, newer and more beautiful shape, so does this Self, after having thrown off this body and dispelled all ignorance, make unto himself another, newer and more beautiful shape, whether it be like the Fathers, or like the Gandharvas, or like the Devas, or like Pragapati, or like Brahman, or like other beings.* The two figures hold the same doctrine through two named procedures: the slow walk of the caterpillar across the grass-blade boundary, and the smith's reshaping of one piece of gold into a newer figure. The list of possible new shapes — Fathers, Gandharvas, Devas, Pragapati, Brahman, other beings — is given without ranking; the Self may take any of these forms in the new body it approaches.

Verse 5 gives the karma doctrine in its compressed form. *That Self is indeed Brahman, consisting of knowledge, mind, life, sight, hearing, earth, water, wind, ether, light and no light, desire and no desire, anger and no anger, right or wrong, and all things.* The Self is identified with Brahman across an inclusive substantive list. Then: *as a man is like this or like that, according as he acts and according as he behaves, so will he be: — a man of good acts will become good, a man of bad acts, bad. He becomes pure by pure deeds, bad by bad deeds.* The doctrine is one of the Brihadaranyaka's earliest sustained formulations of the karma-and-rebirth principle, and it sits at verse 5 of the Brahmana following directly on the goldsmith simile that had named the new shape's range. Verse 5 closes with the desire-will-deed chain: *And here they say that a person consists of desires. And as is his desire, so is his will; and as is his will, so is his deed; and whatever deed he does, that he will reap.*

Verse 6 holds the verse on attachment and the two paths. *To whatever object a man's own mind is attached, to that he goes strenuously together with his deed; and having obtained the end (the last results) of whatever deed he does here on earth, he returns again from that world (which is the temporary reward of his deed) to this world of action.* The verse names the desirer's return as conditioned by the temporariness of the reward. The contrast follows in prose: *So much for the man who desires. But as to the man who does not desire, who, not desiring, freed from desires, is satisfied in his desires, or desires the Self only, his vital spirits do not depart elsewhere, — being Brahman, he goes to Brahman.* The two paths are named: the desirer who returns, the non-desirer who *being Brahman, goes to Brahman*.

Verse 7 holds the snake-slough simile. The introductory line at the open: *On this there is this verse: "When all desires which once entered his heart are undone, then does the mortal become immortal, then he obtains Brahman."* Then the prose: *And as the slough of a snake lies on an ant-hill, dead and cast away, thus lies this body; but that disembodied immortal spirit (prana, life) is Brahman only, is only light.* The figure is one of the Brihadaranyaka's most-cited statements of the body-and-spirit distinction at the moment of liberation: the body is the cast-off skin, the spirit is the snake that has moved on, with the only-light identification given as the seal.

Janaka's first offer of the Brahmana is given on its own short passage at `5.1111`: *Sir, I give you a thousand.* The form is compressed from the prior Brahmanas' offers — no animal-specification, no *speak on for the sake of (my) emancipation* — and Yajnavalkya neither accepts nor refuses; he continues teaching at verse 8 with the introductory line *On this there are these verses*.

Verses 8 through 22 give the long sloka-and-prose section. Fifteen successive verses work the doctrine of the Self and the path of those who know Brahman through varied forms. Verse 8: *The small, old path stretching far away has been found by me. On it sages who know Brahman move on to the Svarga-loka (heaven), and thence higher on, as entirely free.* Verse 9: *On that path they say that there is white, or blue, or yellow, or green, or red; that path was found by Brahman, and on it goes whoever knows Brahman, and who has done good, and obtained splendour.* The five-color list of the path here pairs with the five-color list of the Hita veins at `5.1067` of the Third Brahmana — the same colors (white, blue, yellow, green, red) given on the same Adhyaya's veins-of-the-heart and now on the path-of-the-knower.

Verse 10 gives the famous paradox of knowledge-and-non-knowledge: *All who worship what is not knowledge (avidya) enter into blind darkness: those who delight in knowledge, enter, as it were, into greater darkness.* The line is paired with the Isa Upanishad's verses 9–11; the Brihadaranyaka here gives it inside the Adhyaya's substantive teaching. Verse 11 names the unblessed worlds: *There are indeed those unblessed worlds, covered with blind darkness. Men who are ignorant and not enlightened go after death to those worlds.* Verse 12 holds the *I am He* identification: *If a man understands the Self, saying, "I am He," what could he wish or desire that he should pine after the body.* Verse 13 holds the *patched-together hiding-place* figure: *Whoever has found and understood the Self that has entered into this patched-together hiding-place, he indeed is the creator, for he is the maker of everything, his is the world, and he is the world itself.* Verse 14 names the urgency of present knowledge: *While we are here, we may know this; if not, I am ignorant, and there is great destruction. Those who know it, become immortal, but others suffer pain indeed.* Verse 15 holds the fearlessness-condition: *If a man clearly beholds this Self as God, and as the lord of all that is and will be, then he is no more afraid.*

Verse 16 names the Self as that behind which the year revolves: *He behind whom the year revolves with the days, him the gods worship as the light of lights, as immortal time.* Verse 17 names the five beings: *He in whom the five beings and the ether rest, him alone I believe to be the Self, — I who know, believe him to be Brahman; I who am immortal, believe him to be immortal.* Verse 18 holds the *life of life, eye of the eye, ear of the ear, mind of the mind* formula — one of the most-cited four-clause forms in the Upanishadic literature: *They who know the life of life, the eye of the eye, the ear of the ear, the mind of the mind, they have comprehended the ancient, primeval Brahman.* Verse 19 gives the diversity-warning: *By the mind alone it is to be perceived, there is in it no diversity. He who perceives therein any diversity, goes from death to death.* Verse 20 holds the eternal-being identification: *This eternal being that can never be proved, is to be perceived in one way only; it is spotless, beyond the ether, the unborn Self, great and eternal.* Verse 21 holds the wise-Brahmana procedural advice: *Let a wise Brahmana, after he has discovered him, practise wisdom. Let him not seek after many words, for that is mere weariness of the tongue.*

Verse 22 is the longest of the slokas-section, given partly in prose. *And he is that great unborn Self, who consists of knowledge, is surrounded by the Pranas, the ether within the heart. In it there reposes the ruler of all, the lord of all, the king of all. He does not become greater by good works, nor smaller by evil works. He is the lord of all, the king of all things, the protector of all things. He is a bank and a boundary, so that these worlds may not be confounded. Brahmanas seek to know him by the study of the Veda, by sacrifice, by gifts, by penance, by fasting, and he who knows him, becomes a Muni. Wishing for that world (for Brahman) only, mendicants leave their homes.* The verse continues with the line that echoes the Fifth Brahmana of the Third Adhyaya verbatim: *Knowing this, the people of old did not wish for offspring. What shall we do with offspring, they said, we who have this Self and this world (of Brahman)? And they, having risen above the desire for sons, wealth, and new worlds, wander about as mendicants. For desire for sons is desire for wealth, and desire for wealth is desire for worlds. Both these are indeed desires only.* The chain — *desire for sons → wealth → worlds* — is the same chain Yajnavalkya had given Kahola Kaushitakeya at the close of the Fifth Brahmana of the Third Adhyaya at `5.718`–`5.719`, in nearly identical wording. The verse then gives the *neti neti* line again: *He, the Self, is to be described by No, no! He is incomprehensible, for he cannot be comprehended; he is imperishable, for he cannot perish; he is unattached, for he does not attach himself; unfettered, he does not suffer, he does not fail.* The form is the four-clause *neti neti* given in the same wording as Book 3 Part 9's verse 26 at `5.921`. The verse closes on the doctrine that the knower is not affected by his deeds: *Him (who knows), these two do not overcome, whether he says that for some reason he has done evil, or for some reason he has done good — he overcomes both, and neither what he has done, nor what he has omitted to do, burns (affects) him.*

Verse 23 closes the substantive answer. *This has been told by a verse (Rik): "This eternal greatness of the Brahmana does not grow larger by work, nor does it grow smaller. Let man try to find (know) its trace, for having found (known) it, he is not sullied by any evil deed."* The Rik-quotation gives the form of the closing inside the Brahmana itself, and the closing prose then follows: *He therefore that knows it, after having become quiet, subdued, satisfied, patient, and collected, sees self in Self, sees all as Self. Evil does not overcome him, he overcomes all evil. Evil does not burn him, he burns all evil. Free from evil, free from spots, free from doubt, he becomes a (true) Brahmana; this is the Brahma-world, O King,' — thus spoke Yagnavalkya.* The five-fold *quiet, subdued, satisfied, patient, and collected* names the form of life of the knower; the closing seal *this is the Brahma-world, O King* repeats the seal of the Third Brahmana's verse 32 (*An ocean is that one seer, without any duality; this is the Brahma-world, O King*).

Janaka's reply at `5.1134` is the second kingdom-and-self surrender of the Adhyaya: *Sir, I give you the Videhas, and also myself, to be together your slaves.* The line pairs with the closing line of the Second Brahmana at `5.1025` — *Here are the Videhas, and here am I (thy slave)* — with one inflection altered: *to be together your slaves* in the present passage, *here am I (thy slave)* in the prior. The two surrenders sit at the close of the two Brahmanas in which Yajnavalkya's substantive teaching has reached its closing seal — fearlessness in the Second Brahmana, the Brahma-world in the Fourth — and Janaka's offer in both cases follows the seal directly.

Verses 24 and 25 give the final two concluding verses on the great unborn Self. Verse 24: *This indeed is the great, the unborn Self, the strong, the giver of wealth. He who knows this obtains wealth.* Verse 25 holds the closing line of the Brahmana: *This great, unborn Self, undecaying, undying, immortal, fearless, is indeed Brahman. Fearless is Brahman, and he who knows this becomes verily the fearless Brahman.* The Brahmana closes on the term *fearless* (abhaya) — the same term that closed the Second Brahmana at `5.1024` — held in compounded repetition: *Fearless is Brahman; he who knows this becomes verily the fearless Brahman.* The Brahmana ends here with a substantive seal, not with the suspended close of the Third.

## Context

The Brihadaranyaka Upanishad is one of the two earliest of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads, sharing that position with the Chandogya. It belongs to the Shukla Yajurveda branch of the Vedic corpus and forms the closing portion of the Shatapatha Brahmana — the *Aranyaka* in its name marks it as a forest-text recited at the late stages of Vedic study. Modern scholarship places the Brihadaranyaka in roughly the eighth to sixth century BCE, prior to the Buddha; the text is anonymous, and the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship. The text is the longest of the principal Upanishads and is typically structured in six *adhyayas* (chapters), each containing several *brahmanas* (sections).

Book 4, Part 4 holds three of the most-cited Upanishadic loci of the karma-and-rebirth doctrine in concentrated form. The first is the karma doctrine itself at verse 5. *As a man is like this or like that, according as he acts and according as he behaves, so will he be: — a man of good acts will become good, a man of bad acts, bad. He becomes pure by pure deeds, bad by bad deeds.* The line is one of the Brihadaranyaka's principal sources for the doctrine that the moral quality of one's acts conditions the form of one's next birth, and it sits at verse 5 of the Brahmana directly after the goldsmith simile that had named the new shape's range as Fathers, Gandharvas, Devas, Pragapati, Brahman, or other beings. The doctrine is named openly for the first time in the Adhyaya here; the Second Brahmana of the Third Adhyaya had given the first hint of it in Yajnavalkya's private answer to Artabhaga (*what they said was karman, what they praised was karman, viz. that a man becomes good by good work, and bad by bad work* at `5.698`), but the present locus gives it as openly stated public doctrine inside the Janaka–Yajnavalkya teaching.

The second is the two-paths distinction at verse 6. *So much for the man who desires. But as to the man who does not desire, who, not desiring, freed from desires, is satisfied in his desires, or desires the Self only, his vital spirits do not depart elsewhere, — being Brahman, he goes to Brahman.* The doctrine is the Upanishadic literature's principal pre-Vedanta source for the distinction between the path of the desirer (who is reborn) and the path of the non-desirer (who attains Brahman without further birth). The Brihadaranyaka here sets the two paths inside the rebirth-doctrine of verse 5 rather than treating them as separate cosmological traditions; the doctrine of liberation is held as the alternative to the doctrine of rebirth, with both worked through the same mechanism of desire.

The third is the verbatim echo of the Fifth Brahmana of the Third Adhyaya at verse 22. *They, having risen above the desire for sons, wealth, and new worlds, wander about as mendicants. For desire for sons is desire for wealth, and desire for wealth is desire for worlds. Both these are indeed desires only.* The same chain — *desire for sons → wealth → worlds* — and the same wandering-mendicant teaching had been given by Yajnavalkya to Kahola Kaushitakeya in the public *brahmodya* contest at the close of Book 3, Part 5. The Fourth Brahmana of the Fourth Adhyaya gives the same teaching in nearly identical wording inside the private Janaka–Yajnavalkya audience. The two loci hold the same doctrine across the Adhyaya boundary, with the public-contest form and the private-instruction form working the same chain of desires through the same procedure of mendicant renunciation. Reading the two loci alongside each other recovers the form the Brihadaranyaka has when it gives the same teaching across two Adhyayas with the same wording held in both registers.

The Brahmana also holds the closing of the Adhyaya's principal Janaka–Yajnavalkya teaching. The Adhyaya's first three Brahmanas had built toward an unfinished discourse: the First closed with declined-reward and the instruction explicitly held as incomplete; the Second closed with fearlessness as the substantive seal and Janaka's first kingdom-and-self surrender; the Third closed mid-teaching at verse 38 without a seal. The Fourth Brahmana resumes the Third's discourse, completes the death-and-rebirth-and-karma teaching, gives fifteen slokas of the path-and-Self doctrine, holds the great unborn Self in verse 22, and closes on the fearless Brahman in verse 25. Janaka's second kingdom-and-self surrender at `5.1134` and the closing line *Fearless is Brahman, and he who knows this becomes verily the fearless Brahman* together hold the Brahmana's closing as the Adhyaya's first sustained conclusion since the open of the Adhyaya at `5.936`. The Fifth and Sixth Brahmanas of the Adhyaya will work other material; the Fourth is the closing point of the Adhyaya's Janaka–Yajnavalkya substantive teaching.

The closing form is the Adhyaya's second appearance of fearlessness. The Second Brahmana had given the form *O Ganaka, you have indeed reached fearlessness — thus said Yagnavalkya* at `5.1024`; the Fourth Brahmana gives the form *Fearless is Brahman, and he who knows this becomes verily the fearless Brahman* at `5.1137`. The two closings sit at the close of the Adhyaya's two principal teaching-Brahmanas, with the Second giving the term as a state of the king and the Fourth giving it as the substance of Brahman itself. The repetition is not redundant; it is the doctrine's progression from the king's reaching-of-fearlessness to the substance of Brahman as the fearless itself.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the Brahmana resumes the prior Brahmana's discourse without reset, and the relation between the two Brahmanas should be read at the level of single continuous teaching rather than two distinct lectures. The Third Brahmana's verse 38 — *as policemen, magistrates, equerries, and governors gather round a king who is departing, thus do all the senses (pranas) gather round the Self at the time of death* — is followed directly by the Fourth Brahmana's verse 1 — *Now when that Self, having sunk into weakness, sinks, as it were, into unconsciousness, then gather those senses (pranas) around him* — and the same verb (*gather*) and the same object (*the senses, pranas*) carry across the Brahmana boundary. Reading the two Brahmanas together as one suspended-and-resumed discourse rather than as two segmented Brahmanas recovers the form the Adhyaya has when its longest sustained teaching is split at a Brahmana boundary that is, in literary terms, a paragraph-break.

Second, the Brahmana's slokas should be read at full length rather than collapsed. Verses 8–22 give fifteen successive slokas-and-prose verses on the path of those who know Brahman, and each verse holds a different rhetorical procedure: the discovered path (8), the colored path (9), the knowledge-and-non-knowledge paradox (10), the unblessed worlds (11), the *I am He* identification (12), the patched-together hiding-place (13), the urgency of present knowledge (14), the fearlessness-condition (15), the year-revolving Self (16), the five-beings rest (17), the *life of life* four-clause (18), the no-diversity warning (19), the eternal-being identification (20), the wise-Brahmana procedural advice (21), and the great-unborn-Self prose verse (22). Reading the slokas as a sequence of fifteen separate verse-units rather than collapsed into a single doctrinal sermon recovers the Brahmana's literary procedure: the doctrine of the knower of Brahman is given through fifteen distinct rhetorical machines held in the same sloka-section.

- **First offer-passage at `5.1111`.** Janaka's first offer — *Sir, I give you a thousand* — sits on its own short passage between verse 7 and verse 8 with no verse-marker. The form is compressed from the prior Brahmanas' offers (no animal-specification, no *speak on for the sake of (my) emancipation*); Yajnavalkya neither accepts nor refuses, and the next passage opens verse 8 with *On this there are these verses*.
- **Verse-8 footnote on the introduction.** Passage `5.1112` carries the verse-8 marker and the introductory line *On this there are these verses* with footnote `[*1]` attached to the verse-8 marker. Müller's note glosses the verse-8 introduction as an editorial prelude rather than a substantive verse of its own.
- **Verse-9 footnote on the colored path.** Passage `5.1114` carries verse 9 with footnote `[*4]` on the five-color list (white, blue, yellow, green, red). The same five-color list had appeared at `5.1067` of the Third Brahmana on the Hita veins; the present footnote gives the path-of-the-knower the same color-set.
- **Verse-11 split mid-clause.** Verse 11 — the unblessed worlds — is split across passages `5.1116` and `5.1117` mid-clause at *those unblessed worlds, / covered with blind darkness*. Passage `5.1116` carries footnote `[*6]` at the open of the verse.
- **Verse-17 split mid-clause.** Verse 17 — the five-beings rest — is split across passages `5.1123` and `5.1124` mid-clause at *be the Self, — I who / know, believe him to be Brahman*. Passage `5.1123` carries footnote `[*5]` on *the five beings*.
- **Verse-22 multi-passage span with footnote cluster.** Verse 22 — the great-unborn-Self prose verse — runs across passages `5.1129`, `5.1130`, and `5.1131` with three substantive sub-sections: the great-unborn-Self prose with the *bank and a boundary* clause and the *Brahmanas seek to know him* line (`5.1129`, with footnotes `[*4]` on *ether within the heart* and `[*5]` on *bank*); the people-of-old prose on the desire-for-sons-wealth-worlds chain (`5.1130`); and the *neti neti* line on the same chain (`5.1131`, with footnotes `[*1]` on *this Self and this world (of Brahman)* and `[*2]` on *No, no!*). The verse spans three passages without internal verse-markers; the *neti neti* line at `5.1131` matches the four-clause form at `5.1024` of the Second Brahmana and at `5.921` of the Ninth Brahmana of the Third Adhyaya.
- **Janaka's second offer at `5.1134`.** The closing of Yajnavalkya's substantive teaching at verse 23 is followed at `5.1134` by Janaka's second kingdom-and-self surrender — *Sir, I give you the Videhas, and also myself, to be together your slaves* — on its own short passage with no verse-marker. The line pairs with the closing line of the Second Brahmana at `5.1025`. The two surrenders are the only two appearances in the Adhyaya of the kingdom-and-self offering.
- **Verse-24 split with two footnotes.** Verse 24 is split across passages `5.1135` and `5.1136` mid-clause at *the great, the unborn Self, the / strong*. Passage `5.1135` carries footnote `[*4]` on the verse-24 opening; passage `5.1136` carries footnote `[*1]` on *strong*.
- **Closing seal at `5.1137`.** Passage `5.1137` carries verse 25 — the closing seal of the Brahmana — on a single short passage without footnote: *This great, unborn Self, undecaying, undying, immortal, fearless, is indeed Brahman. Fearless is Brahman, and he who knows this becomes verily the fearless Brahman.* The Brahmana ends here without a *held his peace* form, without a further offer, and without a Janaka-reply. The Adhyaya's principal Janaka–Yajnavalkya substantive teaching closes on the substantive seal of the fearless Brahman.

Passage `5.1138` is the "Fifth Brahmana" header that opens Book 4, Part 5 of the Brihadaranyaka and is not part of this entity. The library chapter continues past this entity into the rest of the Fourth Adhyaya, the Fifth Adhyaya at `5.1181`, and the remaining portions of the volume; the Brihadaranyaka extends well beyond the boundaries of library chapter 5. About nine minutes' reading.

## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 4, Part 3 — paired immediate-predecessor Brahmanas of the Fourth Adhyaya holding a single suspended-and-resumed discourse split at a Brahmana boundary; the Third Brahmana works the doctrine of the Self through the light-cascade, the three-states framework, four slokas, the recurring movement-cycle, the Hita-veins-and-dream-fears, the deep-sleep negation catalogue, the eight senses' inseparability, the ocean-as-one-seer, the bliss-cascade, and the death-and-departure section closing at verse 38 with *all the senses (pranas) gather round the Self at the time of death* — the Fourth Brahmana opens directly with *Yagnavalkya continued* and works the doctrine forward through the Self's departure at death (verse 1's *gather those senses (pranas) around him*), the rebirth doctrine in two famous similes, the karma doctrine, the two paths of the desirer and non-desirer, fifteen slokas, the great unborn Self with the *neti neti* line, and the closing on the fearless Brahman. The two Brahmanas form a single continuous teaching held across a paragraph-break boundary; the verb *gather* and the object *the senses (pranas)* carry verbatim from the Third Brahmana's closing verse to the Fourth Brahmana's opening verse, and the substantive content moves from the moment-of-death narrative of the Third into the after-death rebirth narrative of the Fourth.
- **structural-parallel** → Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 4, Part 2 — paired closures of the Adhyaya's two principal teaching-Brahmanas with fearlessness and kingdom-and-self surrender held in the same form; the Second Brahmana closes the substantive teaching with *O Ganaka, you have indeed reached fearlessness — thus said Yagnavalkya* at `5.1024` and Janaka's reply *Here are the Videhas, and here am I (thy slave)* at `5.1025`, the Fourth Brahmana closes the substantive teaching with verse 25's *Fearless is Brahman, and he who knows this becomes verily the fearless Brahman* at `5.1137` and Janaka's reply *Sir, I give you the Videhas, and also myself, to be together your slaves* at `5.1134` (placed before verses 24–25's final seal). The Adhyaya's two appearances of the kingdom-and-self surrender sit at the close of the two Brahmanas in which Yajnavalkya's substantive teaching has reached its closing-seal-on-fearlessness; the doctrine of fearlessness moves between the two Brahmanas from the king's reaching-of-fearlessness in the Second to the substance of Brahman as the fearless itself in the Fourth.
- **structural-parallel** → Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 3, Part 5 — paired Brihadaranyaka loci of the desire-for-sons-wealth-worlds chain and the wandering-mendicant teaching given in nearly verbatim wording in two different settings; the Fifth Brahmana of the Third Adhyaya gives Yajnavalkya's substantive answer to the public challenger Kahola Kaushitakeya: *When Brahmanas know that Self, and have risen above the desire for sons, wealth, and (new) worlds, they wander about as mendicants. For a desire for sons is desire for wealth, a desire for wealth is desire for worlds. Both these are indeed desires* at `5.718`, the Fourth Brahmana of the Fourth Adhyaya gives the same teaching at verse 22's prose section at `5.1130`–`5.1131`: *they, having risen above the desire for sons, wealth, and new worlds, wander about as mendicants. For desire for sons is desire for wealth, and desire for wealth is desire for worlds. Both these are indeed desires only.* The two loci hold the same doctrine across the Adhyaya boundary in nearly identical wording, with the public-contest form in the Third Adhyaya and the private-instruction form in the Fourth — the same text held in both registers without paraphrase.
