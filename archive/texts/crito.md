# Plato, Crito

The *Crito* is a short Platonic dialogue set in Socrates' prison cell in the days between his trial and his execution. Crito, a long-standing friend, has come at dawn to urge Socrates to escape: the bribes are arranged, the destination is prepared, and the time is short. The dialogue is the conversation that follows. Crito's appeals — to friendship, to reputation, to the obligation Socrates owes his sons — are answered by Socrates with a methodical examination of whether the act of escape would be just; the dialogue closes with Socrates impersonating the Laws of Athens, who address him in the second person and ask what he would say to them if he ran. The dialogue is short — about thirty pages of modern translation — and one of the earliest of Plato's surviving works.

## Context

Plato's dialogues are conventionally arranged into three groups by modern scholarship: early (Socratic), middle, and late. The *Crito* belongs to the early group, alongside the *Apology*, the *Euthyphro*, the *Charmides*, the *Laches*, the *Lysis*, and the *Protagoras* — short dialogues centered on Socrates and on the practice of questioning, generally agreed to have been written in the 390s BCE in the years after Socrates' execution in 399. The *Apology*, *Euthyphro*, *Crito*, and *Phaedo* are often grouped together as "the trial and death of Socrates" — four dialogues that frame Socrates' final weeks across the events of his indictment, trial, imprisonment, and death.

The *Crito* takes place in Socrates' cell in the days after his conviction in the *Apology*. Athenian custom forbade execution while the sacred ship sent each year to Delos was still at sea; the ship's return is imminent as Crito arrives. The dialogue runs to about thirty pages in modern translation, with two voices throughout — Socrates and Crito — and a third voice, the personified Laws of Athens, that speaks in the closing third through Socrates' impersonation. The dialogue ends with Socrates declining to escape; the *Phaedo*, the next dialogue in the death sequence, narrates his last day and execution.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the dialogue moves through three distinct argumentative stages. Crito opens with several practical and personal arguments for escape — the loss to his friends, the disgrace of having failed to save Socrates, the obligation to provide for Socrates' sons. Socrates responds first by distinguishing the opinion of the many from the opinion of those who know; he then turns to whether the act of escape would itself be just, regardless of how it was forced on him; and the dialogue closes with the imagined speech of the Laws of Athens, who speak from the moment Socrates attempts to leave. Reading the dialogue with the three stages held visible — Crito's case, Socrates' analysis of justice, the Laws' answering speech — recovers the form the dialogue has.

Second, the closing speech of the Laws is impersonated, not narrated. Socrates does not simply argue that the Laws would object; he gives the Laws a voice and speaks in their voice, addressing himself in the second person. The figure is striking: the man condemned by the Laws of Athens speaks as the Laws to himself, asking what he would say to them if he ran. Reading the closing pages with attention to the change in voice — Socrates becoming the Laws addressing Socrates — keeps the figure the dialogue gives.

- **structural-parallel** → Epictetus, Enchiridion, Chapters 1–5 — paired short Greek-language ethical texts on what is and is not within one's power; the Enchiridion sets the distinction between the two as its opening teaching, the *Crito* dramatizes a single instance — Socrates judging that justice is within his power and life or death is not.
