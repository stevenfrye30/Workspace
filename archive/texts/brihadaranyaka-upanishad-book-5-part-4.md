# Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 5, Part 4

Book 5, Part 4 of the Brihadaranyaka Upanishad — the Fourth Brahmana of the Fifth Adhyaya — works the Adhyaya forward in a form that chains directly to the prior Brahmana. The Third Brahmana had given the triple identification *Pragapati is the heart, is this Brahman, is all this* and the *hridaya* etymology of *hri-da-yam*; the Fourth Brahmana opens with the parenthetical *(heart)* at its first clause — *This (heart) indeed is even that, it was indeed the true (Brahman)* — and continues the prior Brahmana's substantive identification without restating it. The Adhyaya's compact Brahmanas are not isolated units; they form a chained sequence, and the Fourth Brahmana's first word *This* and the parenthetical *(heart)* hold the chain explicitly. Müller's parenthetical gloss preserves what the demonstrative carries in the underlying Sanskrit: the same heart the prior Brahmana had named.

The substantive teaching of the Brahmana is held on a single verse 1 split across two passages. The substance, expanded fully:

*This (heart) indeed is even that, it was indeed the true (Brahman). And whosoever knows this great glorious first-born as the true Brahman, he conquers these worlds, and conquered likewise may that (enemy) be! yes, whosoever knows this great glorious first-born as the true Brahman; for Brahman is the true.*

The substance moves through three operations. The first is the chained identification: the heart is *that* (the prior Brahmana's *that*), and *that* was *the true* (the *satya*, the substantive truth-name of Brahman). The chain runs through three substantive equivalents — heart, that, the true Brahman — and adds *the great glorious first-born* as the fourth substantive name. The first-born (*jyeshtha*, *prathamaja*) is a Vedic epithet of cosmic priority; the same epithet is used in the Aitareya and the Chandogya Upanishads at their opening identification-passages.

The second operation is the conquest-and-curse formula. *And whosoever knows this great glorious first-born as the true Brahman, he conquers these worlds, and conquered likewise may that (enemy) be!* The substantive promise — *he conquers these worlds* — is the substantive benefit of the knowledge. The optative imprecation that follows — *and conquered likewise may that (enemy) be!* — is held in the same syntactic clause and is given in optative imprecation-form. Müller's parenthetical *(enemy)* glosses the underlying Sanskrit referent as the knower's adversary. The exclamation-point at the close of the imprecation is preserved in the digitized source as the formal mark of the curse-form. The combined conquest-and-curse formula holds the *vidyā* (meditation-knowledge) procedure of the Adhyaya in its competitive register: knowing the substantive identification yields conquest of the worlds, and the knower's enemy is bound by the same knowledge to be conquered.

The third operation is the verbatim restatement and the closing seal. *Yes, whosoever knows this great glorious first-born as the true Brahman; for Brahman is the true.* The phrase *whosoever knows this great glorious first-born as the true Brahman* is repeated verbatim from its first appearance inside the conquest-formula, with the affirmative particle *yes,* linking the second occurrence. The closing seal *for Brahman is the true* holds the substantive equation — *satya brahman*, Brahman as the true — and ends the Brahmana on the same substantive name the Brahmana's first clause had given. The Brahmana opens with *the true (Brahman)* and closes with *Brahman is the true*; the form is a circle, with the same identification given at the open and the close.

## Context

The Brihadaranyaka Upanishad is one of the two earliest of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads, sharing that position with the Chandogya. It belongs to the Shukla Yajurveda branch of the Vedic corpus and forms the closing portion of the Shatapatha Brahmana — the *Aranyaka* in its name marks it as a forest-text recited at the late stages of Vedic study. Modern scholarship places the Brihadaranyaka in roughly the eighth to sixth century BCE, prior to the Buddha; the text is anonymous, and the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship. The text is the longest of the principal Upanishads and is typically structured in six *adhyayas* (chapters), each containing several *brahmanas* (sections).

Book 5, Part 4 holds two of the Adhyaya's distinctive features in concentrated form. The first is the chaining-back to the prior Brahmana. The Adhyaya's first three Brahmanas had given three distinct compact procedures: the Purnam mantra and Om-as-ether-as-Brahman (Brahmana 1), the Pragapati-and-three-classes parable on the syllable Da (Brahmana 2), and the *hridaya* etymology with the triple identification of Pragapati with the heart with Brahman (Brahmana 3). The Fourth Brahmana's opening parenthetical *(heart)* references the prior Brahmana's substantive name without restating it, and the substantive identification *the true Brahman* held inside the clause picks up the *Brahman* of the prior triple identification. The Adhyaya's procedure is to hold compact identifications in chained sequence rather than independent doctrinal units; the Fourth Brahmana is the first explicit chain-reference and sets the procedure for the Adhyaya's later Brahmanas.

The second is the substantive name *satya* (the true). The Brihadaranyaka holds the *satya* doctrine in multiple loci: the Third Brahmana of the Second Adhyaya gives the doctrine *there are two forms of Brahman, the material and the immaterial, the mortal and the immortal, the solid and the fluid, sat and tya — sat-tya, true*, with the *neti neti* close on *the True of the True*; the Fourth Brahmana of the Fifth Adhyaya gives the same doctrine in compressed form, with the substantive equation *Brahman is the true* (*satya brahman*) at the open and at the close. The two loci hold the same substantive name across two distinct procedures — the long cosmic-and-bodily mapping of the Second Adhyaya and the compressed identification-chain of the Fifth Adhyaya — and the *satya* doctrine carries through the Upanishad as one of its principal substantive names of Brahman.

The Brahmana also holds the Adhyaya's first imprecation. *Conquered likewise may that (enemy) be!* The optative imprecation-form is rare in the Brihadaranyaka — the contest of the Third Adhyaya had not used it, and the Janaka–Yajnavalkya teaching of the Fourth Adhyaya had not used it. The Fifth Adhyaya's compact procedural Brahmanas introduce it here, and the form will be carried through the Adhyaya's later Brahmanas in similar substantive imprecation-curses. The form holds the Vedic background of the *vidyā* (meditation-knowledge) tradition as an active competitive procedure: the knowledge yields not only the substantive benefit (conquest of the worlds) but also the binding of the knower's adversary by the same knowledge.

The closing form is the same compressed substantive seal that the Adhyaya's other Brahmanas have used. *For Brahman is the true.* No closing narrator's line, no *held his peace* form, no doctrinal recapitulation. The Brahmana ends inside its substance.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the Brahmana should be read as the second Brahmana of a chained pair with the Third Brahmana. The First and Second Brahmanas of the Adhyaya had been independent units (Purnam mantra; Pragapati parable); the Third and Fourth Brahmanas form a paired-substantive sequence. The Third Brahmana names the heart-as-Brahman identification and the *hridaya* etymology; the Fourth Brahmana picks up the heart-identification at its opening parenthetical and works it into the *true Brahman* substantive name. Reading the two Brahmanas together — neither collapsed into a single doctrine nor separated into wholly independent compact teachings — recovers the Adhyaya's procedure of chained identification across consecutive compact Brahmanas.

Second, the imprecation should be preserved as part of the Brahmana's substantive form. The clause *and conquered likewise may that (enemy) be!* is given in optative-form with the exclamation-mark of the imperative, and Müller's parenthetical *(enemy)* glosses the underlying referent. Reading the imprecation as part of the *vidyā* — neither smoothed away nor expanded into a doctrinal-statement — recovers the Adhyaya's procedural form: the meditation-knowledge of the substantive identification yields both the substantive benefit (conquest) and the substantive binding of the adversary (curse).

The Brahmana contains several boundary features that should be preserved honestly:

- **Verse-1 opening with chain-reference.** Passage `5.1194` opens the substantive teaching with the demonstrative *This* and the parenthetical *(heart)* — *This (heart) indeed is even that, it was indeed the true (Brahman)* — referencing the prior Brahmana's substantive name without restating it. Müller's parenthetical gloss preserves the substantive referent in the digitized source.
- **Verse-1 split mid-clause.** Verse 1 is split across passages `5.1194` and `5.1195` mid-clause at *yes, whosoever knows this great / glorious first-born as the true Brahman*. The split falls at the open of the verbatim restatement of the *whosoever knows* clause.
- **Footnote numbering irregularity.** Passage `5.1194` carries footnote `[*2]` on *the true* and footnote `[*3]` on *may that (enemy) be*. No footnote `[*1]` is given on any passage of the Brahmana. The numbering irregularity is preserved as found in the digitized source; Müller's footnote sequence may begin at `[*2]` for this Brahmana, or `[*1]` may attach at the unmarked Brahmana header at `5.1193` and not be displayed in the body. The substantive content of the verse is unaffected.
- **Conquest-and-curse formula on a single passage.** Passage `5.1194` carries the substantive promise *he conquers these worlds* and the optative imprecation *and conquered likewise may that (enemy) be!* on the same passage as the chained identification. The exclamation-point at the close of the imprecation is preserved in the digitized source.
- **Verbatim repetition across the verse-split.** The phrase *whosoever knows this great glorious first-born as the true Brahman* appears twice — once at the open of the conquest-formula at `5.1194` and once after the verse-split at `5.1195`. The *yes,* particle links the second occurrence to the first, and the second occurrence is given as a verbatim restatement before the closing seal.
- **Closing seal at `5.1195`.** The Brahmana closes on the substantive seal *for Brahman is the true* on passage `5.1195`. No closing narrator's line, no *held his peace* form, no further commentary. The closing seal is the substantive equation *satya brahman* in its English-translation form.

Passage `5.1196` is the "Fifth Brahmana" header that opens Book 5, Part 5 of the Brihadaranyaka and is not part of this entity.
