# Taittiriyaka Upanishad, Book 1, Part 3

Book 1, Part 3 of the Taittiriyaka Upanishad — the Third Anuvaka of the Sikshavalli — opens the substantive instruction the Second Anuvaka had announced. The Anuvaka begins with a short joint invocation: *saha nau yaśaḥ saha nau brahmavarcasam* — "may glory come to both of us together, may Vedic light belong to both of us" — placing teacher and pupil under one wish before the lesson begins. The lesson is then introduced as the *upanishad* of the *samhita*, the inner meaning of the union of letters, given under five heads: the worlds, the heavenly lights, knowledge, offspring, and the self. Each of the five is then unfolded by the same fourfold template — *pūrva-rūpa* (former element), *uttara-rūpa* (latter element), *sandhi* (their union), and the mediator through which the union takes place. Earth and heaven, joined by ether, with the union taking place through Vayu the air. Agni and the sun, joined by water, with the union taking place through lightning. Teacher and pupil, joined by knowledge, with the union taking place through the recitation of the Veda. Mother and father, joined by offspring, with the union taking place through procreation. Lower jaw and upper jaw, joined by speech, with the union taking place through speech itself. The Anuvaka closes with the line that these are the great Samhitas — *iti mahāsamhitāḥ* — and with the formula that the one who knows these Samhitas as here explained is united with offspring, with cattle, with Vedic light, with food, and with the world of heaven.

## Context

The Taittiriyaka Upanishad is one of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads — a small group of texts traditionally received as the philosophical core of the late Vedic literature. It belongs to the Krishna Yajurveda branch of the Vedic corpus and to the Taittiriya school of that branch; the Upanishad sits inside the Taittiriya Aranyaka. Modern scholarship places it among the older prose Upanishads, in roughly the sixth to fifth century BCE — earlier than the verse Upanishads of the Katha and Mundaka. The text is anonymous; the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship.

Book 1, Part 3 sits inside the Sikshavalli, the chapter on *śikṣā*. The First Anuvaka set the protective frame; the Second Anuvaka named the topic — the six headings of pronunciation; the Third Anuvaka turns the technical name *samhita* (the union of letters at sandhi) into the organizing image of a wider lesson, treating the same word as the form by which the worlds, the heavenly lights, knowledge, offspring, and the self are also held together. The procedure is characteristic of the late-Vedic prose Upanishads: a term from ritual or grammar is taken up, used as an anchor, and shown to apply at progressively wider scales — from the joining of two letters in recitation to the joining of two parents in offspring and the joining of earth and heaven in the cosmos. The teaching is held inside the framing the First and Second Anuvakas opened; the substance the Sikshavalli will continue to develop in the anuvakas that follow.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the Anuvaka is built on a fixed template repeated across five fields. Each field receives the same four-part articulation — former element, latter element, union, mediator — and the cumulative effect is the recognition the closing line states: *iti mahāsamhitāḥ*, these are the great Samhitas. Reading the Anuvaka with attention to the recurring template, and to the slight rotation of vocabulary that gives each field its own form (ether for the worlds, water for the heavenly lights, knowledge for teacher and pupil, offspring for the parents, speech for the body) recovers the form the lesson has and the place the form holds in the Upanishad's wider procedure.

Second, the Anuvaka teaches by paired correspondence rather than by argument. The five fields — cosmos, lights, learning, family, body — are not arranged in an order of priority and not unified into a single doctrine; they are placed beside one another, each given the same shape, and the work of recognition is the reader's. Reading the Anuvaka as a list of paired figures, allowing each pairing to register in its own field before moving to the next, rather than collapsing them into a single thesis, keeps the form the late-Vedic school-lesson has at its opening.

## The text

The text is held in the Archive library under `upanishads-muller-part2`, chapter 5 (Müller translation, 1884 — *The Sacred Books of the East*, Volume XV; via SacredTexts.com). Library chapter 5 is shared across twelve Archive entities — four parts of the Katha Upanishad, the six parts of the Mundaka Upanishad, and Books 1, Parts 1 and 2 of the Taittiriyaka — and continues here into the Third Anuvaka without break in the chapter. Book 1, Part 3 of the Taittiriyaka — the entity here — occupies passages `5.169` through `5.179`. Passage `5.169` is the "Third Anuvaka" header. Passages `5.170` through `5.179` carry the body of the Anuvaka in Müller's prose presentation; the verse-numbered markers visible in the source are `1.` at the start of `5.170`, `2.` at the start of `5.173`, and `4.` at the start of `5.179`. The expected `3.` marker — which would naturally fall at the start of `5.174` ("Next, with regard to the heavenly lights") — is not present in the digitized source and appears to be a transcription artifact carried over from the OCR of Müller's volume; the surrounding text is otherwise complete and continuous, and the Anuvaka itself has no missing content. Passage `5.180` is the "Fourth Anuvaka" header that opens Book 1, Part 4 of the Taittiriyaka and is not part of this entity. The library chapter continues past this entity into the rest of the Taittiriyaka and the remaining Upanishads of the volume. About six minutes' reading.

## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Taittiriyaka Upanishad, Book 1, Part 2 — paired immediately consecutive Anuvakas of the Sikshavalli; the Second Anuvaka announces the topic — *let us explain Siksha, the doctrine of pronunciation* — and lists its six headings, the Third Anuvaka turns the technical term *samhita* (one of those headings, the union of letters) into the organizing image of a wider lesson on the joining of paired elements at five scales — the announcement and the first substantive instruction of the late-Vedic school-lesson, given in two adjacent units.
- **structural-parallel** → Analects, Book 1 (Xue Er) — paired short pedagogical openings that teach by paired correspondence rather than by argument; the Analects opens with a series of brief observational verses placing learning, friendship, and rectitude beside one another without collapsing them into a single thesis, the Taittiriyaka's Third Anuvaka teaches the *samhita* through the same procedure — five fields placed beside one another, each given the same shape — both texts holding the form of the school-lesson in two distinct civilizational vocabularies.
- **structural-parallel** → Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 4 (Jnana Karma Sanyasa Yoga) — paired Sanskrit passages that frame teaching as a transmissible discipline; the Gita's Chapter 4 develops the line that the imperishable yoga is told in succession from teacher to disciple and that the disciple may receive it through respectful approach and questioning, the Taittiriyaka's Third Anuvaka places the teacher–pupil pair as one of the five fields of the *samhita* — joined by knowledge through the recitation of the Veda — both texts placing the recitation between teacher and pupil at the centre of how the teaching reaches the next generation.
