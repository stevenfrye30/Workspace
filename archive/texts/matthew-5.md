# Gospel of Matthew, Chapter 5

Chapter 5 of the Gospel of Matthew is the opening of the Sermon on the Mount, the longest sustained discourse in the Gospel. Jesus ascends a mountain in Galilee, sits down with his disciples, and begins to teach the crowds gathered below. The chapter divides into three principal sections after the two-verse setting: the Beatitudes (5:3–12), eight short blessings on those who are poor in spirit, mourning, meek, hungry for righteousness, merciful, pure in heart, peacemakers, and persecuted; the metaphors of salt and light (5:13–16); and a sequence of six intensifications of the Mosaic law (5:21–48), each turning on the formula "You have heard… but I say unto you." The discourse continues through chapters 6 and 7.

## Context

The Gospel of Matthew is the first of the four canonical Gospels in the New Testament. Modern scholarship dates its composition to roughly 80 to 90 CE, after the destruction of the Jerusalem Temple in 70 CE, in a setting often associated with Antioch in Syria. The text is anonymous; the attribution to Matthew the tax collector, one of Jesus' disciples, is from the second century and is stable in the tradition by the time of Papias, around 130 CE. Matthew draws on at least two earlier sources — the Gospel of Mark, which it expands and rearranges, and a collection of sayings (commonly designated Q) shared with the Gospel of Luke — together with material distinctive to Matthew alone.

Chapters 5 to 7 of the Gospel form a single sustained discourse, traditionally called the Sermon on the Mount. It is the first of five long teaching discourses around which the Gospel is organized. Chapter 5 is the opening of the discourse. After two introductory verses that set the scene (5:1–2), the chapter falls into three principal blocks: the Beatitudes (5:3–12), the metaphors of salt and light (5:13–16), and a longer section on the Mosaic law (5:17–48), in which a four-verse statement on the Law and the Prophets (5:17–20) is followed by six intensifications of specific commands.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the chapter proceeds from blessing to instruction by way of metaphor. The Beatitudes are short formulaic blessings, each in the form "Blessed are X, for Y"; the salt-and-light section is a pair of brief metaphors addressed in the second person plural ("Ye are the salt of the earth… Ye are the light of the world"); the closing section is six longer instructions, each turning on a "Ye have heard that it was said… but I say unto you" contrast. Reading the chapter with the change of mode visible — blessing, then metaphor, then intensified instruction — recovers a structural feature that recitation can smooth into a single continuous teaching.

Second, the formula "Ye have heard that it was said… but I say unto you" is repeated six times in 5:21–48: on murder (5:21–26), adultery (5:27–30), divorce (5:31–32), oaths (5:33–37), retaliation (5:38–42), and love of enemies (5:43–48). The repetition is the figure. Reading the closing section as a six-part sequence built on the same formula, rather than as scattered ethical maxims, follows the way the chapter is built.
