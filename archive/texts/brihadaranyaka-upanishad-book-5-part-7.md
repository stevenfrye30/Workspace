# Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 5, Part 7

Book 5, Part 7 of the Brihadaranyaka Upanishad — the Seventh Brahmana of the Fifth Adhyaya — is one of the Brihadaranyaka's most compact Brahmanas, matching the Third and Sixth Brahmanas of the Adhyaya at two passages and one body passage. The Adhyaya has alternated through several procedural shapes — the Purnam mantra and Om-as-ether-as-Brahman triple identification (Part 1), the Pragapati-and-three-classes parable on the syllable Da (Part 2), the *hridaya* etymology with three substantive benefits (Part 3), the *true Brahman* chained identification with conquest-and-curse (Part 4), the cosmogony plus *satyam* etymology plus paired body-anatomies (Part 5), and the *manomaya purusha* within the heart with the *vrīhi-yava* size-simile (Part 6). The Seventh Brahmana works the procedural form again at its most compact: a single substantive identification, a *nirukti* etymology of one Sanskrit word, and a substantive benefit for the knower, all on one continuous sentence.

The substance of the Brahmana is held in three substantive operations on the single body passage at `5.1204`:

1. **Substantive identification**: *They say that lightning is Brahman.* The substantive equation names lightning (*vidyut*) as Brahman. The form is the same compact substantive equation the Adhyaya's earlier Brahmanas had given for Pragapati (Part 3), the heart (Parts 3 and 4), and the *manomaya purusha* (Part 6) — the substantive subject is now *vidyut*.
2. ***Nirukti* etymology**: *because lightning (vidyut) is called so from cutting off (vidanat)*. The Sanskrit word *vidyut* (lightning) is derived from the Sanskrit *vidanat* (cutting off, from the verbal root *vid-* + *do*, to sever). Müller's translation preserves both terms in parenthetical form, with the etymology given as the *because*-clause that grounds the substantive identification. The form holds the *upāsana-akshara* (meditation-syllable) tradition's procedure of grounding substantive identifications in the etymological seed of the Sanskrit word.
3. **Substantive benefit through pun**: *Whosoever knows this, that lightning is Brahman, him (that Brahman) cuts off from evil, for lightning indeed is Brahman.* The substantive benefit is that the knower is cut off from evil — by *that Brahman* (Müller's parenthetical preserves the substantive subject of the cutting action) — through the same cutting-off action that lightning performs. The pun runs on the verbal root: lightning *cuts* (*vidanat*); the knower is *cut off* from evil. The substantive efficacy of the *vidyā* runs through the same etymological seed as the substantive identification.

The closing of the verse — *for lightning indeed is Brahman* — gives the Brahmana its circular form. The Brahmana opens on *lightning is Brahman*, gives the etymology and the substantive benefit, and closes on *lightning indeed is Brahman*. The same identification appears at the open and at the close, with the etymology and the substantive benefit held inside.

## Context

The Brihadaranyaka Upanishad is one of the two earliest of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads, sharing that position with the Chandogya. It belongs to the Shukla Yajurveda branch of the Vedic corpus and forms the closing portion of the Shatapatha Brahmana — the *Aranyaka* in its name marks it as a forest-text recited at the late stages of Vedic study. Modern scholarship places the Brihadaranyaka in roughly the eighth to sixth century BCE, prior to the Buddha; the text is anonymous, and the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship. The text is the longest of the principal Upanishads and is typically structured in six *adhyayas* (chapters), each containing several *brahmanas* (sections).

Book 5, Part 7 holds two of the Adhyaya's distinctive features in concentrated form. The first is the *nirukti* etymological procedure on a single Sanskrit word. The Adhyaya has worked through three prior etymological procedures: the Second Brahmana's parable of the threefold *Da* (*Damyata, Datta, Dayadham*); the Third Brahmana's *hridaya* etymology of *hri-da-yam* with three substantive benefits; the Fifth Brahmana's *satyam* etymology of *sa-t(i)-yam* with the substantive doctrine that the middle is the untrue. The Seventh Brahmana works the *nirukti* procedure on a single Sanskrit word — *vidyut* — without the syllabic decomposition of the prior etymologies. The form is the most compressed instance of the *nirukti* procedure in the Adhyaya: not three syllables and three benefits, but one word and one benefit. The substantive root *vid-* (to sever, to cut) carries both the natural-phenomenon name (*vidyut*, lightning) and the substantive benefit (*the knower is cut off from evil*); the etymology is the substance of the *vidyā*.

The second is the substantive-action benefit-form. The Brahmana names the substantive benefit through the same verbal action that the natural phenomenon performs. Lightning cuts the sky (*vidanat*); the substantive Brahman cuts the knower off from evil (*that Brahman cuts off from evil*); the cutting-action runs across the natural and the substantive registers as the same root-verb. The form holds the doctrine that the substantive benefit of the *vidyā* is not a separate effect but the same action the natural phenomenon performs, transferred from the cosmic-natural register to the substantive-spiritual register through knowledge of the substantive identification. The Adhyaya's earlier Brahmanas had given substantive benefits in distinct forms — being brought offerings, conquest of the worlds, going to heaven, destruction of evil — and the Seventh Brahmana's *cut off from evil* benefit holds the same form of the *vidyā*'s substantive efficacy worked through the etymological action of the named subject.

The closing form is the Adhyaya's standard compact close. *For lightning indeed is Brahman.* The same substantive equation that opened the verse closes it, with no further commentary, no narrator's voice, no *held his peace* form. The Brahmana ends inside its substance.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the Brahmana's three operations should be read as one continuous substantive identification rather than as three separate doctrinal points. The substantive equation (*lightning is Brahman*), the *nirukti* etymology (*vidyut from vidanat*), and the substantive benefit (*cut off from evil*) sit on a single sentence in Müller's translation, and their interdependence is preserved in the underlying Sanskrit's compound-form. Reading the three as one continuous identification — neither expanded into a doctrine of natural-phenomena-as-Brahman nor flattened into a generic statement of substantive-knowledge benefit — recovers the Brahmana's literary form: one substantive equation, one etymological seed, one substantive benefit, all moving through the same verbal root.

Second, the parenthetical *(that Brahman)* in the closing clause should be preserved as Müller's translation-form rather than as a doctrinal addition. The substantive subject of the cutting-action is named in parenthesis to clarify the pronoun-reference in the underlying Sanskrit; the parenthetical is part of the digitized source's translation procedure. Reading the parenthetical as substantive content — *him (that Brahman) cuts off from evil* — preserves the Brahmana's substantive identification of the cutting-Brahman with the lightning-Brahman of the opening clause.

The Brahmana contains a small set of boundary features that should be preserved honestly:

- **Single-passage body.** The Brahmana's entire substantive content sits on one passage, `5.1204`. No multi-passage span, no internal verse-divisions beyond the verse-1 marker, no narrative interruptions. The form matches the Third and Sixth Brahmanas of the Adhyaya as among the most compact in the Brihadaranyaka.
- **Footnote numbering irregularity.** Passage `5.1204` carries footnote `[*3]` on the etymology *from cutting off (vidanat)*. No footnote `[*1]` or `[*2]` is given on any passage of the Brahmana. The numbering irregularity matches the form of the Fourth Brahmana of the Adhyaya at `5.1194` (which begins at `[*2]`) and the Sixth Brahmana at `5.1202` (which also begins at `[*2]`); the Seventh Brahmana begins at `[*3]`. The numbering reflects Müller's footnote-sequencing across the Adhyaya rather than within each Brahmana, and the substance of the verse is unaffected.
- **Three substantive operations in one continuous sentence.** The verse-1 body holds the substantive equation, the *nirukti* etymology, and the substantive benefit in one continuous sentence with no internal verse-numbering or punctuation-break beyond the periods that separate the substantive clauses. Müller's parentheticals — *(vidyut)* on the named subject, *(vidanat)* on the etymological seed, *(that Brahman)* on the substantive subject of the cutting-action — preserve the underlying Sanskrit terms and substantive references in the digitized source.
- **No closing seal beyond the circular affirmation.** The Brahmana closes on *for lightning indeed is Brahman* — the same substantive equation that opened the verse, with the *indeed* emphatic added. No closing narrator's line, no *held his peace* form, no further commentary. The form is the Adhyaya's standard compact close, matching the closing forms of Parts 3, 4, 5, and 6.

Passage `5.1205` is the "Eighth Brahmana" header that opens Book 5, Part 8 of the Brihadaranyaka and is not part of this entity. ## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 5, Part 6 — paired immediate-predecessor Brahmanas of the Fifth Adhyaya holding the most compact procedural form in the Brihadaranyaka. The Sixth Brahmana works the *manomaya purusha* identification within the heart with the *vrīhi-yava* size-simile and universal sovereignty as the closing clause: *That person, under the form of mind (manas), being light indeed, is within the heart, small like a grain of rice or barley. He is the ruler of all, the lord of all — he rules all this, whatsoever exists.* The Seventh Brahmana works the lightning-as-Brahman identification with the *vidyut/vidanat* etymology and the cutting-off-from-evil benefit as the closing clause. Both Brahmanas are two passages long with one body passage holding the entire teaching, and both close on circular affirmations of the substantive equation. The two Brahmanas hold the Adhyaya's most compressed form across paired procedural shapes — the *manomaya* substantive identification with size-simile in the Sixth, the *vidyut* substantive identification with etymology in the Seventh.
- **structural-parallel** → Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 5, Part 3 — paired Brahmanas of the Fifth Adhyaya holding *nirukti* etymological derivations as the substantive form of the *vidyā*. The Third Brahmana works the *hridaya* (heart) etymology of *hri-da-yam* with three substantive benefits — being brought offerings, being brought gifts, going to heaven — across three substantive syllables. The Seventh Brahmana works the *vidyut* (lightning) etymology of *vidanat* with one substantive benefit — being cut off from evil — across one Sanskrit word without syllabic decomposition. The two Brahmanas hold paired *nirukti* procedures at different scales: three syllables and three benefits in the Third, one word and one benefit in the Seventh. The Fifth Brahmana works the *satyam* etymology of *sa-t(i)-yam* inside the cosmogonic chain *water → the true → Brahman → Pragapati → the Devas* and the substantive doctrine that the middle syllable is the untrue enclosed by the true. The Seventh Brahmana works the *vidyut* etymology of *vidanat* inside the substantive equation *lightning is Brahman* and the substantive doctrine that the cutting-off action of lightning is the cutting-off-from-evil benefit of the knower. Both Brahmanas hold the *vidyā* procedure with the substantive identification, the etymological seed, and the substantive benefit in continuous form, with the Seventh Brahmana giving the procedure in radically more compressed scale than the Fifth.
