# Mundaka Upanishad, Book 3, Part 1

Book 3, Part 1 of the Mundaka Upanishad — the First Khanda of the Third Mundaka — opens the closing book of the Upanishad. The opening verses set the most-cited image of the Mundaka: two birds, inseparable friends, cling to the same tree; one of them eats the sweet fruit, the other looks on without eating. On the same tree, the verse continues, man sits grieving, bewildered by his own impotence (*an-isa*); but when he sees the other lord (*isa*) contented and knows his glory, his grief passes away. From the figure the Khanda turns to the recognition: when the seer sees the brilliant maker as the Person who has his source in Brahman, he is wise — and shaking off good and evil, he reaches the highest oneness, free from passions. The verses that follow name the path — by truthfulness, by penance, by right knowledge, by abstinence — and hold the line that the true prevails, not the untrue (*satyam eva jayate*), the way of the gods on which the old sages, satisfied in their desires, proceed to the highest place of the True One. The Khanda gathers the recapitulating verses — that the true Brahman shines forth grand, divine, inconceivable, smaller than small, far beyond what is far and yet near here, hidden in the cave of the heart; that he is not apprehended by the eye or by speech or by the other senses, nor by penance or good works, but seen when the nature has been purified by the serene light of knowledge — and closes with the verse that whatever state the man whose nature is purified imagines, and whatever desires he desires, that state he conquers and those desires he obtains.

## Context

The Mundaka Upanishad is one of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads — a small group of texts traditionally received as the philosophical core of the late Vedic literature. It is associated with the Atharvaveda branch of the Vedic corpus and is composed in Sanskrit verse. Modern scholarship places its composition somewhat later than the Katha, in roughly the fourth to second century BCE; the principal Upanishads as a group are dated to the centuries on either side of the Buddha. The text is anonymous; the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship.

Book 3, Part 1 is the fifth of the six parts of the Mundaka and continues directly from Book 2, Part 2. The Second Mundaka set out the source from which the world emerges and the inward approach by which the seeker reaches it; the Third Mundaka opens by giving the recognition that the inward approach has prepared. The literary movement is from instruction into seeing. The two-birds figure is not new doctrine — it is the same recognition the preceding parts have developed, condensed into a single image; the line *satyam eva jayate* is not a metaphysical claim but a placement of the path on which the recognition is held; the verses on what cannot be apprehended by the senses are returns of formulations the Katha and the earlier Mundaka parts have already given. The verse on the two birds is shared in close form with the older Rigveda (1.164.20) and with the Shvetashvatara Upanishad (4.6); the shared verse marks one of the older threads of the Upanishadic literature.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the Khanda is built on return rather than novelty. Earlier parts of the Mundaka named the two kinds of knowledge, the source from which the world emerges, the bow-and-arrow figure of inward approach, the centering of the heart where the arteries meet like spokes around a nave. The Third Mundaka does not introduce new instruction; it gathers what has been given. The two birds are an image of the duality the earlier verses have prepared; the "smaller than small, far yet near" line is a return of imagery the Katha and the earlier Mundaka khandas have already used; the verse on the Self not apprehended by the senses is a return of a formula the Katha gave in the Second Valli. Reading the Khanda with attention to which earlier figure each verse is gathering — and to the slight rotation of vocabulary that gives each return its place in the closing book — recovers the form the teaching has.

Second, the Khanda holds recognition through observation rather than declaration. The seer who sees the brilliant maker, the one who shakes off good and evil and reaches the highest oneness, the one whose nature has been purified by the serene light of knowledge — these are presented as what is seen by such a seer, not as conclusions argued from premises. The line that the true prevails sits inside the same observational register: it is placed on the way of the gods that the old sages have walked, not given as a doctrine to be defended. Reading the verses as observation of what the prepared seer sees, rather than as positions to be held, keeps the form the Mundaka gives this part of its closing instruction.

## The text

The text is held in the Archive library under `upanishads-muller-part2`, chapter 5 (Müller translation, 1884 — *The Sacred Books of the East*, Volume XV; via SacredTexts.com). Library chapter 5 is shared across the Katha entries (Book 1, Part 3 and Book 2, Parts 1–3) and the Mundaka entries already in the Archive (Book 1, Parts 1 and 2 at passages `5.77` through `5.105`, Book 2, Part 1 at passages `5.106` through `5.118`, and Book 2, Part 2 at passages `5.119` through `5.131`); Book 3, Part 1 of the Mundaka — the entity here — occupies passages `5.132` through `5.144`. Passage `5.132` is the "Third Mundaka" book header and `5.133` is the "First Khanda" part header. Passages `5.134` through `5.144` carry the ten numbered verses of the Khanda, with verse 10 split across passages `5.143` and `5.144` where Müller's editorial paragraphing interrupts the verse mid-line. Passage `5.145` is the "Second Khanda" header that opens Book 3, Part 2 of the Mundaka and is not part of this entity. The library chapter continues past this entity into the closing Khanda of the Mundaka and the remaining Upanishads of the volume (the next book in the chapter, the Taittiriyaka, opens at passage `5.160`). Ten verses; about eight minutes' reading.

## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 15 (Purushottama Yoga) — paired Sanskrit passages on a duality of persons within a single field; the Gita's Chapter 15 distinguishes the perishable (*kshara*) and the imperishable (*akshara*) as two persons in the world and names a third, the highest Person (*purushottama*), who upholds them both, the Mundaka's Third Mundaka opens with the figure of the two birds clinging to the same tree, one eating the fruit and the other looking on, with the seer's recognition of the second as the lord whose grief passes away — paired structural figures of duality-and-witness within the same Sanskrit philosophical line.
- **structural-parallel** → Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 18 (Moksha Sanyasa Yoga) — paired closing teachings; the Gita's Chapter 18 is Krishna's final recapitulation of the entire teaching, gathering action, knowledge, devotion, and renunciation into a single concluding instruction, the Mundaka's Third Mundaka opens the closing book of the Upanishad by gathering its earlier figures — the two kinds of knowledge, the source of the elements, the inward approach — into a sequence of recognitions held in a single Khanda, the same form of culminating recapitulation in the two texts.
- **structural-parallel** → Katha Upanishad, Book 2, Part 3 — paired Upanishadic closings on what cannot be apprehended by the senses; the Katha's Sixth Valli holds the line that the Self cannot be reached by speech, by mind, or by the eye, and is apprehended by the one who says "He is" (Katha 2.3.12), the Mundaka's First Khanda holds the closely related line that he is not apprehended by the eye, nor by speech, nor by the other senses, not by penance or good works, but seen when the nature has been purified by the serene light of knowledge (Mundaka 3.1.8) — paired moments in the same Upanishadic family, the same recognition given in two close vocabularies at the close of each text.
