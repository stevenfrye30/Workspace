# Egyptian Book of the Dead, Spell 125

Spell 125 of the *Book of the Dead* — the chapter known as the Declaration of Innocence — is the funerary text in which the deceased addresses the court of the underworld and lists the wrongs he has not done. It is the principal chapter for the judgment of the dead in the New Kingdom afterlife: the heart is weighed against the feather of Ma'at while the deceased recites a long accounting of his moral life. Among the dozens of spells gathered into the *Book of the Dead* corpus, Spell 125 is the one most often reproduced, the most fully illustrated, and the chapter in which the deceased gives the most direct account of the life he has led.

## Context

The *Book of the Dead* is the modern name for what the Egyptians called the *Spells for Going Forth by Day* — a collection of funerary texts placed in tombs to assist the deceased in passing through the dangers of the underworld. The corpus took shape in the New Kingdom (c. 1550–1070 BCE) as the successor to the older Coffin Texts and Pyramid Texts, and was copied onto papyrus rolls, linen, and tomb walls for roughly fifteen hundred years. There is no single canonical *Book of the Dead*; each surviving copy is a selection drawn from a much larger common stock of around two hundred numbered spells. The numbering used by modern editors is a nineteenth-century convention established by the Egyptologist Richard Lepsius in 1842.

Spell 125 is the chapter for the judgment scene. Its core consists of two declarations — the deceased addresses Osiris and his court, then turns to a council of forty-two assessor gods, naming each in turn and stating one specific wrong he has not committed. The longest and most elaborately illustrated copy is on the Papyrus of Ani (British Museum EA 10470), a roll about seventy-eight feet long made for the scribe Ani around 1250 BCE.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, Spell 125 is liturgical in form and forensic in function. The two declarations are spoken by the deceased; they are not narrative but speech. The forty-two short statements ("I have not committed sin... I have not killed... I have not stolen...") read as a recitation rather than a list, and reading the text in a single sitting recovers a rhythm that page-by-page reading flattens.

Second, the spell is a chapter, not a book. It belongs to a much larger funerary corpus and was copied alongside dozens of other spells, but it is also internally complete: the deceased enters the Hall of the Two Truths, speaks the two declarations, names each assessor god, and is judged. Reading Spell 125 alone — as a discrete unit — is closer to the way it functioned for an Egyptian copyist or a modern reader than reading the *Book of the Dead* as a continuous book.

## The text

The text is held in the Archive library under `book-of-dead-renouf`, chapter 25 (Renouf and Naville translation, 1904 — Project Gutenberg edition). Spell 125 itself runs roughly seven to ten pages in modern translation, including both declarations and the address to the assessors; about half an hour's reading.

## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Plato, Apology — both are accounts of a life given before a court of judgment; the two declarations of Spell 125 perform the same function as Socrates' defense, in a different forensic geometry.
- **structural-parallel** → Augustine, Confessions, Book I — both are first-person accounts spoken before a divine listener; Spell 125 is a funerary judgment-account, the *Confessions* a later moral-accounting in autobiographical form.
