# Taittiriyaka Upanishad, Book 2, Part 7

Book 2, Part 7 of the Taittiriyaka Upanishad — the Seventh Anuvaka of the Brahmanandavalli — extends the creation formula the Sixth Anuvaka opened. The opening sloka, set off in Müller's quotation marks, gives the creation-and-self-making line: *asad vā idam agra āsīt; tato vai sad ajāyata; tad ātmānaṁ svayam akuruta; tasmāt tat sukṛtam ucyata iti* — *in the beginning this was non-existent; from it was born what exists; that made itself its Self, therefore it is called the Self-made.* The prose that immediately follows the sloka turns the recognition: *yad vai tat sukṛtam, raso vai saḥ; rasaṁ hy evāyaṁ labdhvānandī bhavati* — *that which is Self-made is a flavour; for only after perceiving a flavour can any one perceive pleasure.* The line is brief and is among the most-quoted in the Upanishadic corpus: *raso vai saḥ* — *he is flavour itself* — naming Brahman as the source from which the perceiving of pleasure first becomes possible. The Anuvaka then asks the rhetorical question that the line lives inside: *who could breathe, who could breathe forth, if that bliss did not exist in the ether of the heart?* and answers in compact form: *eṣa hy evānandayāti* — *for he alone causes blessedness.* The Anuvaka closes with the recognition the Brahmanandavalli has been preparing: when one finds freedom from fear and rest in that which is invisible, incorporeal, undefined, unsupported, then one has obtained the fearless — and with the caveat that the smallest distinction made in that fearlessness reintroduces fear; that fear, the Anuvaka adds, exists only for one who thinks himself wise, and not for the true sage. The closing pointer *on this there is also this sloka* turns the reader toward the Eighth Anuvaka.

## Context

The Taittiriyaka Upanishad is one of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads — a small group of texts traditionally received as the philosophical core of the late Vedic literature. It belongs to the Krishna Yajurveda branch of the Vedic corpus and to the Taittiriya school of that branch; the Upanishad sits inside the Taittiriya Aranyaka. Modern scholarship places it among the older prose Upanishads, in roughly the sixth to fifth century BCE — earlier than the verse Upanishads of the Katha and Mundaka. The text is anonymous; the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship.

Book 2, Part 7 sits inside the Brahmanandavalli, the Second Valli of the Taittiriyaka. The Sixth Anuvaka left the recursive descent behind and turned to pupil-questions and the *so 'kāmayata* creation formula; the Seventh Anuvaka does not return to the recursive form either, but it extends the creation formula and adds two of the most-cited recognitions of the Brahmanandavalli — the *raso vai saḥ* line on Brahman as flavour, and the *abhaya* recognition on Brahman as the place of fearlessness. The Anuvakas that follow this one will continue to develop the recognition in further forms, including the famous "examination of bliss" calculation in the Eighth Anuvaka and the closing recognitions of the Valli; for now, the Seventh Anuvaka holds the moment at which the recognition the recursive descent had reached is given two of its most condensed names.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the Anuvaka holds the same paratactic motion the Sixth Anuvaka opened — sloka, prose, recognition, caveat, pointer — and adds two compact lines that are among the most-quoted of the Upanishadic corpus. *Raso vai saḥ* — *he is flavour itself* — names Brahman as the first source of any taste of pleasure; the rhetorical question that follows it places the same recognition in the heart-ether. Reading the Anuvaka with attention to the brevity of these lines, and to how they sit inside the longer prose that surrounds them, recovers the form the Brahmanandavalli has when it works in its most compressed mode.

Second, the Anuvaka adds a caveat that does not soften the recognition but locates it. The fearlessness obtained in the invisible, the incorporeal, the undefined, the unsupported is not unconditional: *if he makes but the smallest distinction in it, there is fear for him.* The caveat is given in the same calm prose as the recognition itself, and it is paired with the line that this fear belongs to the one who thinks himself wise rather than to the true sage. Reading the Anuvaka with the caveat held alongside the recognition, rather than separated from it, keeps the form the late-Vedic prose has when it is most careful about what it is naming.

## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Taittiriyaka Upanishad, Book 2, Part 6 — paired immediately consecutive Anuvakas of the Brahmanandavalli; the Sixth Anuvaka closed the recursive descent, opened the pupil-question form, and gave the *so 'kāmayata* creation formula and the *sat/tyat* distinction, the Seventh Anuvaka extends the creation formula into the *Self-made* and adds the *raso vai saḥ* and *abhaya* recognitions. The dialogic and creation motion that the Sixth Anuvaka opened continues in the Seventh, with two compact recognitions added inside the same paratactic prose.
- **structural-parallel** → Mundaka Upanishad, Book 2, Part 1 — paired Sanskrit creation formulas and the recognition that the source has entered into what it has sent forth; the Mundaka's First Khanda of the Second Mundaka holds the long unfolding *from the Imperishable various beings come forth and return there again* and names the cosmic Person whose body is the elements (Mundaka 2.1.1–4), the Taittiriyaka's Seventh Anuvaka holds the closely related recognition in two short formulas — *that made itself its Self, therefore it is called the Self-made; he is flavour itself; for he alone causes blessedness*. Both texts hold the same moment of recognition that the source becomes the world it has sent forth, and that the recognition of this is the recognition of bliss.
- **structural-parallel** → Katha Upanishad, Book 2, Part 3 — paired Upanishadic recognitions of fearlessness reached when distinction is set aside; the Katha's Sixth Valli closes with the line *when all the ties of the heart are severed here on earth, then the mortal becomes immortal* (Katha 2.3.15) and reaches the imperishable through the abandonment of all desires, the Taittiriyaka's Seventh Anuvaka holds the closely related line that the fearless is obtained when one rests in that which is invisible, incorporeal, undefined, unsupported, with the caveat that the smallest distinction made in it reintroduces fear. Paired moments where the late-Vedic literature places fearlessness in the absence of distinction.
