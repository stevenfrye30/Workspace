# Taittiriyaka Upanishad, Book 1, Part 9

Book 1, Part 9 of the Taittiriyaka Upanishad — the Ninth Anuvaka of the Sikshavalli — is the *svādhyāya-pravacana* lesson, named for the closing formula that recurs through the whole Anuvaka: *and learning and practising the Veda*. The form of the Anuvaka is a refrain. Each named obligation is given, and then the same formula follows. The night, *and learning and practising the Veda*. The true, *and learning and practising the Veda*. Penance, *and learning and practising the Veda*. Restraint, *and learning and practising the Veda*. Tranquility, *and learning and practising the Veda*. The fires to be consecrated, *and learning and practising the Veda*. The Agnihotra sacrifice, *and learning and practising the Veda*. Guests to be entertained, *and learning and practising the Veda*. Man's duty, *and learning and practising the Veda*. Marriage, *and learning and practising the Veda*. Children's children, *and learning and practising the Veda*. The Anuvaka closes with the brief contest of three named teachers, each given as the proponent of a single priority. *Satyavakas Rathitara thinks that the true only is necessary. Taponitya Paurasishti thinks that penance only is necessary. Naka Maudgalya thinks that learning and practising the Veda only are necessary — for that is penance, that is penance.* The doubled close — *tad eva tapaḥ, tad eva tapaḥ* — gives the verse its sealing repetition.

## Context

The Taittiriyaka Upanishad is one of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads — a small group of texts traditionally received as the philosophical core of the late Vedic literature. It belongs to the Krishna Yajurveda branch of the Vedic corpus and to the Taittiriya school of that branch; the Upanishad sits inside the Taittiriya Aranyaka. Modern scholarship places it among the older prose Upanishads, in roughly the sixth to fifth century BCE — earlier than the verse Upanishads of the Katha and Mundaka. The text is anonymous; the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship.

Book 1, Part 9 sits inside the Sikshavalli, the chapter on *śikṣā*. The Anuvaka turns from the Eighth Anuvaka's praise of the syllable that opens recitation to the praise of the practice that holds it: *svādhyāya-pravacana*, the disciplined daily learning and recitation of the Veda. The Anuvaka does not argue that *svādhyāya* is the highest discipline; it lists each of the named obligations of the Brahmana's life and shows that each is paired with *svādhyāya*, and then it gives the floor to three named teachers who each, in their own emphatic single voice, name what they hold to be the necessary thing. The Anuvaka has been kept in liturgical and pedagogical use ever since for the doubled closing line — *tad eva tapaḥ, tad eva tapaḥ* — that names study itself as penance.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the Anuvaka is built almost entirely on a single repeated formula. Each clause has the same shape — a named obligation, followed by *and learning and practising the Veda* — and the cumulative effect is that the formula is heard twelve times in close succession before the closing turn to the three teachers. Reading the Anuvaka aloud, allowing the refrain to register through repetition rather than through commentary, recovers the form the late-Vedic school-lesson has when its instruction is most direct.

Second, the closing contest is short and not adjudicated. Three named teachers are given in turn, each given a single emphatic position, and the Anuvaka ends without the text choosing among them. The closing line — *for that is penance, that is penance* — sits inside Naka Maudgalya's voice, not inside an editorial verdict. Reading the Anuvaka with the three voices held alongside one another, rather than treating Naka Maudgalya's view as the text's settled doctrine, keeps the form the school-lesson has — the disagreement is preserved, and the refrain that preceded it is the part the lesson asks the student to take away.

## The text

The text is held in the Archive library under `upanishads-muller-part2`, chapter 5 (Müller translation, 1884 — *The Sacred Books of the East*, Volume XV; via SacredTexts.com). Library chapter 5 is shared across eighteen Archive entities — four parts of the Katha Upanishad, the six parts of the Mundaka Upanishad, and Books 1, Parts 1 through 8 of the Taittiriyaka — and continues here into the Ninth Anuvaka without break in the chapter. Book 1, Part 9 of the Taittiriyaka — the entity here — occupies passages `5.208` through `5.211`. Passage `5.208` is the "Ninth Anuvaka" header and carries its own footnote reference `[*1]` — Müller's editorial annotation of the Anuvaka as a whole. Passage `5.209` carries verse 1 with the long enumerative refrain in a single passage, opening with the verse marker `1.` and Müller's bracketed editorial gloss "(What is necessary?)" before the first item. Passage `5.210` opens with the editorial sub-marker `(1-6)` carried over from the source — Müller's bracketed grouping that signals the closing two items of the refrain (marriage, children's children) as continuations of verse 1 rather than as new verses of their own. Passage `5.211` carries the closing portion of the verse with the three named teachers and Naka Maudgalya's doubled close. Passage `5.212` is the "Tenth Anuvaka" header that opens Book 1, Part 10 of the Taittiriyaka and is not part of this entity. The library chapter continues past this entity into the rest of the Taittiriyaka and the remaining Upanishads of the volume. About three minutes' reading.

## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Taittiriyaka Upanishad, Book 1, Part 8 — paired immediately consecutive Anuvakas of the Sikshavalli; the Eighth Anuvaka praises the syllable that opens recitation by listing the contexts in which *Om* stands first, the Ninth Anuvaka praises the practice that holds recitation by listing the obligations under which *svādhyāya-pravacana* stands beside — the same paratactic procedure of the late-Vedic school-lesson, applied to the syllable and to the practice in two adjacent units.
- **structural-parallel** → Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 4 (Jnana Karma Sanyasa Yoga) — paired Sanskrit passages that name *svādhyāya* among the central disciplines; the Gita's Chapter 4 places *svādhyāya-jñāna-yajña* — the sacrifice of study and the sacrifice of knowledge — inside the long catalogue of *yajñas* by which the recipient is prepared for the teaching (Gita 4.28), the Taittiriyaka's Ninth Anuvaka places the same practice — *svādhyāya-pravacana*, learning and practising the Veda — beside every named obligation as the discipline without which the others do not stand. Both texts give the same recognition that study itself is the practice that holds the rest, in close vocabulary across the late-Vedic prose and the epic verse.
- **structural-parallel** → Analects, Book 1 (Xue Er) — paired short pedagogical openings on learning-and-practising as the central discipline; the Analects opens with the line *xue er shi xi zhi* — "to learn and to practise it at proper intervals, is this not a pleasure?" (1.1) — placing learning and practising at the head of the text as its own self-justifying activity, the Taittiriyaka's Ninth Anuvaka places the same learning and practising of the Veda as the refrain that pairs every other obligation, and ends with Naka Maudgalya's emphatic line that learning and practising the Veda is itself penance. Both texts hold learning-and-practising as the discipline at the centre of the school's life, in two distinct civilizational vocabularies.
