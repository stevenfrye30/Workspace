# Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 5 (Karma Sannyasa Yoga)

Chapter 5 of the Bhagavad Gita — the *Karma Sannyasa Yoga*, conventionally translated "The Yoga of Renouncing the Fruits of Action" — returns directly to the tension Arjuna has been raising through the early Gita: which is better, renunciation of works, or work performed without attachment? The chapter opens with Arjuna's complaint that Krishna has praised both at different times and a request to be told plainly which is the better way. Krishna's reply, sustained across twenty-nine verses, is that the two are not opposed: each leads to the same end, but the path of disciplined action is more reliably available than renunciation alone, and the practiced yogi performs and renounces in the same act. The middle of the chapter restates the disposition Krishna has been building toward — the yogi acts without attachment, sees the same in all beings, finds joy within rather than in the senses — and the closing verses give a short practical instruction on holding the inner self steady within outward action.

## Context

The Bhagavad Gita is a 700-verse philosophical poem in eighteen chapters, set inside Book VI (the *Bhishma Parva*) of the Mahabharata. Most scholars place its present form between roughly the second century BCE and the second century CE — a layered composition that took shape during the long growth of the epic from an older bardic core. The traditional attribution is to Vyasa, the legendary compiler of the Mahabharata; the historical authorship is unknown.

Chapter 5 returns to a question Arjuna has been raising since Chapter 3: if the inner self is undisturbed, is renunciation of action better than disciplined action? Chapter 3 had given a partial answer (action cannot be avoided); Chapter 4 had expanded the frame (the wise see action in inaction; sacrifice is the form of binding-free action). Chapter 5 is shorter than the chapters that precede it — twenty-nine verses — and largely synthesizes rather than introduces. Its argument is that the apparent opposition between renunciation (*sannyasa*) and the yoga of action (*karma yoga*) is not a real one: each leads to the same end, but the path of action without attachment is the more available of the two, and the practiced yogi performs and renounces in the same act. The chapter closes with a short practical passage on the inner disposition that holds the self steady — the breath fixed between the brows, the senses controlled, the mind resting in the lord of all sacrifice.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the chapter is mostly restatement. Renunciation, action without attachment, the equal vision of the yogi who sees the same in brahmin and outcaste, the inner joy that does not depend on the senses — these are themes Chapters 2–4 have already laid down. Chapter 5 returns to them and works through their relations once more. Reading the chapter as compact synthesis, rather than as new doctrine, follows the way the chapter is composed.

Second, the chapter alternates between two registers — outward conduct and inward disposition — without separating them. A passage on what the yogi does in the world (acts without attachment, sees the same in all beings) sits beside a passage on the inner state that makes such conduct possible (the joy found within, the steady attention, the breath held between the brows). Reading the chapter with the alternation visible, rather than expecting outer and inner to be addressed in separate stretches, recovers the form Krishna gives his answer.

## The text

The text is held in the Archive library under `bhagavad-gita`, chapter 5 (Edwin Arnold's verse translation, *The Song Celestial*, 1885 — Project Gutenberg edition). Arnold's verse translation compresses the chapter's twenty-nine Sanskrit shlokas into five English verse-paragraphs followed by a closing colophon line (passages `5.1`–`5.6` in the library's verse-level subdivision). ## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 4 (Jnana Yoga) — the immediate Gita-internal pair; Chapter 4 expands the argument with the lineage of teachers, the doctrine of recurring divine births, and the practice of sacrifice; Chapter 5 contracts the argument back to the original tension between renunciation and action and synthesizes the answer Krishna has been building.
- **structural-parallel** → Dhammapada, Chapter 2 (Heedfulness) — paired short chapters built on a single returning contrast; the Dhammapada's *Appamāda-vagga* turns twelve verses on the contrast between heedfulness and its lapse, Chapter 5 of the Gita turns twenty-nine verses on the contrast between renunciation and disciplined action and the practiced inner attention that resolves the two.
- **structural-parallel** → Epictetus, Enchiridion, Chapters 1–5 — paired short instructional sequences on inward freedom within outward circumstance; the Enchiridion sets the distinction between what is and is not within one's power and works it across the practices of desire, attachment, action, and the source of disturbance, Chapter 5 of the Gita sets the inner disposition that performs action without binding and renounces without withdrawing from the world.
