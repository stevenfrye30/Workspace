# Song of Songs, Chapter 1

Chapter 1 of the Song of Songs opens the only book of the Hebrew Bible composed entirely as voiced love poetry. After a one-verse superscription attributing the book to Solomon, the chapter proceeds without narration, as a sequence of speeches: a woman's desire for a man, her self-description before the daughters of Jerusalem, her question about where to find him, his answering praise, and the close of the chapter in mutual address. The voices alternate; the speakers are not labeled; the setting changes from line to line.

## Context

The Song of Songs — *Shir HaShirim* in Hebrew, "the song of songs," a Hebrew superlative construction meaning "the greatest of songs" — is the shortest of the books of the Ketuvim (Writings) and the most lyrical book of the Hebrew Bible: eight short chapters, 117 verses in total. The book is composed entirely as a sequence of love poems, with two principal voices — a young woman and a young man — and occasional choral responses. There is no narrative frame, no prophetic context, and no instructional address. Within the Jewish tradition, the book is one of the Five Megillot, the five short scrolls read on the major festivals; it is read at Passover.

The book carries Solomon's name in its opening verse — "the song of songs, which is Solomon's" — and tradition long associated it with him; modern scholarship treats the Solomonic attribution as a literary frame and dates the composition to the Persian or early Hellenistic period (roughly fifth to third centuries BCE), with the Hebrew showing late features and Persian and possibly Greek loanwords. Chapter 1 is the opening of the book. After the superscription (1:1), the chapter is composed of seven short lyric units in alternation: the young woman's voice, choral responses from the group addressed as the daughters of Jerusalem, and the young man's answering speech. The Hebrew text does not label the speakers; their identities are inferred from the gender and number of the verbs and pronouns.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the chapter is composed entirely of voiced speech with no narrator. The Hebrew text supplies no stage directions. The shifts between speakers — woman, man, chorus — are visible only through the gendered grammar of the Hebrew, and English translations either supply speaker labels (as in many modern editions) or leave the changes implicit. Reading the chapter with attention to the change of voice from line to line, rather than as a single continuous utterance, recovers the form the text has.

Second, the speeches move between modes — invitation, self-description, question, answer, and mutual praise — without an underlying narrative connecting them. The woman addresses the man, then turns to the daughters of Jerusalem, then asks the man a question; the man responds in praise; the exchange continues. Reading the chapter with attention to the sequence of speech-acts, rather than to any story they might be told to advance, follows the form.
