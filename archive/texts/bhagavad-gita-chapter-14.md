# Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 14 (Guna Traya Vibhaga Yoga)

Chapter 14 of the Bhagavad Gita — the *Guna Traya Vibhaga Yoga*, conventionally translated "The Yoga of the Distinction Between the Three Qualities" (Arnold's translation gives the chapter the parallel name "The Book of Religion by Separation from the Qualities") — develops the analytic move of Chapter 13 from a single distinction (field and knower) into a threefold classification. The chapter introduces the *gunas* — *sattva* (purity, light, knowledge), *rajas* (passion, activity, restlessness), and *tamas* (darkness, inertia, delusion) — as the three modes through which *prakriti* binds the embodied. Each *guna* is named, defined, given its effect on action, its symptoms, the outcome at death of those it dominates, and the fruit it produces; the triadic comparison runs through the chapter's middle. The closing verses turn from the typology to its transcendence: a portrait of the person who has gone beyond the three, who does not seek any of them when they recede or resist them when they rise, who sits as a witness. The chapter ends with the teaching that unswerving devotion crosses beyond the *gunas* and prepares the absorption in Brahman.

## Context

The Bhagavad Gita is a 700-verse philosophical poem in eighteen chapters, set inside Book VI (the *Bhishma Parva*) of the Mahabharata. Most scholars place its present form between roughly the second century BCE and the second century CE — a layered composition that took shape during the long growth of the epic from an older bardic core. The traditional attribution is to Vyasa, the legendary compiler of the Mahabharata; the historical authorship is unknown.

Chapter 14 continues the late classificatory movement begun in Chapter 13. Where Chapter 13 set the principal distinction — field (*kshetra*) and knower of the field (*kshetrajna*), nature and the conscious witness — Chapter 14 turns to the structure within nature itself and develops it as a threefold classification. The three *gunas* appear briefly in earlier chapters but here receive their sustained treatment. The chapter is short (twenty-seven verses in the standard Sanskrit numbering) and structurally simple: a brief introduction (14.1–4), the threefold definition with each *guna*'s symptoms and effects (14.5–18), and a closing portrait of one who has transcended the three (14.19–27). The comparison-pattern Chapter 14 establishes — three modes named, each given its description and outcome — recurs through the closing chapters of the Gita, where faith, food, action, and the agent are each given the same triadic treatment.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the chapter is built on triadic comparison. Each *guna* is treated in turn — defined, given its effect, named with its symptoms, traced through its outcome at death and the fruit it produces — and then the chapter returns to the triad and treats it again from another angle. Reading the chapter with the threefold structure held visible, rather than as a continuous argument, recovers the form. The same comparison-pattern Chapter 14 establishes will be repeated in the closing chapters for other categories — faith, food, action, agent — so noticing how the pattern works here is preparation for those.

Second, the chapter ends with a portrait that parallels the portrait of the devotee in Chapter 12. The person who has gone beyond the three *gunas* (14.22–25) does not hate the modes when they arise nor long for them when they leave; sits as a witness; is the same in pleasure and pain, gold and clay, blame and praise, friend and foe. The qualities described echo the qualities of the devotee given in 12.13–19, but framed here in terms of the threefold classification rather than the relational portrait. Reading the closing verses with the parallel to Chapter 12 visible — the same character described in two different idioms — recovers the chapter's place in the wider sequence.

## The text

The text is held in the Archive library under `bhagavad-gita`, chapter 14 (Edwin Arnold's verse translation, *The Song Celestial*, 1885 — Project Gutenberg edition). Arnold's verse translation here remains at the moderate compression of Chapter 13: the chapter's twenty-seven Sanskrit shlokas are gathered into eleven English verse-paragraphs followed by a closing colophon line (passages `14.1`–`14.12` in the library's verse-level subdivision). The triadic comparison Krishna develops at verse-by-verse pace in the Sanskrit is held across the English verse-paragraphs, with each *guna* treated in its turn and the comparisons preserved across paragraph boundaries. A modern prose translation by the International Gita Society is held in the library as a parallel translation. About twenty minutes' reading.
