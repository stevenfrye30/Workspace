# Dhammapada, Chapter 2 (Heedfulness)

Chapter 2 of the Dhammapada — the *Appamāda-vagga*, "On Heedfulness" — is a short chapter of twelve verses turning on a single contrast: between the heedful person and the heedless, the wakeful and the sleeper, the attentive and the negligent. The chapter opens with one of the most quoted lines in Buddhist literature — "heedfulness is the path of the deathless; heedlessness is the path of death" — and the eleven verses that follow extend the same contrast across meditation, restraint, wisdom, and the work of the *bhikkhu*, the mendicant who keeps to the path. The form is paired contrast, as in Chapter 1, but the contrast here turns on a single attribute carried through every verse.

## Context

The Dhammapada is one of the books of the Khuddaka Nikaya, the fifth and shortest section of the Sutta Pitaka in the Pali Canon. It collects 423 verses arranged in twenty-six chapters by theme — pairs, heedfulness, mind, flowers, the sage, evil, and so on. The verses are attributed to the Buddha and were transmitted orally for several centuries before being committed to writing in Sri Lanka in the first century BCE. The collection took shape in roughly the third century BCE, in connection with the Theravada transmission of the canon.

Chapter 2 is the *Appamāda-vagga*, "On Heedfulness." Its twelve verses are numbered 21 through 32 in the standard continuous numbering of the Dhammapada. The Pali word *appamāda* — heedfulness, vigilance, diligence, attention — has been variously translated. F. Max Müller's 1881 translation, the version held in the Archive library, renders it as "earnestness" and translates the chapter accordingly throughout; modern translations more commonly use "heedfulness" or "vigilance." Each verse is short — two or four lines in the Pali — and each completes one statement of the contrast.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the chapter is built on a single contrast that returns through every verse. Verse 21 states it (the heedful walk the path of the deathless, the heedless the path of death); the eleven that follow extend the contrast into particular domains — meditation, restraint, the rising of wisdom, the work of the mendicant. Reading the chapter with the same contrast held visible across the twelve verses, rather than as twelve separate aphorisms, recovers the form the chapter has.

Second, the central Pali term *appamāda* names a quality of attention; its opposite, *pamāda*, names the slipping of attention. Müller renders these as "earnestness" and "thoughtlessness," vocabulary that reads in mid-nineteenth-century English; modern translations use "heedfulness" and "heedlessness," "vigilance" and "negligence," or similar pairings. Reading the chapter with the awareness that the English vocabulary varies between translations, but the underlying contrast is the same, keeps the form the verses share.
