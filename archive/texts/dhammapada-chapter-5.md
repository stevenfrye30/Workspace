# Dhammapada, Chapter 5 (The Fool)

Chapter 5 of the Dhammapada — the *Bāla-vagga*, "The Fool" — is a short chapter of sixteen verses on the figure of the *bāla*, the fool. The opening verse states the form: long is the night to one who lies awake, long the mile to one who is tired, and long is life to the fool who does not know the true law (60). The verses that follow keep the fool in view across several positions. The fool is the wrong companion (61); the fool is tormented by his own claims of ownership (62); the fool who recognises his foolishness is at least so far wise (63); the fool gets nothing from association with a wise man as a spoon gets nothing from soup (64–65); the fool is his own greatest enemy (66); the fool calls his evil deed honey until it ripens (69, 71–72); the fool desires precedence and false reputation (73–74). The closing verse turns the chapter on its hinge: there is one road to wealth and another to Nirvana, and the disciple who knows this strives after separation from the world (75).

## Context

The Dhammapada is one of the books of the Khuddaka Nikaya, the fifth and shortest section of the Sutta Pitaka in the Pali Canon. It collects 423 verses arranged in twenty-six chapters by theme — pairs, heedfulness, mind, flowers, the sage, evil, and so on. The verses are attributed to the Buddha and were transmitted orally for several centuries before being committed to writing in Sri Lanka in the first century BCE. The collection took shape in roughly the third century BCE, in connection with the Theravada transmission of the canon.

Chapter 5 is the *Bāla-vagga*, "The Fool." Its sixteen verses are numbered 60 through 75 in the standard continuous numbering of the Dhammapada — and in F. Max Müller's 1881 translation, the version held in the Archive library, the count and numbering match the standard exactly, with no merged or missing verse of the kind that occurs at the end of Chapter 4 (where Müller combines conventional verses 58 and 59 into a single passage). Each verse is short — two or four lines in the Pali — and each completes one statement of the fool, or of the contrast between the fool and the one who is not.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the chapter is built on a single figure — the fool — that returns through every verse, often paired with the wise man as its contrast. Verses 60–62 set the figure: the fool does not know the true law, the fool is no companion, the fool is tormented by what he calls his. Verse 63 turns it: the fool who knows his foolishness is at least so far wise. Verses 64–65 pair the fool's failure to perceive the truth (a spoon in soup) with the intelligent man's quick perception (a tongue in soup). Verses 67–68 pair the deed regretted with the deed not regretted. Verses 69, 71, and 72 work the slow ripening of the fool's evil deed — sweet as honey while it is unripe, unturned like new milk, smouldering under ashes, then bringing sorrow when it becomes known. Verses 73–74 turn the figure to the fool's desire for reputation and precedence. Verse 75 closes the chapter on its hinge: two roads, wealth or Nirvana, and the choice between them. Reading the chapter with the figure of the fool held visible across the sixteen verses, rather than as sixteen separate aphorisms, recovers the form the chapter is built on.

Second, the fool/wise polarity is the chapter's structural form, not a commentary on persons. The verses do not name particular fools or particular wise men; they describe positions a person can occupy and the consequences that follow from each. Reading the chapter as a description of two positions, rather than as a judgement on classes of people, keeps it in its native proportion.
