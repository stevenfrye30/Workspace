# Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 5, Part 8

Book 5, Part 8 of the Brihadaranyaka Upanishad — the Eighth Brahmana of the Fifth Adhyaya — is a compact two-passage Brahmana that introduces a procedural form distinct from the Adhyaya's prior seven Brahmanas. The Adhyaya's prior Brahmanas have worked through substantive identifications (*Om is ether is Brahman*, *Pragapati is the heart is this Brahman is all this*, *the heart is the true Brahman*, *manomaya purusha is within the heart*, *lightning is Brahman*), through *nirukti* etymological derivations (*hri-da-yam*, *sa-t(i)-yam*, *vidyut* from *vidanat*), and through parabolic dialogue (*Damyata, Datta, Dayadham*). The Eighth Brahmana works through none of these. Its substantive form is the *upāsana* (meditation) instruction worked through a single sustained figure: speech meditated on as a cow, with the cow's anatomy mapped to a Vedic ritual catalogue.

The Brahmana opens at `5.1206` with the meditation imperative: *Let him meditate on speech as a cow.* The form *Let him meditate on X as Y* is the standard Vedic-Upanishadic *upāsana* instruction, and it sits at the open of the Brahmana as the first occurrence in the Adhyaya. The Adhyaya's earlier Brahmanas had named substantive identifications without explicit meditation-imperatives (the substantive subject is named as Brahman or as the substantive identification, with the *vidyā* benefit following automatically); the Eighth Brahmana names the meditation as the procedure itself, with the substantive figure (speech-as-cow) given through the imperative-form.

The substantive content of the meditation is given in five steps:

1. **Speech is the cow.** The substantive subject of the meditation is named through the figure: speech (*vāc*) is meditated on as a cow.
2. **The four udders.** *Her four udders are the words Svaha, Vashat, Hanta, and Svadha.* The four udders are mapped to the four Vedic sacrificial calls — the four ritual exclamations through which offerings are made and through which speech reaches the four classes of recipients.
3. **The three-class distribution.** *The gods live on two of her udders, the Svaha and the Vashat, men on the Hanta, the fathers on the Svadha.* The four udders feed three classes of recipients: gods (two udders, Svaha and Vashat — both standard ritual calls to the gods), men (one udder, Hanta — the ordinary food-offering call), and fathers (one udder, Svadha — the ancestral offering call).
4. **The bull.** *The bull of that cow is breath (prana).* The cow's male partner is identified with the breath, the substantive *prāṇa* that the Adhyaya's earlier Brahmanas had named as Brahman (Part 1's *Om is ether is Brahman* paired with the breath in the substantive cosmic identification).
5. **The calf.** *The calf the mind.* The cow's offspring is identified with the mind, with Müller's translation closing on the substantive equation in compressed form (no copula).

The Brahmana ends at the calf-mind identification on the same passage. No closing seal, no narrator's voice, no *held his peace* form, no recapitulation, no substantive benefit-clause for the meditator. The substantive procedure is complete inside the figure.

## Context

The Brihadaranyaka Upanishad is one of the two earliest of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads, sharing that position with the Chandogya. It belongs to the Shukla Yajurveda branch of the Vedic corpus and forms the closing portion of the Shatapatha Brahmana — the *Aranyaka* in its name marks it as a forest-text recited at the late stages of Vedic study. Modern scholarship places the Brihadaranyaka in roughly the eighth to sixth century BCE, prior to the Buddha; the text is anonymous, and the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship. The text is the longest of the principal Upanishads and is typically structured in six *adhyayas* (chapters), each containing several *brahmanas* (sections).

Book 5, Part 8 holds three of the Adhyaya's distinctive features in concentrated form. The first is the *upāsana* meditation-imperative form. The Vedic-Upanishadic literature works *upāsana* instructions across many of its substantive teachings — the Chandogya Upanishad gives extended *upāsana* sequences on the Sāman, the Udgītha, the Gāyatra, and other ritual-and-substantive figures — and the Brihadaranyaka has worked *upāsana* implicitly throughout the Fifth Adhyaya (every *vidyā* identification is implicitly an *upāsana*). The Eighth Brahmana gives the imperative-form explicitly for the first time in the Adhyaya: *upāsīta vācam dhenum* in the underlying Sanskrit — *Let him meditate on speech as a cow*. The form holds the substantive procedure as one of meditation rather than identification, and it sets the substantive subject (speech) and the figure (a cow) as the substantive content of the meditation.

The second is the four-call Vedic ritual catalogue. *Svaha, Vashat, Hanta, Svadha* are the four standard exclamations of the Vedic sacrificial liturgy. *Svāhā* is the call accompanying offerings to the gods (used at the close of the Agnihotra and at most god-directed oblations); *Vaṣaṭ* is the priestly invocation-call used by the Hotṛ at the close of the *yājyā*-mantra; *Hanta* is the ordinary food-offering call used to give food to a guest; *Svadhā* is the ancestral offering call used at *śrāddha* rites and at oblations to the Manes. The four calls together hold the Vedic ritual world: divine, priestly, human, ancestral. The Brihadaranyaka's Eighth Brahmana of the Fifth Adhyaya is one of the early Upanishadic literature's most compact assemblies of the four calls in a single substantive figure, and the substantive doctrine is that the same speech that flows in the four calls is the substantive cow whose udders feed the three classes of recipients (gods, men, fathers).

The third is the three-class recipient structure. The Adhyaya's Second Brahmana had given a three-class structure with gods, men, and Asuras as the askers of Pragapati's instruction; the Eighth Brahmana gives a three-class structure with gods, men, and fathers as the recipients of the cow's milk. The two three-class structures are not identical (Asuras in the Second, fathers in the Eighth), and the substantive form differs (the Asuras receive instruction in the Second, the fathers receive sacrificial offerings in the Eighth). The two Brahmanas hold paired three-class procedures with different third-class members and different substantive procedures, and reading them alongside each other recovers the Adhyaya's recurring three-class shape across distinct procedural forms.

The closing form is the Adhyaya's standard compact close. *The calf the mind.* No closing seal, no narrator's voice. The Brahmana ends inside the substantive figure.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the meditation-imperative should be preserved as the Brahmana's procedural form. The Brahmana does not state a substantive doctrine and ask the reader to assent to it; it instructs the reader to meditate on speech as a cow. Reading the Brahmana with the imperative held as the procedural form — neither smoothed away into a doctrinal statement *speech is a cow* nor flattened into a generic Upanishadic theology — recovers the substantive procedure of the *upāsana*: the meditator holds the figure (speech as cow) inside the meditation, and the substantive doctrine is what the meditator finds in the figure rather than what the Brahmana asserts.

Second, the four-call Vedic ritual catalogue should be preserved at the level of the Sanskrit ritual terms. *Svaha, Vashat, Hanta, Svadha* are not generic exclamations but specific ritual calls, each tied to a specific class of recipient and a specific moment in the Vedic liturgy. Müller's translation preserves the four Sanskrit terms verbatim without translation — *Svaha* is not rendered *Hail!* and *Svadha* is not rendered *Manes-offering!* — and Müller's footnote `[*1]` glosses the substantive content of *Svadha* without smoothing the Sanskrit term into English. Reading the Brahmana with the four calls held as substantive Vedic-ritual terms — the substantive callsigns of the four-class ritual world — recovers the Brahmana's procedural connection between the *upāsana* on speech and the substantive Vedic ritual literature in which the calls function.

## The text

The text is held in the Archive library under `upanishads-muller-part2`, chapter 5 (Müller translation, 1884 — *The Sacred Books of the East*, Volume XV; via SacredTexts.com). Library chapter 5 is shared across seventy-two Archive entities — four parts of the Katha Upanishad, the six parts of the Mundaka Upanishad, twenty-eight anuvakas of the Taittiriyaka, the eleven Brahmanas of the Brihadaranyaka's First and Second Adhyayas, all nine Brahmanas of the Third Adhyaya, all six Brahmanas of the Fourth Adhyaya, and the First through Seventh Brahmanas of the Fifth Adhyaya — and continues here into the Eighth Brahmana. Book 5, Part 8 of the Brihadaranyaka — the entity here — occupies passages `5.1205` and `5.1206`. Passage `5.1205` is the "Eighth Brahmana" header without footnote. Passage `5.1206` carries the body in Müller's prose presentation across one short passage with the verse-numbered marker at `1.` (`5.1206`).

The Brahmana contains a small set of boundary features that should be preserved honestly:

- **Single-passage body.** The Brahmana's entire substantive content sits on one passage, `5.1206`. No multi-passage span, no internal verse-divisions beyond the verse-1 marker, no narrative interruptions. The form matches the Third, Sixth, and Seventh Brahmanas of the Adhyaya as among the most compact in the Brihadaranyaka.
- **Footnote on *Svadha*.** Passage `5.1206` carries footnote `[*1]` on *Svadha* — the fourth of the four Vedic sacrificial calls. Müller's note glosses the substantive ritual function of *Svadha* as the ancestral offering call. The footnote attaches at the closing item of the four-call catalogue and is the only editorial apparatus in the Brahmana.
- **Imperative-form opening.** Passage `5.1206` opens with the imperative *Let him meditate on speech as a cow*. The form is the Adhyaya's first explicit *upāsana* meditation-imperative; the prior Brahmanas had given substantive identifications without explicit meditation-instructions. The imperative is held in the third-person English translation form (*Let him meditate*) preserving the underlying Sanskrit *upāsīta* construction.
- **Four-call ritual catalogue without footnotes on the first three terms.** The four Vedic sacrificial calls — *Svaha, Vashat, Hanta, Svadha* — are given verbatim in Müller's transliteration. Footnotes attach only at *Svadha* (the fourth term); the first three terms (*Svaha, Vashat, Hanta*) carry no footnotes despite working the same Vedic-ritual procedure. Müller's translation preserves the four Sanskrit terms in English transliteration without translation.
- **Family-figure extension at the close.** The substantive content closes on the bull-and-calf identification: *The bull of that cow is breath (prana), the calf the mind.* Müller's parenthetical *(prana)* preserves the underlying Sanskrit term for breath; the calf-mind identification is given in compressed English without copula (*the calf the mind*) — a translation-form irregularity preserved as found in the digitized source. No closing seal beyond this final substantive identification.

Passage `5.1207` is the "Ninth Brahmana" header that opens Book 5, Part 9 of the Brihadaranyaka and is not part of this entity. The library chapter continues past this entity into the rest of the Fifth Adhyaya and the remaining portions of the volume; the Brihadaranyaka extends well beyond the boundaries of library chapter 5. Less than a minute's reading.
