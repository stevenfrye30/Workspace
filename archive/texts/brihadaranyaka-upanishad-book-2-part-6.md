# Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 2, Part 6

Book 2, Part 6 of the Brihadaranyaka Upanishad — the Sixth Brahmana of the Second Adhyaya — closes the Adhyaya with a single literary form: a *vamsha*, a teacher-lineage list, given without surrounding doctrine, dialogue, or instruction. The Brahmana opens with the short framing line *atha vaṁśaḥ* — *Now follows the stem* — and immediately enters the list. Fifty-eight teachers are named in unbroken succession, each named as the disciple of the next, the chain running backward in time from the present holder of the teaching to the Self-Existent at the source.

The opening segment runs through eight teachers — *Pautimashya from Gaupavana, Gaupavana from Pautimashya, Pautimashya from Gaupavana, Gaupavana from Kausika, Kausika from Kaundinya, Kaundinya from Sandilya, Sandilya from Kausika and Gautama, Gautama from Agnivesya* — with the alternation between Pautimashya and Gaupavana at the head reflecting the back-and-forth transmission within a small group of early teachers, and the splitting at *Sandilya from Kausika and Gautama* indicating that the chain branches and rejoins. The continuation runs through twenty-one names — *Agnivesya from Sandilya and Anabhimlata, Sandilya and Anabhimlata from Anabhimlata, Anabhimlata from Anabhimlata, Anabhimlata from Gautama, Gautama from Saitava and Prakinayogya, Saitava and Prakinayogya from Parasarya, Parasarya from Bharadvaga, Bharadvaga from Bharadvaga and Gautama, Gautama from Bharadvaga, Bharadvaga from Parasarya, Parasarya from Vaigavapayana, Vaigavapayana from Kausikayani, Kausikayani from Ghritakausika*. The middle segment runs through twenty-five names — *Ghritakausika from Parasaryayana, Parasaryayana from Parasarya, Parasarya from Gatukarnya, Gatukarnya from Asurayana and Yaska, Asurayana and Yaska from Traivani, Traivani from Aupagandhani, Aupagandhani from Asuri, Asuri from Bharadvaga, Bharadvaga from Atreya, Atreya from Manti, Manti from Gautama, Gautama from Gautama, Gautama from Vatsya, Vatsya from Sandilya, Sandilya from Kaisorya Kapya, Kaisorya Kapya from Kumaraharita, Kumaraharita from Galava, Galava from Vidarbhi-kaundinya, Vidarbhi-kaundinya from Vatsanapat Babhrava, Vatsanapat Babhrava from Pathi Saubhara, Pathi Saubhara from Ayasya Angirasa, Ayasya Angirasa from Abhuti Tvashtra, Abhuti Tvashtra from Visvarupa Tvashtra, Visvarupa Tvashtra from the Asvinau*. The closing segment runs through the last twelve names, where the lineage moves out of the named human teachers and into figures of Vedic and primordial reference: *the Asvinau from Dadhyak Atharvana, Dadhyak Atharvana from Atharvan Daiva, Atharvan Daiva from Mrityu Pradhvamsana, Mrityu Pradhvamsana from Pradhvamsana, Pradhvamsana from Ekarshi, Ekarshi from Viprakitti, Viprakitti from Vyashti, Vyashti from Sanaru, Sanaru from Sanatana, Sanatana from Sanaga, Sanaga from Parameshthin, Parameshthin from Brahman.* The closing line gives the closing seal: *Brahman is Svayambhu, self-existent. Adoration to Brahman.*

## Context

The Brihadaranyaka Upanishad is one of the two earliest of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads, sharing that position with the Chandogya. It belongs to the Shukla Yajurveda branch of the Vedic corpus and forms the closing portion of the Shatapatha Brahmana — the *Aranyaka* in its name marks it as a forest-text recited at the late stages of Vedic study. Modern scholarship places the Brihadaranyaka in roughly the eighth to sixth century BCE, prior to the Buddha; the text is anonymous, and the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship. The text is the longest of the principal Upanishads and is typically structured in six *adhyayas* (chapters), each containing several *brahmanas* (sections).

Book 2, Part 6 closes the Second Adhyaya. The Adhyaya has held five Brahmanas of strikingly different forms: the Gargya Balaki / Ajatasatru dialogue (Brahmana 1), the head-as-cosmic-locus symbolic mapping (Brahmana 2), the *neti neti* and *satyasya satyam* recognition (Brahmana 3), the Yajnavalkya-Maitreyi conversation (Brahmana 4), and the *Madhu-Brahmana* (Brahmana 5). The Sixth Brahmana now closes the Adhyaya in the form most distinct from any of them: pure transmission record. No instruction is given; no recognition is named; no figure is offered. What is given is the chain of teachers by which the doctrine of the Adhyaya has been received, traced backward through fifty-seven generations to *Brahman Svayambhu*, the Self-Existent, who is the source the doctrine has named. The *vamsha* is one of the standard closing forms of the Vedic ritual literature, and the Brihadaranyaka uses it three times — at the close of the Second Adhyaya, the close of the Fourth, and the close of the Sixth — each time gathering a long list of teachers into the Adhyaya's seal. The chain ends in Brahman because the Brihadaranyaka has been teaching that Brahman is the source; the genealogical form is the literary completion of the doctrine.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the Brahmana is built entirely on the form of the list, and the list is the work the Brahmana is doing. Each name is a node in a chain; each chain-link is *X from Y*; the form is unchanging across fifty-eight names. Reading the Brahmana with attention to the form of the chain — the alternations (Pautimashya from Gaupavana, Gaupavana from Pautimashya), the branchings (Sandilya from Kausika and Gautama; the Asvinau as a pair), the moments where the same name appears across multiple positions (Gautama, Bharadvaga, Sandilya, Parasarya recur at different points) — recovers what the *vamsha* is doing. The transmission is not linear; it is a network whose backward reach the list is tracing.

Second, the Brahmana's closing turn from named human teachers into figures of Vedic and primordial reference is the Adhyaya's seal. The list moves through Brahmanic teachers (Pautimashya, Gaupavana, Sandilya, Bharadvaga…) and into the half-mythological Vedic teachers (Ayasya Angirasa, Abhuti Tvashtra, Visvarupa Tvashtra, the Asvinau, Dadhyak Atharvana — figures who have appeared in the Madhu-Brahmana of Part 5), and from there into the genealogical figures of the Vedic literature itself (Mrityu Pradhvamsana, Ekarshi, Viprakitti, Vyashti, Sanaru, Sanatana, Sanaga, Parameshthin), ending with *Brahman is Svayambhu, self-existent.* Reading the closing turn with attention to the way the list passes through the teachers of the previous Brahmana — the Asvinau and Dadhyak Atharvana, who proclaimed the *madhu* doctrine — and continues backward through Vedic and primordial figures to Brahman itself, keeps the form the Brihadaranyaka has when its Adhyaya is sealed by genealogy rather than by formula.

## The text

The text is held in the Archive library under `upanishads-muller-part2`, chapter 5 (Müller translation, 1884 — *The Sacred Books of the East*, Volume XV; via SacredTexts.com). Library chapter 5 is shared across fifty Archive entities — four parts of the Katha Upanishad, the six parts of the Mundaka Upanishad, the thirty-one anuvakas of the Taittiriyaka, the six Brahmanas of the Brihadaranyaka's First Adhyaya, and the First through Fifth Brahmanas of the Second Adhyaya — and continues here into the Sixth Brahmana. Book 2, Part 6 of the Brihadaranyaka — the entity here — occupies passages `5.622` through `5.630`. Passage `5.622` is the "Sixth Brahmana" header; the header carries no footnote of its own.

The Brahmana contains several boundary features that should be preserved honestly:

- **Opening framing line at `5.623`.** Passage `5.623` carries the short framing line *Now follows the stem* — a single phrase on its own short passage — with footnote `[*1]` glossing *the stem* (Sanskrit *vaṁśa*). The verse-number marker `1.` appears at the open of this passage; this `1.` is Müller's external numbering of the list-passages and is not a teacher-position number.
- **Müller's paragraph-of-the-list markers at `5.625` and `5.628`.** Passage `5.625` opens *2. from Agnivesya,* — the verse-number `2.` here is Müller's external paragraph-marker and not a teacher-position number; the substance of the passage is the continuation of teacher position 8 (Gautama from Agnivesya). Passage `5.628` opens *3. from Ghritakausika,* in the same form — Müller's paragraph-marker `3.`, with the substance continuing teacher position 21 (Kausikayani from Ghritakausika). The teacher-position numbers (1 through 58) run continuously within the listing across these external paragraph-markers without renumbering.
- **Multi-passage list distribution.** The teacher-list is distributed across passages `5.624` (positions 1–7 with 8 cut off mid-line at *Gautama*), `5.625` (continuation of 8), `5.626` (positions 9–16, with 17 cut off mid-name at *Bhara*), `5.627` (positions 17–21 with 21 cut off mid-name), `5.628` (continuation of 21), `5.629` (positions 22–45 in a single dense passage), and `5.630` (positions 46–58 in a single dense passage with the closing seal). The mid-line cuts at the end of `5.624` ("Gautama"), `5.626` ("Bhara"), and `5.627` ("Kausikayani / 21") reflect Müller's editorial paragraphing rather than separate verses.
- **Footnotes inside the list.** Footnote `[*1]` at `5.627` on Kausikayani (position 21); footnote `[*2]` at `5.629` on Gatukarnya (position 24); footnote `[*3]` at `5.629` on Asurayana and Yaska (position 25); footnote `[*1]` at `5.630` on Viprakitti (position 51); footnote `[*2]` at `5.630` on the closing *Adoration to Brahman*.
- **Closing seal at `5.630`.** The closing line *57. Parameshthin from Brahman, 58. Brahman is Svayambhu, self-existent. Adoration to Brahman* sits inside passage `5.630` as the final two list-positions and the closing seal. The seal is not given on a separate passage; the genealogy ends inside the same passage that lists the closing teachers, and the *Adoration to Brahman* line is the Adhyaya's close.

Passage `5.631` is the "Third Adhyaya" header that opens Book 3, Part 1 of the Brihadaranyaka — the Janaka Vaideha sacrifice — and is not part of this entity. The library chapter continues past this entity into the rest of the Brihadaranyaka and the remaining portions of the volume; the Brihadaranyaka extends well beyond the boundaries of library chapter 5. About four minutes' reading.

## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 2, Part 5 — paired Adhyaya-closing pair of Brahmanas at opposite ends of the literary spectrum; the Fifth Brahmana ran the *Madhu-Brahmana* with its fourteen-fold cosmic-bodily identifications, the wheel-figure of unity, and the mythological lineage of Dadhyak Atharvana proclaiming the honey-doctrine to the Asvins, the Sixth Brahmana now closes the Adhyaya by tracing the lineage of teaching itself — fifty-eight teachers from Pautimashya backward through the same Asvinau and Dadhyak Atharvana of the previous Brahmana, on to Brahman Svayambhu. The Madhu-Brahmana's mythological figures appear inside the *vamsha* as actual links in the chain.
- **structural-parallel** → Mundaka Upanishad, Book 1, Part 1 — paired Sanskrit teacher-lineage transmissions; the Mundaka's First Khanda opens the Upanishad with the short lineage *Brahma told the knowledge of Brahman to his eldest son Atharvan; Atharvan formerly told it to Angir; he told it to Satyavaha Bharadvaga; Bharadvaga told it in succession to Angiras*, ending with Saunaka approaching Angiras with fuel in hand, the Brihadaranyaka's Sixth Brahmana of the Second Adhyaya closes the Adhyaya with the long lineage of fifty-eight teachers ending with *Brahman is Svayambhu, self-existent. Adoration to Brahman.* Both texts hold the lineage in the same form — *X from Y* — but place it at opposite structural positions: the Mundaka at the opening, the Brihadaranyaka at the close of an Adhyaya.
- **structural-parallel** → Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 4 (Jnana Karma Sanyasa Yoga) — paired Sanskrit teaching-lineage formulas; the Gita's Chapter 4 opens with Krishna naming the imperishable yoga as told to Vivasvan, Vivasvan to Manu, Manu to Ikshvaku — descending through royal sages until lost — and now told again, the Brihadaranyaka's Sixth Brahmana of the Second Adhyaya gives the lineage of teaching as a full stem of fifty-eight teachers. Both texts use the genealogical form to ground the teaching they have given inside a chain of named transmitters; the Gita gives a short royal-sage chain at the opening of an instructional chapter, the Brihadaranyaka gives a long teacher chain at the close of an Adhyaya.
