# Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 3, Part 1

Book 3, Part 1 of the Brihadaranyaka Upanishad — the First Brahmana of the Third Adhyaya — opens the third of the six Adhyayas in a new literary frame. The First Adhyaya worked through cosmogony, ritual mapping, and identity-statements in long prose units; the Second Adhyaya alternated between named dialogue (Gargya/Ajatasatru, Yajnavalkya/Maitreyi) and compact symbolic prose (the head-mapping, the *neti neti* doctrine, the *Madhu-Brahmana*) and closed in a teacher-lineage list; the Third Adhyaya now opens directly into a public ceremonial setting and a sustained contest of named participants. The setting is the sacrifice of King Janaka Vaideha. The named challengers will arrive in succession across the Brahmanas of the Adhyaya; the central named figure throughout is the priest-philosopher Yajnavalkya, who will answer every challenger and finally name the question that no one can ask.

The Brahmana is preceded by a short standalone invocation: *Adoration to the Highest Self (Paramatman)!* — given on its own line as the framing salutation that opens the Adhyaya. The first verse then sets the public frame. *Ganaka Vaideha, the king of the Videhas, sacrificed with a sacrifice at which many presents were offered to the priests of the Asvamedha. Brahmanas of the Kurus and the Pankalas had come thither, and Ganaka Vaideha wished to know which of those Brahmanas was the best read.* The king enclosed one thousand cows; ten padas of gold were tied to the horns of each. The second verse holds the contest's setup. *Ye venerable Brahmanas*, Janaka said, *he who among you is the wisest, let him drive away these cows.* The other Brahmanas dared not. Yajnavalkya turned to his pupil: *Drive them away, my dear.* The pupil answered, *O glory of the Saman!*, and drove the cows away. The Brahmanas became angry: *How could he call himself the wisest among us?*

The court contest is opened by Asvala, the Hotri priest of Janaka. He asked Yajnavalkya, *Are you indeed the wisest among us?* Yajnavalkya answered, *I bow before the wisest (the best knower of Brahman), but I wish indeed to have these cows.* The narrator's line follows: *Then Asvala, the Hotri priest, undertook to question him.*

The first cycle of questions — verses 3 through 6 — concerns freedom. Asvala asks four times the same shape of question, with one variable substituted each time: *Yajnavalkya, everything here (connected with the sacrifice) is reached by X, everything is overcome by X. By what means then is the sacrificer freed beyond the reach of X?* The X moves through *death, day and night, the waxing and waning of the moon, the sky which is as it were without an ascent.* Yajnavalkya answers each in the same shape — naming the priest, the cosmic element, and the bodily faculty as one identification: *By the Hotri priest, who is Agni (fire), who is speech*; *by the Adhvaryu priest, who is the eye, who is Aditya (the sun)*; *by the Udgatri priest, who is Vayu (the wind), who is the breath*; *by the Brahman priest, who is the mind (manas), who is the moon.* Each answer closes with the same formula: *This constitutes freedom, and perfect freedom (from death).*

The narrator's line *Next follow the achievements* marks the transition to the second cycle. The second cycle — verses 7 through 10 — concerns the numbers of the ritual. Asvala asks how many Rik verses, how many oblations, how many deities, how many Stotriya hymns the Hotri, the Adhvaryu, the Brahman, and the Udgatri will employ at this sacrifice; Yajnavalkya answers each with a number (three; three; one; three) and the named items, and identifies each set with what the sacrificer gains by them: *all whatsoever has breath; the Deva world, the Pitri world, the Manushya world; the endless world; the earth, the sky, heaven.* In the closing question on the Stotriya hymns, Yajnavalkya names the three hymns — Puronuvakya, Yagya, Sasya — and identifies each with one of the three breaths in the body — Prana, Apana, Vyana — and with one of the three worlds — earth, sky, heaven.

The Brahmana closes on one short narrative line: *After that Asvala held his peace.* The narrator marks the end of the first challenger's questioning without further commentary. The next Brahmana will introduce the next challenger.

## Context

The Brihadaranyaka Upanishad is one of the two earliest of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads, sharing that position with the Chandogya. It belongs to the Shukla Yajurveda branch of the Vedic corpus and forms the closing portion of the Shatapatha Brahmana — the *Aranyaka* in its name marks it as a forest-text recited at the late stages of Vedic study. Modern scholarship places the Brihadaranyaka in roughly the eighth to sixth century BCE, prior to the Buddha; the text is anonymous, and the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship. The text is the longest of the principal Upanishads and is typically structured in six *adhyayas* (chapters), each containing several *brahmanas* (sections).

Book 3, Part 1 opens the long Janaka Vaideha contest that runs across the Third Adhyaya and into the Fourth. The contest is one of the most-read public-debate sequences of the Upanishadic literature, and Yajnavalkya emerges from it as the central named teacher of the text. Each Brahmana of the Third Adhyaya brings a new challenger; the form is the same — questions and answers, with Yajnavalkya answering and the questioner eventually falling silent — and the substance is gathered from the Vedic ritual vocabulary of priests, sacrifices, and offerings. The closing line *Asvala held his peace* will be repeated in close form across the Brahmanas of the Adhyaya, with each new challenger ending the same way.

The setting — Janaka's *aśvamedha* with Brahmanas of the Kurus and the Pankalas as guests — places the dialogue inside the sacrificial-court frame that the Brihadaranyaka uses elsewhere for its major debates. The Kuru-Pankala region is the same field that the Mahabharata names as the setting of the Bharata war; the Brahmana literature is contemporary with the early epic material, and the public-debate frame is closely connected with the contemporaneous *brahmodya* — the formalized priestly debate at the great sacrifice — the technical name for the type of contest the Brahmana now stages.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the Brahmana works in two cycles of substitution, and the substitution is the form of the contest. Asvala's first cycle asks the same question four times — *how is the sacrificer freed beyond X* — and Yajnavalkya's answers fill the same three-term identification template (priest = element = bodily faculty) at each substitution. Asvala's second cycle asks four ritual-count questions and Yajnavalkya's answers give the count, the named items, and the gain. Reading the dialogue with attention to the form Asvala holds and the form Yajnavalkya answers in — and to the way both cycles close with formulaic seals (*This constitutes freedom, and perfect freedom*; *He gains X by Y*) — recovers the form the Brahmana has and the procedure of the public *brahmodya*.

Second, the Brahmana frames the dialogue inside narrative interruptions. *Then Asvala, the Hotri priest, undertook to question him* opens the first cycle; *Next follow the achievements* opens the second; *After that Asvala held his peace* closes the Brahmana. The narrative interruptions are not commentary; they are markers of the contest's structure, and they distinguish the question-and-answer body from the ceremony that surrounds it. Reading the Brahmana with the narrative interruptions held as part of the structure — neither smoothed away nor expanded into background — keeps the form the Brihadaranyaka has when its ceremony and its dialogue are held together.

## The text

Library chapter 5 is shared across fifty-one Archive entities — four parts of the Katha Upanishad, the six parts of the Mundaka Upanishad, the thirty-one anuvakas of the Taittiriyaka, and the twelve Brahmanas of the Brihadaranyaka's First and Second Adhyayas — and continues here into the First Brahmana of the Third Adhyaya. Book 3, Part 1 of the Brihadaranyaka — the entity here — occupies passages `5.631` through `5.676`.

Two structural headers open the entity:

- **Passage `5.631`:** "Third Adhyaya" header — the major structural break between the Second Adhyaya (which closed on the *vamsha* lineage) and the Third.
- **Passage `5.632`:** "First Brahmana" header carrying its own footnote reference `[*1]`.

Passage `5.633` carries the standalone invocation *Adoration to the Highest Self (Paramatman)!* on its own short line. This invocation is given only at the open of the Third Adhyaya in this section of the Upanishad; it is not part of the verse-numbered body of the Brahmana but functions as the Adhyaya's framing salutation.

Passages `5.634` through `5.675` carry the body of the Brahmana in Müller's prose presentation across forty-two short passages, with verse-numbered markers at `1.` (`5.634`), `2.` (`5.635`), `4.` (`5.644`), `5.` (`5.646`), `6.` (`5.649`), `7.` (`5.652`), `8.` (`5.658`), `9.` (`5.664`), and `10.` (`5.668`). Passage `5.676` carries the closing narrative line *After that Asvala held his peace* without a verse-number marker.

The Brahmana contains several boundary features that should be preserved honestly:

- **Verse-3 numbering anomaly.** Passage `5.642` carries what should be verse 3 (Asvala's first freedom-question) with the verse-marker rendered as "1." in the digitized source rather than "3." The substance of the passage — *Yagnavalkya, everything here is reached by death, everything is overcome by death. By what means then is the sacrificer freed beyond the reach of death?* — is correctly the first of Asvala's substantive questions. The "1." marker appears to be a transcription artifact carried over from Müller's volume; the verse-numbers visible at `5.644` (`4.`) and following are consistent with verse 3 having sat at `5.642` in the underlying numbering.
- **Verse-2 multi-passage span.** Verse 2 — Janaka's challenge, Yajnavalkya's pupil driving the cows, the Brahmanas' anger, Asvala's question, Yajnavalkya's reply — runs across passages `5.635`, `5.636`, `5.637`, `5.638`, `5.639`, and `5.640`, with footnote `[*3]` at `5.637` on the pupil's exclamation *O glory of the Saman!*. Passage `5.639` ends mid-sentence at *Are you indeed the* with the continuation at `5.640` resuming *wisest among us, O Yagnavalkya?*
- **Narrative interruption at `5.641`.** *Then Asvala, the Hotri priest, undertook to question him* sits on its own short passage between the setup and Asvala's first cycle of freedom-questions.
- **Verse-3 split.** Verse 3 — Asvala's first freedom-question and Yajnavalkya's answer — is split across passages `5.642` (the question) and `5.643` (the answer).
- **Verse-4 split.** Verse 4 — day and night question and answer — is split across passages `5.644` (the question) and `5.645` (the answer), with footnote `[*1]` at `5.645` on *the eye, who is Aditya*.
- **Verse-5 multi-passage split.** Verse 5 — waxing and waning of the moon — is split across passages `5.646` (the question), `5.647` (the open of the answer ending mid-sentence at *For the*), and `5.648` (the rest of the answer).
- **Verse-6 split.** Verse 6 — the trackless sky — is split across passages `5.649` and `5.650`.
- **Narrative interruption at `5.651`.** *Next follow the achievements* sits on its own short passage between the freedom-cycle and the ritual-count cycle.
- **Verses 7 and 10 multi-passage Q-and-A.** Verses 7 (Rik verses), 8 (oblations), 9 (Brahman priest's deities), and 10 (Stotriya hymns) are each split across multiple passages of question-and-answer dialogue. Verse 7 spans `5.652`–`5.657` (six passages); verse 8 spans `5.658`–`5.663` (six passages, with footnote `[*2]` at `5.661` on the three kinds of oblation and footnote `[*1]` at `5.663` on the Pitri world); verse 9 spans `5.664`–`5.667` (four passages); verse 10 spans `5.668`–`5.675` (eight passages).
- **Closing narrative at `5.676`.** *After that Asvala held his peace* — the closing narrative line on its own short passage with no verse-number marker.

Passage `5.677` is the "Second Brahmana" header that opens Book 3, Part 2 of the Brihadaranyaka and is not part of this entity.
