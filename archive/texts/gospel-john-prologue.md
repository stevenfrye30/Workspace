# Gospel of John, Prologue (John 1:1–18)

The first eighteen verses of the Gospel of John — the Prologue, a compressed poetic opening — open the fourth and last of the New Testament Gospels. They state, in eighteen short verses, what the Gospel will then narrate at length: that the *logos*, the Word, was with God in the beginning; that all things came into being through the Word; that the text presents the Word as becoming flesh and dwelling among human beings; and that John the Baptist was a witness to him. The Prologue is among the foundational texts of the Christian Bible.

## Context

The Gospel of John is the fourth and last of the four canonical Gospels in the New Testament. Modern scholarship places its final form around the end of the first century, between roughly 90 and 110 CE, in a setting often associated with Ephesus or the broader Aegean. The text is anonymous: the figure traditionally identified as the author, John the son of Zebedee, is not named in the Gospel itself, which refers throughout to "the disciple whom Jesus loved." The attribution to John the Apostle dates from the second century and is stable in the tradition by the time of Irenaeus, around 180 CE.

The Prologue stands apart in form and register from the rest of the Gospel. It is widely held to be a hymn — older than the surrounding Gospel, possibly Aramaic or Greek in origin — that the Gospel's editor wrapped into the opening with two short prose insertions about John the Baptist (verses 6–8 and 15). The hymn portion is structured around a small set of repeating words: *was*, *light*, *darkness*, *witness*, *the Word*. It begins with the words "In the beginning" — *en archē* in Greek, the same words that open the Septuagint translation of the Book of Genesis.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the central word is *logos*. Translated *Word* in English since Tyndale, *logos* in first-century Koine Greek carried a wider range than the modern English word: speech, reason, account, principle, argument. Reading the Prologue with the Greek term in view, and with the awareness that the translators have chosen one of its meanings to render, keeps the text closer to its original ambiguity than any single English rendering can.

Second, the Prologue is a hymn placed at the start of a narrative. Its register is poetic and compressed; the narrative that follows it is plain and concrete. Reading the eighteen verses as a single short poem, and then turning to the narrative that begins in verse 19, keeps the join between the two registers visible. The hymn is the doorway, not the building.
