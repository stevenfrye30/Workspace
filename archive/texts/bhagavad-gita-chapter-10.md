# Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 10 (Vibhuti Yoga)

Chapter 10 of the Bhagavad Gita — the *Vibhuti Yoga*, conventionally translated "The Yoga of Divine Manifestations" (Arnold's translation gives the chapter the parallel name "The Book of Religion by the Heavenly Perfections") — expands Krishna's self-description from the relational frame of Chapter 9 into a catalogued list. The chapter falls in three parts. Krishna opens with a short restatement of his standing as the source of all qualities and beings; Arjuna replies, naming Krishna in the highest terms — Parabrahm, the supreme, declared by the named sages Narada, Vyasa, Asita, and Devala — and asks Krishna to describe his manifestations in detail; Krishna then gives the long enumeration the chapter is known for, the *vibhuti* list, in which he names himself in succession as the most representative member of one category after another: Vishnu among the Adityas, Marichi among the Maruts, the sun by day and the moon by night, the Sama-Veda among the Vedas, Indra among the gods, the mind among faculties, the Ganges among rivers, and so through the universe. The chapter closes with Krishna saying that even this list is only a small part.

## Context

The Bhagavad Gita is a 700-verse philosophical poem in eighteen chapters, set inside Book VI (the *Bhishma Parva*) of the Mahabharata. Most scholars place its present form between roughly the second century BCE and the second century CE — a layered composition that took shape during the long growth of the epic from an older bardic core. The traditional attribution is to Vyasa, the legendary compiler of the Mahabharata; the historical authorship is unknown.

Chapter 10 belongs to the Gita's middle movement and prepares for the visionary culmination of Chapter 11. Where Chapter 9 turned Krishna's self-description toward relation and devotion in language emphasizing nearness, Chapter 10 widens the picture again — into a sustained enumeration of the forms through which the universal becomes visible. The chapter's central section, where Krishna catalogues himself as the most exemplary member of category after category (forty-two verses in the standard Sanskrit numbering), is the longest such list in the Gita. The list is preparation: Chapter 11, which follows immediately, translates this enumeration into the visionary scene in which Krishna shows Arjuna his universal form. Chapter 10 supplies the vocabulary by which that vision will be read.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the central section of the chapter is enumerative rather than argumentative. The repeated formula "among X, I am Y" returns through verse after verse — among rivers, the Ganges; among mountains, the Himalaya; among trees, the ashvattha; among horses, Uchchaihshravas; among the Pandava brothers, Arjuna himself. The accumulation is the form. Reading the central section as an open-ended catalogue, rather than as a list to be completed, follows the way the chapter is composed.

Second, Chapter 10 is preparatory. The catalogue does not yet describe a vision; it gives the vocabulary by which a vision can be read. The names of the universe pile up in language; in Chapter 11 the same universe will be shown to Arjuna in a single seeing, which the catalogued vocabulary of Chapter 10 has prepared him to receive. Reading Chapter 10 with the awareness that its catalogue points forward to the visionary scene of Chapter 11, rather than treating the catalogue as the chapter's complete content, recovers the place of the chapter in the wider middle movement.

## The text

The text is held in the Archive library under `bhagavad-gita`, chapter 10 (Edwin Arnold's verse translation, *The Song Celestial*, 1885 — Project Gutenberg edition). Arnold's verse translation compresses the chapter's forty-two Sanskrit shlokas into three English verse-paragraphs followed by a closing colophon line (passages `10.1`–`10.4` in the library's verse-level subdivision) — a heavier compression than in the earlier chapters, where the long catalogued enumeration of Krishna's manifestations is gathered into a single long verse-paragraph (`10.3`). A modern prose translation by the International Gita Society is held in the library as a parallel translation. About twenty-five minutes' reading.
