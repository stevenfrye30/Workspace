# Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 5, Part 13

Book 5, Part 13 of the Brihadaranyaka Upanishad — the Thirteenth Brahmana of the Fifth Adhyaya — works the Adhyaya forward in a four-fold parallel-substitution form. The Adhyaya has used parallel-substitution structures across multiple Brahmanas — the threefold *Da* parable of Part 2, the eight-purusha cycle of Part 5 (cosmic-and-bodily registers), the four-udder catalogue of Part 8, the three-fold cosmic ascent of Part 10, the three-fold "highest penance" iterations of Part 11. The Thirteenth Brahmana works a four-fold substitution with the same procedural shape across four substantive Vedic-or-political categories, each identified with breath (prana) and each grounded in a substantive *nirukti* etymology.

The Brahmana works four iterations of the same four-step procedural shape across four short body passages. The shape, expanded fully:

1. *Next follows the X.*
2. *Verily, breath (prana) is X, for [etymological reason].*
3. *He who knows this, [substantive benefit].*
4. *He obtains union and oneness with X.*

Each iteration substitutes only the substantive category X, the substantive etymological reason, and the substantive benefit-clause. The four iterations together hold the substantive identification of breath with four distinct substantive categories of the Vedic-and-political world.

**Verse 1 at `5.1219` — Uktha**: *Next follows the Uktha. Verily, breath (prana) is Uktha, for breath raises up (utthapayati) all this. From him who knows this, there is raised a wise son, knowing the Uktha; he obtains union and oneness with the Uktha.* The substantive category is the Uktha (the Rig-Vedic recitation, used in the *aśvamedha* and other major sacrifices). The substantive etymology derives *Uktha* from *utthapayati* — *raises up* — and the substantive benefit is the substantive raising of a wise son who knows the Uktha. Müller's footnote `[*1]` attaches at *Uktha*.

**Verse 2 at `5.1220` — Yagus**: *Next follows the Yagus. Verily, breath is Yagus, for all these beings are joined in breath. For him who knows this, all beings are joined to procure his excellence; he obtains union and oneness with the Yagus.* The substantive category is the Yagus (the Yajurveda recitation). The substantive etymology runs through the substantive verb *yuj-* (to join), with the substantive doctrine that all beings are *joined* in breath. The substantive benefit is the substantive joining of all beings to procure the knower's excellence. Müller's footnote `[*2]` attaches at *joined in breath*.

**Verse 3 at `5.1221` — Saman**: *Next follows the Saman. Verily, breath is the Saman, for all these beings meet in breath. For him who knows this, all beings meet to procure his excellence; he obtains union and oneness with the Saman.* The substantive category is the Saman (the Samaveda chant). The substantive etymology runs through the substantive verb of *meeting* (in the underlying Sanskrit, related to *sama-* and *sam-*), with the substantive doctrine that all beings *meet* in breath. The substantive benefit is the substantive meeting of all beings to procure the knower's excellence. The verse carries no footnote; the substantive form is parallel to verses 1, 2, and 4.

**Verse 4 at `5.1222` — Kshatra**: *Next follows the Kshatra. Verily, breath is the Kshatra, for breath is Kshatra, i.e. breath protects (trayate) him from being hurt (kshanitoh). He who knows this, obtains Kshatra (power), which requires no protection; he obtains union and oneness with Kshatra.* The substantive category is Kshatra (the warrior or political power). The substantive etymology is double: *Kshatra* from *trayate* (protects) and from *kshanitoh* (being hurt) — substantively combining the substantive verbs of protection and injury into the substantive name. Müller's editorial *i.e.* preserves the substantive gloss at the open of the etymology. The substantive benefit is the substantive Kshatra-power that *requires no protection* — the knower obtains the substantive form of the political power that cannot itself be hurt. Müller's footnote `[*3]` attaches at *Kshatra*.

The Brahmana ends on the fourth iteration's seal *he obtains union and oneness with Kshatra* without further commentary. The substantive content of the Brahmana is the four iterations together; the substantive seal *He obtains union and oneness with X* closes each iteration verbatim.

## Context

The Brihadaranyaka Upanishad is one of the two earliest of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads, sharing that position with the Chandogya. It belongs to the Shukla Yajurveda branch of the Vedic corpus and forms the closing portion of the Shatapatha Brahmana — the *Aranyaka* in its name marks it as a forest-text recited at the late stages of Vedic study. Modern scholarship places the Brihadaranyaka in roughly the eighth to sixth century BCE, prior to the Buddha; the text is anonymous, and the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship. The text is the longest of the principal Upanishads and is typically structured in six *adhyayas* (chapters), each containing several *brahmanas* (sections).

Book 5, Part 13 holds three of the Adhyaya's distinctive features in concentrated form. The first is the four-fold substitution-form. The Adhyaya works parallel-substitution structures at multiple scales — three-fold (Parts 2, 11), four-fold (Part 8), eleven-fold (in the warp-and-woof ladder of the Sixth Brahmana of the Third Adhyaya), twelve-fold (in the Self-is-dear refrain of the Fifth Brahmana of the Fourth Adhyaya). The Thirteenth Brahmana of the Fifth Adhyaya gives the four-fold form with a substantive procedural unity: each iteration holds breath (prana) as the substantive subject and substantively identifies it with one of four substantive categories. The four-fold form is the Adhyaya's procedural recurrence of the Vedic-categorial substitution-list.

The second is the substantive set of three-Vedas-plus-one-political category. The four substitutions hold the three substantive Vedic-recitation categories (Uktha, Yagus, Saman) and the one substantive political category (Kshatra). The substantive set is unusual — the Vedic literature usually pairs the three recitations with the fourth Veda (Atharva) or with another substantive Vedic category — but the Brahmana here gives the political-power category in place of a fourth substantive Vedic category. The substantive form holds breath as the substantive identification across the substantive Vedic and the substantive political registers. The substantive Kshatra-identification at the close of the Brahmana works the substantive *vidyā* benefit through the substantive procedure of political power: the knower obtains the substantive form of Kshatra that *requires no protection*, the substantive political power that cannot itself be hurt.

The third is the substantive seal *He obtains union and oneness with X*. The substantive form is the Adhyaya's most explicit instance of a substantive *yoga-and-aikya* (union-and-oneness) seal closing each iteration of a parallel-substitution Brahmana. The Eleventh Brahmana had given the substantive seal *He who knows this, conquers the highest world* across three iterations; the Twelfth Brahmana had given the substantive seal *All beings rest on him, all beings delight in him who knows this* at its single close; the Thirteenth Brahmana gives the substantive seal *He obtains union and oneness with X* across four iterations, with X substituted at each iteration. The substantive doctrine is that the substantive *vidyā* benefit is the substantive union-and-oneness with the substantive category that breath is identified with — the substantive form of the *yoga* (union) and *aikya* (oneness) attained through the substantive identification.

The closing form is the Adhyaya's standard compact close. *He obtains union and oneness with Kshatra.* No closing seal beyond the substantive content of the fourth iteration; no narrator's voice. The Brahmana ends inside the substantive content.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the four iterations should be read at full length rather than collapsed into a single statement. The substantive form is the verbatim repetition of the same four-step shape across four substantive categories, and the substantive procedure is the substantive identification of breath with each category through a substantive *nirukti* etymology and the substantive benefit. Reading the iterations alongside each other rather than summarized as *the Brahmana identifies breath with Uktha, Yagus, Saman, and Kshatra* recovers the substantive form: four substantive iterations, four substantive identifications, four substantive seals.

Second, the substantive Kshatra-identification at verse 4 should be preserved as the substantive procedural deviation from the three-Veda set. The first three iterations identify breath with substantive Vedic-recitation categories (Uktha, Yagus, Saman); the fourth identifies it with the substantive political category (Kshatra). The substantive set is unusual — three Vedic plus one political — and the substantive doctrine is that breath holds the substantive identification across the substantive Vedic and the substantive political registers. The substantive Kshatra-form *requiring no protection* is the substantive political form transferred to the knower through the substantive *vidyā*. Reading the Kshatra-iteration with its substantive procedural deviation preserved — neither smoothed away into a fourth-Vedic category nor expanded into a Vedanta-doctrinal interpretation of political power — recovers the substantive form: the substantive identification of breath crosses the boundary between substantive Vedic and substantive political categories.

- **Four-fold verbatim substitution.** The verse-1, verse-2, verse-3, and verse-4 passages each hold one iteration of the same four-step procedural shape *Next follows the X. Verily, breath (prana) is X, for [etymological reason]. He who knows this, [substantive benefit].
