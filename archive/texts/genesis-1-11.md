# Genesis 1–11

The first eleven chapters of the Hebrew Bible — creation, the garden and its loss, Cain and Abel, the long lifespans, the flood, the table of nations, and the tower of Babel. Together they form a single arc, the *primeval history*: the period before Abraham, before any named tribe, before history in the ordinary sense. Read together, they are the Hebrew Bible's account of how humanity arrived at the world the rest of the book describes.

## Context

Genesis 1–11 is the work of multiple hands. Modern critical scholarship distinguishes at least two source threads — the Priestly material (the seven-day creation in chapter 1, the flood's structural framing, the long genealogies) and an older Yahwist strand (the garden in chapter 2, Cain and Abel, the men of renown). The final form was assembled in the Persian period, likely the sixth or fifth century BCE, drawing on materials that had circulated for centuries before that.

Several scenes have older Mesopotamian counterparts. The flood narrative shares structure with the eleventh tablet of the *Epic of Gilgamesh*. The genealogies of long-lived ancestors echo the Sumerian King List. These are not borrowings in any simple sense; they are members of the same Near Eastern literary family, retold to a different end.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the joins between source threads are visible in the text itself. The two creation accounts (1:1–2:3 and 2:4–25) sit side by side without harmonization. The flood narrative oscillates between two animal counts (pairs and sevens) and two flood durations. A careful reader notices the seams; treating them as deliberate rather than as flaws is part of reading the text in its native form.

Second, the world of Genesis 1–11 is not yet the world of Israel. There is no covenant, no chosen line, no land promised. The narrative is universalist — it concerns humanity, not a people. The shift to a chosen line begins at chapter 12 with Abraham. These eleven chapters are best read as the prologue they were composed to be.
