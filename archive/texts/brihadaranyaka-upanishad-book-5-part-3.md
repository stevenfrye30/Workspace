# Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 5, Part 3

Book 5, Part 3 of the Brihadaranyaka Upanishad — the Third Brahmana of the Fifth Adhyaya — is the most compact Brahmana of the Brihadaranyaka so far. The Adhyaya's prior two Brahmanas had been compact in their own ways: the First Brahmana held the Purnam mantra and the Om-as-ether-as-Brahman triple identification across two body passages; the Second Brahmana held the Pragapati and gods-men-Asuras parable with the threefold *Da* injunction across five body passages. The Third Brahmana works the Adhyaya forward in still more compressed form: a single body passage, a single numbered verse, no dialogue, no parable, no narrative frame. The Brahmana opens, gives the substantive teaching, and ends.

The teaching of the Brahmana is held in three parts on the one body passage. The first part is the triple identification: *Pragapati is the heart, is this Brahman, is all this.* The named figure of Part 2 — Pragapati, the father of the threefold descendants — returns at the open of Part 3 and is identified with the heart (*hridaya*), with Brahman, and with the all (*idam sarvam*). The four-fold equation runs through the substantive cosmic-cosmological terms: the named creator-figure, the bodily organ, the absolute, and the totality. The form holds the same substantive identification-procedure that the Adhyaya's First Brahmana had given in Om = ether = Brahman; the Third Brahmana gives a different identification-set with Pragapati at the open and the heart, Brahman, and the all as the three substantive equivalents.

The second part is the etymological breakdown of the heart. *The heart, hridaya, consists of three syllables.* The Sanskrit word *hridaya* is parsed into three syllables — *hri*, *da*, and *yam* — and each syllable is treated as a *nirukti*-style etymological key. The form is the *nirukti* tradition's procedure of treating each syllable of a word as a substantive seed of meaning rather than as a phonetic component of a single semantic unit; the same procedure is given in the early Sanskrit grammatical tradition under the name of *adhyāropa* or *upāsana-akshara* (meditation-syllable) analysis.

The third part is the substantive content of each syllable. *One syllable is hri, and to him who knows this, his own people and others bring offerings.* The substantive verb *bring offerings* is *āharaṇti* in the underlying Sanskrit; the syllable *hri* is taken as the *upāsana-akshara* seed of the verb. *One syllable is da, and to him who knows this, his own people and others bring gifts.* The substantive verb *bring gifts* is *dadati*; the syllable *da* is the seed of *dāna* (giving), the same root that the prior Brahmana's *Datta* (give) had used. *One syllable is yam, and he who knows this, goes to heaven (svarga) as his world.* The substantive verb *goes* is *yāti*; the syllable *yam* is the seed of the going-to-heaven action. The three substantive benefits — being brought offerings, being brought gifts, going to heaven — hold the form of life of the knower of the heart's three syllables.

The Brahmana ends with no closing seal. The substantive answer at the close of the third syllable — *he goes to heaven (svarga) as his world* — is the last line of the Brahmana. No narrator's voice is given; no *held his peace* form; no closing recapitulation. The Adhyaya's compact procedure here is the most compressed in the Upanishad: the substantive teaching ends inside its own substance.

## Context

The Brihadaranyaka Upanishad is one of the two earliest of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads, sharing that position with the Chandogya. It belongs to the Shukla Yajurveda branch of the Vedic corpus and forms the closing portion of the Shatapatha Brahmana — the *Aranyaka* in its name marks it as a forest-text recited at the late stages of Vedic study. Modern scholarship places the Brihadaranyaka in roughly the eighth to sixth century BCE, prior to the Buddha; the text is anonymous, and the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship. The text is the longest of the principal Upanishads and is typically structured in six *adhyayas* (chapters), each containing several *brahmanas* (sections).

Book 5, Part 3 holds three of the Adhyaya's distinctive features in concentrated form. The first is the triple-syllable structure. The Adhyaya's Second Brahmana had given the threefold *Da* — *Damyata, Datta, Dayadham* — as the substantive teaching of Pragapati's parable. The Third Brahmana gives the threefold *hri-da-yam* of the word *hridaya* as the substantive teaching of the heart's identity with Pragapati. The two Brahmanas hold the Adhyaya's three-syllable form across paired procedures: the prior Brahmana's three-syllable form is doctrinal injunction (*be subdued, give, be merciful*); the present Brahmana's three-syllable form is etymological-cosmological. The middle syllable *da* — meaning *give* — is the same substantive seed in both Brahmanas; the Third Brahmana takes the syllable from the prior Brahmana's *Datta* injunction into the present Brahmana's *bring gifts* substantive benefit. The Adhyaya's compact procedure works through related triple-structures across consecutive Brahmanas.

The second is the triple-identification at the open. *Pragapati is the heart, is this Brahman, is all this.* The form holds Pragapati — the named father of Part 2 — as identical with the bodily organ, with the absolute, and with the totality. The Adhyaya's First Brahmana had given the form *Om (is) ether, (is) Brahman* as a triple identification of the syllable, the cosmic medium, and the absolute. The Third Brahmana gives the same triple-identification procedure with Pragapati at the substantive opening, the heart at the bodily seat, Brahman at the absolute, and the all as the totality. The two identifications hold the same form across the Adhyaya, with the substantive pivot in different terms but the substantive shape the same.

The third is the *nirukti* (etymological) procedure on the syllables. The breaking of *hridaya* into *hri-da-yam* and the substantive treatment of each syllable as a meditation-seed is one of the Upanishadic literature's earliest sustained instances of the *upāsana-akshara* form. The same form is used at length in the Chandogya Upanishad's *Sāman*-syllable analysis and at the close of the Brihadaranyaka's own First Brahmana of the First Adhyaya in the analysis of the *aśva-medha* horse-sacrifice's syllables. The Third Brahmana of the Fifth Adhyaya gives the procedure in its most compact form: three syllables, three benefits, one body passage. The Vedanta tradition has read the Brahmana as an early instance of the *vidyā* (meditation-knowledge) form that the later Upanishads will work through extended meditative procedures, with each substantive identification serving as a knowledge-yielding contemplation rather than as a logical-doctrinal proposition.

The closing form is the Adhyaya's most abrupt close so far. The First Brahmana had closed on the substantive seal *one knows through it all that has to be known*; the Second Brahmana had closed on the imperative *Therefore let that triad be taught, Subduing, Giving, and Mercy*. The Third Brahmana closes on *he who knows this, goes to heaven (svarga) as his world* — the substantive benefit of the third syllable, with no closing seal beyond the benefit itself. The teaching ends inside its substance.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the Brahmana's compression should be preserved as its literary form. The Brahmana is deliberately short; it gives one substantive teaching on one body passage and ends. Reading the Brahmana with its compression preserved — neither expanded into a doctrine of the heart-as-Brahman nor flattened into a generic Upanishadic statement on the *nirukti* tradition — recovers the form the Adhyaya has when its substantive teachings are given in the smallest possible scale. The Adhyaya works the form of the compact-doctrinal Brahmana across multiple short Brahmanas; Part 3 is the most compressed instance.

Second, the relation between the *nirukti* form and the substantive benefits should be read as the Brahmana's substantive doctrine. The three syllables of the heart are not phonetic components of a Sanskrit word; they are meditation-seeds, with each syllable yielding the substantive benefit through the related Sanskrit verb (*āharaṇti, dadati, yāti*). The benefits — being brought offerings, being brought gifts, going to heaven — are the substantive worldly-and-otherworldly results of knowing each syllable. The form holds the *vidyā* (meditation-knowledge) procedure: knowing the syllable is the same as receiving the substantive benefit. Reading the Brahmana with the *vidyā* form held distinct from a doctrinal-statement form recovers the early Upanishadic procedure of meditation-knowledge as substantive efficacy rather than as logical proposition.

## The text

The text is held in the Archive library under `upanishads-muller-part2`, chapter 5 (Müller translation, 1884 — *The Sacred Books of the East*, Volume XV; via SacredTexts.com). Library chapter 5 is shared across sixty-seven Archive entities — four parts of the Katha Upanishad, the six parts of the Mundaka Upanishad, twenty-eight anuvakas of the Taittiriyaka, the eleven Brahmanas of the Brihadaranyaka's First and Second Adhyayas, all nine Brahmanas of the Third Adhyaya, all six Brahmanas of the Fourth Adhyaya, and the First and Second Brahmanas of the Fifth Adhyaya — and continues here into the Third Brahmana of the Fifth Adhyaya. Book 5, Part 3 of the Brihadaranyaka — the entity here — occupies passages `5.1191` and `5.1192`. Passage `5.1191` is the "Third Brahmana" header without footnote. Passage `5.1192` carries the body in Müller's prose presentation across one short passage with the verse-numbered marker at `1.` (`5.1192`).

The Brahmana contains a small set of boundary features that should be preserved honestly:

- **Single-passage body.** The Brahmana's entire substantive content sits on one passage, `5.1192`. No multi-passage span, no internal verse-divisions beyond the verse-1 marker, no narrative interruptions. The form is the most compact in the Brihadaranyaka.
- **Verse-1 footnote on the first syllable's benefit.** Passage `5.1192` carries footnote `[*1]` on *offerings* — the substantive verb of the *hri*-syllable's benefit. Müller's note glosses the *nirukti* relation between the syllable and the underlying Sanskrit verb *āharaṇti*. The footnote is the only editorial apparatus in the Brahmana; the second and third syllables (*da* and *yam*) carry no further footnotes despite working the same *nirukti* procedure.
- **Triple-identification opening.** The opening clause *Pragapati is the heart, is this Brahman, is all this* is given inside the verse-1 marker without further punctuation between the four substantive terms. The form is the Sanskrit *iti–iti* identification-list rendered into Müller's English with serial-comma copulas; the substantive content is the triple-equation of Pragapati, the heart, Brahman, and the all.
- **Three-syllable substantive ladder.** The verse-1 body holds three substantive iterations on *hri*, *da*, and *yam* in succession. Each iteration uses the same form *One syllable is X, and to him who knows this, [substantive benefit]*. The substantive benefits — being brought offerings, being brought gifts, going to heaven — are given on the same passage in succession with no internal verse-numbering or narrative-break between them.
- **No closing seal.** The Brahmana ends abruptly on the third syllable's benefit *goes to heaven (svarga) as his world*. No closing narrator's line, no recapitulation, no *held his peace* form, no doctrinal seal beyond the substantive benefit itself. The Brahmana's close is the close of the third *nirukti* iteration.

Passage `5.1193` is the "Fourth Brahmana" header that opens Book 5, Part 4 of the Brihadaranyaka and is not part of this entity. The library chapter continues past this entity into the rest of the Fifth Adhyaya and the remaining portions of the volume; the Brihadaranyaka extends well beyond the boundaries of library chapter 5. Less than a minute's reading.
