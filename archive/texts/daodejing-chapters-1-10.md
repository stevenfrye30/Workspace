# Daodejing, Chapters 1–10

The first ten chapters of the Daodejing — the *Classic of the Way and Virtue* — open the central text of Daoism. They state, in compressed and often paradoxical lines, the relation between the unsayable Way (*dao*) and the world of named things, and the conduct that follows from taking that relation seriously: yielding rather than contending, governing without forcing, and the modeling of action on water and the valley. The Daodejing is one of the shortest classical Chinese books and one of the most translated books in the world.

## Context

The Daodejing is an 81-chapter collection of short gnomic chapters in classical Chinese, traditionally attributed to Laozi (Lao Tzu), a semi-legendary sixth-century BCE archivist of the Zhou court. Modern scholarship places the assembled text in the fourth century BCE on the basis of the Guodian bamboo-slip manuscripts (c. 300 BCE) and the Mawangdui silk manuscripts (c. 200 BCE), which preserve earlier and partly different recensions of the same material. The received text, in the form most widely read, descends from the third-century CE recension of Wang Bi.

The book is in two parts. Part one — the *Dao* — comprises chapters 1 through 37 and opens the standard text. Part two — the *De*, on power or virtue — comprises chapters 38 through 81. (The Mawangdui manuscripts reverse the order, opening with the *De*.) Chapters 1–10 belong to the opening *Dao* section. Chapter 1 names the project: the way that can be spoken is not the constant way. The chapters that follow turn the same orientation onto complementarity, governance without contention, the impartiality of heaven and earth, the strength of water, and the discipline of knowing when to stop.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the chapters are short — typically a few lines each in Chinese, a paragraph in English — and they do not build a system in the way a treatise does. They circle. Reading the first ten chapters as a sequence of related angles on one orientation, rather than as ten discrete teachings, is closer to the way the text is shaped.

Second, the lines often rest on paradox: the way that can be told is not the way; the sage acts by not acting; what yields prevails. The paradoxes are not riddles to be decoded but compact statements that depend on the ordinary meanings of their terms. Reading them at face value, without supplying a hidden esoteric sense, leaves the text where the text leaves itself.

## The text

The text is held in the Archive library under `tao-te-ching` (Legge translation, 1891 — Project Gutenberg edition of *The Sacred Books of the East*, Volume XXXIX). The library indexes Legge's translation in grouped sections; Daodejing chapters 1–10 are held across library chapters 1–7. Ten short chapters; about half an hour's reading.

## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Dhammapada, Chapter 1 — both are short opening chapters of compact wisdom-verse traditions; the Twin Verses on mental orientation, the opening of the Daodejing on conduct shaped by the Way.
- **structural-parallel** → Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 2 — both reorient action away from force; the Gita through action without attachment to its fruits, the Daodejing through non-contention and yielding.
