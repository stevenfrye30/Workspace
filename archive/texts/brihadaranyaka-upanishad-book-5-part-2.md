# Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 5, Part 2

Book 5, Part 2 of the Brihadaranyaka Upanishad — the Second Brahmana of the Fifth Adhyaya — works the Adhyaya forward in a literary register distinct from the First Brahmana's. The First Brahmana had given the Purnam mantra and the Om-as-ether-as-Brahman triple identification across two compressed body passages in third-person aphoristic-mantric voice. The Second Brahmana opens the Adhyaya's narrative procedure with a parable: *The threefold descendants of Pragapati, gods, men, and Asuras (evil spirits), dwelt as Brahmakarins (students) with their father Pragapati.* The Adhyaya's first named participants are now given. Pragapati is the father; his three classes of descendants are the students; the dialogue runs between the father and the three classes in succession.

The Brahmana works through three parallel iterations across verses 1, 2, and 3, each iteration holding the same dialogic shape with one variable substituted. The shape, expanded fully:

1. *[Class] said to him: 'Tell us (something), Sir.'*
2. *He told them the syllable Da.*
3. *Then he said: 'Did you understand?'*
4. *They said: 'We did understand. You told us "[Word]," [Imperative].'*
5. *'Yes,' he said, 'you have understood.'*

The five-step shape is held verbatim across the three iterations, with only the asking-class (gods, men, Asuras) and the heard-word (*Damyata*, *Datta*, *Dayadham*) and the imperative-gloss (*Be subdued*, *Give*, *Be merciful*) substituted. Each class hears the same syllable *Da* differently, depending on what each class needs to hear; the substantive doctrine is that the same revelation answers different students with different injunctions when each student receives it.

Verse 1 holds the gods at `5.1186`. *Having finished their studentship the gods said: 'Tell us (something), Sir.' He told them the syllable Da. Then he said: 'Did you understand?' They said: 'We did understand. You told us "Damyata," Be subdued.' 'Yes,' he said, 'you have understood.'* The gods are by their nature unrestrained — *the gods love what is mysterious, and dislike what is evident*, the Second Brahmana of the Fourth Adhyaya at `5.1015` had named them — and the syllable *Da* speaks to them as the imperative *Damyata*: be subdued, exercise self-restraint.

Verse 2 holds the men at `5.1187`–`5.1188`. The verse is split across two passages with an editorial bracket *[paragraph continues]* preserved at the open of `5.1188`: *Then the men said to him: 'Tell us something, / [paragraph continues] Sir.'* The split falls inside the men's request-formula at the open of the verse. The men hear: *Datta — Give.* The substantive injunction-form names what men need: the gift, the open hand, the renunciation of grasping.

Verse 3 holds the Asuras at `5.1189`. *Then the Asuras said to him: 'Tell us something, Sir.' He told them the same syllable Da. Then he said: 'Did you understand?' They said: 'We did understand. You told us, "Dayadham," Be merciful.' 'Yes,' he said, 'you have understood.'* The Asuras — the *evil spirits* of Müller's parenthetical gloss — are by their nature cruel, and the syllable *Da* speaks to them as the imperative *Dayadham*: be merciful, hold mercy in the place of cruelty. The three iterations are now closed.

The closing passage at `5.1190` gathers the three injunctions into a single closing line. *The divine voice of thunder repeats the same, Da Da Da, that is, Be subdued, Give, Be merciful. Therefore let that triad be taught, Subduing, Giving, and Mercy.* The line attributes the triple repetition to the *divine voice of thunder* — the natural voice that speaks in the sky as *Da Da Da* — and makes the three syllables of thunder the substantive teaching of the Brahmana. The closing seal *Therefore let that triad be taught, Subduing, Giving, and Mercy* names the imperative-form of the doctrine in the third person, with the three substantive imperatives — *damyata, datta, dayadhvam* — given in their substantive abstract form: subduing, giving, mercy.

## Context

The Brihadaranyaka Upanishad is one of the two earliest of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads, sharing that position with the Chandogya. It belongs to the Shukla Yajurveda branch of the Vedic corpus and forms the closing portion of the Shatapatha Brahmana — the *Aranyaka* in its name marks it as a forest-text recited at the late stages of Vedic study. Modern scholarship places the Brihadaranyaka in roughly the eighth to sixth century BCE, prior to the Buddha; the text is anonymous, and the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship. The text is the longest of the principal Upanishads and is typically structured in six *adhyayas* (chapters), each containing several *brahmanas* (sections).

Book 5, Part 2 is one of the most-cited brief Brahmanas of the Upanishadic literature. The threefold injunction *damyata, datta, dayadhvam* — Be subdued, Give, Be merciful — has been received by the tradition as the Brihadaranyaka's substantive ethical core and has carried into the later literature as the most-quoted moral triad of the Upanishads. T. S. Eliot's *The Waste Land* (1922) quotes the three Sanskrit imperatives at the close of the poem's fifth section, "What the Thunder Said," with the same form and the same source-citation Müller's translation gives here; Eliot's note in the published apparatus names the Brihadaranyaka 5.2 as the locus.

The Brahmana also holds the Adhyaya's first sustained named-participant passage. The Fifth Adhyaya's First Brahmana had named Kauravyayaniputra on a single short attribution-line; the Second Brahmana names Pragapati as the father of three classes of descendants and works the dialogue across the three classes in succession. The form is parabolic rather than dialogic in the *brahmodya* sense of Books 3 and 4: there is no contest, no public assembly, no king, no offer of cattle, no *held his peace* form. The class-of-asker is collective — *the gods*, *the men*, *the Asuras* — rather than individual, and Pragapati's confirmations are short and verbatim across the three iterations. The form sets the Adhyaya's procedure as one of compressed parable rather than long dialogue.

The closing on the divine voice of thunder is one of the Upanishadic literature's most-cited natural-voice attributions. The thunder is named in the underlying Sanskrit as *stanayitnu* — the rumbling — and the three rumblings of the thunder are named as *Da Da Da*, holding the same three syllables the parable has worked through. The form is one of the Upanishad's earliest instances of the cosmic-natural-voice as the source of doctrine: nature itself speaks the teaching that Pragapati gave to his descendants. The substantive imperative *let that triad be taught, Subduing, Giving, and Mercy* names the doctrine in its abstract substantive form — *dama* (subduing), *dāna* (giving), *dayā* (mercy) — as a triad to be transmitted to future students.

The closing form is the Adhyaya's first imperative-form closing. The Brihadaranyaka's prior Brahmanas had closed on silence (the contest's *held his peace* form), on death (the Sakalya Brahmana), on private withdrawal (Artabhaga at the Second Brahmana of the Third Adhyaya), on lineage (the *vamsha* Brahmanas), on declined-reward (the First Brahmana of the Fourth Adhyaya), on Janaka's surrender (the Second and Fourth Brahmanas of the Fourth Adhyaya), on physical departure for the forest (the Maitreyi recension), on knowledge-sufficiency (Part 1's *one knows through it all that has to be known*). The Second Brahmana of the Fifth Adhyaya closes with the imperative *Therefore let that triad be taught*: the doctrine names its own transmission-procedure into the future.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the three iterations should be read at full length, not collapsed into a single statement. The Brahmana's procedure is the three-fold parallel form held verbatim across three classes of askers, and reading the iterations in full — neither summarized as *Pragapati gave the three classes the same syllable Da and they heard it as three different injunctions* nor abbreviated to the closing thunder-line — recovers the form the Adhyaya has when it gives its substantive doctrine through the verbatim repetition of a five-step dialogue across three askers. The form is the same form the contest's prior Brahmanas had used (Asvala's substitutional questions, Gargi's warp-and-woof ladder, Uddalaka's antaryamin formula); the Second Brahmana of the Fifth Adhyaya gives the form in the smallest possible scale, with three iterations rather than four or eleven or twenty-one.

Second, the relation between the same syllable and the different interpretations should be read as the Brahmana's substantive doctrine. The same syllable *Da* answers each class differently because each class arrives at it with a different need; Pragapati confirms each interpretation as *understood*. The doctrine is that the same revelation gives different injunctions to different recipients, and that the rightness of each interpretation is held inside the recipient's standing rather than inside the syllable's content. Reading the Brahmana with the syllable's identity-across-interpretations preserved — the same *Da* given three times, three different things heard — recovers the form the Upanishad has when it holds doctrine as relational to the recipient rather than as a single fixed substance.

## The text

The text is held in the Archive library under `upanishads-muller-part2`, chapter 5 (Müller translation, 1884 — *The Sacred Books of the East*, Volume XV; via SacredTexts.com). Library chapter 5 is shared across sixty-six Archive entities — four parts of the Katha Upanishad, the six parts of the Mundaka Upanishad, twenty-eight anuvakas of the Taittiriyaka, the eleven Brahmanas of the Brihadaranyaka's First and Second Adhyayas, all nine Brahmanas of the Third Adhyaya, all six Brahmanas of the Fourth Adhyaya, and the First Brahmana of the Fifth Adhyaya — and continues here into the Second Brahmana of the Fifth Adhyaya. Book 5, Part 2 of the Brihadaranyaka — the entity here — occupies passages `5.1185` through `5.1190`. Passage `5.1185` is the "Second Brahmana" header without footnote. Passages `5.1186` through `5.1190` carry the body in Müller's prose presentation across five short passages, with verse-numbered markers at `1.` (`5.1186`), `2.` (`5.1187`), and `3.` (`5.1189`).

The Brahmana contains several boundary features that should be preserved honestly:

- **Verse-1 single-passage opening with framing setup.** Passage `5.1186` carries verse 1 — the opening framing line on the threefold descendants of Pragapati and the gods' first iteration — on a single passage without internal splits and without footnotes. The framing line *The threefold descendants of Pragapati, gods, men, and Asuras (evil spirits), dwelt as; Brahmakarins (students) with their father Pragapati* sits at the open of verse 1; the substantive iteration *Having finished their studentship the gods said* follows on the same passage. The semicolon irregularity — *dwelt as; Brahmakarins* with the semicolon between *as* and *Brahmakarins* — is preserved as found in the digitized source; it is an OCR transcription artifact carried over from Müller's volume and does not affect the substance of the verse.
- **Verse-2 split with editorial bracket.** Verse 2 — the men's iteration — is split across passages `5.1187` and `5.1188` mid-clause at *Tell us something, / [paragraph continues] Sir*. Passage `5.1188` opens with the editorial bracket *[paragraph continues]* preserved in the digitized source. The split falls at the open of the men's request-formula, inside the *Tell us something, Sir* clause.
- **Verse-3 single-passage iteration.** Passage `5.1189` carries verse 3 — the Asuras' iteration — on a single passage without internal splits and without footnotes. The substantive content is held verbatim from the verse-1 and verse-2 forms with only the asking-class and the heard-word substituted.
- **Closing passage at `5.1190` with no verse-marker.** Passage `5.1190` carries the closing line on the divine voice of thunder and the substantive seal *Therefore let that triad be taught, Subduing, Giving, and Mercy*. The line is not given a verse-number; the closing of the Brahmana is held outside the verse-numbered body. The form is a closing-narrator's voice in the same register as the opening framing line of verse 1, and reads as a closing-frame to the three iterations rather than as a fourth iteration.
- **No footnotes anywhere in the Brahmana.** Müller's translation gives no footnotes on any of the five body passages. The form is distinct from the First Brahmana of the Fifth Adhyaya, which had carried four footnotes in its four passages. The Second Brahmana's substantive content — the parable, the syllable Da, the three interpretations, the divine voice of thunder, the closing triad — is given entirely without editorial apparatus.

Passage `5.1191` is the "Third Brahmana" header that opens Book 5, Part 3 of the Brihadaranyaka and is not part of this entity. The library chapter continues past this entity into the rest of the Fifth Adhyaya and the remaining portions of the volume; the Brihadaranyaka extends well beyond the boundaries of library chapter 5. About one minute's reading.

## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 5, Part 1 — paired immediate-predecessor Brahmanas of the Fifth Adhyaya, both compressed in scale but in distinct literary registers. The First Brahmana opens the Adhyaya in a mantric-aphoristic register with the Purnam mantra and the Om-as-ether-as-Brahman triple identification across two body passages, attributed to the named teacher Kauravyayaniputra in the *iti vacanāt* citation-form. The Second Brahmana opens the Adhyaya's parabolic-narrative procedure with Pragapati and the threefold descendants across five body passages, with three parallel iterations on the syllable *Da* and the three interpretations. The two Brahmanas hold the Adhyaya's opening across two distinct forms: the substantive mantra in third-person voice and the substantive parable with named-collective dialogues. Reading the two together recovers the Adhyaya's procedure as one of compressed multi-form opening rather than a single literary mode.
- **structural-parallel** → Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 1, Part 3 — paired Brihadaranyaka loci on the gods-and-Asuras contest. The Third Brahmana of the First Adhyaya gives the Devas-and-Asuras contest at the *udgītha*, with the Devas asking each sense in turn — speech, breath as smell, eye, ear, mind — to sing the chant for them, each pierced with evil by the Asuras and overwhelmed, only the breath in the mouth (*mukhya prāṇa*) scattering the Asuras like a ball of earth striking a stone. The Second Brahmana of the Fifth Adhyaya gives the same gods/Asuras pair (with men added as the third class) in a different procedural setting: not the contest at the *udgītha* but the parable of the threefold injunction, with all three classes (gods, men, Asuras) as students of Pragapati. The Brihadaranyaka holds the gods-and-Asuras pair across both Adhyayas with the same named figures held in different literary procedures — the First Adhyaya's contest-narrative and the Fifth Adhyaya's parable-of-the-syllable.
- **structural-parallel** → Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 16 — paired Sanskrit teachings on the divine and the demonic registers of conduct. The Sixteenth Chapter of the Bhagavad Gita (*Daivasura-Sampad-Vibhaga Yoga*) holds the distinction between the *daivi* (divine) and *āsuri* (demonic) qualities, with the divine qualities including fearlessness, purity, generosity, self-restraint, and non-injury — and the demonic qualities including hypocrisy, arrogance, conceit, anger, harshness. The Second Brahmana of the Fifth Adhyaya of the Brihadaranyaka holds the same divine/demonic register-distinction across the gods/Asuras pair (with men as the middle term) and gives the same substantive ethical triad — self-restraint, giving, mercy — that the Gita's *daivi* qualities catalogue includes. The Gita works the doctrine through extended classification across two long verse-sets; the Brihadaranyaka works it through three short parallel iterations of the syllable *Da*. The two texts hold paired teaching of the moral triad of restraint-giving-mercy in extremely different scales, with the Brihadaranyaka's compressed parable as the early-Upanishadic source-form and the Gita's classification-list as the later epic elaboration.
