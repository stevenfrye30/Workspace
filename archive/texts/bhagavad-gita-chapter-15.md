# Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 15 (Purushottama Yoga)

Chapter 15 of the Bhagavad Gita — the *Purushottama Yoga*, conventionally translated "The Yoga of the Supreme Person" (Arnold's translation gives the chapter the parallel name "The Book of Religion by attaining the Supreme") — is a short, image-dense chapter that condenses the analytic and classificatory work of Chapters 13 and 14 into a single sustained figure and a closing doctrine. The chapter opens with an inverted tree — the Aswattha (the banyan or peepal) — whose roots reach upward into the imperishable and whose branches extend downward into the world; the leaves are the Vedic hymns; the branches are nourished by the *gunas*; and the practice the chapter prescribes is to cut the firmly-rooted tree with the axe of non-attachment and seek the abode from which the original person flowed. The chapter then describes the individual self as a portion of Krishna that enters and departs bodies, traces Krishna's pervasion of the world's light and heat and nourishment, and closes with the doctrine of the three persons: the perishable, the imperishable, and the supreme person (*purushottama*) beyond both.

## Context

The Bhagavad Gita is a 700-verse philosophical poem in eighteen chapters, set inside Book VI (the *Bhishma Parva*) of the Mahabharata. Most scholars place its present form between roughly the second century BCE and the second century CE — a layered composition that took shape during the long growth of the epic from an older bardic core. The traditional attribution is to Vyasa, the legendary compiler of the Mahabharata; the historical authorship is unknown.

Chapter 15 stands within the Gita's late movement and continues the work of Chapters 13 and 14, but in a different register. Where those chapters had set distinctions and classifications in discursive form — field and knower, the three *gunas*, the lists of qualities — Chapter 15 condenses similar material into image. The chapter is short (twenty verses in the standard Sanskrit numbering) and tightly organized: an opening tree-image (15.1–4), an account of the embodied self entering and leaving bodies (15.5–11), a description of Krishna's pervasion of the world's elements (15.12–15), and a closing doctrine of the three persons (15.16–20). The chapter ends with Krishna saying that the most secret teaching has now been imparted; the chapters that follow turn to other classifications — the divine and the demoniac in Chapter 16, the three kinds of faith in Chapter 17, and the long summary in Chapter 18.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the chapter is image-organized rather than argument-organized. The opening inverted tree, the wind that carries scent, the moon that nourishes plants with juice, the digestive fire that cooks food in the body, the light of sun and moon and fire — each image carries the chapter forward without an explicit argumentative connection. Reading Chapter 15 with the awareness that the images are doing the work the discursive distinctions of Chapters 13 and 14 had done, rather than expecting the same kind of stepped argument, recovers the form the chapter has.

Second, the principal image is inverted. The tree of the world has its roots above, in the imperishable, and its branches below, in the perishable; the practice the chapter names — cutting the tree with non-attachment and seeking the supreme abode — moves from the branches back toward the root. Reading the chapter with attention to this inversion, rather than treating the tree as a generic image of growth, follows the way the chapter is composed.

## The text

The text is held in the Archive library under `bhagavad-gita`, chapter 15 (Edwin Arnold's verse translation, *The Song Celestial*, 1885 — Project Gutenberg edition). Arnold's verse translation here is unusually close to the Sanskrit verse-by-verse: the chapter's twenty Sanskrit shlokas correspond to fifteen English verse-paragraphs followed by a closing colophon line (passages `15.1`–`15.16` in the library's verse-level subdivision). The lighter compression — closer to Chapter 11's near-line-by-line rendering than to the heavier compression of Chapters 12 and 14 — preserves the chapter's image-sequence: each image holds its own verse-paragraph rather than being absorbed into a longer summary. A modern prose translation by the International Gita Society is held in the library as a parallel translation. About fifteen minutes' reading.
