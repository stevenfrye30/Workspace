# Qur'an, Sura 1 (Al-Fatiha), Verses 1–7

Sura 1 of the Qur'an — *Al-Fatiha*, "The Opening" — is the seven-verse sura that stands at the head of the Qur'an and is the most-recited passage of the text. It opens with praise of God, turns to direct second-person address, and closes with a petition for guidance along *the straight path*. The sura is the *fātiha* in two senses: the opening of the book, and the opening of each unit of canonical prayer, where it is recited at the beginning of every *rak'a*.

## Context

The Qur'an is the central scripture of Islam: 114 suras in classical Arabic, arranged in the canonical text roughly from longest to shortest after the short opening sura that stands first. Sura 1, *Al-Fatiha*, occupies that opening position — seven short verses placed before Sura 2 (*Al-Baqara*) and the long suras that follow. Islamic tradition places Al-Fatiha among the Meccan revelations of Muhammad's early prophetic activity (c. 610–622 CE).

Al-Fatiha is the most recited passage of the Qur'an. It is the obligatory recitation at the opening of each *rak'a*, the unit of canonical prayer, so that a Muslim performing the five daily prayers recites the sura at minimum seventeen times a day. The sura is also recited at births, marriages, funerals, and on most occasions of formal address. Its compactness, the rhyme of its verse-ends, and the directness of its movement from praise to petition do not fully carry across in translation.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the sura pivots between two grammatical persons. Verses 1–4 speak of God in the third person — "Praise be to God, Lord of the worlds, the Merciful, the Compassionate." Verse 5 turns sharply to direct address: "It is You we worship, and You we ask for help." Verses 6–7 stay in the second person, asking to be guided along *the straight path*. Reading the sura with the pivot at verse 5 visible — third-person praise, then second-person petition — recovers a structural feature that recitation can smooth into a single continuous flow.

Second, the closing verse names three categories without identifying individuals: those upon whom favor has been bestowed, those upon whom there is wrath, and those who have gone astray. The sura asks placement in the first against the second and third, but does not specify who falls in any. Later Islamic exegesis offers identifications of all three; the sura itself does not. Reading the closing verse with the categories left unfilled, rather than as supplied by later commentary, keeps the form the text has.
