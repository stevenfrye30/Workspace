# Augustine, Confessions, Book I

Augustine, in his forties and recently a bishop, addresses God in the second person and begins by trying to remember being an infant. The *Confessions* is the first sustained Christian autobiography; Book I is its shortest book and contains the whole shape of the project in miniature — praise, self-examination, and the testing of memory against what only God can have witnessed.

## Context

Written between roughly 397 and 400 in Hippo Regius. Augustine is by this point past his Manichaean years, past Milan, past his mother's death, and is composing an account of his own life as a single sustained prayer. The text is stable: the modern critical edition (Verheijen, CCSL 27) finds no recension issues that affect Book I.

The genre is new. Latin had philosophical self-examination (Cicero, Seneca) and confessional liturgy, but not a long prose work in which a named individual addresses God in the second person about himself. Whatever the *Confessions* is doing, it is doing it for the first time.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the second person is literal — every sentence is addressed to God, not to the reader. The reader is overhearing.

Second, Book I keeps reaching for what cannot be remembered: infancy, language acquisition, the moment desire begins. Augustine does not pretend to recover these directly. He reasons from what he can see in infants now, then qualifies what that can prove. The argumentative care is part of the prayer.
