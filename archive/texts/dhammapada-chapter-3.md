# Dhammapada, Chapter 3 (Thought)

## Primary Text

F. Max Müller, *The Dhammapada*, Sacred Books of the East, Vol. X (1881). Public domain via Project Gutenberg. Verses 33–43, the *Citta-vagga* — "The Mind."

**33.** As a fletcher makes straight his arrow, a wise man makes straight his trembling and unsteady thought, which is difficult to guard, difficult to hold back.

**34.** As a fish taken from his watery home and thrown on dry ground, our thought trembles all over in order to escape the dominion of Mara (the tempter).

**35.** It is good to tame the mind, which is difficult to hold in and flighty, rushing wherever it listeth; a tamed mind brings happiness.

**36.** Let the wise man guard his thoughts, for they are difficult to perceive, very artful, and they rush wherever they list: thoughts well guarded bring happiness.

**37.** Those who bridle their mind which travels far, moves about alone, is without a body, and hides in the chamber (of the heart), will be free from the bonds of Mara (the tempter).

**38.** If a man's thoughts are unsteady, if he does not know the true law, if his peace of mind is troubled, his knowledge will never be perfect.

**39.** If a man's thoughts are not dissipated, if his mind is not perplexed, if he has ceased to think of good or evil, then there is no fear for him while he is watchful.

**40.** Knowing that this body is fragile like a jar, and making this thought firm like a fortress, one should attack Mara (the tempter) with the weapon of knowledge, one should watch him when conquered, and should never rest.

**41.** Before long, alas! this body will lie on the earth, despised, without understanding, like a useless log.

**42.** Whatever a hater may do to a hater, or an enemy to an enemy, a wrongly-directed mind will do us greater mischief.

**43.** Not a mother, not a father will do so much, nor any other relative; a well-directed mind will do us greater service.

## Atlas notes

Chapter 3 of the Dhammapada — the *Citta-vagga*, "The Mind" — is a short chapter of eleven verses turning on the figure of thought as something trembling, restless, and difficult to hold. The chapter opens with the *fletcher-and-arrow* simile — the wise man straightening his unsteady thought as a fletcher straightens his arrow — and the verses that follow extend the same picture across other images: the fish out of water, the body fragile as a jar, the mind hidden in the chamber of the heart. The chapter closes on the contrast between a mind ill-directed and a mind well-directed, a contrast that carries forward the paired-contrast form of Chapters 1 and 2.

## Context

The Dhammapada is one of the books of the Khuddaka Nikaya, the fifth and shortest section of the Sutta Pitaka in the Pali Canon. It collects 423 verses arranged in twenty-six chapters by theme — pairs, heedfulness, mind, flowers, the sage, evil, and so on. The verses are attributed to the Buddha and were transmitted orally for several centuries before being committed to writing in Sri Lanka in the first century BCE. The collection took shape in roughly the third century BCE, in connection with the Theravada transmission of the canon.

Chapter 3 is the *Citta-vagga*, "The Mind." Its eleven verses are numbered 33 through 43 in the standard continuous numbering of the Dhammapada. The Pali word *citta* — mind, thought, heart, attention — is rendered variously across the chapter by F. Max Müller's 1881 translation: he uses "thought" in some verses (33, 34, 36, 38, 39, 42, 43) and "mind" in others (35, 37, 40, 41), with no apparent rule. Modern translations more commonly render *citta* uniformly as "mind." Each verse is short — two or four lines in the Pali — and each completes one statement.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the chapter is built on a single figure that returns through every verse: thought as something restless, hard to hold, prone to escape, and in need of being trained. Verse 33 states it through the arrow simile; verse 34 through the fish-out-of-water simile; verses 35 and 36 through the work of taming and guarding; verse 37 through the bridle; verse 40 through the body-as-jar and thought-as-fortress; verse 41 through the body left on the ground like a useless log; verses 42 and 43 through the contrast between the ill-directed and the well-directed mind. Reading the chapter with the same figure held visible across the eleven verses, rather than as eleven separate aphorisms, recovers the form the chapter is built on.

Second, Müller's variation between "thought" and "mind" for the single Pali word *citta* is a translator's choice, not a doctrinal distinction. Reading the chapter with the awareness that the English vocabulary varies between verses, but the underlying term is the same, keeps the form the verses share.
