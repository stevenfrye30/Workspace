# Dhammapada, Chapter 6 (The Wise Man)

Chapter 6 of the Dhammapada — the *Paṇḍita-vagga*, "The Wise Man" — is a short chapter on the figure of the *paṇḍita*, the wise man, taken across several positions. The opening verse states the form: if you see an intelligent man who shows you where true treasures lie, who shows you what is to be avoided, and who administers reproofs, follow him (76). The verses that follow keep the wise in view across companionship and instruction (77–79), self-fashioning (80), composure under blame and praise (81), serenity under the law (82–83), the renunciation of son, wealth, and lordship (84), the rarity of those who cross to the other shore (85–86), the leaving of the dark state for the bright (87), and the freedom of those who are well grounded in knowledge (89). The chapter answers Chapter 5 (The Fool): where the fool is the figure who does not perceive, the wise is the figure who does, who teaches, and who is fashioned by his own work.

## Context

The Dhammapada is one of the books of the Khuddaka Nikaya, the fifth and shortest section of the Sutta Pitaka in the Pali Canon. It collects 423 verses arranged in twenty-six chapters by theme — pairs, heedfulness, mind, flowers, the sage, evil, and so on. The verses are attributed to the Buddha and were transmitted orally for several centuries before being committed to writing in Sri Lanka in the first century BCE. The collection took shape in roughly the third century BCE, in connection with the Theravada transmission of the canon.

Chapter 6 is the *Paṇḍita-vagga*, "The Wise Man." Its verses are numbered 76 through 89 in F. Max Müller's 1881 translation, the version held in the Archive library — thirteen passages, where the standard continuous Pali numbering of the Dhammapada gives fourteen verses (76–89). Müller has combined what is conventionally verses 87 and 88 into a single English period at passage 6.87: the *leaving-the-dark-state-for-the-bright* statement and the *seeking-enjoyment-where-there-seemed-to-be-no-enjoyment* application are run together as one sentence, with no passage numbered 6.88 in the source. Per Atlas-rule **OCR/transcription honesty**, the merged passage is preserved as Müller wrote it; the chapter therefore has thirteen passages here against the standard fourteen verses elsewhere — the same kind of merging that occurs at the close of Chapter 4 (where Müller combines conventional verses 58 and 59). The next chapter opens at passage 7.90. Each verse is short — two or four lines in the Pali — and each completes one statement of the wise.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the chapter is built on the figure of the wise man taken across several positions. Verses 76–78 hold the wise as guide, teacher, and chosen companion; verse 79 holds him as one who drinks in the law; verse 80 holds him as the self-fashioner — the well-maker leads water, the fletcher bends the arrow, the carpenter bends the log, the wise fashion themselves; verses 81–82 hold him as the rock-not-shaken-by-wind and the deep-still-lake; verse 83 holds him as the one who walks on whatever befall and is neither elated nor depressed; verse 84 holds him as one who renounces son, wealth, lordship, and unfair gain; verses 85–86 hold him as the rare crosser of the shore; verse 87 holds him as the one who leaves the dark state for the bright; verse 89 holds him as the one well grounded in the elements of knowledge and free even in this world. Reading the chapter with the figure of the wise held visible across the thirteen passages, rather than as thirteen separate aphorisms, recovers the form the chapter is built on.

Second, Müller's merging of conventional verses 87 and 88 into a single passage at 6.87 is a translator's choice, not an absence of the second statement. The leaving-the-dark-state statement and the seeking-enjoyment-where-there-was-none statement are both there, joined into one English sentence. Reading the passage with the awareness that the standard fourteenth verse is preserved inside, rather than missing, keeps the form of the chapter intact even where the numbering departs from the standard.

## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Dhammapada, Chapter 5 (The Fool) — the immediate predecessor on the shelf and the polar pair with this chapter; Chapter 5 with sixteen verses on the fool's self-deception, false ownership, and the slow ripening of his deeds, Chapter 6 with thirteen passages on the wise man's companionship, self-fashioning, composure, and crossing to the other shore. The two chapters share the *bāla / paṇḍita* polarity that the Dhammapada returns to often.
- **structural-parallel** → Dhammapada, Chapter 4 (Flowers) — the earlier chapter with the same kind of Müller merging-irregularity at its close; Chapter 4 combines conventional verses 58 and 59 at passage 4.58, Chapter 6 combines conventional verses 87 and 88 at passage 6.87. The merging is a recurring feature of Müller's 1881 translation, not unique to either chapter.
- **structural-parallel** → Analects, Books 1–2 — the Confucian shelf-pair on the figure of the wise person; the Analects' opening books with short statements on the *junzi*, his companionship, his composure, and his self-cultivation, Dhammapada 6 with thirteen verses on the *paṇḍita*, his teachers, his composure, and his self-fashioning. Verse 80's craft simile (well-makers lead water, fletchers bend arrows, carpenters bend wood, wise people fashion themselves) holds the same figure of self-cultivation as a craft that the Analects works through different vocabulary.
- **structural-parallel** → Epictetus, Enchiridion, Chapters 1–5 — paired short instructional chapters on the discipline of composure under what one cannot control; the Enchiridion's opening practices on accepting what is not in one's power, Dhammapada 6 verses 81–83 on the wise man rock-not-shaken-by-wind, serene-like-deep-lake, and neither-elated-nor-depressed under happiness or sorrow.
