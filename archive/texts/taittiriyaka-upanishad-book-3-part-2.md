# Taittiriyaka Upanishad, Book 3, Part 2

Book 3, Part 2 of the Taittiriyaka Upanishad — the Second Anuvaka of the Bhriguvalli — completes the sentence the First Anuvaka left open and returns its dialogue to the same point at which the First Anuvaka closed. The sentence that ended *He performed penance. Having performed penance—* is finished here in three short clauses: *annaṁ brahmeti vyajānāt* — *he perceived that food is Brahman.* The reason follows in the same form Varuna had given as the Brahman-definition in the First Anuvaka: *for from food these beings are produced; by food, when born, they live; and into food they enter at their death.* Bhrigu returns to his father with the same opening line he gave at the threshold of the Valli — *adhīhi bhagavo brahmeti* — *Sir, teach me Brahman.* Varuna's reply does not give a new direction but turns the dialogue back to its method: *tapasā brahma vijijñāsasva, tapo brahma* — *try to know Brahman by penance, for penance is the means of knowing Brahman.* The Anuvaka closes on the same line and the same em-dash that closed the First — *He performed penance. Having performed penance—* — and the dialogue carries directly across the Anuvaka boundary into the Third, where Bhrigu's next recognition will be reached.

## Context

The Taittiriyaka Upanishad is one of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads — a small group of texts traditionally received as the philosophical core of the late Vedic literature. It belongs to the Krishna Yajurveda branch of the Vedic corpus and to the Taittiriya school of that branch; the Upanishad sits inside the Taittiriya Aranyaka. Modern scholarship places it among the older prose Upanishads, in roughly the sixth to fifth century BCE — earlier than the verse Upanishads of the Katha and Mundaka. The text is anonymous; the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship.

Book 3, Part 2 is the second of the ten anuvakas of the Bhriguvalli. The Anuvaka makes the literary engine of the Valli visible in full. The First Anuvaka set the request, the avenues, the Brahman-definition, and the means; the Second Anuvaka now reveals the recursive procedure those terms were preparing. Each Anuvaka of the Valli will hold the same shape: an opening clause that completes the previous Anuvaka's open sentence with Bhrigu's recognition (X is Brahman); a reasoning clause that repeats the Brahman-definition with X in place of *that*; Bhrigu's return to Varuna with the same opening line *Sir, teach me Brahman*; Varuna's reply that turns the dialogue back to penance; and the closing line *He performed penance. Having performed penance—* on the em-dash that opens the next Anuvaka. The substance of the recognitions traverses the same five terms the Brahmanandavalli unfolded as recursive sheaths — food, breath, mind, understanding, bliss — but the Bhriguvalli holds them in dialogic form, with Bhrigu's progressive insufficiency as its driving figure.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the Anuvaka is built on an exact repetition of structure that depends on incompleteness. The recognition is reached, but it is partial; the father sends the son back to penance; the next Anuvaka will reach a deeper recognition. Reading the Anuvaka with attention to the way the form requires the recognition to be insufficient — the form would not survive the son's first answer being correct — recovers what the Bhriguvalli is doing. The procedure is iterative: each recognition is true, and each is provisional.

Second, the Anuvaka holds the same Brahman-definition that Varuna gave in the First Anuvaka, but with the variable filled in. *That from whence these beings are born, that by which when born they live, that into which they enter at their death — that is Brahman* becomes *food is Brahman, for from food these beings are produced; by food, when born, they live; and into food they enter at their death.* The form of the definition does not change; only the named substance does. Reading the Anuvaka with the First Anuvaka's definition held alongside, attending to the substitution rather than to a separate doctrine of food, keeps the form the dialogic procedure has at its first iteration.

## The text

The text is held in the Archive library under `upanishads-muller-part2`, chapter 5 (Müller translation, 1884 — *The Sacred Books of the East*, Volume XV; via SacredTexts.com). Library chapter 5 is shared across thirty-two Archive entities — four parts of the Katha Upanishad, the six parts of the Mundaka Upanishad, the twelve anuvakas of the Sikshavalli, the nine anuvakas of the Brahmanandavalli, and the First Anuvaka of the Bhriguvalli — and continues here into the Second Anuvaka without break in the chapter. Book 3, Part 2 of the Taittiriyaka — the entity here — occupies passages `5.337` through `5.340`. Passage `5.337` is the "Second Anuvaka" header. Passage `5.338` carries Bhrigu's first recognition — *He perceived that food is Brahman* — completing the sentence that ended with the em-dash at the close of the First Anuvaka. Passage `5.339` carries Bhrigu's return to Varuna, his repetition of the opening line *Sir, teach me Brahman*, and Varuna's reply on penance. Passage `5.340` carries the closing line *He performed penance. Having performed penance—*, ending again on the same em-dash that closed the First Anuvaka. The em-dash is not a digitization artifact but a structural feature of the Bhriguvalli, repeated identically at the close of the next eight anuvakas; the dialogue carries directly across each Anuvaka boundary throughout the Valli. Passage `5.341` is the "Third Anuvaka" header that opens Book 3, Part 3 of the Taittiriyaka and is not part of this entity. The library chapter continues past this entity into the rest of the Taittiriyaka and the remaining Upanishads of the volume. About one minute's reading.

## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Taittiriyaka Upanishad, Book 3, Part 1 — paired immediately consecutive Anuvakas of the Bhriguvalli; the First Anuvaka set the inquiry — Bhrigu's request, Varuna's avenues and Brahman-definition, the closing line *He performed penance. Having performed penance—* — the Second Anuvaka completes the sentence with Bhrigu's first recognition that food is Brahman, brings him back to his father, and closes again on the same line and the same em-dash. The dialogic engine of the Valli, set up in the First Anuvaka, is now visible in full.
- **structural-parallel** → Taittiriyaka Upanishad, Book 2, Part 2 — paired moments inside the same Upanishad on food as Brahman; the Brahmanandavalli's Second Anuvaka holds the *annamaya* sloka — *from food are produced all creatures which dwell on earth; then they live by food, and in the end they return to food; for food is the oldest of all beings* — set as the framing recognition of the food-self inside the recursive descent, the Bhriguvalli's Second Anuvaka holds the same recognition in the dialogic form: Bhrigu, after penance, perceives that food is Brahman because from food beings are born, by food they live, into food they enter at death. Two registers of the same recognition — recursive descent and progressive dialogue — in the same Upanishad's two vallis.
- **structural-parallel** → Katha Upanishad, Book 1, Part 2 — paired Upanishadic moments where the disciple's first answer is found to be insufficient and the teacher returns him to inquiry; the Katha's Second Valli opens with Yama's distinction between *śreyas* and *preyas* and his praise of Nachiketas for refusing the easier boons in order to press for the deeper teaching, the Bhriguvalli's Second Anuvaka holds the closely related procedure: Bhrigu reaches the recognition that food is Brahman and returns to Varuna for further teaching, and Varuna sends him back to penance. Both texts hold the form of teaching in which a partial answer is honored as a step rather than as the destination.
