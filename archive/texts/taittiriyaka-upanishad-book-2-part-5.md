# Taittiriyaka Upanishad, Book 2, Part 5

Book 2, Part 5 of the Taittiriyaka Upanishad — the Fifth Anuvaka of the Brahmanandavalli — completes the recursive descent the Second Anuvaka inaugurated. The opening sloka places the *vijñānamaya* under its framing recognition: *vijñānaṁ yajñaṁ tanute, karmāṇi tanute api ca; vijñānaṁ devāḥ sarve brahma jyeṣṭham upāsate; vijñānaṁ brahma ced veda, tasmāc cen na pramādyati* — *understanding performs the sacrifice, it performs all sacred acts; all Devas worship understanding as Brahman, as the oldest; if a man knows understanding as Brahman, and if he does not swerve from it, he leaves all evils behind in the body and attains all his wishes.* The line that follows the sloka closes the *vijñānamaya* loop with the recursive identity-formula the Valli has been using: *the embodied Self of this, consisting of understanding, is the same as that of the former, consisting of mind.* The Anuvaka then turns the recursive formula on the next inner sheath — and finds that the next inner sheath is the last. *Different from this, which consists of understanding, is the other inner Self, which consists of bliss.* The recursive opening returns; the closing supports do not. The anatomy of the *ānandamaya* is given in the vocabulary of joy. *Priya* (joy) is its head; *moda* (satisfaction) is its right arm; *pramoda* (great satisfaction) is its left arm; *ānanda* (bliss) is its trunk. And then the support — the foundation that has been a different term at each previous level (*pratiṣṭhā* of food, breath, mind, knowledge) — is named directly: *brahma puccham pratiṣṭhā* — Brahman is the support. The descent ends here. The Anuvaka closes with the same pointer that has carried the previous anuvakas — *on this there is also the following sloka* — turning the reader toward the Sixth Anuvaka, where the Valli will leave the recursive form and turn to the longer instruction on Brahman as bliss that the Valli is named for.

## Context

The Taittiriyaka Upanishad is one of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads — a small group of texts traditionally received as the philosophical core of the late Vedic literature. It belongs to the Krishna Yajurveda branch of the Vedic corpus and to the Taittiriya school of that branch; the Upanishad sits inside the Taittiriya Aranyaka. Modern scholarship places it among the older prose Upanishads, in roughly the sixth to fifth century BCE — earlier than the verse Upanishads of the Katha and Mundaka. The text is anonymous; the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship.

Book 2, Part 5 sits inside the Brahmanandavalli, the Second Valli of the Taittiriyaka. The Fifth Anuvaka closes the recursive structure the Valli has been carrying since the Second Anuvaka. Where the Second through Fourth Anuvakas each placed the previously-introduced sheath under its own sloka and introduced the next inner sheath, the Fifth Anuvaka does the same for the *vijñānamaya*, but the next sheath it introduces is the last: the *ānandamaya* whose support is named directly as Brahman. The literary engine of the Valli — sloka, prose, recursive formula, anatomical mapping, pointer — has reached the bottom of its inward descent. The Anuvakas that follow the Fifth will not extend the recursion further; instead, the Valli will turn to the Sixth Anuvaka's existence/non-existence sloka and to the long meditation on the bliss of Brahman that gives the Brahmanandavalli its name.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the Anuvaka holds the same recursive form the Valli has been holding for three anuvakas, and it holds it through the descent's last step. Reading the Anuvaka with the Second, Third, and Fourth Anuvakas held alongside it, attending to where the form persists and where it shifts, recovers what the Brahmanandavalli's recursion has been doing — the same procedure carried out at five successive levels of inwardness, with the named support of each level (the support of food, breath, mind, knowledge, bliss) eventually arriving at a name that is no longer a fifth thing in a list but the source the descent was approaching from the start.

Second, the Anuvaka does not announce its own closing. The recursive formula returns at the *ānandamaya* in the same shape it had at every previous level — *the former is filled by this; it also has the shape of man; like the human shape of the former is the human shape of the latter* — and the anatomical mapping fills in its terms in the same shape as before. What changes is only that the named support of the *ānandamaya* is *brahma*. The descent is closed without ceremony, by the recursion's own internal logic. Reading the closing without forcing it into a separate culmination — letting the recursion arrive at its own end on the same terms it began on — keeps the form the Brahmanandavalli has at this point.

## The text

The text is held in the Archive library under `upanishads-muller-part2`, chapter 5 (Müller translation, 1884 — *The Sacred Books of the East*, Volume XV; via SacredTexts.com). Library chapter 5 is shared across twenty-six Archive entities — four parts of the Katha Upanishad, the six parts of the Mundaka Upanishad, the twelve anuvakas of the Sikshavalli (Book 1) of the Taittiriyaka, and the First through Fourth Anuvakas of the Brahmanandavalli (Book 2, Parts 1 through 4) — and continues here into the Fifth Anuvaka without break in the chapter. Book 2, Part 5 of the Taittiriyaka — the entity here — occupies passages `5.249` through `5.252`. Passage `5.249` is the "Fifth Anuvaka" header. Passage `5.250` carries the opening sloka in Müller's quotation marks together with the recursive identity-line closing the *vijñānamaya* loop; the passage ends mid-word at "min" — a digitization artifact for "mind" carried over from Müller's volume; the surrounding text is otherwise complete and continuous. Passage `5.251` carries the introduction of the *ānandamaya* with the recursive formula and the complete anatomical mapping — joy as head, satisfaction as right arm, great satisfaction as left arm, bliss as trunk, and Brahman as support. The naming of Brahman as the support of the innermost sheath is the closing of the recursion that began at the Second Anuvaka; Müller renders the line as a continuation of the same prose paragraph rather than as a separately marked verse, in keeping with the Brahmanandavalli's prose presentation throughout. Passage `5.252` is the closing pointer "On this there is also the following Sloka:". Passage `5.253` is the "Sixth Anuvaka" header that opens Book 2, Part 6 of the Taittiriyaka and is not part of this entity. The library chapter continues past this entity into the rest of the Taittiriyaka and the remaining Upanishads of the volume. About three minutes' reading.

## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Taittiriyaka Upanishad, Book 2, Part 4 — paired immediately consecutive Anuvakas of the Brahmanandavalli; the Fourth Anuvaka placed the *manomaya* under its own sloka and used the recursive formula to introduce the *vijñānamaya*, the Fifth Anuvaka places the *vijñānamaya* under its own sloka and uses the same recursive formula to introduce the *ānandamaya* — the same procedure the Valli has carried since the Second Anuvaka, now reaching the level at which the named support is Brahman itself. The recursion completes inside its own form.
- **structural-parallel** → Mundaka Upanishad, Book 2, Part 2 — paired Sanskrit passages on Brahman as the innermost ground; the Mundaka's Second Khanda holds the recognition that *in the highest golden sheath there is the Brahman without passions and without parts; that is pure, that is the light of lights, that is it which they know who know the Self* (Mundaka 2.2.9), the Taittiriyaka's Fifth Anuvaka holds the closely related recognition through the *ānandamaya*: the innermost sheath, in the language of joy, with Brahman directly named as its support. Paired moments where the late-Vedic literature reaches the recognition that the inmost level of the embodied being is itself the supreme reality.
- **structural-parallel** → Katha Upanishad, Book 2, Part 3 — paired Upanishadic culminations of inward progression; the Katha's Sixth Valli closes with the line that *he who knows that highest Brahman becomes Brahman* (Katha 2.3.9), the Taittiriyaka's Fifth Anuvaka closes the recursive descent of the Brahmanandavalli with Brahman named as the support of the innermost sheath — both texts arriving at the same recognition by different procedures: the Katha by the closing seal of a long dialogue, the Taittiriyaka by the internal logic of its recursion finding the source it has been approaching from the first sheath.
