# Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 5, Part 14

Book 5, Part 14 of the Brihadaranyaka Upanishad — the Fourteenth Brahmana of the Fifth Adhyaya — is a substantially more developed Brahmana than the Adhyaya's prior compact units. The Adhyaya's prior thirteen Brahmanas have run between two passages (most compact) and five passages, with substantive content typically held inside one or two body passages. The Fourteenth Brahmana works the substantive Gayatri-doctrine through eight numbered verses across fourteen passages. The form returns to the more extended substantive scale that the Adhyaya's Second Brahmana (the Pragapati-Da-Da-Da parable) and the Fifth Brahmana (the cosmogony plus *satyam* etymology plus paired body-anatomies) had used, and brings back the named-figure dialogue with Ganaka Vaideha that the Adhyaya had not held since the Fourth Adhyaya.

The Brahmana works through five substantive procedural shapes in sequence. The first is the **foot-by-foot analysis of the Gayatri** (verses 1–3). The Gayatri is the most-cited Vedic meter, and its substantive form is twenty-four syllables in three feet of eight syllables each, plus a fourth foot that is held as transcendent in the substantive Vedic-Upanishadic tradition. The Brahmana works each of the first three feet through three substantive identifications, and the fourth foot through three substantive names.

**Verse 1 at `5.1224`** gives the first foot: *The words Bhumi (earth), Antariksha (sky), and Dyu (heaven) form eight syllables. One foot of the Gayatri consists of eight syllables. This (one foot) of it is that (i.e. the three worlds). And he who thus knows that foot of it, conquers as far as the three worlds extend.* The substantive identification is the first foot = the three worlds; the substantive benefit is the substantive conquest of the three worlds. Müller's footnote `[*1]` attaches at *Dyu*.

**Verse 2 at `5.1225`** gives the second foot: *The Rikas, the Yagumshi, and the Samani form eight syllables. One foot (the second) of the Gayatri consists of eight syllables. This (one foot) of it is that (i.e. the three Vedas, the Rig-veda, Yagur-veda, and Sama-veda). And he who thus knows that foot of it, conquers as far as that threefold knowledge extends.* The substantive identification is the second foot = the three Vedas; the substantive benefit is the substantive conquest of the threefold knowledge.

**Verse 3 at `5.1226`–`5.1227`** gives the third foot and turns to the fourth: *The Prana (the up-breathing), the Apana (the down-breathing), and the Vyana (the back-breathing) form eight syllables. One foot (the third) of the Gayatri consists of eight syllables. This (one foot) of it is that (i.e. the three vital breaths). And he who thus knows that foot of it, conquers as far as there is anything that breathes.* The substantive third-foot identification is the three vital breaths; the substantive benefit is the substantive conquest of all that breathes. Verse 3 then turns to the fourth foot: *And of that (Gayatri, or speech) this indeed is the fourth (turiya), the bright (darsata) foot, shining high above the skies.* The fourth foot is given three substantive names with their substantive glosses: *What is here called turiya (the fourth) is meant for katurtha (the fourth); what is called darsatam padam (the bright foot) is meant for him who is as it were seen (the person in the sun); and what is called paroragas (he who shines high above the skies) is meant for him who shines higher and higher above every sky. And he who thus knows that foot of the Gayatri, shines thus himself also with happiness and glory.*

The second substantive procedural shape is the **resting-chain** (verse 4 at `5.1228`). The substantive form is a chain of substantive supports: *That Gayatri (as described before with its three feet) rests on that fourth foot, the bright one, high above the sky. And that again rests on the True (satyam), and the True is the eye, for the eye is (known to be) true.* The substantive eye-as-True identification is given through a substantive juridical example: *And therefore even now, if two persons come disputing, the one saying, I saw, the other, I heard, then we should trust the one who says, I saw.* The chain continues: *And the True again rests on force (balam), and force is life (prana), and that (the True) rests on life. Therefore they say, force is stronger than the True.* The substantive close gives the etymology of *Gayatri*: *Thus does that Gayatri rest with respect to the self (as life). That Gayatri protects (tatre) the vital breaths (gayas); the gayas are the pranas (vital breaths), and it protects them. And because it protects (tatre) the vital breaths (gayas), therefore it is called Gayatri.* The substantive name *Gayatri* is derived from *tatre* (protects) and *gayas* (vital breaths), with Müller's parentheticals preserving the underlying Sanskrit verbal forms.

The third substantive procedural shape is the **critique of alternative substantive metric** (verses 5–6 at `5.1229`–`5.1231`). Verse 5 holds: *Some teach that Savitri as an Anushtubh verse, saying that speech is Anushtubh, and that we teach that speech. Let no one do this, but let him teach the Gayatri as Savitri.* The substantive critique names the alternative substantive teaching tradition — that the Savitri verse is in Anushtubh meter rather than Gayatri — and substantively rejects it. Verse 6 compares substantive teaching-fees with substantive Gayatri-feet: even the substantive fee of the three worlds, or of the threefold knowledge, or of all that breathes — substantively each obtains only one foot of the Gayatri; the substantive fourth bright foot *cannot be obtained by anybody — whence then could one receive such a fee?*

The fourth substantive procedural shape is the **substantive adoration with curse-and-blessing form** (verse 7 at `5.1232`–`5.1234`). The substantive adoration begins at `5.1232`: *The adoration of that (Gayatri):* and continues at `5.1233`: *O Gayatri, thou hast one foot, two feet, three feet, four feet. Thou art footless, for thou art not known. Worship to thy fourth bright foot above the skies.* The substantive adoration holds the paradoxical form *thou art footless, for thou art not known* — the substantive un-knowability of the fourth foot, paired with the substantive worship at the close. The substantive curse-and-blessing form follows at `5.1233`–`5.1234`: *If one (who knows this) hates someone and says, "May he not obtain this," or "May this wish not be accomplished to him," then that wish is not accomplished to him against whom he thus prays, or if he says, "May I obtain this."* The substantive doctrine names the *vidyā*'s substantive operative power in the substantive curse-and-blessing register.

The fifth substantive procedural shape is the **named-figure dialogue with Ganaka Vaideha** (verse 8 at `5.1235`). The dialogue is the only Adhyaya appearance of Ganaka Vaideha — the same Janaka who held the long Yajnavalkya contest of Book 3 and the audience-dialogues of Book 4 — and it brings back the named royal figure into the substantive teaching. *And thus Ganaka Vaideha spoke on this point to Budila Asvatarasvi: 'How is it that thou who spokest thus as knowing the Gayatri, hast become an elephant and carriest me?'* The substantive narrative is unusual: Budila Asvatarasvi, who had professed knowledge of the Gayatri, has been substantively transformed into an elephant carrying Janaka — apparently a substantive consequence of incomplete knowledge. Budila's substantive answer: *Your Majesty, I did not know its mouth. Agni, fire, is indeed its mouth; and if people pile even what seems much (wood) on the fire, it consumes it all. And thus a man who knows this, even if he commits what seems much evil, consumes it all and becomes pure, clean, and free from decay and death.* The substantive doctrine is that the substantive mouth of the Gayatri — Agni, fire — substantively consumes evil, and the substantive knower of this becomes substantively pure.

The Brahmana closes at `5.1236` with the actual Savitri/Gayatri mantra in pure Sanskrit transliteration without translation: *Tat savitur vrinimahe vayam devasya bhoganam Sreshtham sarvadhatamam turam bhagasya dhimahi.* The substantive form is the substantive Yajurvedic version of the Savitri verse — distinct from the more widely known Rigvedic Savitri *Tat savitur varenyam bhargo devasya dhimahi dhiyo yo nah pracodayat* — and Müller's translation preserves the substantive Sanskrit text without rendering it into English. The substantive close of the Brahmana is the substantive mantra itself.

## Context

The Brihadaranyaka Upanishad is one of the two earliest of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads, sharing that position with the Chandogya. It belongs to the Shukla Yajurveda branch of the Vedic corpus and forms the closing portion of the Shatapatha Brahmana — the *Aranyaka* in its name marks it as a forest-text recited at the late stages of Vedic study. Modern scholarship places the Brihadaranyaka in roughly the eighth to sixth century BCE, prior to the Buddha; the text is anonymous, and the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship. The text is the longest of the principal Upanishads and is typically structured in six *adhyayas* (chapters), each containing several *brahmanas* (sections).

Book 5, Part 14 holds three of the Adhyaya's distinctive features in concentrated form. The first is the substantive Gayatri-doctrine. The Gayatri verse is the most-cited Vedic mantra, addressed to the substantive solar deity Savitr and recited at the substantive Vedic Sandhya rites of dawn and dusk. The substantive form of the Gayatri is twenty-four syllables in three feet of eight, plus a substantive fourth foot held as transcendent in the substantive Vedic-Upanishadic tradition. The Brihadaranyaka's Fourteenth Brahmana of the Fifth Adhyaya gives the most extended Upanishadic teaching on the substantive Gayatri-meter inside the Brihadaranyaka, with the foot-by-foot substantive identification, the substantive resting-chain, the substantive critique of alternative metric, the substantive adoration, and the named-figure dialogue all holding the substantive Gayatri-doctrine across multiple substantive registers.

The second is the substantive return of Ganaka Vaideha. The named royal figure of the Brihadaranyaka's Yajnavalkya cycle — who had presided over the great contest at the close of the Third Adhyaya, who had received Yajnavalkya in audience across the four Brahmanas of the Fourth Adhyaya, and who had not appeared in the Fifth Adhyaya's first thirteen Brahmanas — returns at verse 8 of the Fourteenth Brahmana for a brief substantive exchange with Budila Asvatarasvi. The substantive narrative is unusual: Budila has been substantively transformed into an elephant, and his substantive transformation is explained by his substantive incomplete knowledge of the Gayatri (he did not know the mouth, Agni). The substantive doctrine of the substantive mouth-of-the-Gayatri-as-fire-consuming-all-evil is given through the named-figure dialogue rather than as an abstract identification, and the substantive return of Ganaka Vaideha names the Adhyaya's substantive narrative continuity with the prior Adhyayas.

The third is the substantive Sanskrit-transliterated mantra at the close. The Brihadaranyaka's Fifth Adhyaya has worked through compact substantive teachings throughout, and the Fourteenth Brahmana closes with the substantive Yajurvedic Savitri verse in pure Sanskrit transliteration without English translation — the only substantive mantra preserved in this form across the Adhyaya's fourteen Brahmanas to this point. The substantive doctrine is that the substantive content of the Brahmana (the Gayatri-foot-analysis, the resting-chain, the etymology, the adoration, the named-figure dialogue) is the substantive scaffolding for the substantive recitation of the substantive Savitri verse itself; the substantive close gives the substantive mantra rather than further substantive doctrine. The substantive form holds the substantive Vedic-recitation tradition as the substantive operative form of the *vidyā*: knowing the substantive Gayatri-doctrine is knowing the substantive Savitri-verse, and the substantive close of the Brahmana is the substantive verse itself.

The closing form is one of the Adhyaya's most distinctive. The substantive Sanskrit transliteration of the Savitri verse closes the Brahmana in the substantive recitation-form rather than in the substantive doctrinal-statement-form. The Adhyaya's prior Brahmanas had closed on substantive English-prose seals (substantive identifications, substantive benefit-clauses, substantive *who knows this* forms); the Fourteenth Brahmana closes on the substantive Sanskrit mantra itself.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the substantive foot-by-foot analysis of the Gayatri should be preserved at the level of the substantive 8-syllable count for each foot. The substantive Vedic meter-tradition counts substantive syllables in substantive Sanskrit recitation, and the substantive Gayatri's substantive 24-syllable form is held in three substantive 8-syllable feet. The Brahmana's substantive identifications work through the substantive 8-syllable count: *The words Bhumi, Antariksha, and Dyu form eight syllables. One foot of the Gayatri consists of eight syllables.* Reading the substantive identifications with the substantive syllable-count preserved — the substantive numerical correspondence between the substantive named items (three worlds, three Vedas, three breaths) and the substantive syllable-count of the foot — recovers the substantive form of the Vedic-meter analysis.

Second, the substantive named-figure dialogue at verse 8 should be read alongside the prior Brahmanas' Janaka dialogues rather than as an isolated narrative. The substantive Ganaka Vaideha of the Brihadaranyaka's Books 3 and 4 — who had presided over the substantive Yajnavalkya contest, who had received the substantive Janaka–Yajnavalkya audience teaching, who had offered substantive cattle and substantive kingdom-and-self surrenders — returns at verse 8 of the Fourteenth Brahmana of the Fifth Adhyaya in a substantively different mode. The substantive Janaka here is the substantive recipient of Budila's substantive Gayatri-mouth doctrine rather than the substantive recipient of Yajnavalkya's substantive Self-doctrine. Reading the substantive return alongside the prior Janaka-narratives recovers the substantive form of Janaka as the substantive named royal figure across the Upanishad's multiple substantive teachings.
