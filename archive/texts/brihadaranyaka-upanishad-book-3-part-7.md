# Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 3, Part 7

Book 3, Part 7 of the Brihadaranyaka Upanishad — the Seventh Brahmana of the Third Adhyaya, traditionally cited as the *Antaryamin Brahmana* — continues the Janaka Vaideha contest opened in the First Brahmana and continued through the Second, Third, Fourth, Fifth, and Sixth. The court frame is not reset. The Sixth Brahmana closed on Gargi Vakaknavi held in induced silence by Yajnavalkya's threat that her head would fall off if she asked one more question; the Seventh opens directly into the next challenger, who returns the head-falling threat in the opposite direction. *Then Uddalaka Aruni asked.* The named challenger is identified through his patronymic *Aruni* in the narrator's introduction, and Yajnavalkya will address him through his other patronymic *Gautama* in the substantive replies; the two names sit on the same person across the Brahmana.

The Brahmana mirrors the Sixth's closing. In the Sixth, Yajnavalkya stopped Gargi at the worlds of Brahman with the warning *do not ask too much, lest thy head should fall off*. In the Seventh, Uddalaka opens the question with the same threat directed at Yajnavalkya: *if thou, O Yagnavalkya, without knowing that string and the puller within, drivest away those Brahma-cows, thy head will fall off.* The contest's two head-falling threats sit on either side of the Brahmana boundary, with the priest threatening the challenger on one side and the challenger threatening the priest on the other. The contest's procedure carries the threat across the boundary; the direction reverses.

The opening question is given inside a Gandharva-frame, echoing the Gandharva-frame of the Third Brahmana. The frame is identical at the level of the household — *we dwelt among the Madras in the houses of Patankala Kapya, studying the sacrifice* — and identical at the form-level — *His wife was possessed of a Gandharva, and we asked him: "Who art thou?"* — but the named Gandharva and the substantive doctrine differ. In the Third Brahmana, Bhugyu had reported a Gandharva named Sudhanvan the Angirasa, who had spoken about the Parikshitas; in the Seventh Brahmana, Uddalaka reports a Gandharva named Kabandha Atharvana, who put two questions to Patankala Kapya and the students that neither could answer: *Dost thou know, Kapya, that thread by which this world and the other world, and all beings are strung together?* and *Dost thou know, Kapya, that puller (ruler) within (antaryamin), who within pulls (rules) this world and the other world and all beings?* The two not-knowings are reported verbatim — *I do not know it, Sir* on each — and the Gandharva's closing word names the knower of both as the knower of everything: *He, O Kapya, who knows that thread and him who pulls it within, he knows Brahman, he knows the worlds, he knows the Devas, he knows the Vedas, he knows the Bhutas (creatures), he knows the Self, he knows everything.* Uddalaka's challenge to Yajnavalkya is to give the two answers Patankala Kapya could not.

Yajnavalkya's first move is confidence. *O Gautama, I believe I know that thread and the puller within.* Uddalaka's pushback is one of the contest's most-cited lines: *Anybody may say, I know, I know. Tell what thou knowest.* The contest's procedure here is altered. Where Asvala, Artabhaga, Bhugyu, Ushasta, Kahola, and Gargi had each asked their substantive questions and received their answers without pausing to verify the priest's standing to answer, Uddalaka holds the procedure at the threshold. The reply is not given until the assertion has been authenticated by substantive content.

Verse 2 gives the thread. *Vayu (air) is that thread, O Gautama. By air, as by a thread, O Gautama, this world and the other world, and all creatures are strung together. Therefore, O Gautama, people say of a dead person that his limbs have become unstrung; for by air, as by a thread, O Gautama, they were strung together.* The argument is etymological — the death-language of *unstrung limbs* is given as evidence that the everyday Sanskrit lexicon already holds the doctrine the Gandharva had asked Patankala Kapya about. Vayu had been named by the First Brahmana as the Udgatri priest's element (Vayu = the wind = the breath); Vayu had been named by the Third Brahmana as the cosmic conveyor of the souls of those who have performed the horse-sacrifice. The Seventh Brahmana names Vayu as the universal thread.

Uddalaka's response confirms the answer and turns the dialogue to the second doctrine: *So it is, O Yagnavalkya. Tell now (who is) the puller within.*

The puller-within answer is the Brahmana's substantive body. Verses 3 through 23 are twenty-one substitutional verses, each one given in the same form: *He who dwells in X, and within X, whom X does not know, whose body X is, and who pulls (rules) X within, he is thy Self, the puller (ruler) within, the immortal.* The variable X moves across twenty-one named loci, and the substantive cycle is divided in two by a narrator's interruption at the close of verse 14: *So far with respect to the gods (adhidaivatam); now with respect to beings (adhibhutam).* The two cycles work the same form across two fields.

The cosmic cycle, verses 3–14, holds twelve loci. *Earth, water, fire, sky, air (vayu), heaven (dyu), sun (Aditya), space (disah), moon and stars (kandra-tarakam), ether (akasa), darkness (tamas), light (tegas).* The order works from the watery and fiery foundations of the world through the atmospheric and stellar bodies to the highest atmospheric and luminary categories — the moon and stars and ether — and then to the two limit-conditions of light and darkness. The beings cycle, verses 15–23, holds nine loci. *All beings (the inclusive head-term), breath (prana), tongue (vak), eye, ear, mind, skin, knowledge, seed.* The order works from the totality through the named senses and the cognitive faculty to seed — the body's reproductive and life-bearing material as the closing locus of the cycle.

The closing of verse 23 turns to the negative formula. *Unseen, but seeing; unheard, but hearing; unperceived, but perceiving; unknown, but knowing.* The four-clause negation is paired with the four-clause negation of the Fourth Brahmana (*Thou couldst not see the seer of sight, thou couldst not hear the hearer of hearing, nor perceive the perceiver of perception, nor know the knower of knowledge*); the Seventh Brahmana's form is the *but-construction* — the Self is unseen but sees, unheard but hears — where the Fourth Brahmana's form is the second-person impossibility — *thou couldst not see the seer*. The Seventh closes the negation with the four-clause uniqueness — *There is no other seer but he, there is no other hearer but he, there is no other perceiver but he, there is no other knower but he* — and the same identification line — *This is thy Self, the ruler within, the immortal* — followed by the closer *Everything else is of evil* and the narrator's line: *After that Uddalaka Aruni held his peace.* The closer pairs with the Fifth Brahmana's *Everything else is of evil* and stands one preposition apart from the Fourth Brahmana's *Everything also is of evil*.

## Context

The Brihadaranyaka Upanishad is one of the two earliest of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads, sharing that position with the Chandogya. It belongs to the Shukla Yajurveda branch of the Vedic corpus and forms the closing portion of the Shatapatha Brahmana — the *Aranyaka* in its name marks it as a forest-text recited at the late stages of Vedic study. Modern scholarship places the Brihadaranyaka in roughly the eighth to sixth century BCE, prior to the Buddha; the text is anonymous, and the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship. The text is the longest of the principal Upanishads and is typically structured in six *adhyayas* (chapters), each containing several *brahmanas* (sections).

Book 3, Part 7 is one of the most-cited Brahmanas of the Brihadaranyaka. The doctrine of the *antaryamin* — the puller (ruler) within, who dwells inside every cosmic and bodily locus, whose body that locus is, who is not known by that locus, and who is the immortal Self — is a foundational locus for the later Vedanta literature, and it is one of the principal *prasthanas* the Vedanta-Sutras and their commentaries take up by name. Ramanuja's eleventh-century Vishishtadvaita reading of the *Brahma-Sutras* gives the *antaryami-brahmana* a leading place in its account of the Self–world relation: the locus is the body, the antaryamin is the soul, and the relation between them is the relation between body and soul carried through every level of the cosmos and every level of the human person. Sankara's earlier Advaita reading places the same Brahmana within the doctrine of the inner Self that is identical with Brahman. The Seventh Brahmana sits in the Vedanta literature as the textual source for the *antaryamin* line of doctrine; reading the Brahmana with the substantive cycle held at full length — twenty-one verses without abbreviation — is the form in which the Brahmana has been received in the tradition.

The Brahmana also holds two textual signatures of the Adhyaya in concentrated form. The first is the Patankala Kapya household. The Third and Seventh Brahmanas both place their challenger's question inside a Gandharva-frame at the same household — *we wandered about as students, and came to the house of Patankala Kapya* in the Third, *we dwelt among the Madras in the houses of Patankala Kapya, studying the sacrifice* in the Seventh — with two different Gandharvas (Sudhanvan the Angirasa and Kabandha Atharvana) and two different doctrines (the destination of the Parikshitas and the antaryamin). The repetition of the household across the Adhyaya is a literary signature of the contest's narrative form; the form makes a second use of the same setting without naming it as a return.

The second is the head-falling threat. The Sixth Brahmana closed with Yajnavalkya threatening Gargi: *do not ask too much, lest thy head should fall off*. The Seventh opens with Uddalaka threatening Yajnavalkya: *thy head will fall off* if he drives away the Brahma-cows without knowing the antaryamin. The two threats sit on either side of the Brahmana boundary in mirror reversal — priest-to-challenger, challenger-to-priest — and the form preserved across the boundary is the head-falling-off form. Reading the two Brahmanas with the threat-mirror held alongside each other recovers the Adhyaya's contest as a form that holds the same threat-language in opposite directions across consecutive Brahmanas.

The closing form is silence again. The contest's seven closings now sit in the pattern silence (voluntary) / private withdrawal / silence (voluntary) / silence (voluntary) / silence (voluntary) / silence (induced) / silence (voluntary). The Adhyaya's silence-closing predominates; the Second Brahmana's private withdrawal and the Sixth's induced silence remain the two deviations.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the Brahmana's substantive cycle works through twenty-one substitutional verses held verbatim. The form *He who dwells in X, and within X, whom X does not know, whose body X is, and who pulls (rules) X within, he is thy Self, the puller (ruler) within, the immortal* is given in the same wording across each of the twenty-one loci, with only the variable X changing. Reading the cycle with the verbatim repetition kept exactly as the source has it — neither smoothed away nor compressed into *and likewise for water, fire, and the rest* — recovers the form the Brahmana has when its substance is the doctrine of the inner ruler held across every cosmic and bodily locus. The Brahmana works the doctrine through the procedure of full restatement at every step.

Second, the substantive body is broken in the middle by a single short narrator's line. *So far with respect to the gods (adhidaivatam); now with respect to beings (adhibhutam).* The line sits between verse 14 and verse 15 on its own short passage. It does not give a substantive teaching; it marks the structural division of the antaryamin-cycle into the cosmic field (verses 3–14) and the field of beings (verses 15–23). The contest's prior Brahmanas had used narrator's interruptions as field-markers in the First Brahmana — *Then Asvala, the Hotri priest, undertook to question him*, *Next follow the achievements*, *After that Asvala held his peace* — and the Seventh Brahmana takes up the same form here. Reading the Brahmana with the *adhidaivatam* / *adhibhutam* division held as a structural feature, not smoothed into the substantive cycle, recovers the form the Brahmana has when its substance is held across two fields rather than one.
