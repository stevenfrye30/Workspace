# Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 7 (Jnana Vijnana Yoga)

Chapter 7 of the Bhagavad Gita — the *Jnana Vijnana Yoga*, conventionally translated "The Yoga of Knowledge and Discernment" (Arnold's translation gives the chapter the parallel name "The Book of Religion by Discernment") — opens the Gita's middle movement and shifts the dialogue's register. Through Chapter 6, Krishna has been answering Arjuna's practical question — what kind of action is right action? — through successive accounts of the self, of action without attachment, of sacrifice, and of the discipline of attention. In Chapter 7 he turns to a different kind of speech: he begins to describe his own nature, his higher and lower aspects, his pervasion of all things, and the relation in which devotees stand to him. The chapter's thirty verses introduce a register that the later Gita will sustain — Krishna speaking directly of himself — without abandoning the dialogue form or the practical address to Arjuna.

## Context

The Bhagavad Gita is a 700-verse philosophical poem in eighteen chapters, set inside Book VI (the *Bhishma Parva*) of the Mahabharata. Most scholars place its present form between roughly the second century BCE and the second century CE — a layered composition that took shape during the long growth of the epic from an older bardic core. The traditional attribution is to Vyasa, the legendary compiler of the Mahabharata; the historical authorship is unknown.

Chapter 7 opens the Gita's middle movement. The early chapters (2–6) are organized around the question Arjuna's refusal raised — what to do at the present moment — and Krishna's answers stay close to that question. From Chapter 7 forward, the Gita widens its scope: Krishna begins to speak directly of his own nature, his relation to the world, and the place of the devotee. The thirty verses of Chapter 7 introduce three motifs that recur through the later chapters: the distinction between Krishna's lower nature (the eight-fold material that constitutes the visible world) and his higher nature (the life-element by which the world is sustained); his pervasion of perceptible things (he is the taste in water, the light of sun and moon, the fragrance of the earth); and his relation to four kinds of devotees who turn to him — the distressed, the seeker after knowledge, the seeker after gain, and the wise. The chapter closes with the veiling power of *yoga-maya* and the transcendence of those who know him past the opposites.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the chapter alternates between description of reality and direct address to Arjuna without warning. A passage on Krishna's lower and higher natures sits beside a direct address ("hear from me, Arjuna"); a passage on his pervasion of perceptible things sits beside an explanation of why most do not see him; a description of the four kinds of devotees sits beside a personal evaluation of which is closest. Reading the chapter with the alternation between cosmological description and direct address visible, rather than expecting either alone, recovers the form the chapter has.

Second, the chapter widens the Gita's frame rather than abandoning earlier themes. Action without attachment, the disciplined yogi, the steady mind — all return inside Chapter 7's wider description. The wise, the fourth and dearest of the four kinds of devotees Krishna names, are described in terms continuous with the *sthitaprajna* of Chapter 2 and the disciplined yogi of Chapter 6. Reading Chapter 7 as the opening of a wider frame around the Gita's earlier teaching, rather than as the beginning of a separate doctrine, follows the way the chapter is composed.

## The text

The text is held in the Archive library under `bhagavad-gita`, chapter 7 (Edwin Arnold's verse translation, *The Song Celestial*, 1885 — Project Gutenberg edition). Arnold's verse translation compresses the chapter's thirty Sanskrit shlokas into ten English verse-paragraphs followed by a closing colophon line (passages `7.1`–`7.11` in the library's verse-level subdivision). A modern prose translation by the International Gita Society is held in the library as a parallel translation. About twenty minutes' reading.

- **structural-parallel** → Dhammapada, Chapter 1 (The Twin Verses) — paired chapters that ground the visible world in the seer's orientation; the Twin Verses with mental disposition preceding action and outcome, Chapter 7 with Krishna seen or unseen depending on whether the seer has been freed from the veiling of the gunas.
