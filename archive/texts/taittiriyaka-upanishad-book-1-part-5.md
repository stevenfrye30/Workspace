# Taittiriyaka Upanishad, Book 1, Part 5

Book 1, Part 5 of the Taittiriyaka Upanishad — the Fifth Anuvaka of the Sikshavalli — returns from the prayer of the Fourth Anuvaka to the form of paired correspondence the Third Anuvaka had given, now extended across a fourfold rather than a fivefold scheme. The opening line names the three sacred interjections — the *vyāhṛti* — *Bhū*, *Bhuvaḥ*, *Suvaḥ*, and adds a fourth, *Mahas*, attributed by Müller to the teacher Mahakamasya and identified with Brahman, with the Self. The Anuvaka then maps the four interjections, in turn, onto four parallel fields. Among the worlds: *Bhū* is this world, *Bhuvaḥ* is the sky, *Suvaḥ* is the other world; *Mahas* is the sun, and all the worlds are increased by the sun. Among the heavenly elements: *Bhū* is Agni (fire), *Bhuvaḥ* is Vayu (air), *Suvaḥ* is Aditya (sun); *Mahas* is the moon, and all the heavenly lights are increased by the moon. Among the Vedas: *Bhū* is the *Ṛk*-verses, *Bhuvaḥ* is the *Sāman*-verses, *Suvaḥ* is the *Yajus*-verses; *Mahas* is Brahman, and all the Vedas are increased by Brahman. Among the breathings: *Bhū* is *prāṇa*, *Bhuvaḥ* is *apāna*, *Suvaḥ* is *vyāna*; *Mahas* is food, and all the breathings are increased by food. The Anuvaka closes with the recapitulation: thus there are these four times four, the sixteen sacred interjections; he who knows these knows Brahman, and all the *devas* bring offerings to him.

## Context

The Taittiriyaka Upanishad is one of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads — a small group of texts traditionally received as the philosophical core of the late Vedic literature. It belongs to the Krishna Yajurveda branch of the Vedic corpus and to the Taittiriya school of that branch; the Upanishad sits inside the Taittiriya Aranyaka. Modern scholarship places it among the older prose Upanishads, in roughly the sixth to fifth century BCE — earlier than the verse Upanishads of the Katha and Mundaka. The text is anonymous; the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship.

Book 1, Part 5 sits inside the Sikshavalli, the chapter on *śikṣā*. The First Anuvaka set the protective frame; the Second Anuvaka named the topic; the Third Anuvaka taught the *samhita* under five paired correspondences; the Fourth Anuvaka turned the same school-form into a teacher's prayer; the Fifth Anuvaka now returns to the correspondence-form and extends it across a fourfold scheme — the *vyāhṛti*, the three sacred interjections of the Vedic recitation expanded to four by the addition of *Mahas*. The Anuvaka stands inside the Sikshavalli's wider procedure: a technical term from the recitation tradition is taken up, its scope is widened, and its applicability is shown across cosmos, light, scripture, and breath. The same procedure that the Third Anuvaka used for the *samhita* is here used for the *vyāhṛti*; the two anuvakas develop the same school-form through different ritual terms.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the Anuvaka is built on the same template repeated across four fields. Each field receives the same four-part articulation — *Bhū*, *Bhuvaḥ*, *Suvaḥ*, *Mahas* — mapped to its own set of named correspondents, and the cumulative effect is the recognition the closing line states: there are these four times four, sixteen in all. Reading the Anuvaka with attention to the recurring template, and to the slight rotation of vocabulary that gives each field its own form (worlds and sun, heavenly elements and moon, Vedas and Brahman, breathings and food), recovers the form the lesson has and the place patterned repetition holds in the late-Vedic school-procedure.

Second, the Anuvaka holds its closing recognition as a consequence of the patterning rather than as an additional doctrine. The line that the one who knows these knows Brahman is not given as a separate teaching — it is given as what follows when the four-by-four scheme has been gathered. The Anuvaka does not argue that Brahman is the source of the four fields; it lays the four fields beside one another, each in the same shape, and lets the closing identification *Mahas is Brahman* — placed across each field — stand. Reading the Anuvaka as a structure rather than as a sequence of statements, allowing the patterning to produce the recognition rather than the recognition to organize the patterning, keeps the form the school-lesson has.

## The text

The text is held in the Archive library under `upanishads-muller-part2`, chapter 5 (Müller translation, 1884 — *The Sacred Books of the East*, Volume XV; via SacredTexts.com). Library chapter 5 is shared across fourteen Archive entities — four parts of the Katha Upanishad, the six parts of the Mundaka Upanishad, and Books 1, Parts 1 through 4 of the Taittiriyaka — and continues here into the Fifth Anuvaka without break in the chapter. Book 1, Part 5 of the Taittiriyaka — the entity here — occupies passages `5.188` through `5.196`. Passage `5.188` is the "Fifth Anuvaka" header. Passages `5.189` through `5.196` carry the body of the Anuvaka in Müller's prose presentation, with the verse-numbered markers visible at `1.` (start of `5.189`), `2.` (start of `5.191`), and `3.` (start of `5.193`). Passages `5.194` and `5.196` open with editorial sub-markers `(1-2)` carried over from the source — Müller's bracketed grouping that signals the closing fourfold mapping (the breathings and food) and the closing recognition ("knows the Brahman; all devas bring offerings to him") as continuations of the numbered verses rather than as new verses of their own. Passage `5.197` is the "Sixth Anuvaka" header that opens Book 1, Part 6 of the Taittiriyaka and is not part of this entity. The library chapter continues past this entity into the rest of the Taittiriyaka and the remaining Upanishads of the volume. About four minutes' reading.

## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Taittiriyaka Upanishad, Book 1, Part 4 — paired immediately consecutive Anuvakas of the Sikshavalli; the Fourth Anuvaka turned the school-form into the teacher's *medha-sūkta* prayer for retentive memory and for students arriving from all sides, the Fifth Anuvaka returns from the petitionary form to the correspondence-form the Third Anuvaka had opened, now extended across the fourfold *vyāhṛti* and four parallel fields — the prayer that staffs the school and the lesson the school recites, given in two adjacent units.
- **structural-parallel** → Analects, Book 1 (Xue Er) — paired short pedagogical passages that teach by ordered patterning rather than by argument; the Analects gathers brief verses on learning, friendship, and rectitude in a sequence whose form does the work — each saying held alongside the others without subordination — the Taittiriyaka's Fifth Anuvaka teaches the *vyāhṛti* by the same procedure: four fields placed beside one another, each given the same shape, the recognition produced by the patterning. Both texts trust the order of the lesson to carry the lesson's weight.
- **structural-parallel** → Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 4 (Jnana Karma Sanyasa Yoga) — paired Sanskrit passages on teaching as disciplined transmission; the Gita's Chapter 4 holds the line that the imperishable yoga is told in succession from teacher to disciple and develops a long catalogue of *yajñas* — material, breath, sense-restraint, study, austerity, knowledge — by which the recipient is prepared, the Taittiriyaka's Fifth Anuvaka holds a related form in the patterned recitation of *vyāhṛti* across cosmos, light, scripture, and breath — both texts placing memorized order at the centre of how the teaching is carried.
