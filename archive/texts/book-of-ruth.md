# Book of Ruth

The Book of Ruth is a short prose narrative in the Hebrew Bible — four chapters, eighty-five verses — set in the time of the judges, before the monarchy. Naomi, an Israelite from Bethlehem, has gone to Moab during a famine with her husband and two sons. All three men die. Naomi returns home in widowhood, accompanied by her Moabite daughter-in-law Ruth, who refuses to leave her with the line *whither thou goest, I will go*. In Bethlehem, the kinsman Boaz takes on the legal obligation of redemption, marries Ruth, and restores the household; their son Obed is the grandfather of King David.

## Context

The Book of Ruth is one of the shortest books of the Hebrew Bible. In the Hebrew canonical order it stands among the *Ketuvim* (Writings), grouped with the four other short scrolls (the *Megillot*) read aloud at major Jewish festivals; Ruth is read at Shavuot. The Christian Old Testament places it differently, between Judges and 1 Samuel, as a transitional narrative leading into the rise of the monarchy.

Modern scholarship generally places the composition of the book in the post-exilic period, the fifth or fourth century BCE, though the narrative itself is set in a much earlier time — "in the days when the judges ruled," before the monarchy. The text is anonymous. Its language is generally clear classical Hebrew prose, with some passages drawing on the legal vocabulary of kinship and inheritance on which the plot turns. The closing genealogy (4:18–22), which traces a ten-generation line ending in David, is widely held to be a separate addition to a slightly earlier narrative core.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the central word of the book is the Hebrew *chesed* — sometimes rendered "loyalty," "faithfulness," or "lovingkindness," but with no precise English equivalent. The narrator uses *chesed* to describe Ruth's commitment to Naomi (1:8, 3:10) and Boaz's response to Ruth (2:20), and the book turns on the recognition that loyalty given without obligation can become the channel through which a household is restored. Reading Ruth with attention to where *chesed* appears, and to what each instance does in the plot, tracks the book's own moral structure.

Second, the book is built around legal procedures. The kinsman-redemption (*go'el*) and the night-scene at the threshing floor (chapter 3) are not abstract themes but specific Israelite legal practices. The dialogue at the city gate (chapter 4), where Boaz formally takes on the redemption in front of witnesses, is a courtroom scene, not a romantic one. Reading the chapters with the legal procedures in view, rather than as a love story alone, keeps the book in proportion.

## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Book of Exodus, Chapters 1–3 — both Hebrew Bible narratives of migration and return shaped by named female actors; Exodus opens with the women who preserve Moses across his early life, Ruth with Naomi and her daughter-in-law returning to Bethlehem in widowhood.
- **structural-parallel** → Ramayana, Ayodhya Kanda, Sections 1–10 — both are domestic narratives turning on exile and kinship; the Ramayana opens with a prince sent into exile by a queen's demand, Ruth opens with a family returning from exile in widowhood and finding restoration through legal kinship.
