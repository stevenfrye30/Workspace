# Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 4 (Jnana Yoga)

Chapter 4 of the Bhagavad Gita — the *Jnana Yoga*, conventionally translated "The Yoga of Knowledge" — continues Krishna's teaching on action and turns to the lineage and authority of what he is teaching. The chapter opens with Krishna saying that he taught this yoga long ago to Vivasvat, the sun, and that it descended through a long line of royal sages before being lost; he is restoring it now to Arjuna, his friend and devotee. Arjuna's question — how Krishna could have taught a figure born before him — gives Krishna the occasion to speak of his repeated divine births in every age in which righteousness declines. The middle of the chapter takes up the relation between action and inaction, the practice of *yajna* (sacrifice) in many forms, and the wise who see action in inaction; the closing verses make knowledge the supreme purifier and end with Krishna instructing Arjuna to cut his doubt with the sword of knowledge and stand.

## Context

The Bhagavad Gita is a 700-verse philosophical poem in eighteen chapters, set inside Book VI (the *Bhishma Parva*) of the Mahabharata. Most scholars place its present form between roughly the second century BCE and the second century CE — a layered composition that took shape during the long growth of the epic from an older bardic core. The traditional attribution is to Vyasa, the legendary compiler of the Mahabharata; the historical authorship is unknown.

Chapter 4 continues the argument of Chapters 2 and 3 but expands its frame. Where Chapters 2 and 3 stayed close to the present situation — Arjuna on the battlefield, the question of whether to act — Chapter 4 introduces three new elements that recur through the rest of the Gita: the long lineage of teachers through which the teaching has been transmitted (4.1–3); the doctrine of Krishna's repeated divine births in every age in which righteousness declines (4.5–9), in which the most-quoted verse of the chapter says that whenever righteousness fails and unrighteousness rises, he comes forth; and the practice of *yajna* — sacrifice in many forms, material, breath-controlled, austerity-based, and knowledge-grounded — as the shape that action takes when it does not bind (4.10–32). The chapter closes by elevating knowledge above other forms of sacrifice and instructing Arjuna to cut his doubt and act.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the chapter alternates between three modes of speech without warning. Krishna gives doctrinal teaching (the nature of action, of sacrifice, of knowledge); he supplies narrative framing (the long lineage through which the teaching descended, his own repeated births); and he gives direct practical instruction (cut your doubt; stand). Reading Chapter 4 with the awareness that the chapter moves between these three modes, rather than as a single doctrinal exposition, recovers the form the chapter has.

Second, the chapter revisits earlier themes from a new angle. Action without attachment, the steady-minded sage, the impossibility of inaction — all introduced in Chapters 2 and 3 — return in Chapter 4 with expanded justification: the action that does not bind is action performed as sacrifice; the wise who see rightly are those who see action in inaction and inaction in action; what burns up the residue of past action is the fire of knowledge. Reading the chapter as the same argument expanded by a new vocabulary, rather than as a separate teaching, follows the way Krishna proceeds across the early Gita.

## The text

The text is held in the Archive library under `bhagavad-gita`, chapter 4 (Edwin Arnold's verse translation, *The Song Celestial*, 1885 — Project Gutenberg edition). Arnold's verse translation compresses the chapter's forty-two Sanskrit shlokas into five English verse-paragraphs followed by a closing colophon line (passages `4.1`–`4.6` in the library's verse-level subdivision). A modern prose translation by the International Gita Society is held in the library as a parallel translation. About twenty-five minutes' reading.

- **structural-parallel** → Analects, Book 2 — paired short discourses in which a teacher's authority is bound up with the line of transmission; the *Analects* preserves Confucius' sayings through the disciples and their disciples across two centuries, Chapter 4 of the Gita opens with Krishna's account of the lineage of royal sages through which his teaching descended and was lost before he restores it to Arjuna.
- **structural-parallel** → Plato, Apology — paired short texts in which a teacher speaks of the long history and seriousness of his own teaching practice; the *Apology* with Socrates explaining the Delphic oracle that started his questioning and the years he has carried it under public discomfort, Chapter 4 with Krishna explaining the long descent of his teaching through the ages and the births in which he comes forth to restore it.
