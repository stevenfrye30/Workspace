# Iliad, Book 1

Book 1 of the Iliad opens one of the two surviving Homeric epics. Its first word in the Greek — *mēnis*, "wrath" — names the subject of the entire poem: the wrath of Achilles, his quarrel with Agamemnon, leader of the Greek expedition, and the consequences this anger will have for both armies before Troy. Book 1 sets that dispute through plague, contested honor, and the withdrawal of Achilles from battle. It is the opening engine of the epic, which will run twenty-four books from this single quarrel.

## Context

The Iliad is one of the two surviving Homeric epics, alongside the Odyssey. It is composed in dactylic hexameter in Homeric Greek and runs to twenty-four books and roughly fifteen thousand seven hundred lines. Modern scholarship places its present text in the late eighth or seventh century BCE, drawing on a long oral tradition behind it; the traditional attribution to a single poet named Homer dates from antiquity itself and is debated. The poems were written down at some point in the early archaic period and stabilized in their received form by the Hellenistic editors of Alexandria.

Book 1 covers the opening dispute and its immediate setup. The action takes place over a few days in the tenth year of the Greek siege of Troy. The plague at the camp, the public quarrel between Agamemnon and Achilles in the assembly, the priest Chryses' return with his daughter, the embassy that takes Briseis from Achilles' tent, Thetis's plea to Zeus on Olympus, and the closing scene of quarrel and reconciliation among the gods are all told in roughly six hundred lines. The book contains the famous invocation to the Muse and establishes the formal pattern — assembly, prayer, sacrifice, divine intervention — that the rest of the poem will repeat in different settings.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the Iliad's first word is *mēnis* — "wrath," or "anger" of a particular kind, distinct in Greek from ordinary anger. The whole poem is named by this word in the opening line. Reading Book 1 not as the opening of a war narrative but as the start of one specific anger — the wrath of Achilles, named as the subject in the first word — keeps the book in proportion with the rest of the poem.

Second, Book 1 alternates between the human and divine planes. The plague is sent by Apollo; the quarrel is between Greek leaders; the resolution moves to Olympus, where Thetis appeals to Zeus and the gods debate among themselves. Reading the book with both planes in view, and noticing where the narrative shifts from one to the other, tracks the structure the poem will use throughout. The action is never only on the field.

## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Odyssey, Book 11 — the Homeric internal pair on the shelf; the Iliad's opening sets the war and the wrath, the Odyssey's Book 11 turns aside from the war's aftermath into the realm of the dead.
- **structural-parallel** → Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 2 — both are foundational openings of major epics, each turning on a hero's response to a battlefield crisis; Achilles withdraws in anger from a war already underway, Arjuna refuses to fight a war about to begin.
- **structural-parallel** → Meditations, Book 2 — both place a man under the pressure of conflict, in opposite registers; Achilles in the Greek camp speaking in public assembly, Marcus on the Danube frontier speaking to himself in a journal.
