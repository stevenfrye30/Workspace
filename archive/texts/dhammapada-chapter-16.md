# Dhammapada, Chapter 16 (Pleasure)

Chapter 16 of the Dhammapada — the *Piya-vagga*, "Pleasure" — is a chapter of twelve verses on the figure of the beloved or pleasant thing as the source of grief and fear. The opening verse states the figure: he who gives himself to vanity and not to meditation, grasping at pleasure, will in time envy the one who exerted himself (209). Verse 210 turns the figure to balance: do not look for the pleasant or the unpleasant — not seeing the pleasant is pain, and seeing the unpleasant is also pain. Verse 211 turns it to the conclusion: *let no man love anything; loss of the beloved is evil. Those who love nothing and hate nothing, have no fetters.* The next five verses hold the chapter's central form — a single phrase repeated five times with one substitution each. *From pleasure comes grief, from pleasure comes fear; he who is free from pleasure knows neither grief nor fear* (212). The same form follows for *affection* (213), *lust* (214), *love* (215), and *greed* (216). Verse 217 turns the figure to its other side: he who possesses virtue and intelligence, who is just, speaks the truth, and does what is his own business — the world will hold him dear. Verse 218 turns it to the upward stream: he in whom a desire for the Ineffable has sprung up, who is satisfied in his mind, whose thoughts are not bewildered by love, is called *urdhvamsrotas*, carried upwards by the stream. The closing pair turns the figure to homecoming. Verse 219 holds the figure of kinsmen, friends, and lovers who salute a man returning from afar. Verse 220 holds the figure that the same is true of one's good works in the next world — *his good works receive him who has done good, and has gone from this world to the other;--as kinsmen receive a friend on his return.*

## Context

The Dhammapada is one of the books of the Khuddaka Nikaya, the fifth and shortest section of the Sutta Pitaka in the Pali Canon. It collects 423 verses arranged in twenty-six chapters by theme — pairs, heedfulness, mind, flowers, the sage, evil, and so on. The verses are attributed to the Buddha and were transmitted orally for several centuries before being committed to writing in Sri Lanka in the first century BCE. The collection took shape in roughly the third century BCE, in connection with the Theravada transmission of the canon.

Chapter 16 is the *Piya-vagga*, "Pleasure" — the Pali *piya* meaning "dear, beloved, pleasant, agreeable," with the chapter turning on the consequences of holding things or persons under that name. Its twelve verses are numbered 209 through 220 in the standard continuous numbering of the Dhammapada — and in F. Max Müller's 1881 translation, the version held in the Archive library, the count and numbering match the standard exactly, with no merged or missing verse of the kind that occurs at the close or middle of Chapters 4, 6, 8, 11, and 14. Müller renders *piya* variously through the chapter — "pleasure," "beloved," "affection," "love" — so that the central five-fold sequence (212–216) holds five English nouns where the underlying Pali nouns are also variously distributed across attachment vocabulary. Each verse is short — two or four lines in the Pali — and each completes one statement of the figure.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the chapter's central figure is the five-fold parallel sequence at verses 212–216. The form is: *from X comes grief, from X comes fear; he who is free from X knows neither grief nor fear.* The five Xs are pleasure, affection, lust, love, and greed. The sequence does not distinguish among them as different things; it holds them together as the same thing under five names. Reading the five verses as a single figure repeated, rather than as five separate aphorisms about different attachments, recovers the form the chapter is built on. The chapter holds the doctrine: every form of holding-as-dear binds the holder to grief and fear, and freedom from holding is freedom from grief and fear.

Second, the chapter does not stop at the negation. Verse 217 turns to the man whom the world holds dear — virtuous, intelligent, just, truthful, attentive to his own business. Verse 218 turns to the man in whom a desire for the Ineffable (Nirvana) has sprung up, who is *urdhvamsrotas*, carried upwards by the stream — Müller's preserved Sanskrit term keeps the figure intact. Verses 219 and 220 close the chapter on the figure of return: the man who comes home from afar is welcomed by kinsmen, and the man who has done good is similarly welcomed by his good works in the next world. The closing figure is one of arrival and reception, not negation. Reading the chapter with this turn in view, rather than as a sustained doctrine of detachment alone, keeps the form the chapter has.

## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Dhammapada, Chapter 15 (Happiness) — the immediate predecessor on the shelf, with the same kind of patterned-quartet structure but inverted in direction; Chapter 15 with an opening quartet on living happily free of hatred, ailment, greed, and possession (verses 197–200), Chapter 16 with a central five-fold sequence on the grief and fear that come from pleasure, affection, lust, love, and greed (verses 212–216). The two chapters share the figure of the same form repeated across substitutions, with Chapter 15 holding the freed state and Chapter 16 holding the bound state.
- **structural-parallel** → Dhammapada, Chapter 9 (Evil) — the earlier chapter with the same kind of paired-aphorism logic for moral consequence; Chapter 9 verses 121–122 on the gradual filling of the pot with evil or with good and verses 127–128 on the no-place-to-hide refrain shared between escape from evil and escape from death, Chapter 16 verses 219–220 on kinsmen welcoming the home-comer paired with good works welcoming the doer in the next world. Both chapters work the figure of consequence as both immediate and continuing.
- **structural-parallel** → Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 2 — a Sanskrit philosophical pair on the sources of grief; the Gita's second chapter on attachment, fear, and grief as the conditions Arjuna must overcome, Dhammapada 16 verses 212–216 on the same emotional chain — pleasure, affection, lust, love, greed — as the sources of grief and fear. Both texts hold the figure that emotional binding precedes suffering.
- **structural-parallel** → Epictetus, Enchiridion, Chapters 1–5 — the Stoic shelf-pair on freedom from emotional disturbance; the Enchiridion's opening practices on accepting what is not in one's power and remaining undisturbed, Dhammapada 16 verse 211 on loving nothing and hating nothing as the absence of fetters. Both texts hold the figure that the absence of attachment is itself a positive state.
