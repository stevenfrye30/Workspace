# Taittiriyaka Upanishad, Book 3, Part 3

Book 3, Part 3 of the Taittiriyaka Upanishad — the Third Anuvaka of the Bhriguvalli — is the second iteration of the dialogic engine the Second Anuvaka set out. The form is identical; the substance moves one step further inward. The sentence the Second Anuvaka left open with the em-dash *Having performed penance—* is completed here in the same three short clauses as before, with one substitution: *prāṇo brahmeti vyajānāt* — *he perceived that breath is Brahman.* The reasoning clause repeats the form Varuna had given as the Brahman-definition in the First Anuvaka, with breath in the place of *that*: *for from breath these beings are born; by breath, when born, they live; into breath they enter at their death.* Bhrigu returns to his father with the same opening line he gave at the threshold of the Valli and at the close of the Second Anuvaka — *adhīhi bhagavo brahmeti* — *Sir, teach me Brahman.* Varuna's reply is identical to the reply he gave before: *try to know Brahman by penance, for penance is the means of knowing Brahman.* The Anuvaka closes on the same line and the same em-dash that closed the First and the Second — *He performed penance. Having performed penance—* — and the dialogue carries directly across the Anuvaka boundary into the Fourth, where the next recognition will be reached.

## Context

The Taittiriyaka Upanishad is one of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads — a small group of texts traditionally received as the philosophical core of the late Vedic literature. It belongs to the Krishna Yajurveda branch of the Vedic corpus and to the Taittiriya school of that branch; the Upanishad sits inside the Taittiriya Aranyaka. Modern scholarship places it among the older prose Upanishads, in roughly the sixth to fifth century BCE — earlier than the verse Upanishads of the Katha and Mundaka. The text is anonymous; the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship.

Book 3, Part 3 is the third of the ten anuvakas of the Bhriguvalli. The dialogic engine is now visible as a steady iteration. The First Anuvaka set the request, the avenues, the Brahman-definition, and the means; the Second Anuvaka completed the sentence with food as the recognition; the Third Anuvaka now completes the same sentence with breath as the recognition. The recognitions Bhrigu reaches will continue to traverse the same five terms the Brahmanandavalli unfolded as recursive sheaths — food, breath, mind, understanding, bliss — but the Bhriguvalli holds them in dialogic form, with each Anuvaka making the same substitution inside the same sentence-template. The two Vallis hold the same five recognitions through two different procedures: the Brahmanandavalli works recursively in prose-and-sloka, the Bhriguvalli works iteratively in question-and-penance.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the Anuvaka is repetition with one substitution, and the repetition is the form of the teaching. The first sentence completes the previous Anuvaka's open sentence in the same three clauses, with one term substituted: where food was named, breath is now named. Reading the Anuvaka with attention to what is held stable across the iterations — the sentence-template, the return to the father, the request, the reply, the closing line — and to what shifts (the named term), recovers the form the Bhriguvalli has when its iteration is at its plainest. The repetition is not redundancy; it is the procedure by which the substitutions become visible.

Second, the Anuvaka is short and almost entirely formulaic, and yet it is not the same as the Anuvaka before. The two iterations differ in only one word, and the work of the Valli is happening in that word. Reading the Anuvaka aloud and noticing where attention catches — at the substitution, at the return-line repeated unchanged, at the em-dash that closes the Anuvaka exactly as the previous one was closed — keeps the form the Bhriguvalli is using and the place where its progress is being made.

## The text

The text is held in the Archive library under `upanishads-muller-part2`, chapter 5 (Müller translation, 1884 — *The Sacred Books of the East*, Volume XV; via SacredTexts.com). Library chapter 5 is shared across thirty-three Archive entities — four parts of the Katha Upanishad, the six parts of the Mundaka Upanishad, the twelve anuvakas of the Sikshavalli, the nine anuvakas of the Brahmanandavalli, and the First and Second Anuvakas of the Bhriguvalli — and continues here into the Third Anuvaka without break in the chapter. Book 3, Part 3 of the Taittiriyaka — the entity here — occupies passages `5.341` through `5.344`. Passage `5.341` is the "Third Anuvaka" header. Passage `5.342` carries Bhrigu's second recognition — *He perceived that breath is Brahman* — completing the sentence that ended with the em-dash at the close of the Second Anuvaka, and carrying footnote reference `[*1]` on the named term *breath*. Passage `5.343` carries Bhrigu's return to Varuna, the renewed request *Sir, teach me Brahman*, and Varuna's reply on penance — the prose is identical in vocabulary to the corresponding passage in the Second Anuvaka. Passage `5.344` carries the closing line *He performed penance. Having performed penance—*, ending again on the same em-dash that closed the First and Second Anuvakas. The em-dash is structural and identical across the iterations, repeated identically through the next several Anuvakas. Passage `5.345` is the "Fourth Anuvaka" header that opens Book 3, Part 4 of the Taittiriyaka and is not part of this entity. The library chapter continues past this entity into the rest of the Taittiriyaka and the remaining Upanishads of the volume. About one minute's reading.

Two registers of the same recognition — recursive descent and progressive dialogue — in the same Upanishad's two vallis.
- **structural-parallel** → Mundaka Upanishad, Book 2, Part 1 — paired Sanskrit passages on breath as the first emergence from the source; the Mundaka's First Khanda of the Second Mundaka holds the cascade *from him is born breath, mind, and all organs of sense, ether, air, light, water, and the earth* (Mundaka 2.1.3), placing breath among the first elements that come forth from the Imperishable, the Bhriguvalli's Third Anuvaka places breath in the same proximity to the source through the Brahman-definition — that from which beings are born, by which they live, into which they enter at death. Paired moments in the late-Vedic literature where breath is named at the boundary between the source and what comes from it.
