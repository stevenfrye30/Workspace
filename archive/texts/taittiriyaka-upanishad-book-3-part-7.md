# Taittiriyaka Upanishad, Book 3, Part 7

Book 3, Part 7 of the Taittiriyaka Upanishad — the Seventh Anuvaka of the Bhriguvalli — is the moment at which the Valli leaves the recognition-cycle behind. The Sixth Anuvaka closed the cycle with Bhrigu's last recognition — that bliss is Brahman — and named the closing as *the knowledge of Bhrigu and Varuna, exalted in the highest heaven*; the Seventh Anuvaka opens a new movement that does not return to the dialogic frame. Bhrigu and Varuna are not named here. There is no em-dash carrying a sentence into this Anuvaka, and no em-dash carries a sentence out of it. The Anuvaka is short and self-contained, and three more anuvakas in the same form will follow before the Bhriguvalli moves on. The first sentence is a single rule: *annaṁ na nindyāt, tad vratam* — *let him never abuse food, that is the rule.* The reasoning that follows places food at the joint between breath and body: *breath is food, the body eats the food; the body rests on breath, breath rests on the body; this is the food resting on food.* The closing formula is the one that sealed the Sixth Anuvaka and that will return at the close of the Eighth and the Ninth: *he who knows this food resting on food, rests exalted, becomes rich in food, and able to eat food (healthy), becomes great by offspring, cattle, and the splendour of his knowledge of Brahman, great by fame.*

## Context

The Taittiriyaka Upanishad is one of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads — a small group of texts traditionally received as the philosophical core of the late Vedic literature. It belongs to the Krishna Yajurveda branch of the Vedic corpus and to the Taittiriya school of that branch; the Upanishad sits inside the Taittiriya Aranyaka. Modern scholarship places it among the older prose Upanishads, in roughly the sixth to fifth century BCE — earlier than the verse Upanishads of the Katha and Mundaka. The text is anonymous; the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship.

Book 3, Part 7 is the seventh of the ten anuvakas of the Bhriguvalli and the first of three short food-rule anuvakas that follow the close of the recognition-cycle. The Seventh, Eighth, and Ninth Anuvakas share the same shape: a short rule about food (never abuse food, never shun food, acquire much food), a reasoning sentence that places food at a joint between two paired terms (breath and body, water and light, earth and ether), and the same closing reward formula. The form is brief, paratactic, and self-contained; the dialogic engine of the recognition-cycle is set aside. The food-theme of the closing reward formula — *rich in food, able to eat food* — has been kept by the Anuvaka itself: the Valli, having traversed the five recognitions and returned to bliss, now stays for three short anuvakas with the level at which the recognitions began, and gives the rules under which food is to be held.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the Anuvaka has changed mode. No em-dash carries the sentence in or out; no question, no penance, no return. The recognition-cycle's iteration has been replaced by a different iteration — three short anuvakas, each with the shape *rule, reasoning, formula*. Reading the Anuvaka with attention to what has fallen away from the previous form (the dialogic frame, the Brahman-definition template, the substitution) and what has arrived in its place (the rule, the paired-term reasoning, the reward formula) recovers the form the Bhriguvalli has at this turn.

Second, the closing formula is the link between the recognition-cycle and the food-rules. The Sixth Anuvaka closed the cycle with this formula: *he who knows this becomes exalted, becomes rich in food, and able to eat food (healthy), becomes great by offspring, cattle, and the splendour of his knowledge of Brahman, great by fame.* The Seventh Anuvaka closes its food-rule with nearly the same formula. Reading the formula as the bridge — what the Valli wants the disciple to carry from the recognition-cycle into the next series of short rules — keeps the Seventh Anuvaka's continuity with the Sixth visible while letting its different form be seen.

## The text

The text is held in the Archive library under `upanishads-muller-part2`, chapter 5 (Müller translation, 1884 — *The Sacred Books of the East*, Volume XV; via SacredTexts.com). Library chapter 5 is shared across thirty-seven Archive entities — four parts of the Katha Upanishad, the six parts of the Mundaka Upanishad, the twelve anuvakas of the Sikshavalli, the nine anuvakas of the Brahmanandavalli, and the First through Sixth Anuvakas of the Bhriguvalli — and continues here into the Seventh Anuvaka without break in the chapter. Book 3, Part 7 of the Taittiriyaka — the entity here — occupies passages `5.356` through `5.358`. Passage `5.356` is the "Seventh Anuvaka" header. Passage `5.357` carries the short opening rule *Let him never abuse food, that is the rule* in a single sentence. Passage `5.358` carries the reasoning sentence on food, breath, and body, followed by the closing reward formula — both held in a single prose passage in Müller's source. There are no internal verse-numbered markers in this Anuvaka; the recognition-cycle's em-dash carryover does not appear here, and no em-dash will appear in any of the remaining anuvakas of the Bhriguvalli — the structural feature is specific to the recognition-cycle anuvakas (Second through Sixth) and does not extend past it. Passage `5.359` is the "Eighth Anuvaka" header that opens Book 3, Part 8 of the Taittiriyaka and is not part of this entity. The library chapter continues past this entity into the rest of the Taittiriyaka and the remaining Upanishads of the volume. About one minute's reading.

## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Taittiriyaka Upanishad, Book 3, Part 6 — paired immediately consecutive Anuvakas of the Bhriguvalli at the boundary between two of its forms; the Sixth Anuvaka closed the recognition-cycle with Bhrigu's last recognition that bliss is Brahman and sealed the cycle with the closing formula on food, offspring, cattle, and fame, the Seventh Anuvaka opens a new short series — three food-rule anuvakas — that uses the same closing formula but no longer holds the dialogic frame. The Sixth ends one engine; the Seventh starts another.
- **structural-parallel** → Taittiriyaka Upanishad, Book 2, Part 7 — paired moments inside the same Upanishad on food, flavour, and the source of bliss; the Brahmanandavalli's Seventh Anuvaka holds the line *raso vai saḥ* — *he is flavour itself* — and the rhetorical question *who could breathe, who could breathe forth, if that bliss did not exist in the ether of the heart?* — placing the recognition of Brahman as the source of all flavour and breath in the heart, the Bhriguvalli's Seventh Anuvaka holds a more practical form of the same continuity: food and breath, body and breath, food resting on food. Both texts hold breath, food, and bliss in close relation, the one as recognition and the other as rule.
- **structural-parallel** → Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 15 (Purushottama Yoga) — paired Sanskrit passages on food, breath, and the body; the Gita's Chapter 15 names Krishna as *vaiśvānara* — the digestive fire seated in the body of living beings, joined with the up-breath (*prāṇa*) and the down-breath (*apāna*), who consumes the four kinds of food (Gita 15.14) — the Bhriguvalli's Seventh Anuvaka holds a related figure in shorter form: *breath is food, the body eats the food; the body rests on breath, breath rests on the body.* Both texts place food, breath, and body in mutual support, and both place the recognition of that mutual support at the centre of the practical knowing.
