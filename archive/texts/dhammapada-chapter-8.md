# Dhammapada, Chapter 8 (The Thousands)

Chapter 8 of the Dhammapada — the *Sahassa-vagga*, "The Thousands" — is a chapter built on a single rhetorical figure: the numerical contrast. The form is "even though X be a thousand … one Y is better." The opening triplet states it through speech: a thousand senseless words against one word of sense (100); a thousand-word Gatha of senseless words against one word of a Gatha (101); a hundred Gathas made of senseless words against one word of the law (102). The next pair turns the form to conquest: a man who conquers a thousand times a thousand men in battle is less than the man who conquers himself, whose victory neither god nor Gandharva nor Mara with Brahman can undo (103–104). A second triplet turns it to ritual: a hundred years of monthly thousand-sacrifices, a hundred years of forest fire-worship, and a year of offerings together are all less than a single moment's homage to one whose soul is grounded in true knowledge (106–108). A short verse turns it to the elders: greeting and revering the aged increases life, beauty, happiness, and power (109). The closing six-fold sequence holds the form steady: a hundred years vicious and unrestrained against one day virtuous (110); a hundred years ignorant against one day wise (111); a hundred years idle against one day strong (112); and three further pairs against seeing beginning-and-end (113), the immortal place (114), and the highest law (115).

## Context

The Dhammapada is one of the books of the Khuddaka Nikaya, the fifth and shortest section of the Sutta Pitaka in the Pali Canon. It collects 423 verses arranged in twenty-six chapters by theme — pairs, heedfulness, mind, flowers, the sage, evil, and so on. The verses are attributed to the Buddha and were transmitted orally for several centuries before being committed to writing in Sri Lanka in the first century BCE. The collection took shape in roughly the third century BCE, in connection with the Theravada transmission of the canon.

Chapter 8 is the *Sahassa-vagga*, "The Thousands." Its verses are numbered 100 through 115 in F. Max Müller's 1881 translation, the version held in the Archive library — fifteen passages, where the standard continuous Pali numbering of the Dhammapada gives sixteen verses (100–115). Müller has combined what is conventionally verses 104 and 105 into a single English period at passage 8.104: the *self-conquered-better-than-all* statement and the *not-god-not-Gandharva-not-Mara-with-Brahman* statement are run together as one sentence, with no passage numbered 8.105 in the source. Per Atlas-rule **OCR/transcription honesty**, the merged passage is preserved as Müller wrote it; the chapter therefore has fifteen passages here against the standard sixteen verses elsewhere — the third instance of the same kind of merging in Müller's translation, after Chapter 4 (verses 58–59) and Chapter 6 (verses 87–88). The next chapter opens at passage 9.116. Each verse is short — two or four lines in the Pali — and each completes one statement of the contrast.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the chapter is built on a single rhetorical figure — the numerical contrast — that returns through every verse. The pattern is consistent: a quantity of the lesser thing is set against a single instance of the better thing, and the better is found to outweigh. Reading the chapter as one figure repeated through different domains, rather than as fifteen separate aphorisms, recovers the form the chapter is built on. The domains are: speech (100–102), conquest (103–104), sacrifice (106–108), reverence to elders (109), and then a closing six-fold sequence on the value of one day of virtue, wisdom, strength, sight of beginning-and-end, sight of the immortal place, and sight of the highest law (110–115). The closing sequence is the most tightly composed: six verses, each opening "And he who lives a hundred years … a life of one day is better if …", each turning the contrast on a different attribute.

Second, Müller's merging of conventional verses 104 and 105 into a single passage at 8.104 is a translator's choice, not an absence of the second statement. The self-conquered-better-than-all statement and the not-undone-by-god-or-Gandharva-or-Mara-with-Brahman statement are both there, joined into one English sentence. Reading the passage with the awareness that the standard sixteenth verse is preserved inside, rather than missing, keeps the form of the chapter intact even where the numbering departs from the standard.

## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Dhammapada, Chapter 7 (The Venerable) — the immediate predecessor on the shelf; Chapter 7 with ten verses on the *arahant*, the one whose journey is finished, Chapter 8 with fifteen verses on the numerical-contrast figure that values one moment of homage to such a one above a hundred years of sacrifice (verses 106–108) and one day of seeing the highest law above a hundred years of not seeing it (verse 115).
- **structural-parallel** → Dhammapada, Chapter 4 (Flowers) and Chapter 6 (The Wise Man) — the earlier chapters with the same kind of Müller merging-irregularity at their close; Chapter 4 combines verses 58 and 59 at passage 4.58, Chapter 6 combines verses 87 and 88 at passage 6.87, Chapter 8 combines verses 104 and 105 at passage 8.104. The merging is a recurring feature of Müller's 1881 translation, not unique to any one chapter.
- **structural-parallel** → Dhammapada, Chapter 1 (The Twin Verses) — the opening pair on the shelf; Chapter 1 with paired-verse contrasts on mind, action, and outcome, Chapter 8 with extended numerical contrasts. Both chapters work by setting two states or two outcomes side by side and letting the contrast carry the teaching, but Chapter 8 makes the contrast quantitative — a thousand against one, a hundred years against one day.
- **structural-parallel** → Analects, Books 1–2 — the Confucian shelf-pair with comparable rhetorical compression; the Analects' opening books with short contrasts between the *junzi* and the *xiaoren* and brief valuations of one practice over another, Dhammapada 8 with verses 103–104 on self-conquest as greater than conquering thousands and verse 109 on revering elders as the source of the four increases. Both work by terse comparative judgement.
