# Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 2, Part 2

Book 2, Part 2 of the Brihadaranyaka Upanishad — the Second Brahmana of the Second Adhyaya — is a short, dense Brahmana that does not continue the dialogue between Gargya Balaki and Ajatasatru that opened the Adhyaya. Where the First Brahmana ran across twenty numbered verses with named speakers, narrative interruption, and demonstration in the form of a sleeping man, the Second Brahmana opens immediately into a different mode: impersonal symbolic prose, four short numbered verses, and a sustained mapping of the openings of the head onto cosmic figures and named Rishis. The frame, narrators, and dialogic apparatus of Part 1 are not carried forward.

The opening verse holds an unusual figure: *verily he who knows the babe (kumāra) with his place, his chamber, his post, and his rope, he keeps off the seven relatives who hate him.* The figure is then unfolded — the babe is *the inner life*; his place is *this (body)*; his chamber is *this (head)*; his post is *the vital breath*; his rope is *the food*. The seven relatives are not unfolded directly here; the next verse will give the seven openings of the eye as cosmic figures, which the tradition aligns with the seven hostile relations.

The second verse holds the cosmic eye-mapping. *Then the seven imperishable ones approach him.* Each part of the eye is given as the locus of a cosmic figure: *there are the red lines in the eye, and by them Rudra clings to him*; *there is the water in the eye, and by it Parganya clings to him*; *there is the pupil, and by it Aditya the sun clings to him*; *there is the dark iris, and by it Agni clings to him*; *there is the white eye-ball, and by it Indra clings to him*; *with the lower eye-lash the earth, with the upper eye-lash the heaven clings to him.* The mapping is given in a single uninterrupted catalogue.

The third verse opens with the framing line *on this there is this Sloka* and gives the sloka in Müller's quotation marks: *there is a cup having its mouth below and its bottom above. Manifold glory has been placed into it. On its lip sit the seven Rishis, the tongue as the eighth communicates with Brahman.* The prose gloss that follows the sloka identifies the cup as the head: its mouth is the (mouth) below, its bottom is the (skull) above; the *manifold glory placed into it* are the senses, intelligences, and breaths.

The fourth and final verse identifies the seven Rishis on the lip of the cup with the seven openings of the head: *these two (the two ears) are the Rishis Gautama and Bharadvaga; the right Gautama, the left Bharadvaga. These two (the eyes) are the Rishis Visvamitra and Gamadagni; the right Visvamitra, the left Gamadagni. These two (the nostrils) are the Rishis Vasishtha and Kasyapa; the right Vasishtha, the left Kasyapa. The tongue is Atri.* The Brahmana ends without a closing line; the next passage in the library opens the Third Brahmana.

## Context

The Brihadaranyaka Upanishad is one of the two earliest of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads, sharing that position with the Chandogya. It belongs to the Shukla Yajurveda branch of the Vedic corpus and forms the closing portion of the Shatapatha Brahmana — the *Aranyaka* in its name marks it as a forest-text recited at the late stages of Vedic study. Modern scholarship places the Brihadaranyaka in roughly the eighth to sixth century BCE, prior to the Buddha; the text is anonymous, and the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship. The text is the longest of the principal Upanishads and is typically structured in six *adhyayas* (chapters), each containing several *brahmanas* (sections).

Book 2, Part 2 is the Second Brahmana of the Second Adhyaya. The First Brahmana opened the Adhyaya with the long Gargya Balaki / Ajatasatru dialogue and closed with the *spider with its thread* and *small sparks from fire* figure for emergence and the formula *the Upanishad of that Self is "the True of the True."* The Second Brahmana does not extend that dialogue. Instead, it places a short symbolic-anatomical mapping of the openings of the head as the locus of cosmic figures and named Rishis. The mapping of the *seven Rishis* onto the seven openings of the head is one of the older Brihadaranyaka identifications and is preserved in close form in the Atharva-veda and the Brahmana literature; the figure of the *cup having its mouth below and its bottom above* for the head returns in later Vedanta commentary. The Brahmana is brief but tightly structured, holding cosmic figures, named Rishis, and bodily anatomy in the same mapping without subordinating one to the other.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the Brahmana is a mode-shift from Part 1, and the shift is structural, not transitional. The dialogue between Gargya Balaki and Ajatasatru does not resume here; no named speakers appear; no narrator interrupts. The Brahmana works in the impersonal symbolic-prose mode that the First Adhyaya used at length and that the Second Adhyaya now drops into between dialogues. Reading the Brahmana with attention to the absence of dialogic apparatus — and to the way the Adhyaya as a whole holds different literary modes in adjacent units rather than fusing them — recovers the form the Brihadaranyaka has when its modes alternate.

Second, the Brahmana's mapping is built on the head as the cosmic locus. The seven openings of the head — two ears, two eyes, two nostrils, tongue — are the same seven, given twice: once as the seven imperishable cosmic figures clinging through the parts of the eye (Rudra, Parganya, Aditya, Agni, Indra, earth, heaven), and once as the seven named Rishis sitting on the lip of the cup (Gautama, Bharadvaga, Visvamitra, Gamadagni, Vasishtha, Kasyapa, Atri). Reading the Brahmana with the two seven-fold mappings held alongside one another, attending to the head as the same locus addressed twice in different vocabularies, keeps the form the Brihadaranyaka has at this compact unit.

## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 2, Part 1 — paired immediately consecutive Brahmanas of the Second Adhyaya that hold opposed literary modes; the First Brahmana ran the long Gargya Balaki / Ajatasatru dialogue across twenty verses with named speakers, narrative interruption, demonstration, and the closing *spider with its thread* figure, the Second Brahmana drops the dialogue entirely and works in impersonal symbolic prose across four short verses on the head as cosmic locus. The Adhyaya's first two units demonstrate the Brihadaranyaka's procedure of holding different literary modes in adjacent Brahmanas without fusing or transitioning between them.
- **structural-parallel** → Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 1, Part 2 — paired Sanskrit cosmic-anatomical mappings within the same Upanishad; the First Adhyaya's Second Brahmana mapped the cosmic body of *prāṇa* — *the head was the Eastern quarter, and the arms this and that quarter; then the tail was the Western quarter, and the two legs this and that quarter; the sides were the Southern and Northern quarters, the back heaven, the belly the sky, the dust the earth* — the Second Adhyaya's Second Brahmana now maps the human head — its eye, its openings — as the locus of the seven imperishable ones and the seven Rishis. Both texts hold the body and the cosmos as the same locus addressed in different vocabularies.
- **structural-parallel** → Mundaka Upanishad, Book 2, Part 1 — paired Sanskrit anatomical-cosmic mappings of the cosmic Person; the Mundaka's First Khanda of the Second Mundaka holds the cosmic Person whose head is the sky, whose eyes are the sun and the moon, whose ears are the quarters, whose speech is the Vedas disclosed, whose breath is the wind, whose heart is the universe (Mundaka 2.1.4) — anatomy mapped outward to the cosmos — the Brihadaranyaka's Second Brahmana of the Second Adhyaya maps in the opposite direction, placing the cosmic figures (Rudra, Parganya, Aditya, Agni, Indra, earth, heaven; and the seven named Rishis) inside the openings of the human head. Paired moments where the late-Vedic literature works the cosmos-and-body identity from each direction.
