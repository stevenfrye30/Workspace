# Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 5, Part 6

Book 5, Part 6 of the Brihadaranyaka Upanishad — the Sixth Brahmana of the Fifth Adhyaya — is one of the Brihadaranyaka's most compact Brahmanas, matching the Third Brahmana of the Adhyaya at two passages and one body passage. The Adhyaya's prior five Brahmanas have worked through five distinct compact procedures — the Purnam mantra and Om-as-ether-as-Brahman triple identification (Brahmana 1), the Pragapati-and-three-classes parable on the syllable Da (Brahmana 2), the *hridaya* etymology on Pragapati-and-heart-as-Brahman (Brahmana 3), the *true Brahman* chained identification with conquest-and-curse (Brahmana 4), and the cosmogony plus *satyam* etymology plus paired body-anatomies of the solar and right-eye persons (Brahmana 5). The Sixth Brahmana works the seventh of these procedures: the *manomaya purusha* (the person under the form of mind), substantively located within the heart and given the *vrīhi-yava* (rice-and-barley) size-simile that the Upanishadic literature works at length.

The teaching of the Brahmana is held in five substantive identifications in one continuous sentence on the single body passage at `5.1202`:

1. **That person, under the form of mind (manas)** — *manomaya* in the underlying Sanskrit — is the substantive subject of the teaching. The form names the purusha as constituted in the mind-substance, with Müller's parenthetical *(manas)* preserving the underlying technical term.
2. **Being light indeed** — the luminous nature of the manomaya purusha. Footnote `[*2]` attaches at this clause; Müller's note glosses the substantive content of the light-attribution.
3. **Within the heart** — the bodily locus. The Adhyaya's Third Brahmana had identified Pragapati with the heart and worked the *hridaya* etymology; the Fourth Brahmana had identified the heart with the true Brahman; the Sixth Brahmana now places the manomaya purusha *within* the heart as the substantive seat of the person.
4. **Small like a grain of rice or barley** — the *vrīhi-yava* size-simile. The simile names the substantive smallness of the purusha through two named grains, and is one of the Upanishadic literature's most-quoted figures of the Self's bodily-interior dimension. The same simile is given in extended form at the close of the Sixth Khanda of the Third Prapathaka of the Chandogya Upanishad with the famous *aṇīyān vrīher vā yavād vā* (smaller than a grain of rice or barley) line.
5. **The ruler of all, the lord of all — he rules all this, whatsoever exists** — universal sovereignty. The substantive close of the sentence holds the doctrine that the same small manomaya purusha within the heart is at once the cosmic ruler of all that exists.

The Brahmana ends inside the closing sovereignty-clause. No closing seal beyond the substantive content, no *held his peace* form, no narrator's voice, no recapitulation. The form is the same compressed-substantive close that Parts 3, 4, and 5 of the Adhyaya have used.

## Context

The Brihadaranyaka Upanishad is one of the two earliest of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads, sharing that position with the Chandogya. It belongs to the Shukla Yajurveda branch of the Vedic corpus and forms the closing portion of the Shatapatha Brahmana — the *Aranyaka* in its name marks it as a forest-text recited at the late stages of Vedic study. Modern scholarship places the Brihadaranyaka in roughly the eighth to sixth century BCE, prior to the Buddha; the text is anonymous, and the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship. The text is the longest of the principal Upanishads and is typically structured in six *adhyayas* (chapters), each containing several *brahmanas* (sections).

Book 5, Part 6 holds three of the Adhyaya's distinctive features in concentrated form. The first is the *manomaya purusha* substantive name. The Upanishadic literature gives the *manomaya* (mind-formed) Self as one of the principal substantive identifications of the inner person — the Taittiriyaka Upanishad's Second Brahmana holds the *manomaya* as the third of the *kosha* (sheath) identifications between the *prāṇamaya* and the *vijñānamaya*. The Sixth Brahmana of the Fifth Adhyaya gives the same substantive identification in compressed form, with no surrounding *kosha*-doctrine and no further sheath-identifications, naming the *manomaya purusha* directly as the substantive subject of the heart-locus teaching.

The second is the *vrīhi-yava* simile. The Upanishadic literature works the small-as-a-grain figure across multiple loci, and the Brihadaranyaka's Sixth Brahmana of the Fifth Adhyaya gives the simile in its compressed substantive form: *small like a grain of rice or barley*. The Chandogya Upanishad at its Sixth Khanda of the Third Prapathaka holds the same simile in extended *aṇīyān vrīher vā yavād vā* form, with the substantive closing on the doctrine that the same purusha is *jyāyān prithivyāḥ* (greater than the earth) — the same paradox of small-and-great that the Sixth Brahmana of the Fifth Adhyaya gives in its closing clause: *small like a grain of rice or barley. He is the ruler of all, the lord of all — he rules all this, whatsoever exists.* The substantive paradox of the small purusha and the universal ruler is held in one continuous sentence in the Brihadaranyaka.

The third is the chained heart-locus across the Adhyaya. The Third, Fourth, and Sixth Brahmanas of the Fifth Adhyaya all work the heart as the substantive seat of their teachings: the Third with *Pragapati is the heart, is this Brahman, is all this* and the *hridaya* etymology; the Fourth with *This (heart) indeed is even that, it was indeed the true (Brahman)* and the conquest-and-curse formula; the Sixth with the *manomaya purusha* within the heart and the *vrīhi-yava* simile. The Adhyaya's heart-locus chain is one of its principal procedural features, with the Fifth Brahmana's right-eye-and-solar-orb pairing as the only substantive deviation from the chain. Reading the three heart-Brahmanas together — the Third's etymology, the Fourth's conquest-and-curse, and the Sixth's *manomaya* substantive identification — recovers the Adhyaya's procedure of compact substantive teachings worked through the same bodily seat across distinct procedures.

The closing form is the Adhyaya's standard compact close. *He rules all this, whatsoever exists.* No closing seal, no narrator's voice, no *held his peace* form. The Brahmana ends inside its substance.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the Brahmana's substantive content should be read as one continuous five-part identification rather than as a sequence of distinct doctrines. The five identifications — *manomaya purusha*, light-nature, heart-locus, *vrīhi-yava* size-simile, and universal sovereignty — sit in a single English sentence in Müller's translation, and their parallelism is preserved in the underlying Sanskrit's compound-form. Reading the five as one continuous identification recovers the substantive form of the *vidyā* (meditation-knowledge) at the close of the Adhyaya's heart-locus chain.

Second, the *vrīhi-yava* simile should be preserved as the substantive small-and-great paradox of the Brahmana. The simile names the purusha as *small like a grain of rice or barley* and immediately follows with the substantive statement of his universal rulership. The closing of the sentence — *he rules all this, whatsoever exists* — sits on the same passage as the size-simile and the heart-locus and the *manomaya* identification. The substantive doctrine is not that the purusha is small (alone) or great (alone) but that the same purusha is at once both, with the small-as-a-grain figure at the bodily-interior dimension and the universal-sovereignty figure at the cosmic dimension. The *vrīhi-yava* simile is the figure of the paradox, not a single dimensional claim.

- **Single-passage body.** The Brahmana's entire substantive content sits on one passage, `5.1202`. No multi-passage span, no internal verse-divisions beyond the verse-1 marker, no narrative interruptions. The form matches the Third Brahmana of the Adhyaya as the most compact in the Brihadaranyaka.
- **Footnote irregularity.** Passage `5.1202` carries footnote `[*2]` on *being light indeed*. No footnote `[*1]` is given on any passage of the Brahmana. The numbering irregularity matches the form of the Fourth Brahmana of the Adhyaya at `5.1194`, where footnotes also begin at `[*2]` rather than `[*1]`. The substance of the verse is unaffected, and the irregularity is preserved as found in the digitized source rather than silently repaired.

The form is the Adhyaya's standard compact close, matching the closing forms of Parts 3, 4, and 5.

## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 5, Part 5 — paired immediate-predecessor Brahmanas of the Fifth Adhyaya holding paired purusha-loci across the cosmic and bodily registers. The Fifth Brahmana works the dual identification of *the true* with the Aditya (the sun) — the cosmic seat — and with the person in the right eye — the bodily seat — with the mutual-resting clause *These two rest on each other* and the body-Vyāhṛti correspondence applied to both. The Sixth Brahmana works the *manomaya purusha* at a third bodily locus — within the heart, small like a grain of rice or barley — with universal sovereignty as the closing clause. The two Brahmanas hold paired substantive purusha-identifications across three distinct bodily-and-cosmic seats: the solar orb, the right eye, and the heart's interior. The Adhyaya's procedure of compact substantive teachings continues across the Brahmana boundary with the substantive seat changing.
- **structural-parallel** → Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 5, Part 3 — paired Brahmanas of the Fifth Adhyaya holding the heart as the substantive seat of compact identifications. The Third Brahmana opens with *Pragapati is the heart, is this Brahman, is all this* and works the *hridaya* etymology of *hri-da-yam* with three substantive benefits. The Sixth Brahmana opens with *That person, under the form of mind (manas), being light indeed, is within the heart* and works the *manomaya purusha* identification with the *vrīhi-yava* size-simile and universal sovereignty. Both Brahmanas are two passages long, both work the heart as the substantive seat, and both close with substantive doctrines worked through compact procedural forms — etymology in the Third and size-simile-and-sovereignty in the Sixth. The two Brahmanas form the Adhyaya's two most-compact units and hold the heart-locus across two distinct procedural shapes.
- **structural-parallel** → Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 4, Part 2 — paired Brihadaranyaka loci on the substantive purusha within the heart. The Second Brahmana of the Fourth Adhyaya holds the right-eye person named as Indha and identified as Indra mysteriously, with the wife Virag in the left eye and the heart's interior anatomy (meeting-place, food, covering, road, Hita veins) as their substantive seat — five named parts of the heart's interior given in extended doctrinal form across multiple verses. The Sixth Brahmana of the Fifth Adhyaya gives the same heart-as-substantive-seat doctrine in radically compressed form: the *manomaya purusha* within the heart, small like a grain of rice or barley, ruler of all. The two Brahmanas hold paired substantive teachings on the purusha within the heart across two distinct scales — the Fourth Adhyaya's extended five-part heart-anatomy and the Fifth Adhyaya's compressed five-part single-sentence identification.
