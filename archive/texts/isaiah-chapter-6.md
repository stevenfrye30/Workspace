# Isaiah, Chapter 6

Chapter 6 of the Book of Isaiah is the call vision of the prophet. In the year that king Uzziah died — around 740 BCE — Isaiah sees the Lord enthroned in the Temple, surrounded by six-winged seraphim who cry "Holy, holy, holy is the Lord of hosts; the whole earth is full of his glory." A seraph purifies Isaiah's lips with a coal from the altar; when God asks who will go for him, Isaiah answers "Here am I; send me." The commission that follows is paradoxical: Isaiah is sent to harden the people's hearts, not to soften them, until the cities lie wasted.

## Context

The Book of Isaiah is the longest of the prophetic books of the Hebrew Bible — sixty-six chapters, conventionally divided by modern scholarship into three parts. The first part (chapters 1–39) preserves material attributed to the historical prophet Isaiah of Jerusalem, who prophesied in the eighth century BCE under several kings of Judah; the second part (chapters 40–55), known as Second or Deutero-Isaiah, is composed by an anonymous prophet of the Babylonian exile around the middle of the sixth century BCE; the third part (chapters 56–66) is post-exilic, composed in the late sixth or fifth century BCE. The book reached its final form through a long process of redaction.

Chapter 6 belongs to the first part of the book and is generally regarded as the original call vision of Isaiah of Jerusalem himself. It opens with the historical anchor "in the year that king Uzziah died" — around 740 BCE — and is one of the rare passages in the prophetic literature that narrates a prophet's commission as a first-person account. The chapter falls between the earlier oracles of chapters 1–5 and the long Syro-Ephraimite cycle that begins in chapter 7; some scholars hold that chapter 6 was originally the opening of an earlier collection and was placed in its current position by a later redactor.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the threefold "Holy, holy, holy" of the seraphim (6:3) is one of the most precisely echoed lines in later religious tradition. The Hebrew phrase — *qadosh, qadosh, qadosh* — repeats the word three times as a form of intensification, the closest Hebrew can come to "most holy"; the line later became the Trisagion of Christian and Jewish liturgy. Reading the verse with the awareness that it is a syntactic device, not a theological formula, recovers the form the text gives.

Second, the commission Isaiah receives is difficult. God sends Isaiah not to soften the people's hearts, but to make them "fat" — slow, unhearing — and to keep them from understanding, "lest they see with their eyes, and hear with their ears, and understand with their heart, and convert, and be healed." The closing dialogue ("How long, O Lord?" — until the cities are wasted) does not relieve the difficulty. Reading the chapter with this commission held visible, rather than rounded off into a more familiar prophetic framing, keeps the strangeness the text has.
