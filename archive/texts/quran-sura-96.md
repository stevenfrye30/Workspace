# Qur'an, Sura 96 (Al-'Alaq), Verses 1–19

Sura 96 of the Qur'an — *Al-'Alaq*, "The Clot" — is the sura Islamic tradition associates with the first revelation. Its opening verses (96:1–5) are traditionally identified as the words spoken to Muhammad by the angel Gabriel in the cave of Hira on Mount Nur near Mecca, around 610 CE. The remaining verses (96:6–19) are placed by the tradition at a later occasion of revelation but stand together with the first five as part of the same sura in the canonical arrangement. The sura is short — nineteen verses — and its opening five are among the most widely-known passages of the Qur'an.

## Context

The Qur'an is the central scripture of Islam: a 114-sura collection in classical Arabic, understood within Islamic tradition as the speech of God transmitted to Muhammad over a span of roughly twenty-three years (c. 610–632 CE), first in Mecca and later in Medina. The text was assembled in writing in the decades following Muhammad's death; the standard codex, the *mushaf*, was fixed by the third caliph ʿUthmān around 650 CE and is the basis of all later transmissions. The suras in the canonical arrangement are not in chronological order: they are arranged roughly from longest to shortest, after a short opening sura (al-Fatiha).

Sura 96 stands in the late part of this order despite its early traditional date. In the chronological sequence reconstructed by both classical Islamic scholarship and Western philology, verses 1 through 5 of Sura 96 are commonly placed first, or among the first, of the revelations. The narrative of Muhammad receiving these verses from Gabriel — the angel taking him three times in the embrace, the command "Iqra'!" ("Recite!" or "Read!"), Muhammad's terror, and his return to his wife Khadija to be wrapped in cloth — is preserved in the early biographical and hadith literature.

In the tradition's account, verses 1 through 5 form one revelation and verses 6 through 19 form another, addressed against a hostile man at the Ka'ba in Mecca who prevents prayer. The two parts share the same sura number in the Qur'anic order but are placed at different occasions.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, *qur'an* in Arabic means *recitation*. The text was an oral utterance before it was a book, and its rhythm and rhyme structure are the architecture of the Arabic. Reading Sura 96 with the awareness that any English version is a translation of an oral text — whose rhymes, sound-patterns, and pauses cannot fully cross — is closer to the form. The Arabic passages most worth hearing in recitation, even without the language, are the closing verses (96:9–19), where the rhyme is sharp and short.

Second, the sura is composed of two distinct revelations. The opening five verses are the call to recite and the brief account of human creation and the gift of the pen. The remaining fourteen verses are a different occasion: a warning against a named opponent who forbids prayer at the Ka'ba. Reading 96:1–5 and 96:6–19 as two short pieces brought together in one sura, rather than as a single continuous statement, tracks the way classical Islamic exegesis treats the chapter.
