# Taittiriyaka Upanishad, Book 3, Part 4

Book 3, Part 4 of the Taittiriyaka Upanishad — the Fourth Anuvaka of the Bhriguvalli — is the third iteration of the dialogic engine and the central one. The sentence the Third Anuvaka left open with the em-dash *Having performed penance—* is completed here in the same three short clauses with one substitution: *mano brahmeti vyajānāt* — *he perceived that mind is Brahman.* The reasoning clause repeats the form Varuna had given as the Brahman-definition in the First Anuvaka, with mind in the place of *that*: *for from mind these beings are born; by mind, when born, they live; into mind they enter at their death.* Bhrigu returns to his father with the same opening line he has given at the threshold of the Valli and at the close of every iteration so far — *adhīhi bhagavo brahmeti* — *Sir, teach me Brahman.* Varuna's reply is identical to the reply he has given before: *try to know Brahman by penance, for penance is the means of knowing Brahman.* The Anuvaka closes on the same line and the same em-dash that have closed the First, Second, and Third Anuvakas — *He performed penance. Having performed penance—* — and the dialogue carries directly across the Anuvaka boundary into the Fifth, where the next recognition will be reached.

## Context

The Taittiriyaka Upanishad is one of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads — a small group of texts traditionally received as the philosophical core of the late Vedic literature. It belongs to the Krishna Yajurveda branch of the Vedic corpus and to the Taittiriya school of that branch; the Upanishad sits inside the Taittiriya Aranyaka. Modern scholarship places it among the older prose Upanishads, in roughly the sixth to fifth century BCE — earlier than the verse Upanishads of the Katha and Mundaka. The text is anonymous; the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship.

Book 3, Part 4 is the fourth of the ten anuvakas of the Bhriguvalli and the central one of the five recognitions Bhrigu will reach. The dialogic engine is now stable. Each of the three recognitions so far — food, breath, mind — has filled the same sentence-template in the same form: *X is Brahman, for from X these beings are born; by X, when born, they live; into X they enter at their death.* The Anuvaka is identical in structure to the Second and Third; only the named term has changed. Two recognitions remain — understanding and bliss — and the iteration will continue with the same form for both, until the engine reaches the level at which the procedure itself stops and the closing recognition is given.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the iteration is now at its central point, and the form has fully shown itself. Three substitutions have been made — food, breath, mind — and the iteration is unchanged in everything else. Reading the Anuvaka with the previous two iterations held alongside it, attending to the place where the substitution sits inside the sentence-template, recovers the form the Bhriguvalli is using when its iteration is at its plainest.

Second, the substitutions move inward in the same order the Brahmanandavalli's recursive descent moved inward, and the order is the order of the *pañca-kośa* teaching the two Vallis share. Reading the Anuvaka with attention to the placement of mind between breath and understanding — to the order of the iteration as well as to the substitution itself — keeps the form the Bhriguvalli has when it is most clearly aligned with the Brahmanandavalli's deeper structure. The two Vallis hold the same five terms in the same order, the one through recursive descent and the other through iterative dialogue.

Book 3, Part 4 of the Taittiriyaka — the entity here — occupies passages `5.345` through `5.348`. Passage `5.345` is the "Fourth Anuvaka" header. Passage `5.346` carries Bhrigu's third recognition — *He perceived that mind is Brahman* — completing the sentence that ended with the em-dash at the close of the Third Anuvaka. Passage `5.347` carries Bhrigu's return to Varuna, the renewed request *Sir, teach me Brahman*, and Varuna's reply on penance — the prose is identical in vocabulary to the corresponding passages in the Second and Third Anuvakas. Passage `5.348` carries the closing line *He performed penance. Having performed penance—*, ending again on the same em-dash that closed the First, Second, and Third Anuvakas. The em-dash is structural and identical across the iterations. Passage `5.349` is the "Fifth Anuvaka" header that opens Book 3, Part 5 of the Taittiriyaka and is not part of this entity. The library chapter continues past this entity into the rest of the Taittiriyaka and the remaining Upanishads of the volume. About one minute's reading.

## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Taittiriyaka Upanishad, Book 3, Part 3 — paired immediately consecutive Anuvakas of the Bhriguvalli; the Third Anuvaka completed the open sentence with the recognition that breath is Brahman, the Fourth Anuvaka completes the same open sentence with the recognition that mind is Brahman. The two iterations differ in only one term inside an otherwise identical sentence-template; the central iteration of the Valli now sits between breath and understanding.
- **structural-parallel** → Taittiriyaka Upanishad, Book 2, Part 4 — paired moments inside the same Upanishad on mind as Brahman; the Brahmanandavalli's Fourth Anuvaka holds the framing sloka of the *manomaya* — *yato vāco nivartante, aprāpya manasā saha; ānandaṁ brahmaṇo vidvān, na bibheti kutaścana* — *he who knows the bliss of that Brahman, from whence all speech, with the mind, turns away unable to reach it, he never fears* — set as the framing recognition of the mind-self inside the recursive descent, the Bhriguvalli's Fourth Anuvaka holds the recognition in dialogic form: Bhrigu, after penance, perceives that mind is Brahman because from mind beings are born, by mind they live, into mind they enter at death. Two registers of the same recognition — recursive descent and progressive dialogue — in the same Upanishad's two vallis.
- **structural-parallel** → Katha Upanishad, Book 1, Part 3 — paired Sanskrit passages on the placement of mind in the inward hierarchy; the Katha's Third Valli holds the line *beyond the senses there are the objects, beyond the objects there is the mind, beyond the mind there is the intellect, beyond the intellect the great Self, beyond the Great the Undeveloped, beyond the Undeveloped the Person, and beyond the Person nothing* (Katha 1.3.10–11), the Bhriguvalli's Fourth Anuvaka places mind as the third recognition inside an iteration that will move on to understanding and bliss. Both texts hold mind in the same position — neither at the surface of the senses nor at the inmost ground — and both texts move inward from it.
