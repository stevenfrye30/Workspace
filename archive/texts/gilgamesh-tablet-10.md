# Epic of Gilgamesh, Tablet X

Tablet X of the Standard Babylonian *Epic of Gilgamesh* takes the king to the edge of the world. Worn by grief over his friend Enkidu and refusing to accept his own mortality, Gilgamesh has crossed the mountains of the sunset and reached the cosmic ocean. The tablet contains his successive encounters with Siduri the alewife at her tavern by the sea, Urshanabi the boatman, and finally Utnapishtim, the one human granted eternal life after the flood. Tablet X ends as Gilgamesh states his fear of death and Utnapishtim begins his reply — the reply that fills Tablet XI.

## Context

The Standard Babylonian *Epic of Gilgamesh* is an eleven-tablet poem in Akkadian, assembled in the late second millennium BCE. The traditional attribution is to Sin-leqi-unninni, a Babylonian priest-scholar of the Kassite period (c. 1300–1100 BCE), who reworked older Sumerian and Old Babylonian material into the recension that survives. The fullest copies come from the seventh-century BCE library of Ashurbanipal at Nineveh — the same source from which George Smith first read out the deluge tablet in 1872.

Tablet X is the penultimate tablet. It opens with Gilgamesh, dressed in animal skins and worn by long travel, arriving at the seashore at the world's edge. The scenes that follow are episodes: Siduri counseling him from her tavern, Urshanabi ferrying him over the Waters of Death with punting poles, the arrival at Utnapishtim's island, and Utnapishtim's first questions to the visitor. The tablet ends with Gilgamesh naming his fear of death and Utnapishtim beginning his reply.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, Tablet X is best read together with the tablets on either side. Tablet IX takes Gilgamesh through the mountain pass and the path of the sun; Tablet XI is Utnapishtim's flood story, told in answer to the question Tablet X poses. Reading Tablet X as one stage of a single arc — wandering, encounter, arrival, question — keeps the tablet's place in the epic visible. It does not stand alone the way Tablet XI sometimes can.

Second, the speeches of Siduri and Urshanabi in Tablet X are not framed as wisdom teachings the reader is meant to lift out and quote. They are episodes inside the king's exhausted refusal. Gilgamesh hears them, does not accept them, and presses on. Reading the famous passages of Tablet X — Siduri's counsel to enjoy life, Utnapishtim's questions about the king's appearance — as moments inside Gilgamesh's grief rather than as detachable maxims tracks the form the tablet was given.

## The text

The Archive library does not hold Tablet X as a clean reconstructed unit. The library's nineteenth- and early-twentieth-century Mesopotamian holdings — George Smith's 1876 *Chaldean Account of Genesis*, the same source the library uses for Tablet XI's deluge material, and Stephen Langdon's 1917 *Epic of Gilgamesh*, which covers Old Babylonian fragments only — predate the modern reconstruction of the Standard Babylonian recension. For Tablet X as a clean reconstructed unit, Andrew George's 2003 Oxford edition is the recommended reference; Tablet X itself is roughly 320 lines, about an hour's reading.
