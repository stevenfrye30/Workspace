# Dhammapada, Chapter 4 (Flowers)

Chapter 4 of the Dhammapada — the *Puppha-vagga*, "Flowers" — is a short chapter built around a single image: the flower. The verses move through several uses of it. Flowers are what the seeker, like a clever man finding the right one, must learn to recognise (44–45). Flowers are what the distracted gatherer is busy with when death carries him off (47–48). The bee that takes nectar without injuring the flower is the figure of the sage (49). A beautiful flower without scent and one with scent are the figures of fruitless and fruitful speech (51–52). The scent of virtue surpasses the scent of sandalwood and other perfumes (54–56). And a lily growing from a heap of rubbish is the figure of the disciple shining forth among those who walk in darkness (58). The chapter holds the figure steady through fifteen verses, turning it through different positions.

## Context

The Dhammapada is one of the books of the Khuddaka Nikaya, the fifth and shortest section of the Sutta Pitaka in the Pali Canon. It collects 423 verses arranged in twenty-six chapters by theme — pairs, heedfulness, mind, flowers, the sage, evil, and so on. The verses are attributed to the Buddha and were transmitted orally for several centuries before being committed to writing in Sri Lanka in the first century BCE. The collection took shape in roughly the third century BCE, in connection with the Theravada transmission of the canon.

Chapter 4 is the *Puppha-vagga*, "Flowers." Its verses are numbered 44 through 58 in F. Max Müller's 1881 translation, the version held in the Archive library — fifteen passages, where the standard continuous Pali numbering of the Dhammapada gives sixteen verses (44–59). Müller has combined what is conventionally verses 58 and 59 into a single English period at passage 4.58: the *lily-on-the-rubbish-heap* simile and the *disciple-shining-among-those-who-walk-in-darkness* application are run together as one sentence. Per Atlas-rule **OCR/transcription honesty**, the merged passage is preserved as Müller wrote it; the chapter therefore has fifteen passages here against the standard sixteen verses elsewhere. The next chapter opens at passage 5.60. Each verse is short — two or four lines in the Pali — and each completes one statement of the figure.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the chapter is built on a single figure — the flower — that returns through every verse. Verses 44–45 use the right-flower as the figure for the path of virtue; verses 47–48 use the gatherer-of-flowers as the figure for distraction overtaken by death; verse 49 uses the bee-and-flower as the figure for the sage; verses 51–52 use scentless-and-scented flowers as the paired contrast for fruitless and fruitful speech; verses 54–56 use the scent of flowers as the figure for the carrying power of virtue; verse 58 uses the lily-on-rubbish as the figure for the disciple. Reading the chapter with the flower held visible across the fifteen passages, rather than as fifteen separate aphorisms, recovers the form the chapter is built on.

Second, Müller's merging of conventional verses 58 and 59 into a single passage at 4.58 is a translator's choice, not an absence of the second statement. The lily-on-rubbish simile and the disciple-shining-among-the-blinded application are both there, joined by *thus* into one English sentence. Reading the passage with the awareness that the standard sixteenth verse is preserved inside, rather than missing, keeps the form of the chapter intact even where the numbering departs from the standard.
