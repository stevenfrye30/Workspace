# Book of Job, Chapters 1–2

The first two chapters of the Book of Job — the prose prologue — open one of the wisdom books of the Hebrew Bible. In a few short scenes, a righteous man named Job loses his property, his children, and his health on consecutive days, on the strength of a wager between God and a member of the divine council called the *satan*, the accuser. The prologue ends with Job sitting among the ash of his ruined life, and three friends arriving from far away to sit with him in silence. The long poetic dialogues that fill the rest of the book begin from this scene.

## Context

The Book of Job is one of the three principal wisdom books of the Hebrew Bible, alongside Proverbs and Ecclesiastes. Its setting is non-Israelite — Job is from the land of Uz, a place east of Israel that the text never identifies more precisely — and its protagonist is not a member of the chosen line. The book is in two parts of unequal length and very different style: a short prose frame, comprising chapters 1–2 and the closing chapter 42, and a long central section in poetry (chapters 3 to 41) of dialogues between Job and three friends, a fourth speaker named Elihu, and finally God speaking from the whirlwind.

The composition is layered. The prose frame may draw on an older folk-tale about the figure of Job; the poetic dialogues are the work of a different and probably later hand. Modern scholarship places the assembled book in the Persian period — roughly the sixth through fourth centuries BCE — though the underlying story is older. The Hebrew text contains some of the most difficult vocabulary and most contested readings in the Hebrew Bible, particularly in the poetic chapters; the prose prologue is comparatively clear.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the prose prologue is a frame, not a moral. Chapters 1 and 2 are written with a fairy-tale economy — *there was a man in the land of Uz* — and they do not yet argue anything. They set up a situation. The argument starts in chapter 3 when Job opens his mouth. Reading 1–2 as the setting of a problem rather than as the answer to one keeps the rest of the book available.

Second, the satan in chapters 1–2 is not the later figure of Christian or Islamic tradition. The Hebrew word here, *ha-satan*, takes the definite article — *the* satan — and names a role rather than a personal name: a member of the divine council whose function is to test, accuse, and inspect. Reading the prologue with this distinction in view, rather than against the assumptions a later reader brings to the word, recovers the scene the original audience would have heard.
