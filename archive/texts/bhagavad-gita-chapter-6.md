# Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 6 (Dhyana Yoga)

Chapter 6 of the Bhagavad Gita — the *Dhyana Yoga*, conventionally translated "The Yoga of Meditation" (Arnold's translation gives the chapter the parallel name *Atma Samyama Yoga*, "The Book of Self-Restraint") — continues Krishna's reply to Arjuna and turns inward, from disciplined action toward the practice of disciplined attention. The chapter opens by completing the synthesis of Chapter 5 — the true renouncer is the yogi who acts without attachment — and then describes the practical setting of meditation: a clean place, a firm seat, the body held in a steady posture, the senses gathered, the mind fixed on one point. Moderation in food, sleep, and recreation is the condition; the state arrived at is described as the seeing of the self in the self. Arjuna asks how this is possible given the mind's restlessness; Krishna answers with two short terms — practice (*abhyasa*) and dispassion (*vairagya*). The chapter closes with the question of what becomes of the yogi who fails to attain, and Krishna's reply that no effort is lost: the unsuccessful yogi is reborn into favorable circumstances and continues.

## Context

The Bhagavad Gita is a 700-verse philosophical poem in eighteen chapters, set inside Book VI (the *Bhishma Parva*) of the Mahabharata. Most scholars place its present form between roughly the second century BCE and the second century CE — a layered composition that took shape during the long growth of the epic from an older bardic core. The traditional attribution is to Vyasa, the legendary compiler of the Mahabharata; the historical authorship is unknown.

Chapter 6 stands at the close of the Gita's first major movement. Chapters 2–6 take up the question Arjuna's refusal raised — what is right action, given the situation he finds himself in? — and answer it from successive angles: the imperishable self and action without attachment (Chapter 2), the impossibility of inaction and the welfare of the world (Chapter 3), the lineage of the teaching and the practice of sacrifice (Chapter 4), the synthesis of renunciation and action-yoga (Chapter 5), and the inner discipline of attention that holds the answer steady (Chapter 6). The chapter that follows — Chapter 7 — turns to a different register, in which Krishna begins to speak of his own divine nature directly and the Gita takes up the metaphysical and devotional themes the early chapters have only implied. Chapter 6 is the consolidation of the early Gita's instructional arc.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the chapter alternates between practical instruction and psychological description without separating them. A passage on the physical setting of meditation — a clean place, a steady seat, an upright body — sits beside a passage on the mind's behavior, its restlessness, its tendency to wander, its capacity to come to rest; a passage on moderation in food and sleep sits beside the description of the state in which the self is seen in the self. Reading the chapter with the alternation visible, rather than as either a how-to manual or a doctrinal exposition, recovers the form the chapter has.

Second, the chapter develops earlier arguments rather than introducing new ones. The disciplined yogi who sees the same in all beings, the mind held steady, the action that does not bind, the renunciation that is also action — all of these have been stated in Chapters 2–5. Chapter 6 returns to them through the practice that holds them together. Reading the chapter as the consolidation of the early Gita's instructional arc, rather than as the beginning of a new doctrine, follows the way the chapter is composed.

## The text

The text is held in the Archive library under `bhagavad-gita`, chapter 6 (Edwin Arnold's verse translation, *The Song Celestial*, 1885 — Project Gutenberg edition). Arnold's verse translation compresses the chapter's forty-seven Sanskrit shlokas into twelve English verse-paragraphs followed by a closing colophon line (passages `6.1`–`6.13` in the library's verse-level subdivision). A modern prose translation by the International Gita Society is held in the library as a parallel translation. About thirty minutes' reading.

## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 5 (Karma Sannyasa Yoga) — the immediate Gita-internal pair; Chapter 5 contracts the early argument into a synthesis of renunciation and action-yoga and ends with a short practical passage on inner disposition, Chapter 6 takes that practical close and develops it into a sustained chapter on the practice of disciplined attention.
- **structural-parallel** → Dhammapada, Chapter 2 (Heedfulness) — paired short chapters on the cultivation of attention; the Dhammapada's *Appamāda-vagga* turns twelve verses on the contrast between heedfulness and its lapse, Chapter 6 of the Gita gives forty-seven verses on the practical setting and inner conditions of the disciplined attention the early Gita has been building toward.
- **structural-parallel** → Epictetus, Enchiridion, Chapters 1–5 — paired short instructional sequences on inner composure under outward conditions; the Enchiridion sets the distinction between what is and is not within one's power and works it across desire, attachment, action, and the source of disturbance, Chapter 6 of the Gita sets the inner state — moderate, steady, attentive, undistracted — that performs disciplined action without binding.
