# Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 8 (Akshara Brahma Yoga)

Chapter 8 of the Bhagavad Gita — the *Akshara Brahma Yoga*, conventionally translated "The Yoga of the Imperishable Brahman" (Arnold's translation gives the chapter the parallel name "The Book of Religion by Devotion to the One Supreme God") — opens with Arjuna re-entering the dialogue. He puts a series of direct questions to Krishna: what is Brahman; what is the inner self; what is action; what are the technical terms the previous chapter has used (*adhibhuta*, *adhidaiva*, *adhiyajna*); and how can the self-controlled know Krishna at the time of death? Krishna answers each in short statements and then turns the chapter toward the practical question that motivated Arjuna's last question: what a person remembers at the moment of death is what he becomes, and steady remembrance is built through long practice rather than summoned at the end. The closing third of the chapter steps outside the immediate question — the cosmic day and night of Brahma, the two paths departing souls take, the imperishable beyond the manifest and unmanifest — and frames the practical center at successively larger scales.

## Context

The Bhagavad Gita is a 700-verse philosophical poem in eighteen chapters, set inside Book VI (the *Bhishma Parva*) of the Mahabharata. Most scholars place its present form between roughly the second century BCE and the second century CE — a layered composition that took shape during the long growth of the epic from an older bardic core. The traditional attribution is to Vyasa, the legendary compiler of the Mahabharata; the historical authorship is unknown.

Chapter 8 belongs to the Gita's middle movement. Where Chapter 7 widened the frame from the discipline of attention to the description of Krishna's nature, Chapter 8 takes up a question Chapter 7's ending had raised — those who know him at the time of departure go beyond death — and develops it. Arjuna re-enters the dialogue with direct questions about the technical terms Chapter 7 has used and asks how the self-controlled may know Krishna at the moment of death. Krishna's answer occupies the rest of the twenty-eight verses: short statements on the terms, the principle that what a person remembers at death is what he becomes, the practice of holding the mind on the supreme through life, the cosmic time-scales of Brahma's day and night, the two paths departing souls take, and the highest abode. The chapter consolidates the middle movement's widening cosmological frame around the practical question of orientation at the end of life.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the chapter is structured as question and layered answer. Arjuna asks his questions in the opening verses; Krishna answers each, but the answers are not strict definitions — they are short statements that gesture toward the meaning each technical term carries, after which the chapter moves on. Reading the chapter as a sequence of layered images rather than as a glossary, and accepting that some terms (like *adhyatma* or *adhibhuta*) receive only a one-line characterization, follows the form Krishna gives the answer.

Second, the chapter returns repeatedly to remembrance and orientation. The middle of the chapter — its practical center — turns on the principle that what a person remembers at the moment of death is what he becomes, and that this remembrance is built through long practice rather than summoned at the end. The cosmic descriptions that follow — the day and night of Brahma, the two paths after death, the imperishable beyond the manifest and unmanifest — frame the same point at successively larger scales. Reading the chapter with the practical center held visible, rather than treating the cosmological passages as separate teaching, recovers the form the chapter has.

## The text

The text is held in the Archive library under `bhagavad-gita`, chapter 8 (Edwin Arnold's verse translation, *The Song Celestial*, 1885 — Project Gutenberg edition). Arnold's verse translation compresses the chapter's twenty-eight Sanskrit shlokas into twelve English verse-paragraphs followed by a closing colophon line (passages `8.1`–`8.13` in the library's verse-level subdivision). - **structural-parallel** → Plato, Apology — paired short texts in which a teacher articulates the proper relation to death; the *Apology* with Socrates explaining to the jurors that death holds no terror for one who has lived examining himself, Chapter 8 with Krishna explaining that what a person remembers at death is what he becomes and that the practice of remembrance is built through life.
- **structural-parallel** → Gospel of Matthew, Chapter 5 — paired short texts that ground the future state of a life in present orientation; the Beatitudes with their formula "Blessed are X, for they shall Y" attaching present condition to inherited end, Chapter 8 with the principle that the mind's long-cultivated direction determines what is reached at the moment the body is left.
