# Taittiriyaka Upanishad, Book 3, Part 9

Book 3, Part 9 of the Taittiriyaka Upanishad — the Ninth Anuvaka of the Bhriguvalli — closes the short food-rule triplet that began at the Seventh. The same triple form returns: rule, paired-term reasoning, reward formula. The rule: *annaṁ bahu kurvīta, tad vratam* — *let him acquire much food, that is the rule.* The reasoning pairs the rule with the cosmic terms — *the earth is food, the ether eats the food; the ether rests on the earth, the earth rests on the ether; this is the food resting on food.* The Anuvaka closes with the formula that has now been used four times in close succession (the Sixth Anuvaka closing the recognition cycle, the Seventh on breath and body, the Eighth on water and light, and now the Ninth on earth and ether): *he who knows this food resting on food, rests exalted, becomes rich in food, and able to eat food (healthy), becomes great by offspring, cattle, and the splendour of his knowledge of Brahman, great by fame.* Three rules — never abuse food, never shun food, acquire much food — and three pairs — breath/body, water/light, earth/ether — close on the same reward.

## Context

The Taittiriyaka Upanishad is one of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads — a small group of texts traditionally received as the philosophical core of the late Vedic literature. It belongs to the Krishna Yajurveda branch of the Vedic corpus and to the Taittiriya school of that branch; the Upanishad sits inside the Taittiriya Aranyaka. Modern scholarship places it among the older prose Upanishads, in roughly the sixth to fifth century BCE — earlier than the verse Upanishads of the Katha and Mundaka. The text is anonymous; the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship.

Book 3, Part 9 is the ninth of the ten anuvakas of the Bhriguvalli. The Sixth Anuvaka closed the recognition cycle and named the closing as the *knowledge of Bhrigu and Varuna*; the Seventh, Eighth, and Ninth Anuvakas hold three short food-rule iterations that share a common reward formula and that move the paired-term reasoning out from breath and body, through water and light, to earth and ether. The triplet has now closed; the Tenth Anuvaka opens a different motion. The pairing in this Ninth Anuvaka — earth and ether — is the most cosmic of the three pairs and connects the food-rule sequence to the wider Vedic scheme of correspondences between the body and the cosmos that runs through the principal Upanishads.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the Anuvaka closes the food-rule triplet without announcing the closure. The same form is repeated; the same formula is given; only the substance of the paired terms changes. Reading the Anuvaka with the Seventh and Eighth held alongside it, attending to the progression of the pairs (breath/body, water/light, earth/ether) and to the unchanging reward, recovers what the triplet has been doing — three short rules placed in succession, each given the same standing.

Second, the cosmic level of the Ninth Anuvaka's pair is the level the recognition-cycle did not name and that the rest of the Bhriguvalli has not visited. The recognition-cycle moved from food, breath, mind, understanding, to bliss; the food-rules moved from body and breath, through water and light, to earth and ether. The two sequences traverse different fields. Reading the Anuvaka with attention to the placement of earth and ether at the close — the cosmic ground of the food-rule sequence — keeps the form the Bhriguvalli has when its food-theme reaches its widest extent.

## The text

The text is held in the Archive library under `upanishads-muller-part2`, chapter 5 (Müller translation, 1884 — *The Sacred Books of the East*, Volume XV; via SacredTexts.com). Library chapter 5 is shared across thirty-eight Archive entities — four parts of the Katha Upanishad, the six parts of the Mundaka Upanishad, the twelve anuvakas of the Sikshavalli, the nine anuvakas of the Brahmanandavalli, and the First through Eighth Anuvakas of the Bhriguvalli — and continues here into the Ninth Anuvaka without break in the chapter. Book 3, Part 9 of the Taittiriyaka — the entity here — occupies passages `5.362` and `5.363`. Passage `5.362` is the "Ninth Anuvaka" header. Passage `5.363` carries the full content of the Anuvaka in a single prose passage — the rule, the earth-and-ether reasoning, and the reward formula — without the editorial paragraphing that split the Seventh and Eighth Anuvakas across multiple passages. The passage ends mid-word at "great b" where Müller's standard closing "great by fame" is cut off in the digitized source — a transcription artifact carried over from the OCR of Müller's volume that does not affect the readability of the line. Passage `5.364` is the "Tenth Anuvaka" header that opens Book 3, Part 10 of the Taittiriyaka and is not part of this entity. The library chapter continues past this entity into the rest of the Taittiriyaka and the remaining Upanishads of the volume. Under one minute's reading.

The triplet of food-rules sits in two adjacent units that finish together, the form unchanged across all three.
- **structural-parallel** → Taittiriyaka Upanishad, Book 2, Part 7 — paired moments inside the same Upanishad on food, nourishment, and the recognition that bliss is the source of breath and life; the Brahmanandavalli's Seventh Anuvaka holds *raso vai saḥ* — *he is flavour itself* — and the line *who could breathe, who could breathe forth, if that bliss did not exist in the ether of the heart?*, the Bhriguvalli's Ninth Anuvaka holds the closely related continuity in practical form: earth, ether, food, and breath in mutual support. Both texts hold the recognition that food, breath, and the ether of the heart are bound together in the same source.
- **structural-parallel** → Mundaka Upanishad, Book 2, Part 1 — paired Sanskrit passages on the elements as the field through which life is supported; the Mundaka's First Khanda of the Second Mundaka holds the cascade *from him is born breath, mind, and all organs of sense, ether, air, light, water, and the earth, the support of all* (Mundaka 2.1.3) — the elements named as what comes forth from the source — the Bhriguvalli's Ninth Anuvaka holds earth and ether at the cosmic boundary of the food-rule, with each resting on the other. Paired moments where the late-Vedic literature places earth, ether, and the support of life in close relation.
