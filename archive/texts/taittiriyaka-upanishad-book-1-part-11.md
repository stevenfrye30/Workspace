# Taittiriyaka Upanishad, Book 1, Part 11

Book 1, Part 11 of the Taittiriyaka Upanishad — the Eleventh Anuvaka of the Sikshavalli — is the convocation address: the speech a teacher gives the student at the end of the period of Vedic study, when the student returns to household life. Its first sentence places the address: *vedam anūcyācāryo'ntevāsinam anuśāsti* — "after having taught the Veda, the teacher instructs the pupil." Then the imperatives. *Satyam vada. Dharmam cara. Svādhyāyān mā pramadaḥ.* Speak the truth. Do thy duty. Do not neglect the study of the Veda. After bringing the teacher the proper gift, do not cut off the line of children. Do not swerve from the truth. Do not swerve from duty. Do not neglect what is useful. Do not neglect greatness. Do not neglect the learning and the teaching of the Veda. Do not neglect the works due to the Gods and the Fathers. Then the formula that has carried the Anuvaka into Indian moral life ever since: *mātṛdevo bhava, pitṛdevo bhava, ācāryadevo bhava, atithidevo bhava* — let thy mother be to thee like unto a god; let thy father be to thee like unto a god; let thy teacher be to thee like unto a god; let thy guest be to thee like unto a god. Only blameless actions are to be regarded; only the good works of those who came before are to be observed; whatever is given is to be given with faith, with joy, with modesty, with fear, with kindness. The Anuvaka turns to the case of doubt — about a sacred act, or about conduct — and gives the rule: do as the Brahmanas of good judgment, not too severe and devoted to duty, would do; and with regard to things that have been spoken against, do as the same Brahmanas would do. The closing seal — *eṣa ādeśaḥ, eṣa upadeśaḥ, eṣā vedopaniṣat, etad anuśāsanam* — names the address itself as *the rule, the teaching, the true purport (Upanishad) of the Veda*.

## Context

The Taittiriyaka Upanishad is one of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads — a small group of texts traditionally received as the philosophical core of the late Vedic literature. It belongs to the Krishna Yajurveda branch of the Vedic corpus and to the Taittiriya school of that branch; the Upanishad sits inside the Taittiriya Aranyaka. Modern scholarship places it among the older prose Upanishads, in roughly the sixth to fifth century BCE — earlier than the verse Upanishads of the Katha and Mundaka. The text is anonymous; the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship.

Book 1, Part 11 is the most-quoted Anuvaka of the Sikshavalli and the one most often excerpted independently — recited at university convocations across India, embedded in textbooks, drawn on as a compact statement of *dharma*. Its place in the Valli is structural: the Sikshavalli has spent ten anuvakas placing the recitation, naming the disciplines, and giving the verses to be carried, and the Eleventh now gives the address that sends the student into household life with the recitation and the disciplines intact. The closing identification of the address as *the Upanishad of the Veda* — the *eṣā vedopaniṣat* line — names this Anuvaka inside the school's own self-understanding as the practical core of the teaching: not the metaphysical recognitions of later vallis, but the rule under which the recognitions can be carried out.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the Anuvaka is built on imperatives. *Satyam vada, dharmam cara* — the verbs are direct, in the second person, and the constructions are short. The middle of the Anuvaka turns from the negative imperatives (*do not swerve, do not neglect*) to the positive *like unto a god* formula and then to the formula of *with faith, with joy, with modesty, with fear, with kindness*. Reading the Anuvaka aloud, allowing the imperative form to register before any attempt to systematize the content, recovers the form the convocation address has — the speech a teacher gives the student, in the rhythm in which it is given.

Second, the Anuvaka makes its closing rule about doubt without abstraction. When in doubt about a sacred act or about conduct, the Anuvaka does not give a principle; it gives an example: *do as the Brahmanas of good judgment do — those who are not too severe, and who are devoted to duty.* The form is the form of practical instruction in the late-Vedic school: principle by example, judgment located in named persons rather than in abstract categories. Reading the Anuvaka with the closing rule held alongside the long list of imperatives, rather than as a separate doctrine of moral epistemology, keeps the form the convocation address has — instruction that prepares the student to act, not to theorize about acting.

## The text

The text is held in the Archive library under `upanishads-muller-part2`, chapter 5 (Müller translation, 1884 — *The Sacred Books of the East*, Volume XV; via SacredTexts.com). Library chapter 5 is shared across twenty Archive entities — four parts of the Katha Upanishad, the six parts of the Mundaka Upanishad, and Books 1, Parts 1 through 10 of the Taittiriyaka — and continues here into the Eleventh Anuvaka without break in the chapter. Book 1, Part 11 of the Taittiriyaka — the entity here — occupies passages `5.215` through `5.221`. Passage `5.215` is the "Eleventh Anuvaka" header. Passages `5.216` through `5.220` carry the four numbered verses of the Anuvaka in Müller's prose presentation, with the verse-numbered markers visible at `1.` (start of `5.216`), `2.` (start of `5.217`), `3.` (start of `5.218`), and `4.` (start of `5.220`). Verse 3 is split across passages `5.218` and `5.219` where Müller's editorial paragraphing interrupts the verse mid-line. Passage `5.221` opens with the editorial sub-marker `(1-7)` carried over from the source — Müller's bracketed grouping that signals the closing seal as a continuation of verse 4 rather than as a separate verse. Passage `5.222` is the "Twelfth Anuvaka" header that opens Book 1, Part 12 of the Taittiriyaka (the closing peace mantra of the Sikshavalli) and is not part of this entity. The library chapter continues past this entity into the rest of the Taittiriyaka and the remaining Upanishads of the volume. About four minutes' reading.
