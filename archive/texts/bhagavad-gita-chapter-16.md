# Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 16 (Daivasura Sampad Vibhaga Yoga)

Chapter 16 of the Bhagavad Gita — the *Daivasura Sampad Vibhaga Yoga*, conventionally translated "The Yoga of the Distinction Between Divine and Demonic Endowments" (Arnold's translation gives the chapter the parallel name "The Book of the Separateness of the Divine and Undivine") — returns from the symbolic condensation of Chapter 15 to direct ethical contrast. The chapter opens with a long list of qualities Krishna calls the divine endowment (*daivi sampad*) — fearlessness, purity of heart, steadfastness, charity, self-restraint, sacrifice, study, austerity, uprightness, non-violence, truth, freedom from anger, renunciation, peace, compassion, gentleness, modesty, vigor, forgiveness, fortitude, freedom from malice, freedom from pride — and a much shorter list of the demonic endowment (*asuri sampad*): ostentation, arrogance, conceit, anger, harshness, ignorance. The chapter then develops the demonic in detail across most of its remaining verses — the thinking it produces, the actions it issues in, the consequences it brings — and closes with the three gates of hell (desire, anger, greed) and the instruction to take scripture as authority for what should and should not be done.

## Context

The Bhagavad Gita is a 700-verse philosophical poem in eighteen chapters, set inside Book VI (the *Bhishma Parva*) of the Mahabharata. Most scholars place its present form between roughly the second century BCE and the second century CE — a layered composition that took shape during the long growth of the epic from an older bardic core. The traditional attribution is to Vyasa, the legendary compiler of the Mahabharata; the historical authorship is unknown.

Chapter 16 belongs to the Gita's late classificatory movement and continues the work of Chapters 13, 14, and 15 — distinction, threefold typology, image — in a different register. Where Chapters 13 and 14 set their distinctions in analytic terms and Chapter 15 condensed them into image, Chapter 16 turns to direct moral contrast: the divine endowment and the demonic endowment, the qualities and consequences of each. The chapter is short (twenty-four verses in the standard Sanskrit numbering) and structurally simple: a list of divine qualities (16.1–3), a brief naming of demonic ones (16.4), the principle that the divine leads to liberation and the demonic to bondage (16.5), a long unfolding of the demonic — its thinking, its actions, the rebirths it brings (16.6–20) — and a closing teaching on the three gates of hell and on scripture as authority (16.21–24). The chapter's tone is more forceful than the Gita's general register, and the demonic receives notably more attention than the divine.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the chapter is asymmetric. The divine endowment is named in a single sweep at the opening; the demonic is named compactly at 16.4 and then developed in sustained detail across the chapter's middle. Reading the chapter with the asymmetry held visible — the divine assumed and listed, the demonic followed step by step into its consequences — recovers the form the chapter has and the work it is doing.

Second, the demonic section moves through escalating consequences. The opening identifies the qualities (ostentation, arrogance, anger); the middle traces the thinking those qualities produce ("this is mine and this also will be mine; I have killed that enemy and will kill those others"; "I am the lord, the enjoyer, perfect, mighty, happy"); the close traces the rebirths into demonic wombs that follow. Reading the demonic section as a sequence — quality, thinking, action, consequence — rather than as a single moral indictment, follows the way the chapter is composed.

## The text

The text is held in the Archive library under `bhagavad-gita`, chapter 16 (Edwin Arnold's verse translation, *The Song Celestial*, 1885 — Project Gutenberg edition). Arnold's verse translation here returns to a moderate compression: the chapter's twenty-four Sanskrit shlokas are gathered into eight English verse-paragraphs followed by a closing colophon line (passages `16.1`–`16.9` in the library's verse-level subdivision). The two opening lists — the divine endowment in 16.1–3 and the demonic in 16.4 — are preserved as enumeration; the longer development of the demonic across the rest of the chapter is held across several verse-paragraphs that follow the Sanskrit's escalation from quality to thinking to consequence. A modern prose translation by the International Gita Society is held in the library as a parallel translation. About fifteen minutes' reading.

## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 15 (Purushottama Yoga) — the immediate Gita-internal pair; Chapter 15 condenses earlier teaching into image and the doctrine of the three persons, Chapter 16 returns from image to direct ethical contrast and gives the divine and demonic endowments as paired sets of qualities and consequences.
- **structural-parallel** → Gospel of Matthew, Chapter 5 — paired short chapters that organize ethical teaching through opposition; the Sermon on the Mount with its sequence of antitheses ("Ye have heard that it was said… but I say unto you") setting received practice against an intensified instruction, Chapter 16 of the Gita with the divine and demonic endowments named as paired lists and the consequences of each followed through to their ends.
- **structural-parallel** → Dhammapada, Chapter 2 (Heedfulness) — paired short chapters built on a single binary contrast that runs through the chapter and attaches to different ends; the *Appamāda-vagga* with the heedful walking the path of the deathless and the heedless the path of death, Chapter 16 with the divine endowment leading to liberation and the demonic to bondage.
