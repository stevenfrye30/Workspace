# Dhammapada, Chapter 7 (The Venerable)

Chapter 7 of the Dhammapada — the *Arahanta-vagga*, "The Venerable" — is a short chapter of ten verses on the figure of the *arahant*, the one who has finished the journey. The opening verse states the form: there is no suffering for him who has finished his journey, abandoned grief, freed himself on all sides, and thrown off all fetters (90). The verses that follow keep the figure in view across several positions. The venerable depart with thoughts well-collected, like swans leaving their lake (91). Their path is difficult to track, like the path of birds in the air (92–93). The gods even envy them, for their senses are well broken in like horses (94). They are tolerant like the earth, firm like Indra's bolt, clear like a lake without mud (95). Their thought, word, and deed are quiet (96). They have cut all ties and know the uncreated (97). And wherever the venerable dwell, in hamlet or forest, on water or dry land, that place is delightful — for the passionless find delight where the world finds none (98–99).

## Context

The Dhammapada is one of the books of the Khuddaka Nikaya, the fifth and shortest section of the Sutta Pitaka in the Pali Canon. It collects 423 verses arranged in twenty-six chapters by theme — pairs, heedfulness, mind, flowers, the sage, evil, and so on. The verses are attributed to the Buddha and were transmitted orally for several centuries before being committed to writing in Sri Lanka in the first century BCE. The collection took shape in roughly the third century BCE, in connection with the Theravada transmission of the canon.

Chapter 7 is the *Arahanta-vagga*, "The Venerable." Its ten verses are numbered 90 through 99 in the standard continuous numbering of the Dhammapada — and in F. Max Müller's 1881 translation, the version held in the Archive library, the count and numbering match the standard exactly, with no merged or missing verse of the kind that occurs at the close of Chapter 4 and Chapter 6 (where Müller combines two conventional verses into a single passage). The Pali word *arahant* — venerable, saint, one who has destroyed the *āsavas*, one worthy — is rendered by Müller as "venerable" in the chapter title and as "Arhanta" inside verse 98; modern translations more commonly transliterate as "arahant" or render as "saint" or "perfected one." Each verse is short — two or four lines in the Pali — and each completes one statement of the figure.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the chapter is built on the single figure of the venerable taken across positions of completion. Verse 90 states it: the journey is finished. Verse 91 turns to departure: thoughts well-collected, leaving home like swans leaving their lake. Verses 92–93 work the trackless path as a paired aphorism: the path of those who have perceived void and unconditioned freedom is difficult to understand, like the path of birds in the air. Verse 94 turns to subdued senses: the gods envy him whose senses are like horses well broken in. Verse 95 turns to firmness: tolerant like the earth, like Indra's bolt, like a lake without mud, no new births in store. Verse 96 turns to quietness: thought, word, deed. Verse 97 turns to the uncreated: ties cut, temptations removed, the greatest of men. Verses 98–99 turn to place: wherever the venerable dwell, that place is delightful — forest or village, water or land — and the passionless find delight where the world finds none. Reading the chapter with the figure of the venerable held visible across the ten verses, rather than as ten separate aphorisms, recovers the form the chapter is built on.

Second, the trackless-path imagery at verses 92–93 is the chapter's distinctive figure. The verses pair: men who live on recognised food, who have perceived void and unconditioned freedom — their path cannot be tracked; men whose appetites are stilled, who have perceived void and unconditioned freedom — their path cannot be tracked. Both close with "like that of birds in the air." Reading the paired verses as one figure with two statements, rather than as two separate aphorisms, keeps the form the verses share.

## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Dhammapada, Chapter 6 (The Wise Man) — the immediate predecessor on the shelf; Chapter 6 with thirteen passages on the *paṇḍita* — his teachers, his self-fashioning, his composure, and his crossing to the other shore — Chapter 7 with ten verses on the *arahant*, the one who has crossed and whose journey is finished. The two chapters work the same arc: the wise are those who set out, the venerable are those who arrive.
- **structural-parallel** → Dhammapada, Chapter 5 (The Fool) — the polar pair to Chapter 7's figure; Chapter 5 with sixteen verses on the fool whose evil deed slowly ripens and who is tormented by what he calls his, Chapter 7 with ten verses on the venerable whose deeds are finished and who calls nothing his.
- **structural-parallel** → Dhammapada, Chapter 1 (The Twin Verses) — the opening pair on the shelf; Chapter 1 with paired-verse contrasts on mind, action, and outcome, Chapter 7 returning to the paired form at verses 92–93 (the trackless path of those who have perceived void and unconditioned freedom).
- **structural-parallel** → Daodejing, Chapters 1–10 — the Chinese contemplative pair on the shelf; both work figures of the one who has arrived through compact verse-units, the Daodejing's opening chapters on the sage who acts without acting and is empty like a vessel, Dhammapada 7 on the venerable whose path cannot be tracked and who is quiet like a lake without mud.
