# Analects, Book 2

The second book of the Analects — *Wei Zheng*, "On Governing" — continues the sayings-collection of Book 1 in a slightly different key. Its twenty-four short sections open with the comparison of governing by virtue to the north polar star, around which the other stars turn; turn from there to a brief remark on the Book of Poetry; pause for Confucius' famous six-stage autobiographical sketch ("At fifteen, I had my mind bent on learning… at seventy, I could follow what my heart desired without transgressing what was right"); and continue through four short exchanges on filial piety with named disciples, the well-known formulation that "learning without thought is labor lost; thought without learning is perilous," and a closing pair of remarks on ritual flattery and the failure to do what one knows to be right.

## Context

The Analects (論語, *Lúnyǔ*, "Discussions and Sayings") is a twenty-book collection of short sayings and conversations involving Confucius (Kongzi, 551–479 BCE) and his named disciples. The book was compiled posthumously by the disciples and their disciples over a period of perhaps two centuries. Modern scholarship places the earliest layers in the fourth century BCE and the assembly of the received twenty-book text by the early Han dynasty (second to first century BCE). The text's transmission was stabilized in the Han period, and the most influential later commentary descends from the Southern Song scholar Zhu Xi (1130–1200), who placed the Analects within his canonical Four Books.

Book 2, *Wei Zheng* — "Conducting Government" — takes its title from the opening words of its first section. It has twenty-four short sections in the standard numbering. The book continues the conversational form of Book 1, mixing Confucius' direct sayings with short exchanges between him and his disciples (Mang I, Fan Ch'ih, Tsze-yu, Tsze-hsia, Tsze-kung, Tsze-chang, and others). Topics shift between sections: government and virtue (2:1, 2:3); the recitation of the Book of Poetry (2:2); the trajectory of a life (2:4); four short exchanges on filial piety with four different disciples (2:5–8); the assessment of persons (2:10); the warming of old knowledge into new (2:11); the relation of learning to thinking (2:15); and ritual flattery and courage in the closing section (2:24).

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the book is short and discontinuous. Each section stands as its own utterance — sometimes Confucius' short statement, sometimes a question from a disciple followed by his reply, sometimes a remark without surrounding context. The sections do not develop a single argument; they sit beside one another. Reading Book 2 as twenty-four short readings, each complete in itself, follows the form. The book is not an essay on government; it is a sequence of recorded fragments arranged by the editor.

Second, the topics shift between political counsel and personal conduct without warning. A passage on governing by virtue (2:1) sits beside a remark on the Book of Poetry (2:2), beside Confucius' autobiographical sketch (2:4), beside four short exchanges on filial piety (2:5–8), beside a saying on learning and thought (2:15). Reading the book with attention to the alternation between public and private domains, rather than expecting them to be addressed in separate stretches, recovers the form the book has.
