# Brihadaranyaka Upanishad, Book 5, Part 5

Book 5, Part 5 of the Brihadaranyaka Upanishad — the Fifth Brahmana of the Fifth Adhyaya — is a composite Brahmana slightly more developed than the Adhyaya's prior compact units. Where Parts 1–4 had run through chained substantive identifications (Om as Brahman, Pragapati as the heart, the heart as the true) and a parabolic narrative (Pragapati and the threefold descendants), the Fifth Brahmana works four distinct procedures across four numbered verses on five short passages: a cosmogonic chain, a *nirukti* etymology of the word *satyam*, the dual identification of *the true* with the sun and the right-eye person, and parallel body-anatomies of the two persons through the three Vedic *Vyāhṛti*s. The Brahmana continues the Adhyaya's compact register but holds four operations rather than one.

The Brahmana opens directly into the cosmogony at verse 1. *In the beginning this (world) was water.* The opening clause is one of the Brihadaranyaka's earliest cosmogonies given in the *agre* (in the beginning) form — the same opening-form the Brihadaranyaka had used at its First Brahmana of the First Adhyaya for the cosmic horse-sacrifice. The substantive chain follows in five short steps: water → the true (*satyam*) → Brahman → Pragapati → the Devas (gods). The chain ends with the *adoration*: *The Devas adore the true (satyam) alone.* The form holds the substantive doctrine that the gods themselves stand in adoration of *the true* — the *satyam* that is identical with Brahman.

The closing of verse 1 turns the cosmogony into a *nirukti* etymology. *This satyam consists of three syllables. One syllable is sa, another t(i), the third yam.* The Sanskrit word *satyam* is broken into three substantive syllables: *sa, t(i), yam*. The middle syllable is rendered with the parenthetical *(i)* — *t(i)* — to mark its compressed form in the underlying Sanskrit. The substantive doctrine on the syllables follows: *The first and last syllables are true, in the middle there is the untrue.* The first and last syllables — *sa* and *yam* — are designated *true* (the same substantive *satyam*); the middle syllable — *t(i)* — is designated *untrue* (*anrita*). The closing line gives the substantive teaching: *This untrue is on both sides enclosed by the true, and thus the true preponderates. The untrue does not hurt him who knows this.* The form holds the *vidyā* (meditation-knowledge) procedure of the Adhyaya: knowing the position of the untrue inside the true gives the knower freedom from the untrue's harm.

Verse 2 turns to the substantive identification of *the true*. *Now what is the true, that is the Aditya (the sun), the person that dwells in yonder orb, and the person in the right eye.* The clause holds a triple identification: the true = the Aditya (the sun) = the person in the solar orb = the person in the right eye. The Adhyaya's prior triple identifications (Om = ether = Brahman in Part 1, Pragapati = heart = Brahman = all in Part 3, heart = that = the true = great glorious first-born = true Brahman in Part 4) have a counterpart here in the substantive identification of *the true* across the cosmic and bodily registers. The cosmic seat is the sun; the bodily seat is the right eye; the mediating substantive name is *the true*.

Verse 2 then holds the mutual-resting clause. *These two rest on each other, the former resting with his rays in the latter, the latter with his pranas (senses) in the former.* The cosmic person rests his rays in the bodily person; the bodily person rests his *pranas* (senses) in the cosmic person. The form is one of the Brihadaranyaka's most-cited statements of the cosmic-bodily mutual-rest doctrine, and it closes verse 2 with the dying-state observation: *When the latter is on the point of departing this life, he sees that orb as white only, and those rays (of the sun) do not return to him.* The dying person — at the moment of departure — sees the sun's orb as white only, with the rays not returning to him. The line names the substantive death-state through which the bodily person disconnects from the cosmic person at the time of dying.

Verses 3 and 4 work parallel body-anatomies through the three Vedic *Vyāhṛti*s — *Bhuh, Bhuvah, Svar*. Verse 3 holds the person in the solar orb. *Of the person in that (solar) orb Bhuh is the head, for the head is one, and that syllable is one; Bhuvah the two arms, for the arms are two, and these syllables are two; Svar the foot, for the feet are two, and these syllables are two.* The body-syllable correspondence runs in three steps: Bhuh (the first *Vyāhṛti*, one syllable) = head (one); Bhuvah (the second *Vyāhṛti*, two syllables) = arms (two); Svar (the third *Vyāhṛti*, two syllables in its expanded *suvar* recitation) = foot (two). The closing seal of verse 3: *Its secret name is Ahar (day), and he who knows this destroys (hanti) evil and leaves (gahati) it.* The secret name (*upaniṣad*-name in the technical sense — the meditation-name) is **Ahar** (day), and the closing pun on *hanti* (destroys) and *gahati* (leaves) gives the substantive benefit of the meditation.

Verse 4 holds the person in the right eye in the same form. *Of the person in the right eye Bhuh is the head, for the head is one, and that syllable is one; Bhuvah the two arms, for the arms are two, and these syllables are two; Svar the foot, for the feet are two, and these syllables are two. Its secret name is Aham (ego), and he who knows this, destroys (hanti) evil and leaves (gahati) it.* The body-anatomy is the same; the secret name is different — **Aham** (ego, *I*) — and the closing pun is the same. The two persons share the body-syllable correspondence and the closing pun-on-evil; they differ only in the secret name (*Ahar* vs *Aham*).

The Brahmana ends on verse 4's substantive closing line. No closing seal, no narrator's voice, no *held his peace* form, no recapitulation. The Adhyaya's compact procedure here gives the Brahmana's close inside its substantive teaching.

## Context

The Brihadaranyaka Upanishad is one of the two earliest of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads, sharing that position with the Chandogya. It belongs to the Shukla Yajurveda branch of the Vedic corpus and forms the closing portion of the Shatapatha Brahmana — the *Aranyaka* in its name marks it as a forest-text recited at the late stages of Vedic study. Modern scholarship places the Brihadaranyaka in roughly the eighth to sixth century BCE, prior to the Buddha; the text is anonymous, and the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship. The text is the longest of the principal Upanishads and is typically structured in six *adhyayas* (chapters), each containing several *brahmanas* (sections).

Book 5, Part 5 holds three of the Adhyaya's most distinctive features in concentrated form. The first is the third etymological-syllable teaching of the Adhyaya. The Second Brahmana had given the threefold *Da* — *Damyata, Datta, Dayadham* — across three classes of askers; the Third Brahmana had given the three syllables of *hridaya* (heart) — *hri, da, yam* — with three substantive benefits; the Fifth Brahmana now gives the three syllables of *satyam* (the true) — *sa, t(i), yam* — with the middle syllable singled out as *the untrue* enclosed by the true on either side. The Adhyaya works the *upāsana-akshara* (meditation-syllable) procedure across three Brahmanas with three different Sanskrit words, and the syllable *yam* recurs across the *hridaya* etymology and the *satyam* etymology as the closing syllable in both cases. The structural recurrence preserves the Adhyaya's procedural unity through the syllabic procedure even as the substantive teaching shifts from injunction (Part 2) to bodily-cosmic identification (Part 3) to ontology of truth (Part 5).

The second is the cosmogony at the open of verse 1. *In the beginning this (world) was water.* The same form had been given at the First Brahmana of the First Adhyaya — the Brihadaranyaka's opening — with the cosmic horse-sacrifice as the substantive content. The Fifth Brahmana of the Fifth Adhyaya gives the *agre* form again with a different cosmogonic chain: water → the true → Brahman → Pragapati → Devas. The doctrine of the gods adoring the true holds Pragapati as the production-point between Brahman and the Devas, and locates *the true* (*satyam*) as the substantive name of Brahman that the gods adore. The cosmogony's brevity — five steps in a single sentence — is one of the Brihadaranyaka's most compressed cosmogonic chains, and it sits at the open of the Fifth Brahmana as the substantive ground for the *satyam* etymology that follows.

The third is the parallel body-anatomy of the two persons. The Adhyaya's earlier compact Brahmanas had worked single substantive identifications (Om = Brahman; Pragapati = heart; heart = the true). The Fifth Brahmana works two parallel identifications — the person in the solar orb and the person in the right eye — through the same body-syllable correspondence and the same pun-on-evil closing. The two persons share the *Vyāhṛti* correspondence (Bhuh = head, Bhuvah = arms, Svar = foot) but differ in their secret names (Ahar / Aham). The pairing of the cosmic-and-bodily figures recalls the doctrine the Second Brahmana of the Fourth Adhyaya had named explicitly through Indha and Virag — the right-eye person and his wife in the left eye — and the Third Brahmana of the Fourth Adhyaya had named through the dual-state analysis of waking and dream. The Fifth Brahmana of the Fifth Adhyaya gives the same cosmic-and-bodily pairing in compressed *upāsana-akshara* form.

The closing form is the Adhyaya's standard compact close. No *held his peace*, no kingdom-offer, no closing seal beyond the substantive content of the last verse. The Brahmana ends inside its teaching.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the Brahmana's four verses should be read as four parallel procedures rather than as a single doctrinal statement. Verse 1 is cosmogony plus *nirukti*; verse 2 is dual identification plus mutual-resting plus dying-state; verses 3 and 4 are paired body-anatomies through the *Vyāhṛti*s with paired secret names. Reading the four verses with each procedure held distinct from the others — neither collapsed into a single doctrine of *the true* nor flattened into a generic statement of cosmic-bodily correspondence — recovers the Brahmana's literary form: four operations on the same substantive matter, each with its own procedural shape.

Second, the *nirukti* on *satyam* should be preserved at the level of letter-form. The middle syllable is rendered with the parenthetical *(i)* — *t(i)* — to mark its compressed form in the underlying Sanskrit. The substantive teaching that the middle syllable is *the untrue* (*anrita*) and that the first and last (*sa* and *yam*) are *the true* (*satyam*) holds a doctrine of the position-of-falsehood inside truth: the untrue is held inside the true on both sides, and the true preponderates by enclosure. Reading the *nirukti* with the substantive doctrine preserved — neither smoothed away into a generic statement of truth nor expanded into a Vedanta-doctrinal interpretation — recovers the *vidyā*'s substantive promise: knowing the position of the untrue inside the true gives the knower freedom from the untrue's harm.

The Brahmana contains several boundary features that should be preserved honestly:

- **Verse-1 four-footnote cluster on cosmogony and etymology.** Passage `5.1197` carries verse 1 — the cosmogony plus the *satyam* etymology — with four successive footnotes: `[*1]` on *the true*, `[*2]` on *Pragapati*, `[*3]` on *the third*, and `[*4]` on *the untrue*. Müller's notes attach at the cosmogony's substantive pivot (the true), at Pragapati as the cosmogonic intermediate, at the third syllable, and at the substantive designation of the middle syllable as the untrue. The four-footnote density on a single passage is the densest in the Adhyaya so far.
- **OCR irregularity at `5.1197`.** Passage `5.1197` carries the line *Water produced the true [*1], and the true is 'Brahman.* — the stray apostrophe before *Brahman* is preserved in the digitized source. The apostrophe is a transcription artifact carried over from Müller's volume; the substantive content of the cosmogony is unaffected, and the irregularity is preserved as found rather than silently repaired.
- **Letter-form rendering of the middle syllable.** Passage `5.1197` renders the second syllable of *satyam* as *t(i)* with the parenthetical *(i)* preserved as the digitized source has it. The form marks the compressed substantive content of the syllable in the underlying Sanskrit; Müller's translation preserves the rendering with the parenthetical, and the Archive preserves it without smoothing into either *t* or *ti*.
- **Verse-2 single-passage substantive content.** Passage `5.1198` carries verse 2 — the dual identification of *the true* with the Aditya and the person in the right eye, the mutual-resting clause, and the dying-state observation — on a single passage without internal splits and without footnotes. The substantive content runs across three sub-clauses (identification, mutual-resting, dying-state) with no internal verse-numbering or narrative break.
- **Verse-3 footnote on *Svar*.** Passage `5.1199` carries verse 3 — the body-anatomy of the person in the solar orb — with footnote `[*1]` on *Svar*. Müller's note glosses the syllable-count of *Svar* — the third *Vyāhṛti* is conventionally read as *suvar* in some Vedic recitations, giving it two syllables for the foot-correspondence. The footnote attaches at the substantive number-correspondence inside the body-syllable mapping.
- **Verses 3 and 4 paired body-anatomies.** Passages `5.1199` and `5.1200` give the same body-syllable correspondence — Bhuh = head (1), Bhuvah = arms (2), Svar = foot (2) — across two parallel substantive subjects (the person in the solar orb, the person in the right eye). The two passages hold verbatim the same form *Bhuh is the head, for the head is one, and that syllable is one; Bhuvah the two arms, for the arms are two, and these syllables are two; Svar the foot, for the feet are two, and these syllables are two*, with only the substantive subject-name (*the person in that (solar) orb* / *the person in the right eye*) and the secret name (*Ahar* / *Aham*) differing across the two verses.
- **Closing pun on *hanti* and *gahati*.** Both verses 3 and 4 close with the same substantive pun-on-evil: *he who knows this destroys (hanti) evil and leaves (gahati) it*. The pun runs on the Sanskrit verbs *han* (to destroy) and *gā* (to leave) and is preserved in Müller's translation with the parenthetical glosses. The form holds the *vidyā* substantive benefit of the meditation-name knowledge.

Passage `5.1201` is the "Sixth Brahmana" header that opens Book 5, Part 6 of the Brihadaranyaka and is not part of this entity.
