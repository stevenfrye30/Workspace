# Taittiriyaka Upanishad, Book 3, Part 1

Book 3, Part 1 of the Taittiriyaka Upanishad — the First Anuvaka of the Bhriguvalli, the Third Valli — opens the third and last of the three vallis of the Taittiriyaka. The Brahmanandavalli closed in the previous Archive entity with the formal seal *iti upaniṣat*; the Bhriguvalli opens with a new peace mantra — the same *Saha navavatu* mantra that opened the Brahmanandavalli, given again here as the framing recitation for the Third Valli — and immediately enters a different literary mode. The mode is dialogue: father and son, teacher and disciple. *Bhṛgur vai vāruṇir varuṇaṁ pitaram upasasāra; adhīhi bhagavo brahmeti* — *Bhrigu Varuni went to his father Varuna, saying: Sir, teach me Brahman.* Varuna's first answer names the avenues by which the inquiry will proceed — *annam, prāṇaṁ, cakṣuḥ, śrotraṁ, manaḥ, vācam* — food, breath, the eye, the ear, mind, speech. Varuna then gives the formal definition the Bhriguvalli will work with for the rest of the Valli: *yato vā imāni bhūtāni jāyante, yena jātāni jīvanti, yat prayanty abhisaṁviśanti, tad vijijñāsasva. tad brahma.* — *that from whence these beings are born, that by which, when born, they live, that into which they enter at their death, try to know that. That is Brahman.* The Anuvaka closes on the means: Bhrigu performs penance. The closing sentence is incomplete — *He performed penance. Having performed penance—* — the dialogue carrying directly across the Anuvaka boundary into the Second, where Bhrigu's first answer will be given.

## Context

The Taittiriyaka Upanishad is one of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads — a small group of texts traditionally received as the philosophical core of the late Vedic literature. It belongs to the Krishna Yajurveda branch of the Vedic corpus and to the Taittiriya school of that branch; the Upanishad sits inside the Taittiriya Aranyaka. Modern scholarship places it among the older prose Upanishads, in roughly the sixth to fifth century BCE — earlier than the verse Upanishads of the Katha and Mundaka. The text is anonymous; the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship.

Book 3, Part 1 sits inside the Bhriguvalli, the Third Valli of the Taittiriyaka. Where the Sikshavalli worked in school-lessons and recitational discipline, and the Brahmanandavalli worked in recursive descent and reflective prose, the Bhriguvalli works in dialogue. The form will be characteristic and consistent across the Valli: Bhrigu approaches his father with the question *adhīhi bhagavo brahmeti* — *teach me Brahman*; Varuna gives a directional instruction; Bhrigu performs penance; Bhrigu returns with a recognition; Varuna sends him back to penance; the recognition deepens. The recognitions Bhrigu will reach — that food is Brahman, that breath is Brahman, that mind is Brahman, that understanding is Brahman, that bliss is Brahman — traverse the same five sheaths that the Brahmanandavalli had unfolded recursively, but the Bhriguvalli holds them in the form of a son's progressive understanding rather than as a textual structure. The Brahman-definition Varuna gives in this First Anuvaka — *that from whence these beings are born, by which they live, into which they enter at death* — is one of the most-cited Upanishadic formulas, repeated in close form across the principal Upanishads and central to the later Vedanta literature.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the Anuvaka is short and almost entirely set up. There is no recognition reached here, no answer given. Bhrigu asks, Varuna gives the avenues and the definition, Bhrigu goes to perform penance — and the Anuvaka closes mid-sentence on the way to the next. Reading the Anuvaka with attention to the form of the opening — request, instruction, departure for penance — and to the way the closing em-dash carries the dialogue into the next Anuvaka, recovers the form the Bhriguvalli has at its threshold. The Valli does not give its first recognition until the Second Anuvaka.

Second, the Brahman-definition Varuna offers is given as a question rather than as a doctrine. *Tad vijijñāsasva* — *try to know that* — is in the imperative, addressed to Bhrigu. The clauses that follow — that from which beings are born, by which they live, into which they enter at death — locate the inquiry rather than settle it. Reading the definition as direction rather than as conclusion, and watching how Bhrigu will work with it through the rest of the Valli, keeps the form the Bhriguvalli has — a teaching given as a question and worked through by penance and return.

## The text

The text is held in the Archive library under `upanishads-muller-part2`, chapter 5 (Müller translation, 1884 — *The Sacred Books of the East*, Volume XV; via SacredTexts.com). Library chapter 5 is shared across thirty-one Archive entities — four parts of the Katha Upanishad, the six parts of the Mundaka Upanishad, the twelve anuvakas of the Sikshavalli (Book 1) of the Taittiriyaka, and the nine anuvakas of the Brahmanandavalli (Book 2) — and continues here into the Bhriguvalli (Book 3) without break in the chapter. Book 3, Part 1 of the Taittiriyaka — the entity here — occupies passages `5.329` through `5.336`. Passage `5.329` is the "Third Valli" book header; passage `5.330` is the subtitle of the Third Valli, "Or, the Chapter of Bhrigu"; passage `5.331` carries the *Saha navavatu* peace mantra that opens the Bhriguvalli, with its own footnote reference `[*1]`; passage `5.332` is the "First Anuvaka" part header. Passages `5.333` through `5.336` carry the body of the Anuvaka in Müller's prose presentation, without verse-numbered markers — the Bhriguvalli, like the Brahmanandavalli, does not number its anuvaka-internal verses in the same way the Sikshavalli did. Passage `5.336` ends with an em-dash — *He performed penance. Having performed penance—* — the sentence syntactically incomplete, with the continuation landing at the start of the Second Anuvaka. This is not a digitization artifact but a feature of the Bhriguvalli's literary form: the dialogue carries directly across the Anuvaka boundaries throughout the Valli, with each Anuvaka closing on Bhrigu's departure for penance and the next opening on the recognition that follows. Passage `5.337` is the "Second Anuvaka" header that opens Book 3, Part 2 of the Taittiriyaka and is not part of this entity. The library chapter continues past this entity into the rest of the Taittiriyaka and the remaining Upanishads of the volume. About two minutes' reading.

## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Taittiriyaka Upanishad, Book 2, Part 9 — paired closure-and-opening across the Valli boundary; the Ninth Anuvaka of the Brahmanandavalli closed with the formal seal *iti upaniṣat* and the recognition that the knower is freed from regret about deeds, the First Anuvaka of the Bhriguvalli opens with a new peace mantra and immediately enters dialogue between Bhrigu and Varuna. The Upanishad continues in the same body but moves from the close of one Valli into the opening of another with a clear shift of literary mode: prose-and-sloka into question-and-penance dialogue.
- **structural-parallel** → Mundaka Upanishad, Book 1, Part 1 — paired Upanishadic openings on the inquirer approaching the teacher with the request for knowledge of Brahman; the Mundaka's First Khanda opens with Saunaka approaching Angiras *with fuel in hand* and asking *kasmin nu bhagavo vijñāte sarvam idam vijñātam bhavati* — *Sir, what is that through which, if it is known, everything else becomes known?* — the Taittiriyaka's First Anuvaka of the Bhriguvalli opens with Bhrigu approaching his father Varuna with the request *adhīhi bhagavo brahmeti* — *Sir, teach me Brahman.* Paired Upanishadic moments where the disciple's approach and the teacher's first instruction set the frame for the inquiry that follows.
- **structural-parallel** → Katha Upanishad, Book 1, Part 1 — paired Upanishadic openings in which a son figure approaches a teacher and presses his question; the Katha's First Valli opens with Nachiketas, sent by his angry father to Yama the god of death, where Yama receives him as guest and offers him three boons — and the boy presses the question that will carry the rest of the Upanishad — the Taittiriyaka's First Anuvaka of the Bhriguvalli opens with Bhrigu approaching his own father Varuna and pressing the same question. Two adjacent moments in the principal Upanishads where the inquirer's relation to the teacher is held inside a family relation and the question of Brahman is asked from inside it.
