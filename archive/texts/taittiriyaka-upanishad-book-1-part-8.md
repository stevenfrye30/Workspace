# Taittiriyaka Upanishad, Book 1, Part 8

Book 1, Part 8 of the Taittiriyaka Upanishad — the Eighth Anuvaka of the Sikshavalli — is the *Omkara*-praise of the Valli: a single short Anuvaka in which ten brief statements are gathered around the recitation of the syllable *Om*. The first three statements give the equivalences: *Om* means Brahman; *Om* means all this; *Om* means obedience — when they have been told *Om, speak*, they speak. The next five statements list the moments at which the recitation of the syllable opens what is recited. After *Om* the Samans are sung; after *Om* the hymns are recited; after *Om* the Adhvaryu gives the response; after *Om* the Brahman-priest gives the orders; after *Om* the sacrificer permits the Agnihotra. The last two statements turn the Anuvaka inward to its own setting — the lecture: when a Brahmana is going to begin his lecture, he says *Om*; he says *Om, may I acquire Brahman* — and by saying so, the closing line says, he acquires the Veda.

## Context

The Taittiriyaka Upanishad is one of the principal (*mukhya*) Upanishads — a small group of texts traditionally received as the philosophical core of the late Vedic literature. It belongs to the Krishna Yajurveda branch of the Vedic corpus and to the Taittiriya school of that branch; the Upanishad sits inside the Taittiriya Aranyaka. Modern scholarship places it among the older prose Upanishads, in roughly the sixth to fifth century BCE — earlier than the verse Upanishads of the Katha and Mundaka. The text is anonymous; the principal Upanishads are received in the tradition as *śruti*, the revealed Vedic literature, without identified authorship.

Book 1, Part 8 sits inside the Sikshavalli, the chapter on *śikṣā*. The Anuvaka turns the Sikshavalli's wider concern with recitation onto its first and shortest subject — the syllable that opens it. Where the Fifth and Sixth Anuvakas worked the *vyāhṛti* outward and inward, and the Seventh Anuvaka gathered fivefold lists, the Eighth Anuvaka is shorter and more concentrated: it gives the syllable *Om*, names the equivalences in which it stands, and lists the contexts in which it is the opening word — the Vedic chant, the priestly response, the household sacrifice, the lecture in the school. The Anuvaka is small but central; the syllable it praises is the syllable on which every preceding Anuvaka of the Sikshavalli (and the Upanishad as a whole) opens.

## Reading note

Two habits help. First, the Anuvaka is built on a single procedure — list the contexts in which *Om* is the opening word — and presents the procedure ten times in succession. The form is paratactic: each statement is short, parallel to the others, and offered without elaboration. Reading the Anuvaka aloud, allowing the parallelism to register before any attempt to draw it into a single doctrine, recovers the form the praise has — *Om* is named as the opening word by being placed at the opening of one short clause after another.

Second, the Anuvaka closes inward without changing its form. The first statements name *Om* in absolute terms (it means Brahman, it means all this, it means obedience); the middle statements list the priestly settings in which it opens recitation; the closing two statements turn to the lecture itself — the speaker about to teach says *Om, may I acquire Brahman* — and the line that follows says: *he thus acquires the Veda*. The closing turn is not an additional doctrine; it is the same procedure (locate where *Om* opens) applied to the Anuvaka's own school setting. Reading the closing two statements as the same form turned inward, rather than as a separate teaching, keeps the Anuvaka's compactness intact.

## The text

The text is held in the Archive library under `upanishads-muller-part2`, chapter 5 (Müller translation, 1884 — *The Sacred Books of the East*, Volume XV; via SacredTexts.com). Library chapter 5 is shared across seventeen Archive entities — four parts of the Katha Upanishad, the six parts of the Mundaka Upanishad, and Books 1, Parts 1 through 7 of the Taittiriyaka — and continues here into the Eighth Anuvaka without break in the chapter. Book 1, Part 8 of the Taittiriyaka — the entity here — occupies passages `5.206` and `5.207`. Passage `5.206` is the "Eighth Anuvaka" header. Passage `5.207` carries the full body of the Anuvaka in a single passage. Müller's prose presentation runs ten short statements end-to-end, each marked with its own number (`1.` through `10.`) inline, rather than splitting them across passages — a different formatting choice from the surrounding anuvakas, where Müller's footnotes typically interrupt the verse mid-line. There are no footnotes inside this passage, and no editorial sub-markers. Passage `5.208` is the "Ninth Anuvaka" header (carrying its own footnote reference `[*1]`) that opens Book 1, Part 9 of the Taittiriyaka and is not part of this entity. The library chapter continues past this entity into the rest of the Taittiriyaka and the remaining Upanishads of the volume. About one minute's reading.

## Related

- **structural-parallel** → Taittiriyaka Upanishad, Book 1, Part 7 — paired immediately consecutive Anuvakas of the Sikshavalli; the Seventh Anuvaka holds three pairs of fivefold lists with the closing rule that the inside set completes the outside set, the Eighth Anuvaka holds a single tenfold list gathered around the syllable *Om* — the same paratactic procedure of the late-Vedic school-lesson, applied in two adjacent units to two different ritual subjects.
- **structural-parallel** → Mundaka Upanishad, Book 2, Part 2 — paired Sanskrit passages on *Om* as the syllable that holds the recitation; the Mundaka's Second Khanda holds the figure that *Om* is the bow, the Self the arrow, Brahman the aim, the arrow becoming one with the target as the seeker becomes one with Brahman (Mundaka 2.2.4), the Taittiriyaka's Eighth Anuvaka places the same syllable at the opening of every priestly act and every lecture, naming it as the equivalent of Brahman and as the opening of the recitation that acquires the Veda — paired moments in the late-Vedic literature where the syllable is given the same central place.
- **structural-parallel** → Katha Upanishad, Book 1, Part 2 — paired Upanishadic passages on *Om* as the goal of the Vedic teaching; the Katha's Second Valli, in the early portion of Yama's substantive instruction, names the syllable *Om* as the word which all the Vedas record, which all penances proclaim, and which is the goal — *etad evākṣaraṁ brahma, etad evākṣaraṁ param* (Katha 1.2.15–17) — the Taittiriyaka's Eighth Anuvaka holds the same recognition in the form of the school-lesson, *Om iti brahma, Om itīdaṁ sarvam*, with the syllable named as Brahman, as all this, and as the opening of every recitation by which the Veda is acquired.
