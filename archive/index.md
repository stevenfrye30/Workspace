# Archive

Old texts, edited with care so they can be read.

This is a working archive of historical and religious writings, prepared page by page: lines unbroken where the original broke them for layout, footnotes clicked rather than chased, names and places steady across translations. Each text is read before it is placed. Nothing here is scraped.

It is a slow project, and it grows in the direction of whatever is being read.

## What is here

The fullest collection at present is the Christian texts: Augustine, the desert tradition, early liturgies, contested letters — grouped by period and origin, footnoted throughout.

Other traditions will appear as they earn their place.

### On this shelf

**[Daodejing, Chapter 1](entity.html?id=daodejing-chapter-1)** (c. 4th c. BCE, China) — The opening of the *Tao Te Ching*: four short verses naming what cannot be named — the gate of all that is subtle and wonderful. Legge's 1891 translation.

**[Dhammapada, Chapter 1 (The Twin Verses)](entity.html?id=dhammapada-chapter-1)** (c. 3rd c. BCE) — Twenty paired verses opening the Dhammapada, on mind, action, and outcome. The pairing is the form. Müller's 1881 translation.

**[Mundaka Upanishad, Book 1, Part 1](entity.html?id=mundaka-upanishad-book-1-part-1)** (c. 4th–2nd c. BCE, India) — The opening of the Mundaka: Saunaka asks Angiras what one knowledge gives knowledge of everything else, and is answered with the distinction between the higher knowledge and the lower. Müller's 1884 translation.

**The Didache** (late 1st – early 2nd c.) — Earliest known Christian manual; on baptism, prayer, and the ethics of the "two ways." Short enough to sit with in a single evening.

**Sayings of the Desert Fathers** (3rd – 4th c., Egypt) — Fragmentary wisdom from the first Christian hermits. Each saying is a single scene; the form itself teaches the tradition's register.

**[Augustine, Confessions, Book I](entity.html?id=augustine-confessions-book-1)** (c. 400) — The opening of the first Christian autobiography. Augustine addressing God, on infant memory and the shape of early desire.

**Pseudo-Dionysius, Divine Names** (late 5th c., Syrian) — Foundational text of apophatic theology: God known by what he is not. Dense enough that one chapter is a day's reading.

**[The Cloud of Unknowing](entity.html?id=cloud-of-unknowing)** (late 14th c., England) — Foundational Western apophatic contemplation: God reached by a stirring of love rather than thought. Underhill's 1922 modernization of the anonymous Middle English.

**Julian of Norwich, Revelations of Divine Love** (c. 1395, England) — Middle English reflections on visions received during a near-death illness. The Short Text version, as she wrote it first.

### A page left open

A brother came to Scetis to visit Abba Moses and asked him, "Father, give me a word." The old man said to him, "Go, sit in your cell, and your cell will teach you everything."

— *Sayings of the Desert Fathers*

## What is not here

This is not a complete library. It is not a substitute for a scholarly edition. It is not a search engine for religious quotations.

It is a small, careful room, holding the texts I have read or am reading, edited in the way I would want to read them.
